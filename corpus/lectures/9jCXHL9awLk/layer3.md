## §1. What a safety factor actually is [00:03]

`§1.p1` As a consultant for a large company, it was a little interesting. They were mostly structural and civil engineers, so I'll talk to you a little bit about bridges and buildings. But because of my work with Tom Eagar, I was getting all sorts of work as well — I was the go-to guy for mechanics and materials. Out of three or four hundred people I ended up becoming more and more diversified as time was going on. Professor Eagar asked me if I wanted to prepare this relatively general talk, because today we'll talk about safety factor as it applies to different industries.

`§1.p2` The idea here is not to be super specific. There are a few ideas I want to carry across, and one of them is: we don't really design structures stronger than they need to be. When we say we have a safety factor of three, that's based on a nominal analysis. It doesn't take into account all the worst-case scenarios that we know of, or that we don't know of. When you take everything into account, it's more from experience that we know if we build it twice as strong, we should be able to go through a 50- or 100-year life with a probability of failure on the order of 10 to the minus six.

`§1.p3` So the safety factor of two is not for somebody to say, "I have a safety factor of two, well, I'm going to put only half the bolts, I don't really need the others." That's not how it works. In reality you only have a margin of say twenty percent, and it's there for whatever mistakes along the way, or improper use in any condition that was not considered in the original design, plus normal statistics.

`§1.p4` There are two definitions. One is failure load versus allowable load. The second one — yield stress versus working stress — is more for pressure vessels. You'll already see, moving forward, that pressure vessels have a higher safety factor, but that relates to how it's calculated. If you're doing a working-stress analysis, you're already saying, I'm not taking into account water hammer, for example. So you know you're going to have higher stresses than what you're designing for. The analysis is scaled back into something simpler.

`§1.p5` If you want to do a more sophisticated analysis you'll say, well, I know my pressure is going to be twice as high because of transient situations, so I double the stress level at the bottom, and then I can reduce my safety factor by a factor of two. It turns out to be the same. It depends on how detailed you're going to be. You also have to be really clear, and that's why it's based on experience, on what you mean by that safety factor. Is it divided by the allowable load or the working stress? If you don't know that, you'll have a problem defining what your safety factor should be for a given structure.

`§1.p6` When somebody working in automotive says they use a safety factor of three, they have a very particular way of calculating capacity, the failure load, and what is an allowable load for that structure. They put sensors on the wheels and go through rough roads and say, this is the highest load. Or they just take the weight of the automobile and divide it by four. That makes a big difference when it comes down to determining the safety factor you need.

## §2. Industry-by-industry safety factors [06:23]

`§2.p1` We have a big range of numbers. The failure of the canal levee in New Orleans was very unfortunate, and when they went back to the design — safety factor of 1.3. It turns out that's what they designed it for, and it wasn't enough. When Professor Eagar talked about learning from disaster, that's an example. Even though you were supposed to have that thirty percent margin — and it's big, it's huge — it's relatively easy to analyze and inspect. There were issues with the soil, there were a lot of things going on that were hard to predict 50 or 100 years before. That's why in the end they didn't have the capacity they thought they had.

`§2.p2` In that one the water level was not higher than what they designed for. When we do an analysis, we look at two things: the capacity of the structure and the load applied to it. The load was taken into account in the design — it's not as if there was an earthquake happening at the same time, it was just water level. The capacity was not quite what they thought it would be through the design life.

`§2.p3` Aircraft structures — I put a 1.5 safety factor, because 1.6 maybe would be the one I've seen most. I worked for a bit at Bombardier Aerospace; they make small airframes. At the end of the day you go to a lower safety factor around the landing gear. It's typically very well inspected, very well determined. They have sensors everywhere to know exactly what the load history is for the landing gear, and therefore they can afford a lower safety factor. You save a tremendous amount of weight. And in a very controlled condition, you reduce the risk for something completely unusual to happen. If you have regular inspections you're more likely to pick up on small cracks that will grow, than if you say I'm going to build it strong enough and it's not going to be accessible for inspection, and we hope everything is okay for 20 years. That happens — in some small aircraft structures you can't really see inside the wings, so they don't really get inspected. And then all of a sudden you have cracks six inches, eight inches long, that were not predicted by design because of twisting modes that are harder to evaluate.

`§2.p4` So your inspection comes into play, and how precisely you know the load. If you have a tension member and the aircraft weighs only so much, you have to know the landing load very precisely anyway for the overall analysis of the aircraft. So it's relatively easy to make a very accurate analysis. And you use a material that's also very predictable. Steels used there, like 4340, are not going to stress-crack, not going to do anything crazy you wouldn't expect, and it's typically designed for a short life. It's not allowed to corrode. So you can put most of its capacity to use, and that's very unusual. You'd think the landing gear should have a very high safety factor because if it fails it's a big deal. But the probability of failure is low because you don't have that much variation.

`§2.p5` For buildings we have this factor of two. Professor Eagar may have already said that you can think of a factor of two as the difference between static and dynamic. It's not really what happens — you can't consume your overall safety factor just by saying the load may be twice as high, because there are a lot of other things that may be happening that you haven't taken into account. But it gives a starting point. If you put together a trailer or a general structure with a safety factor of two, you try to evaluate the load fairly well, you evaluate your joints fairly precisely. A safety factor of two is a starting point if you don't have a lot of experience in that specific application.

`§2.p6` For pressure vessels, as I mentioned, they have their different ways of evaluating stress level, and that's why in their code they have a very high safety factor. But again it doesn't mean you can pressurize it to four times the nominal pressure, because when you get to a transient situation — a water hammer or anything — you can have a failure. Thermal expansion will also consume some of the capacity. Fatigue is another parameter. If you do a fatigue analysis for what your capacity is, then you can reduce your safety factor, because you just know more, you're less ignorant.

## §3. High-speed boat propeller shaft [12:42]

`§3.p1` I've got two examples here. This is somebody I did a big project for. He had a fancy high-speed boat down in Florida. It's about a four-inch diameter axle, sticking off the hull in the back and connecting the prop. It went only into its second season — about a season and a half — and the prop fell off. He was on a ride, so he was really surprised. He brought the piece in because he wanted to figure out what happened. It was fairly high performance, and it's not really a catastrophic failure — the boat stops, you use the radio, somebody picks you up. So they had a very low safety margin. This was a type of prop that sticks out of the water, so you only have the lower half in the water.

`§3.p2` *[The lecturer searches for a working marker.]* All right, no bad writing today.

`§3.p3` So it's sticking out half of the water. You had a lot of bending in that axle. And as the shaft rotates, what I would expect is to see cracking initiate all around, because that highest load would change location as I rotate — unless I have an unbalance in my prop, in which case it could be localized. The first observation: they had some scars on the external surface of that shaft, right where the seal was. The shaft was moving enough to hit the rigid metallic ring connecting the inside and the outside of the hull. And I was only seeing cracking at that one location.

`§3.p4` Right from the beginning it was clear. Essentially it started out as a clean shaft with mill finish, and then when you start making scars in it — especially running transverse — you reduce the resistance to crack initiation. Under the microscope I found a lot of cracks. That's a general phenomenon: when you see a lot of cracks starting, it means they started all at the same time. If you only see one crack, it means it started and then took over the whole area. The only way to have six different cracks to begin with is they pop out right from the beginning, they compete with each other, and eventually they merge.

`§3.p5` So rapid initiation. We're spending most of the life of that shaft growing a crack from the initiation area all the way to the overload line. In terms of safety factor, it's relatively predictable, if you happen to know the fatigue crack propagation resistance of that shaft. You can tell relatively accurately how many cycles it's going to take, from the first cycle that starts the crack to overload failure.

`§3.p6` What I learned after the fact — after examining it and saying, well, this was going to fail no matter what, this was a relatively low safety factor — was that the designer of this system only expected about 400 hours of service life for these props. The owner was not fully aware of it. And that was just about what they had done.

`§3.p7` So altogether, it did survive the expected service, limited as it was. We're not dealing with a critical application. Maybe they were considering the worst-case scenario, that there would be a scar somewhere on the shaft to get the crack started right from the beginning. This was essentially failure at the expected life. The owner didn't know, or didn't remember. So: low safety factor, a relatively conservative way of evaluating crack propagation. Will it work all the time? No. You can have bigger scars, or higher loads than expected from the movement of the prop. A safety factor of 1.2, in my mind, is fairly low. Given what I saw I would just make it a little stronger. I would try to control the surface condition, try to avoid this cracking, because all the other areas that didn't have these big scars didn't initiate cracks, even though it was rotating. That would essentially increase my practical safety factor, and my service life would be definitely higher.

## §4. Minneapolis I-35W bridge collapse [21:22]

`§4.p1` We'll talk a little bit about the bridge collapse in Minneapolis, which happened in 2007. It wasn't at rush-hour traffic but just before it — late in the afternoon. It was a very hot day. If you're thinking about a steel structure, a very hot day means thermal expansion. It probably had something to do with it. There was a lot of dispute, because this collapse was investigated first by NTSB, then MnDOT took over, and then experts for the different parties involved also got involved. The bridge to begin with had only two main trusses, one on each side, connected in such a way that you fail one of these connections and the bridge is gone. Everybody's analysis proved that.

`§4.p2` Despite that fact, they had URS — a very large construction company — look at the fatigue life. They did a fatigue assessment of the structure after approximately 40 years of service. And they only looked at the members. So you read their report at the end and they say, well, the members are fine, we don't know about the joints. What good does that give us? Nothing. You shouldn't do something to feel more safe while in fact you're not looking at the right thing. Around eighty percent — between 70 and 90 percent — of failures happen at joints, especially in a complex structure. In this case we were looking at gusset plates. So you have members connecting — *[draws on board]* say I have a diagonal here, and another member connecting to a main truss. You put what they call a gusset plate over the whole thing, with a bunch of bolts and rivets connecting everything together on both sides.

`§4.p3` Whenever I present these cases I was involved in, I can't talk about all of it, so I'm mostly going to do pieces that can be helpful to you and are mostly public information. What the people at NTSB had done is, they went back to the design drawing and looked at the thickness of that plate, and somebody said, well, it wasn't thick enough according to the rules of designing back then. That's not good. Because if we start with a safety factor of two but we miss out on the thickness right from day one on the erection drawing, we don't have a safety factor of two anymore — it's less than that. After 40 years that bridge had added lanes, added pavement, corrosion. If you add it all up, there wasn't any safety factor left. The load applied exceeded the capacity of those joints.

`§4.p4` Were there ways to prevent it? Of course. There were inspections going on regularly — not every year, but maybe every five years. They had inspectors. Whether it was a little inspector or a super big inspector, it's a little different when it comes to maintenance. We have a lot of infrastructure everywhere, and essentially the government agencies have to put priorities — how many times they're going to do it, to what extent, and who is going to do it. When we were talking about learning from mistakes, here's another example: after the collapse they added a whole chapter in the inspectors' training class on how to evaluate gusset plates. They realized it wasn't described in enough detail. And it's not that much more complicated. You do need to go back to evaluating the safety factor on the original drawing.

`§4.p5` You expect a steel structure to be relatively deformable. If we look at the different members, they had a very good safety factor. But when it came to the connection, it was a question of — say you need some compressive strength in this part of the leg, and it bows. Now you don't control the angle. It becomes more like a spaghetti. If you do an assessment and you realize your thickness here went down twenty-five percent, in bending twenty-five percent is very significant. It's not just twenty-five percent — it becomes more like a factor of two in buckling resistance. These are the kind of things that have to come into evaluating an old existing structure. Things that were not taken into account at the beginning — we had a factor of safety of two, well, you look at it after the fact, it's not true. You try to figure out if you lost fifty percent of your safety factor, or if you lost all of it. That's what it boils down to. And you hope that it was actually designed with the intended factor of safety, which is not always the case. A very sad story.

`§4.p6` One of the other issues was the rollers, to allow for expansion and contraction of the bridge. It's clear they were not moving as much as they were supposed to, which means the bridge was warping up and down or sideways over time, and that was not taken into account in the original design. If you do it symmetrically — east and west — that may or may not be a problem. But if one side is stuck and the other moves, then you start wrecking your bridge as you go back and forth from one season to the other.

`§4.p7` On a big structure like this, when you're designing it, it's very hard to take all of it into account, and that's why you put it here at a safety factor of two. But like I mentioned in the introduction, it doesn't mean you put half the bolts, because a lot of these are things that are very hard — you don't have the ability in general to create a full 3D nonlinear plastic model that will tell you exactly what's going on in each member, each connection, each gusset plate. It's based on codes and standard methods of designing, and when you go off of it over time for various reasons, then your actual factor of safety is going down, and you hope you accounted for enough of it.

`§4.p8` **Student:** How many people were hurt?

`§4.p9` There were quite a few people on the bridge — more than a hundred. And less than 100 were severely injured or died.

## §5. Two stainless steel pressure-vessel failures [30:39]

`§5.p1` I want to talk to you about two examples. The first is a simple pressurized stainless steel tank in a food processing plant, intended to hold tomato paste or something like that. It's not especially corrosive, but it has to be clean. A nice stainless steel tank. It turns out it was a heated process, so they had a jacket around the tank. The jacket had a lot of chlorides in the insulation, and therefore something they were not expecting did happen — stress corrosion cracking. It started to crack from the outside of the tank. It started as a little crack running this way, and at the same time I also saw cracks going in almost random directions.

`§5.p2` When I see that, and I know my stress level — the hoop stress is twice what the longitudinal stress is on a closed pressure vessel — I know there's a problem. It means my cracking doesn't vary much with my stress level, it's just cracking. So even though I started with a factor of safety of four, which would typically be with respect to yield stress, I can have stress corrosion cracking at a load of one-fifth of that yield stress, depending on the chloride environment and temperature. All of a sudden I don't have a safety factor.

`§5.p3` In that particular case it's really a problem with respect to what I expected the capacity to be. If you expect ductile behavior of a piece of steel and it fails in a brittle manner, that makes a huge difference with respect to safety. That's pretty much what happened with that stainless steel tank. In normal condition it would just crack and leak. What really happened is it started to crack, the cracks all connected, and eventually a big piece came off. It was over-pressurized as well. There were a bunch of issues going on, but one of the main contributors was unexpected failure mode. That's one of the reasons we have a high safety factor.

`§5.p4` I've done another project on a steam piping system connecting in Washington DC. We didn't go to the White House, but it was supplying the whole government building area. We looked at a drip leg that was collecting condensate. So you have a line, and at the bottom of the line you have a jump between different sections. In the manhole you have a little relay that collects the condensate and redistributes it back to the system. The connection at that drip leg was built in an unusual way. It had the proper material thickness, but I'll show you the detail.

`§5.p5` So the pipe is running this way. We have a leg connecting straight through. *[Draws.]* It's really two tubes connected to one another — this one is smaller, that's our leg, and we're going to look at that cap right here. Instead of having a bottom that would look like half a sphere, where you can put a weld and the weld is subjected to the same load as the wall next to it, they had the weld in this corner. And when you pressurize this design detail, it just wants to expand, so you're cracking open those welds.

`§5.p6` You're starting with a safety factor of four, five, eight, whatever it is. Instead of having just a tension stress on the welds — if I blow this up, the way it looked is, they had an end cap like this, the wall here. *[Draws.]* When you pressurize, this cap wants to bend out and become more like half a sphere, creating tension at the root of the weld. The toe is right here, but at the very root of the weld, where you can't really control the quality, you have imperfections, and that's where the highest tension was. So unzipping from that root was not a very hard process. The code says you're supposed to use these kinds of caps, or find a way essentially not to put the highest stress on your welds. Otherwise there's no point in having these thick walls if the capacity of your connection is only half of the capacity of everything else.

`§5.p7` For this application, we were looking at something that was three-eighths of an inch or maybe half an inch thick wall, a little more than maybe three-quarter inch cap, and it had this weld pretty much the same size, but loaded in tension at the root. So no safety factor left. When the transient loads came up with big water hammer, this was really ready to go. That's how you consume safety factor.

`§5.p8` Nobody was able to show exactly who made the decision. It was possibly part of a repair. That's part of the problem sometimes. If you go to a repair, they know they have to use the right thickness, but when it comes to the detail and how to do it, that's probably where they were lacking proper engineering.

## §6. Variability, ignorance, and acceptable failure rates [38:00]

`§6.p1` If we're looking at these factors of safety and trying to decide do we need three, do we need two, 1.2, four, five, eight — I've seen designs with a safety factor of eight. People can still break the stuff, but not the way it's supposed to be used. Even the little propane tanks — if you want to solder something, you go to Home Depot and you buy the little propane tank with a torch on it. They have high safety factors, but if you drop it from six stories high, eventually something bad can happen.

`§6.p2` We talked a little bit about material strength. If I have my steel 4340 in the landing gear and it's going to be inspected, I know what I'm getting. If I deal with a casting, I don't know as much. A lot of times with a casting we have an additional factor of safety we put in, because either we're going to inspect it a lot, or there's going to be something additional — it's not as predictable. If we're looking at the environment, the chlorides from the jacket of that pressure vessel are a good example: if I don't take the risk of slow cracking into account, or if I cannot rule it out, then I need a higher safety factor. Actually I should figure out whether there's a risk for that environment to be there — temperature, moisture, and chlorides in the case of stainless steel. The less you know — that's my factor of ignorance — the more of a safety factor you need. Sometimes it's not a question of effort; for an application you just don't know what the possible environment is, so you have to be more conservative. If you have your landing gear, it's going to be inspected regularly, you don't allow for any significant corrosion to take place — very controlled environment — then you reduce your safety factor.

`§6.p3` We have two aspects to the factor of safety. One is what we don't know. The other is what exactly we ultimately need — what are we willing to go with as far as failure rate? That's independent, and it's typically built from experience. For bridges, you're looking at a 10 to the minus six chance of failure, or 5 times 10 to the minus six, something of that order, per year. That's the probability of a bridge collapsing, approximately. It varies, but it's the kind of average intent, based on all we know about factor of safety and everything, and things we don't know that make that factor of safety go down, and also takes into account all the inspection. It's kind of after-the-fact analysis.

`§6.p4` That number — 5 times 10 to the minus six — will be different if we're looking at a car. We could have a lower number. Most of the time people crush cars even if the car is perfectly fine, because they're not looking where they're going, or they're talking on the phone. So you don't need the same overall low failure rate for a car as for a bridge. You get a lot of people on a bridge, and the bridge is going to be there for much longer than the car.

## §7. Design methods and the BP Macondo example [42:20]

`§7.p1` How do we do this design? This is a topic we elaborate on a lot in the fall class, so I'll just give you an overview. There are different design methods, and I've talked about fatigue crack propagation, the landing gear and those things. That's one way — damage-tolerant analysis. You can have other, simpler designs where you just say, well, I think this is going to be the static load, I'm going to put a safety factor of three on it, and hopefully everything's going to be fine. If you don't have a whole lot of fatigue going on, if you don't have stress cracking or any kind of environmental damage, it'll probably be fine.

`§7.p2` It varies from one industry to the other, so the approach varies, and the safety factor values vary as well, and what people are willing to go with in terms of reliability. One sad example is these blowout preventers for sealing around drill string for drilling. Look at the disaster with the BP spill, or the Macondo well, Deepwater Horizon, however you want to call it. These valves are clearly not designed with a high safety factor. Is that acceptable? It's just the way it is for now, and it still is. One of the reasons: if you have a blowout in normal conditions, you may have a crew of 15, 20 people working there, and they know it's coming, so a lot of times they do have the opportunity to escape.

`§7.p3` I've had four or five well-blowout investigations, and a lot of times nobody gets hurt. It's a lot of money lost, or a lot of environmental damage in the case of the BP spill, but it used to be the sense that it's just money, it's not the end of the world. What happens with the BP spill is different — it was really deep, and it just didn't shut itself up. A lot of times the pressure burst will just blow all the drilling mud and some of the gas to a point where you reduce the pressure in the well itself, and the well collapses, the casing breaks and it seals itself. You lose your well, you have to drill somewhere else because you've lost all the integrity. But it's not every time that it will just blow the gas and the oil out like it did on Macondo. So that's one example of an industry where the American Petroleum Institute is not really prescriptive on what a safety factor should be and how to test it. The practical consequence I've seen is relatively low reliability of those valves.

`§7.p4` Now, how do you make it reliable? We talked about safety factor, but I want to stress there's a lot more to it. If you go back 30 years, when that Minneapolis bridge was put together, they couldn't do even finite element analysis the way we would today for such a bridge. Given the details that were in it — or we wouldn't do the details. If you start with a bridge and you have your safety factor down the road, it's by experience. If we've been building thousands of bridges over a hundred-year period this way and we get the failure rate we're willing to go with, there's no reason to change the overall safety factor. If we're getting more failures — a little bit like is happening with the valves for blowout preventers — then you have to raise your safety factor. Just not good enough.

`§7.p5` If you're coming up with new products, you may do a lot of testing — actual laboratory testing or field testing — to see if you've taken everything into account. You do that a lot in aerospace. I remember the wings for small jets being moved up and down. They hold where the fuselage is with all the bolts on, and just move the tip of the wing at least a foot up and down, going through excursions that may be a lot more than that. The very last cycle was kind of all the way until it breaks, and it was a lot of deflection. When you get into high deflection, now you start thinking about what happens to the seals and the setting for the gas tanks that are in the wings. So it becomes also an assembly-level evaluation of your safety and reliability.

`§7.p6` That happens a lot in automotive. I work on ATVs and relatively smaller structures, where when you put everything together and you have the exhaust — so you have the heat, and you're going to have water coming in when you're running the units — these are not things easily taken into account with the computer. You almost need to run your prototypes and find out if you have a problem. It's a very good way to increase your reliability. It doesn't really apply to the primary structure — you don't want the frame to wreck completely, so a lot of times to know your safety factor for the frame you do need to do some analysis. But if you're really looking into evaluating whether your overall assembly is going to fit the function, there's nothing like a full-scale test or a field test to demonstrate that. It's required for a lot of aerospace assemblies, and they do it also in automotive and with recreational products.

`§7.p7` Calculation is always good, but if you're only going to do calculations, I'd say boost your safety factor, because there's so much you may miss out on in the integration of your assembly — things that are much harder to predict ahead of time.

## §8. Statistics: variability and stochastic safety factor [49:45]

`§8.p1` When we arrive at those safety factors — I mentioned that it's from experience. People didn't do a lot of statistics when they came up with safety factors. Today there are a lot of people who do, and I've got a book here, full of statistics and how you could relate actual failure rate to individual parameters. We'll use some of the ideas just to get our idea about what happens between a safety factor of two and a failure.

`§8.p2` My basic line for you to take out today is: we don't build stuff much stronger than it needs to be. We can't think of the safety factor as something anybody can use for their purpose. If we go to the lab today and we have five welds, and we test them and find the strength in tension — well, probably these five welds were made by one guy. If I have other welders do it, they'll do it differently. Over time, if they're instructed by different people, it will also change. So when you come down to a large population — and I've put basic statistics here, Gaussian, normal distribution — way out here about one standard deviation, so essentially sixty-five percent of the population is in the good area, and then you start having failure for the lower thirty-five percent. That's something that can happen. If I run these five tests, I still have that tail of strength levels that are quite possible, coming from different welders, different inspectors, different instructions. I'm not going to know that.

`§8.p3` After the fact you realize, well, I really need that safety factor, because I have variation. If the variation is tight — that curve is really tight because we're dealing with a machined steel component, no welds, no nothing, like the landing gear — then I'll go with a lower safety factor. My standard deviation is much smaller.

`§8.p4` What we have here is a plot of capacity, the vertical axis, against the stress level. It's centered where we would do the design. Say we have here a safety factor that looks like just about two. We know we'll deviate from that. This one is circular but it doesn't have to be a circle. If I'm dealing with a pressure vessel and I know I don't have transient regimes, I know my stress very well. In the horizontal axis I could make this circle more an ellipse pointing up and down, because I know my stress level — my uncertainty about that one is going down. For that same pressure vessel, if I don't know all the chemicals that may be used in it, what kind of jacket it's going to have, my material variability may grow — that would make a vertical ellipse.
