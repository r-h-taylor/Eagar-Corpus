## Cases referenced in this lecture

### New Orleans canal levee failure
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "New Orleans Canal Levee Failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Levee was designed to a safety factor of 1.3 and failed — illustrates that a "30 percent margin" is not actually a safety margin in practice when soil conditions and design-life uncertainties are factored in. Counter-example to the assumption that any nominal factor above unity is safe.

### Bombardier Aerospace small airframes
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`–`§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bombardier aircraft lightning testing - Canadian border incident"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecturer worked at Bombardier; uses landing-gear inspection and 4340 steel material predictability to illustrate why aerospace can run at 1.5 safety factor. *Note:* the canonical cluster name references a different Bombardier case (lightning testing); this lecture's reference is to landing gear design culture. Proposed sub-case or canon refinement: "Bombardier landing gear safety factor practice."

### High-speed boat propeller shaft (Florida)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`–`§3.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "High-speed boat propeller shaft failure" / "Speedboat propeller shaft fatigue failure" / "Propeller shaft fatigue failure (boat/sport unit)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Fully developed teaching case. Four-inch axle, half-submerged prop, scars at seal interface initiated multiple fatigue cracks. 400-hour design life that the owner didn't know about. Illustrates: (1) low-margin competition designs, (2) the expert-user obligation in maintenance-critical systems, (3) how surface scars consume safety factor.
- **Materials/systems:** Steel shaft, surface-marred at metallic seal ring, rotating bending fatigue, multi-crack initiation pattern.
- **Era:** Recent consulting (relative to 2012).

### Minneapolis I-35W bridge collapse
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "I-35W Minneapolis Bridge collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Fully developed teaching case. The lecturer was personally involved in the investigation (with the original design team). Teaches: (1) URS fatigue review covered members but not joints, (2) the original gusset plate was under-designed per the era's rules (NTSB finding), (3) added lanes, added pavement, corrosion, and stuck rollers consumed any residual margin, (4) post-collapse the inspectors' training curriculum added a chapter on gusset plate evaluation.
- **Materials/systems:** Steel two-truss bridge, gusset-plate riveted/bolted connections, rollers for thermal expansion.
- **Era:** August 2007 collapse; investigated ~2007–2010.

### Food-processing plant stainless steel tomato-paste tank
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Food processing stainless steel tank stress corrosion cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stress corrosion cracking from chlorides in the jacket insulation, on a heated stainless steel pressure vessel. Demonstrates: brittle (SCC-driven) cracking can occur at one-fifth of yield, voiding a nominal safety factor of four. Crack pattern (multiple random orientations) signaled the unexpected failure mode.
- **Materials/systems:** Austenitic stainless steel, chloride SCC, jacketed pressurized tank.

### Washington DC government-area steam piping drip-leg failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`–`§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Washington DC government steam piping drip leg connection failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Drip-leg end cap was welded in a geometry that put the highest tension at the root of the weld (rather than using a hemispherical cap that loads the wall and weld equally). Pressurization opened the joint at the worst location. Teaches: a code-mandated geometry exists for this reason; ad hoc repair geometry consumes safety factor; "no point in thick walls if the connection capacity is half of the wall capacity."
- **Materials/systems:** Steam pipe, drip leg, fillet/groove weld at end-cap-to-leg corner.

### BP Deepwater Horizon / Macondo blowout
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`–`§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "BP Deepwater Horizon blowout preventer failure" / "Deepwater Horizon / BP Macondo well blowout" / "Macondo blowout and near-misses"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Blowout preventers as an example of an industry (API) that is not prescriptive on safety factor for the seal valves. Practical consequence: low reliability of those valves. Contrast with the historical normal — most blowouts blow themselves out and seal because casing collapses; Macondo was deep enough that this didn't happen.
- **Era:** April 2010 disaster; the lecturer notes "four or five well-blowout investigations" in his consulting history.

### Small jet wing fatigue testing
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Small jet wing assembly fatigue testing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Wings flexed a foot up and down through service excursions, with a final cycle to failure showing very large deflections — illustrating assembly-level (not just member-level) safety evaluation, including seal and fuel-tank impacts of large deflection.

### ATV assembly field reliability
- **Anchor:** `§7.p6`, `§11.p1`, `§11.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "ATV exhaust and water ingress assembly failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecturer's own work. Heat from exhaust, water ingress during operation, assembly-level interactions that calculation cannot predict. Used to argue for prototype/field testing as a reliability boost.

### Helicopter blade aluminum spar
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`–`§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Helicopter blade spar stress evaluation case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Worked example for fatigue-based safety factor design. Aluminum spar at leading edge, honeycomb core, aluminum skin. Centrifugal + uplift + drag combine to put peak tension under the nose. Strain-gauge instrumentation near the rotor characterizes the load spectrum. Worked example numbers: 30,000 cycles, 12 KSI design, 18 KSI at 1.5 safety factor.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum spar, fatigue, SN-curve confidence intervals.

### Turbine blade superalloy substitution evaluation
- **Anchor:** `§9.p7`–`§9.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Turbine blade material substitution evaluation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Substitution candidate evaluated against original alloy. Limited fatigue data (weeks per data point) means the 95% confidence interval consumes most of the apparent margin: at 10⁷ cycles, the nominal life of 25 collapses to 17–18 inside the confidence band. Teaches: confidence intervals are not safety factor; you cannot spend the same margin twice.

### Pre-loaded grade-8 bolt connection (crane / bearing)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p1`–`§10.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Preloaded bolt connection in heavy crane or bearing application"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Goodman-diagram worked example. Grade 8 bolt, 150 KSI UTS, 20 KSI fatigue endurance, 120 KSI yield. Pre-loading to ~80 KSI mean stress with the flanges acting as a rigid block reduces the bolt's amplitude excursion by ~6–7×, restoring useful safety. Direct tension indicator (DTI) sensors in nuclear-facility erection inspect that pre-load was achieved.

### Speaker's home-built galvanized-steel / aluminum trailer
- **Anchor:** `§11.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Guest lecturer's home-built mixed-metal trailer"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Personal anecdote. Aluminum cover over galvanized steel frame proved hard to join cleanly. Lesson: think about joints before choosing materials.

### Student spring-steel pressure vessel project
- **Anchor:** `§11.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Student spring-steel pressure vessel proposal"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A student wanted to weld spring steel to maximize strength. The lecturer pointed out that welding spring steel destroys its strength. Used as an example of why material/joining decisions must come early in design.

### Longfellow Bridge (Cambridge–Boston)
