## Cases referenced

### 1973 Arab oil embargo
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1973 Arab oil embargo"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate the five-to-ten-year industrial adaptation timescale. Utilities couldn't switch off oil at first; by the next embargo cycles, they could.
- **Era:** 1973 and subsequent embargo attempts.

### Rolls-Royce M250 BLISK (bladed disk) for helicopters
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Rolls-Royce M250 BLISK (bladed disk) for helicopters"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as the only high-volume cast blisk engine in production (~50,000 units, 30+ years). Used to introduce the collateral-weight-savings argument.
- **Materials/systems:** Cast nickel superalloy blisk.

### Titanium combustor fires in jet engines
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Titanium combustor fires in jet engines"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Why titanium can't be used in the hot section above 900°C — it ignites by dissolving its own oxide. Cited as causing "a number of fires about ten years ago in aircraft engines."
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium, titanium oxide.

### Bell Boeing V-22 Osprey
- **Anchors:** `§2.p6`, `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "V-22 Osprey aircraft"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tilt-rotor that could not have been built without carbon fiber composite — all-composite by necessity. Cost: $15M → $60M per unit. Cited again in §7 to illustrate that military aircraft economics permit full composites where commercial does not.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon fiber composite.

### X-33 space plane (hydrogen tank failure)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "X-33 hydrogen tank adhesive joint failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Half-size shuttle-replacement prototype. Adhesively bonded hydrogen tanks at $250M each, factor-of-safety reduced from 2.0 to 1.05 to meet schedule. Held during cryogenic test, failed on warm-up. $1.3B program cancellation.
- **Materials/systems:** Composite adhesive-bonded hydrogen tank.
- **Era:** Late 1990s.

### Sprague's first law (framing principle, not a case)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sprague's law on material properties"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Bob Sprague (head of materials, GE aircraft engines, Cincinnati): first reported properties of a new material are the best it will ever have. Corollary (Jim Williams): first price is the lowest it will ever have.
- **Note:** Recurring teaching principle Tom invokes across the corpus.

### "Bringing New Materials to Market" (1995 Technology Review article)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Eagar 1995 'Bringing New Materials to Market' Technology Review article"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's own publication. Twenty-year payback for new materials companies; at 8% IRR, $1 invested today must return $20 in twenty years. Used to explain why venture capital avoids materials.

### Oil reserves and twenty-year proven-reserve floor
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Twenty-year oil reserves industry investment floor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Industry maintains twenty years of proven reserves because longer-horizon returns are uneconomic at typical IRRs. Used as parallel example to the materials-investment problem.

### GE samarium cobalt magnet development
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "GE samarium cobalt magnet development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1970s GE research on samarium cobalt — Tom studied this as an MIT undergraduate.

### Sony Walkman and neodymium-iron-boron magnets
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`

### Frank Whittle turbine engine development
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Frank Whittle turbine engine development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** British engineer who invented the turbine engine in the late 1930s. Famous quote about not knowing the conventional wisdom that it was impossible.

### TF-30 jet engine rebuilds from Vietnam
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "TF-30 jet engine rebuilds from Vietnam"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 19-year-old summer job at Naval Air Rework Facility, Norfolk. 500-hour engine lifetimes during Vietnam; today 30,000 hours commercial.

### Single-crystal turbine blades (GE/Pratt & Whitney)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Single-crystal turbine blades (GE/Pratt & Whitney)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $6,000/blade × 100 blades/disc = $600K replacement; engine companies make 40% of profit on 20% of volume from blades/vanes.

### Pratt & Whitney niobium alloy development failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pratt & Whitney niobium alloy development failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** NRC committee anecdote (c. 2004). P&W engine designer convinced his board to spend $18M of corporate profit on oxidation-resistant niobium. Tom, having done his doctoral thesis on niobium-aluminum, knew it was a fool's errand. Result: nothing. Used to illustrate that engine designers and materials engineers should talk.

### National Academy of Sciences Air Force propulsion research committee
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "National Academy of Sciences Air Force propulsion research committee"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's NRC service ~12 years before this lecture (c. 2004). Forty-member committee advising Air Force on $300M/year for improved jet engines.

### Metallic glass (met glass) transformer sheet production
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Metallic glass (met glass) transformer sheet production"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $40M South Carolina plant. 10× lower magnetic losses than oriented silicon iron, but 5× more expensive. Provoked steel industry to improve silicon iron after 40 years of stagnation.

### Boeing 787 all-composite aircraft
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 787 all-composite aircraft"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 787 reached ~80% composite, where 777 had been forced down to 30% composite for cost reasons. Cited as Boeing finally committing to the all-composite vision they'd backed away from on the 777.

### Boeing 777 advanced composites program
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 777 advanced composites program"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Originally intended as all-composite; cost-engineered down to 30% composite, 70% aluminum.

### Clayton Christensen innovator's dilemma research—steel mill cost data
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Clayton Christensen innovator's dilemma research—steel mill cost data"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom names Christensen's *Innovator's Dilemma* (~2001 in Tom's reckoning) as framing the question of whether companies defend the top or the bottom of their market. Foreshadows the steel-industry case to come in later sessions.
