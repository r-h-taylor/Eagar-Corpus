# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Maglay Joining Process (video `8xc71DgqtIk`). This register records editorial decisions in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition.*

## Special status of this recording

This is not a Tom Eagar lecture. It is a promotional/technical film produced by Kawasaki Steel Corporation, shown in class as instructional material. The standard Eagar-lecture conventions (student attribution, stage directions, "okay" handling, soft reordering of Tom's first-pass answers) do not apply. The editorial work here is limited to:

1. Fixing captioner garbling where the corrected reading is unambiguous.
2. Restoring the proper noun "Maglay" throughout (captioner produced "magl," "magay," "magl process," etc.).
3. Sectioning the film at its own narrative breaks.
4. Light smoothing of narrator phrasing ("a natural application" → "an actual application"; "is to remain anticlockwise" → "is left running anticlockwise") where the captioner's transcription is internally implausible and the corrected reading is unambiguous from context.

An editor's note appears at the top of the layer 3 file flagging the recording's non-Eagar status so a reader of the corpus does not mistake the film's voice for Tom's.

## Sectioning

The film falls into five natural units, marked by topic transitions in the narration:
- §1 (00:10–01:17): The application context and the announcement of the process.
- §2 (01:17–02:23): The technical mechanism (electroslag heating, electromagnetic coils).
- §3 (02:23–03:38): The live demonstration with magnetic control on/off.
- §4 (03:38–06:46): Electrode range, bead quality, and the benefits summary.
- §5 (06:46–end): Applications list and Kawasaki Steel closing.

These breaks correspond to the film's own pacing (each transition is signaled by a new narration topic, often with a visible scene change in the source).

## Per-paragraph decisions

### §1.p1
- Joined the layer 2 first two sentences into a continuous opening. "And a new overlaying welding process has been developed" → "A new overlaying welding process has been developed." The leading "And" is a captioner artifact at a sentence break; removing it does not change sense.

### §2.p1
- "SAW [submerged arc welding]" — the bracketed expansion was inserted in layer 2 and is preserved in layer 3. SAW is a standard welding-industry abbreviation; the expansion is for the general reader.

### §3.p1
- "a natural application" → "an actual application." Layer 2 already flagged this as a captioner error ("natural" for "actual"). Layer 3 reads the corrected form.
- "SAW strip electric welder" → "SAW strip electrode welder." The context (the system uses a strip electrode) makes "electric" implausible; "electrode" is the obvious intended word.
- "and this is the secret" → "this is the secret." Removed the conjunctive "and" at a clause boundary for readability.

### §3.p2
- "if the coil on the right is to remain anticlockwise" → "if the coil on the right is left running anticlockwise." The layer 2 phrasing is grammatically intact but reads as captioner-mediated awkwardness; the corrected reading matches the demonstration logic (one coil is left in its previous polarity to show the resulting defect). This is an editorial judgment; if a reviewer prefers, the layer 2 phrasing can be restored at no cost to meaning.

### §4.p4
- "superior anti-corrosion" → "superior anti-corrosion performance." Added "performance" to complete the noun phrase; the bare adjective reads as a captioner truncation.
- "reduction in the repair welding" → "reduction in repair welding." Definite article removed; reads more naturally.

### §5.p1
- "this outstanding welding process" — layer 2 has "the outstanding welding process"; layer 3 uses "this" to match the demonstrative force of the film's closing.

### §5.p2
- "wide electrode overlaying welding process" → "wide-electrode overlaying welding process." Hyphenation of the compound modifier.
- The trailing "[welding industry]" bracket in layer 2 (marking a captioner-truncated final phrase) is resolved in layer 3 to "welding industry" without brackets, since the reading is unambiguous from context.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3 (constitutional constraint)

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
