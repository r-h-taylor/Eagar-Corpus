## Cases referenced

### General Electric boiling water reactor stress corrosion cracking
- **Anchor:** `§1.p9`, `§1.p10`, `§1.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric boiling water reactor stress corrosion cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's marquee case for sensitization in service. GE built 1970s boiling water reactors using 304 stainless steel and incurred a two-billion-dollar worldwide problem from stress corrosion cracking in ultra-pure water with parts-per-million chlorine and oxygen. Motivated industry-wide move to 304L and 304LN.
- **Materials/systems:** 304 stainless, sensitized HAZ; 18-megohm water; trace chloride + oxygen.
- **Era:** 1970s; remediation through 1980s.

### Gas-fired power plant water filter corrosion investigation
- **Anchor:** `§1.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Gas-fired power plant water filter corrosion investigation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's consulting case in Georgia — stainless steel filter baskets in 14-megohm water. Client suspected chloride corrosion; Tom diagnosed it as a thermal/design problem. Used as a foil to set up the GE BWR case that follows.
- **Materials/systems:** stainless steel filter baskets; 14-megohm water.

### MIT Inconel department griddles fabrication
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`, `§2.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT inconel department griddles fabrication"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's "thirty-thousand-dollar griddles" — four Inconel sheet griddles made from surplus material in the early 1990s, used at department steak fries during his tenure as department head. Used to illustrate Inconel as a $30/lb fabricated material and the price scale of nickel-base alloys.
- **Materials/systems:** Inconel sheet (~1000 lb total, four 2'×4' griddles).
- **Era:** early 1990s through Suresh's department headship.

### US Navy all-stainless steel submarine program (AL-6X alloy)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Navy all-stainless steel submarine program (AL-6X alloy)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief aside — Navy ~10 years prior (so circa early 2000s) considered building super-austenitic stainless submarines for non-magnetic signature, then abandoned the program. Sets up the comparison to Chinese naval expansion.
- **Materials/systems:** super-austenitic stainless (6% Mo, added Ni and N).
- **Era:** early 2000s, retrospective.

### Wright brothers' aircraft engine
- **Anchor:** `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Wright brothers' aircraft engine"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Flyer One's aluminum-copper engine block, now in the Smithsonian, sampled and examined by TEM to measure century-long coarsening of Al-Cu precipitates. Used as the founding example of heat-treatable aluminum alloys and as a demonstration of long-term softening in service.
- **Materials/systems:** Al-Cu (2000-series ancestor).
- **Era:** 1903 / examined ~2000s.

### Dayton Walther aluminum truck wheel fatigue case
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Dayton Walther aluminum truck wheel fatigue case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's late-1970s consulting case as a young assistant professor — Dayton Walther was the world's largest tire-rim manufacturer at the time, all in steel, trying to qualify aluminum rims to half-million- or million-mile fatigue limits. Welding-based approach failed; forging at Alcoa Cleveland ultimately succeeded.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum truck wheel forgings.
- **Era:** late 1970s consulting; production now mature at Alcoa Cleveland.

### Concorde supersonic transport operations and economics
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Concorde supersonic transport operations and economics"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's personal flight on the Concorde in the 1990s. Three teaching points: (1) skin-temperature limits speed, not engine thrust; (2) flight short enough to suppress jet lag; (3) maintenance neglected because aircraft fleet too small to spare downtime — broken tray tables, dirty cabin. Sets up aluminum-skin temperature limits and titanium replacement.
- **Materials/systems:** Concorde skin (aluminum-copper, temperature-limited).
- **Era:** Tom's flight ~1990s; aircraft retired 2003.

### Concorde skin temperature limitation
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Concorde skin temperature limitation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stated as a fact ("flew not on speed but on temperature") — Concorde carried skin sensors that gated cruise speed by ambient cold. Functions as the technical hook for the personal anecdote.

### Aluminum baseball bat
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`, `§7.p4`, `§7.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum baseball bat"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Physical-object case. THT-100 scandium-bearing aluminum alloy, 100 ksi yield, ~28-thousandths wall, swaged from extruded tube. The world's largest single use for scandium (~2 tons/year global production). Sold through southern California aerospace-engineer-led firms. Used to land the "highest-strength aluminum is in consumer sports, not aerospace" homework answer.
- **Materials/systems:** THT-100 Al-Sc, extruded + swaged + solutionized + quenched, fine grain via Sc refinement.

### Baseball bat performance study
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`, `§7.p6`, `§7.p7`, `§7.p8`, `§7.p9`
