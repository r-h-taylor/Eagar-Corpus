# Case Index — Lecture 1, Solid State Welding, Spring 2013

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p4`). This lecture is the course opener; it surfaces many cases in passing as motivation, fully developed elsewhere in the corpus.*

## Cases referenced

### LIGO gravitational wave detector — mouse-induced corrosion of vacuum vessel
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`, `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "LIGO gravitational wave detector corrosion failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's consulting trip to Livingston, Louisiana the previous day. Used as opening anecdote (§1) and developed in §6 as the prototype "billion-dollar facility jeopardized by a tiny failure at a joint" — mice nested on stainless steel insulation, urine and feces caused chloride corrosion, leak in 4-km ultra-high-vacuum beam tube.
- **Materials/systems:** 304-class stainless steel, ultra-high vacuum, insulation
- **Era:** 2013 (failure discovered Thanksgiving 2012)

### Manwich hot water tank stress corrosion cracking
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Manwich hot water tank stress corrosion cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Food-processing plant case. 304 stainless hot water tank under insulation, attacked by Clorox cleaning cycle in chloride-oxidizing environment. Tom corrects an earlier reference (apparently from previous lecture) — it was Manwich, not tomato paste.
- **Materials/systems:** 304 stainless steel, chloride stress-corrosion cracking
- **Era:** unspecified (consulting case)

### Liberty ships and SS Schenectady / SS Manhattan brittle fracture
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`, `§3.p5`, `§3.p6`, `§3.p7`, `§3.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady" (closely paired with "1940s welded ship cracking", "USS Manhattan brittle fracture", "WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** The canonical case for "welds create continuous crack paths whereas rivets stop cracks at plate boundaries." 4,694 ships built; 1,442 reported casualties; more than one fracture per ship on average. Schenectady cracked in harbor; SS Manhattan cracked at sea. Used to launch the post-WWII brittle-fracture research history (Weck/British Welding Institute, Pellini/NRL, Cohen-Averbach/MIT).
- **Materials/systems:** mild carbon steel plate, arc-welded merchant hulls
- **Era:** 1941–1945; research aftermath 1946–1950s

### British Welding Institute founding (Richard Weck, Alan Wells)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "British Welding Institute founding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Post-WWII institutional response to ship fractures. Weck (founder) and Wells (research head, who wrote Tom's tenure letter) both named. Cited as the largest welding research center in the world short of the former Soviet Union.
- **Era:** late 1940s–1950s

### Pellini explosion bulge test / Naval Research Lab brittle fracture work
- **Anchor:** `§3.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pellini explosion bulge test development" (and "Navy brittle fracture study - welded vessels and ships")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Second of the three post-WWII brittle-fracture research centers. Pellini at NRL, later came to MIT in retirement.
- **Era:** late 1940s–1970s

### MIT brittle fracture research — Morris Cohen and Ben Averbach
- **Anchor:** `§3.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "MIT brittle fracture research (Cohen, Averbach)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Third of the three post-WWII brittle-fracture research centers. Cited as the origin point for most of what is known about brittle fracture of steels.
- **Era:** late 1940s–1960s

### Big Inch Pipeline
- **Anchor:** `§3.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Big Inch Pipeline"
- **Frame in this lecture:** First critical structure made by arc welding — 30-inch pipeline, Louisiana to New Jersey, 1930. Cited as the historical pivot from riveted to welded long-distance transmission infrastructure.
- **Era:** 1930

### Friction stir welded jet aircraft proposal
- **Anchor:** `§3.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Friction stir welded jet aircraft proposal"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cautionary footnote to the Liberty ship lesson. Late-70s British Welding Institute invention; startup proposed friction-stir welding aluminum/titanium small business jet. Tom skeptical: an SS Manhattan crack in mid-Atlantic gives passengers lifeboats; a fuselage crack at altitude does not. Company went bankrupt before product release.
- **Era:** late 1970s / 1980s

### Bethlehem Steel generator rotor forging — welding feasibility study
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Generator rotor shaft welding feasibility study (US Steel)" — note: Tom names Bethlehem Steel, not US Steel, but mentions both as the only two US producers. Closest aggregate match.
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 1970s consulting at Bethlehem Steel — could a 700-ton generator rotor forging be replaced by welding two smaller forgings, to avoid $2 billion capital investment in new heat-treatment facilities? Tom did not solve it; other technology obviated the need. Used as the case for "largest single product without a joint" and as the historical hinge to battleship-gun-barrel facilities being repurposed for generator rotors.
- **Materials/systems:** large alloy steel forgings, generator rotors
- **Era:** 1970s

### Battleship gun barrel manufacturing infrastructure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Battleship gun barrel manufacturing facilities"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Government-built early-20th-century heat-treatment facilities for long tubular forgings. Repurposed for generator rotors after WWII. Cited as the example of government-funded capability creating downstream commercial industry.
- **Era:** early 20th century

### Intel Pentium chip wire bonding
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Intel chip wire bonding defect scrap problem" — closest match; Tom is showing rejected (ink-dotted) Pentium chips. May also relate to "Pentium 6 / Early 1990s large-format chip bonding."
- **Frame in this lecture:** Physical-object demonstration of "ubiquity from chips to ships" — 400 leads per chip, 20-year-old bonding technology still in use.
- **Materials/systems:** silicon, gold or aluminum wire bonds
- **Era:** early 1990s

### MIT Lincoln Lab 300 GHz radar dish structure (Millstone)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p6`, `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT Lincoln Lab 300 GHz Radar Dish Structure" (and "MIT Millstone radar dome structural fabrication", "MIT Lincoln Lab Quadulan radar dome bogie replacement")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Football-field-sized aluminum structure, tolerance of ~3 mm over 100 yards. Project budget grew from $20M to $100M. No welding shop would bid because tolerances were beyond imagination. Eventually fabricated by a firm doing Electric Boat submarine work. Belmonte's prior firm helped design it. Used to illustrate joint efficiency, gusset-plate design, and welding-shop capacity limits.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum space-frame structure
- **Era:** unspecified consulting era (probably 1990s–2000s)

### Space Shuttle Challenger O-ring
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle Challenger" (and "Space Shuttle O-ring failure")
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1986 Challenger explosion. Tom's reflexive prediction: "I bet it failed at a weld." Correction: O-ring seal — not a weld but still a joint. Used to anchor the "failures happen at joints" thesis.
- **Era:** 1986

### Pelloux quote — "something won't fail unless it's been welded"
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`, `§8.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Pelloux 'something won't fail unless it's been welded' quote"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first year on faculty; MIT mechanical engineering professor Reggie Pelloux's classroom remark, later delivered by Tom in the 1992 Houdremont Lecture to 500 welding engineers at the International Institute of Welding in Montreal. Used as both job-security joke and frame for §7 (why welds actually do fail).
- **Era:** late 1970s–1992

### Ceramic engine development (1990s ceramics hype)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ceramic engine development (1990s ceramics hype)" (and "1980s all-ceramic engine development initiative", "1985-1986 ceramic engine development failure (anticipated, not realized)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mid-1980s/early-1990s ceramic-engine research push. Ceramists "knew nothing" about fracture toughness; spent hundreds of millions trying to build all-ceramic internal combustion engines while metals research starved. Brittleness, chloride corrosion, sub-paper-thickness critical flaws. Used as the foil for "steel is great and joins well" argument.
- **Era:** 1980s–early 1990s
