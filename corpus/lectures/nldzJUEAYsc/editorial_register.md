# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture 33 (`nldzJUEAYsc`), §§1–6. This lecture excerpt is a single 17-minute teaching arc beginning mid-discussion of titanium welding technology and pivoting into Tom's extended recollection of his 1980 Soviet exchange. The arc divides naturally at the moment Tom shifts from "what technology was the Paton Institute using" (welding-process content) to "and here's how I learned about it" (the human/political/travel story).*

## Sectioning rationale

- **§1 (00:02–01:53):** Electroslag welding of titanium. Tom opens mid-thought (Electric Boat, Quonset Point) and quickly establishes that the Paton Institute led the world in electroslag, then shows the two-inch electroslag weld he made at Oregon Graduate Center. The section closes with the clear summary "this is one of the technologies the Soviets used."
- **§2 (01:53–06:34):** Deep TIG. New welding process, new physical sample, new horizontal-position problem. The pedagogical pivot ("but you couldn't weld everything in the vertical position") opens the new teaching unit.
- **§3 (06:34–07:25):** Brief reflective bridge — Gurevich as person, the KGB agent in the room, the value of the exchange. Marks the transition from technical to narrative material. Kept as its own short section because it is the hinge of the lecture.
- **§4 (07:25–12:03):** The Soviets at MIT. Tom names the institutional players (Medovar, Paton, Patarya, Marinsky) and tells the Sadoway/Ukrainian and Flemings/Marin-che stories.
- **§5 (12:03–14:13):** Travel in the USSR — customs, the opera walk-back, the planned Kiev hour.
- **§6 (14:13–end):** The Soviets in Cambridge — sports, houses, the grocery store. Tom signals the section close ("seven-minute break").

Six sections for a 17-minute excerpt is at the upper end of the dial; the density is justified by Tom's rapid shifts between welding metallurgy, personal narrative, and political-cultural observation.

## Conventions applied

Standard catalog conventions (numerals, filler removal, self-correction smoothing, "okay" handling, "you know" / "I mean" handling). No stage directions in this excerpt — Tom references physical samples ("here is one," "this one") but the layer 2 text does not record him producing them on camera as discrete teaching moves; the samples appear to be already laid out. Per spec, stage directions are reserved for the *moves* (producing, holding up, handing around), not for static references.

## Per-paragraph decisions

### §1.p1 — Electric Boat opening
- **Captioner garbling fixed:** L2 opens "Maybe eight WS, but they have a whole facility down at Quonset Point." The "Maybe eight WS" is a captioner artifact at a hard cut; the audible referent is Electric Boat. L3 reads "Electric Boat has a whole facility down at Quonset Point." Logged here because the reading is inferred from context (Quonset Point + pressure hull + $200M building = Electric Boat) rather than transparent.
- **Self-correction smoothed:** "the Paton Institute, was the leader" → "the Paton Institute were the leader" (subject-verb agreement, treating the institute as collective).
- **Filler cut:** "okay, so far as that goes" at the end of the paragraph — closing filler, not a thought-closer; cut.
- **Reordering within paragraph:** Tom's clause order in L2 is "we — I came back, and on my titanium project we took some of our one-inch plate." L3 reads "on my titanium project, when I came back, we took some of our one-inch plate." Minor — restoring the sentence to read forward.

### §1.p2 — The two-inch electroslag weld
- **Cut:** "this thing was painted, or I" — Tom self-corrects to "I put Crystal Clear on it." L3 reads only the corrected form.

### §1.p3 — Section close

L2 has this material at the head of `p3`, immediately followed by the deep-TIG transition; the L3 split is at the natural pedagogical break.

## Soft reorderings logged

1. **§1.p3 / §2.p1 split.** Layer 2 `p3` contains both the closing summary of the electroslag-titanium argument and the pivot to deep TIG. Layer 3 splits this paragraph across the §1 / §2 section boundary. The split is at the natural pedagogical break ("And I won't say any more than that" closes §1; "The next thing..." opens §2). This is a within-paragraph cut at a section boundary, not a reordering across sections — the order of Tom's content is preserved.

## Flags for editor review

- **§4.p1 "He was the Soviet's [Medovar's] professor here."** The antecedent is ambiguous in L2. Reading as "Medovar was Nick Grant's protégé/student" is the most plausible interpretation but uncertain. The bracketed insertion may need to be revised once cross-referenced against Tom's other accounts of Medovar's biography.
- **§1.p1 "Electric Boat."** Inferred from context; the L2 captioner garbling at the cold open ("Maybe eight WS") is severe enough that the reading should be confirmed by an editor familiar with the prior session.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could do this because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases that Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
