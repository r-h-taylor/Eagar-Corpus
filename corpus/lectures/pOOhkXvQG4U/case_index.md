# Case Index — Lecture 23, Welding Metallurgy Spring 2014

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor. Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage.*

This lecture is a teaching session on aluminum welding metallurgy. The case content surfaces lightly in §1 (Concorde digression) and §3 (Canadian aluminum casting repair foreshadow), with the Davenport residual-stress operation in §4 functioning as both teaching unit and recurring case anchor.

## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Concorde skin-temperature limitation
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Concorde skin temperature limitation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate that aluminum aging temperatures (165 C and lower) are not "that hot," so aircraft skin frictional heating becomes a design constraint. The Concorde was speed-limited by skin temperature, not engine thrust — colder atmospheric temperatures permitted faster transatlantic crossings.
- **Materials/systems:** Heat-treated aluminum alloys; thermocouple skin sensors.
- **Era:** 1976–2003 (Concorde service life).

### Concorde retirement and Air France Flight 4590
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Concorde maintenance and operational failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's claim that the 2000 Paris crash (runway debris from a preceding aircraft) was used as cover for retiring a fleet that was already past its useful life. Personal note: Tom mentions consulting for the company that dropped the debris.
- **Era:** July 2000 crash; fleet retired 2003.

### Canadian aluminum casting repair consulting case
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum casting aircraft engine repair consulting case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Foreshadow only — Tom announces the case will be developed in detail on Friday. The shop is one of the only FAA-authorized aluminum casting weld repair operations in the US; they had been using 4043 filler metal since 1976 and were criticized in a Canadian Aviation Authority report following a crash investigation. Used to set up a filler-metal-selection case study.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum casting alloys; 4043 filler metal; aircraft engine castings.
- **Era:** 1976–present; Canadian crash investigation likely circa 2010–2014.
- **Cross-reference:** "Canadian Transportation Board filler metal composition criticism" appears as a related canonical cluster and may refer to the same case from the regulator's side.

### Davenport Iowa heavy aluminum plate stress relief by stretching
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`, `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Davenport Iowa plate stress relief by hydraulic jacking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Alcoa's Davenport Works rolls aluminum aircraft-wing plate up to six inches thick on the world's largest rolling mill, then relieves residual stresses by hydraulically stretching the plate one to three percent (the "T51" temper designation). Tom has visited the facility. Used to teach that aluminum residual stresses cannot be relieved by heat treatment for metallurgical reasons, so a mechanical solution is required.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum alloy 7050; six-inch plate × 10–12 ft wide; 70 ksi yield; one-percent strain.
- **Era:** Contemporary (Alcoa Davenport ongoing operation).
- **Cross-reference:** Related canonical clusters: "Alcoa Davenport aluminum rolling mill thickness variation"; "Boeing aluminum plate residual stress relief (Davenport Iowa)"; "Boeing aluminum wing manufacturing waste"; "Davenport Iowa plate stress relief by hydraulic jacking"; "Davenport Works plate cooling problem." This is a recurring Eagar teaching anchor.

### Aluminum aircraft wing manufacturing waste
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing aluminum wing manufacturing waste"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 80–90% of the heat-treated Alcoa plate is machined away as chips to produce the final wing structure with bosses and thinner sections. Used to motivate why welding is avoided (residual stresses) and machining is preferred despite the waste.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum 7050 plate; Boeing and Airbus aircraft wings.
