## §4. Strength versus toughness [10:50]

`§4.p1` I might as well tell you about toughness right now. What is toughness? We start out historically, we measured the strength of a material in terms of its tensile strength. And the first person to do something like this — *[Tom shows Galileo's beam-bending sketch.]* this is a picture from Galileo's notebook showing the bending of a beam under a load. He described the strength of a beam under a load, and he actually understood that they tend to break back where they attach to the tree trunk. Why is that? It's the maximum bending moment, if you know anything about mechanics. So people knew about strength of materials in the 1880s and they would run a tensile test. *[Tom holds up a tensile test bar.]* Here's the tensile test bar, I'll pass it around. Has what we call a cup-and-cone fracture on the top, which you can't see very well, but if I pass it around you probably can.

`§4.p2` That's kind of 1880s, after Henry Bessemer taught people how to make steel. Andrew Carnegie became the richest man in the world. In today's dollars Andrew Carnegie would be worth about 300 billion dollars. He exceeded Bill Gates or Carlos Slim or any other rich guys today, in terms of his wealth. But it wasn't really until after World War II that we started to take an appreciation for this property called toughness. People actually measured it back in the olden days. *[Tom holds up Charpy bar samples.]* These are some Charpy bars that I actually produced when I was a 25-year-old engineer at Bethlehem Steel. There's half of one which is a brittle fracture, and the other one is the ductile fracture that stopped the impact hammer. The Charpy bar — you make these little one-square-centimeter by ten-centimeter-long bars, you put a little two-millimeter notch very carefully, very precisely machined, and you whack it with a big calibrated hammer. It's just a big pendulum, and 260 foot-pounds of energy comes in, hits it, snaps it in two, or it doesn't snap it. Every time it doesn't snap it you have to spend $400 to recalibrate the machine. They didn't like me, I was coming up with some pretty tough steels. Not that it was great rocket science to make tough steels.

`§4.p3` But what's the difference between strength and toughness? That's the question right now.

`§4.p5` Okay, the force — how much load it will take in pounds, you were going to say. Yeah.

`§4.p6` **Student:** [Energy answer.]

`§4.p7` Right, but all those answers are correct. The way I like to say it — strength is a measure of the force of fracture, toughness is a measure of the energy of fracture. Force is different from energy. A tensile test, I'm just finding how many pounds it takes to rip that thing in two — that's the force of fracture. The pendulum swinging and hitting, in a shock load — usually called an impact load, but it is a shock load — that's the energy of fracture. And it doesn't always have to be an impact or a shock load. As we've learned from fracture mechanics, which came out of the study of glasses in the 1920s, toughness can actually be slow loading.

`§4.p8` Now I'll give you my demonstration, which I've used many times. You'll find me doing this on the History Channel or National Geographic, one of these, when I talked about the *Titanic*. They wanted to ask me about *Titanic*. If I take a piece of paper, which turns out to be a brittle material — and how do I know it's brittle? I'll show you in a little bit. *[Tom takes a piece of paper.]* If I pull on the edge of a piece of paper I can pull with pounds of force. If I put a notch in it, put a defect in the surface, it takes ounces, not pounds. So that defect controls the strength of the material, and that's known as the science of fracture mechanics. And I know it's a brittle material because I can put it back together, and it essentially matches without any significant deformation. If it was a piece of taffy I couldn't put it back together and get the same shape I started with. This piece of glass — hey, I can put crazy glue on it and make it look like new, almost, except for the cracks.

`§4.p9` So brittleness occurs below the yield strength of the material — this is actually a definition — and ductility occurs above the yield strength of the material. What is the yield strength of the material? Well, it's a sort of fuzzy region in between. *[Tom draws on the board.]* If I look at a stress-strain curve, it looks something like that. This is load in pounds versus length, or strain, however scientific you want to be. Somewhere in here there's a change in slope. When it's mostly linear we call it Young's modulus, or Hooke's law — it goes back 400 years. When it's actually a ductile material like a piece of copper or a piece of rubber, it will bend over eventually, and somewhere in this bend-over region is what we want to define as the yield point of the material. We do that most commonly by taking the 0.2 percent offset strain, or sometimes in the old days they used to take 0.5. The reason is, their machines weren't that precise back 80 or 100 years ago to be able to measure 0.2 — that's two parts in a thousand, so they were lucky to do five parts in a thousand. But somewhere there is this funny region in between, but this is brittle and this is ductile. I've actually seen a British dictionary that says anything with half a percent strain or better is ductile, and anything below is brittle. It's not a bad definition.

`§4.p10` But what happens to a brittle material? The brittle-material strength is controlled by the flaws, just like that piece of paper. A ductile material, the strength is controlled by the ductility or the yield strength of the material. It doesn't fail brittlely, it actually stretches before it breaks. And probably five times a year someone comes to me and says, "oh this thing broke in a brittle manner," and I look at it and say, the steel deformed, or the aluminum deformed before it broke, and it didn't fail in a brittle manner — it stretched. That is something that people often miss; it's obvious and they don't look for it.
