### USS *Thresher* loss and SUBSAFE program
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`–`§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Thresher"
- **Frame in this section:** 1962 [transcript says '62; actual date 1963] sea trials out of Portsmouth Naval Shipyard. Main seawater pipe break in reactor compartment flooded the reactor, power loss, partial ballast blow, brief ascent then descent. Crew screams audible from tender above. Nuclear navy shut down 2–3 years; resulted in US Navy SUBSAFE quality-control program, 30 years ahead of world industry.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-80 hull; piping
- **Era:** 1963

### Robert Sprague's law
- **Anchor:** `§10.p4`–`§10.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sprague's law on material properties"
- **Frame in this section:** Two Sprague quotes: (1) brittle materials must be designed in compression — Portland cement / mortar / rock as the world's largest-volume composite, with steel rebar for tension; (2) "material scientists think structure controls properties; materials engineers know defects control properties."

### Jim Williams' corollary
- **Anchor:** `§10.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Williams's law on material costs"
- **Frame in this section:** "Whenever you first hear about the cost of a new material, write it down, because that's the lowest cost you'll ever have."

### 2025 aluminum propeller blade alloy
- **Anchor:** `§10.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: 2025 aluminum propeller-blade alloy (toughness vs. 2024)
- **Frame in this section:** Toughest aluminum alloy; 15× tougher than any other aluminum; named in 1925 right after 2024 Al-Cu. Only application Tom knows: propeller blades. Tom's rhetorical move: a propeller is a critical structure where you want it to bend before snapping.

## Figures referenced (not cases)

- **HY80 steel = $2/lb**, single-crystal turbine blade = **$200/lb**, X-33 composite = **$12,500/lb** (§1) — Tom's recurring three-tier cost framework.
- **0.2% offset strain** as the standard yield-point definition; **0.5%** in the older (pre-precision-machine) era (§4.p9).
- **10,000 ft-lb** dynamic tear energy for low-strength steels; toughness drops off above **180 ksi** yield strength (§8.p1).
- **K1c/σy = 2.5 √in** boundary between plastic yielding and elastic-plastic mixed mode; **K1c/σy = 0.6 √in** boundary between mixed mode and plane-strain brittle fracture (§10.p1).
- **Aluminum modulus ≈ 10 Msi; steel ≈ 30 Msi; molybdenum ≈ 50 Msi; ceramics/tungsten ≈ 60 Msi** (§6.p2, §6.p5).
- **4,694 Liberty ships; 970 fracture casualties; 24 strength-deck fractures; 8 lost** (§7.p3) — the foundational fleet-wide failure statistic.
