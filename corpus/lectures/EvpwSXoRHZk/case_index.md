### Aberdeen Proving Ground RDX contamination

### Newport News carbide shack incident
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Newport News Shipyard carbide shack incident"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Welding engineer racing fire department to a carbide shack fire to prevent water-based suppression (which would generate more acetylene). Illustrates acetylene-from-calcium-carbide on-site generation.

### Thomas Wilson calcium carbide discovery
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Thomas Wilson calcium carbide discovery"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Spray, North Carolina, early 1900s. Wilson trying to synthesize metals in an arc furnace with carbon and limestone, ended up with calcium carbide, ignited it accidentally in the Neuse River. Origin of industrial acetylene production.
- **Era:** early 1900s

### Acetylene cylinder storage (Prest-O-Lite / Allison / Fisher)

## Figures referenced (not cases)

- **Heat intensity ceilings.** Birthday candle vs. MAP gas finger-test. Jet burner: 2,000 W/cm². Electric arc: 10,000 W/cm². Transferred plasma arc: up to 20,000 W/cm². Oxygen-iron combustion: ~100,000 W/cm² (10⁵).
- **Acetylene pressure limit.** ~60–70 PSI for stability.
- **Acetone-acetylene dissolution ratio.** 400:1 by volume.
- **Calcium silicate cylinder porosity.** 92%.
- **Underwater welding depth limit.** ~400–500 feet practical; 30 atmospheres / ~1000 feet laboratory.
- **MAP vs. acetylene combustion intensity.** 36% more heat at ~2–3% temperature difference (squared term above 1600°C).
- **Iron oxide melting point.** 1380°C vs. steel 1536°C.
- **Titanium oxide dissolution onset.** ~900°C.
- **Plasma cutting thickness limit.** ~1.5–2 inches with standard equipment.
