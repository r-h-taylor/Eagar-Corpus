# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Video `MTzYKTMcmJo` — "The Story of Arc Welding," archival promotional film shown in Tom Eagar's Fusion Welding class, Summer 2013. This is **not** a Tom Eagar lecture. The conventions established by the §1 worked example (designed for Tom's spoken teaching) apply here only in adapted form.*

## Special status of this artifact

This is an industrial promotional film, likely produced by Lincoln Electric or a comparable arc-welding equipment manufacturer in the late 1940s. It contains:

1. An unnamed narrator carrying the through-line.
2. Dramatized vignettes with named characters (Robert, the Boss / Ray) and unnamed soldiers, sailors, and a "man in the street" with a narrator's foil.
3. No Tom Eagar voice at all.

The standard editorial dial — cutting filler, smoothing self-corrections, bracketing slips — was designed for Tom's spoken classroom delivery. It does not apply to scripted, edited film narration. The text here is already clean; layer 3 sections and labels speakers but does not edit the prose.

## Conventions applied

- **Speaker attribution.** Each dramatized character is labeled. The unnamed through-line voice is labeled `**Narrator:**`. The voice that prompts the "man in the street" interlude (§5.p2, §8.p1) is labeled `**Narrator's foil:**` — it is distinct in dramatic function from the main narrator but is not given a name in the film.
- **Sectioning.** Eight sections, breaking at the film's own narrative pivots: wartime origins → cast-iron argument → riveting argument → exposition of the arc → repair vignette → industrial sweep → petroleum → buildings and peroration.
- **Captioner repair carried forward from layer 2.** "as it [is fed] into the arc" (§4.p3) reflects a layer 2 insertion to repair a captioner dropout.
- **Uncertain captioner reading flagged.** §1.p3 contains "I know Wold[er] beer insist [?]," which is captioner garbling of dramatized 1940s dialogue. The `[?]` marker is preserved from layer 2; the corrected reading is genuinely unclear (possibly "I know, welder, you insist" or similar) and should be flagged for human review against the film audio if available.

## Conventions that do NOT apply here

- No filler removal (the script has none).
- No self-correction smoothing (scripted delivery).
- No backward-reference trimming (no prior session).
- No stage directions (the film's own cuts are the staging).
- No bracketed slip corrections for Tom (no Tom).
- No "okay" / "you know" handling (scripted prose).

## Per-section notes

### §1 (Wartime origins)
Layer 2 paragraphs p1–p5 carry forward unchanged into §1.p1–p5. The film opens with the narrator's framing, then cuts to two dramatized vignettes (Army truck repair, Navy ship reconditioning). The captioner garble at §1.p3 ("Wold[er] beer insist") is flagged with `[?]`.

### §2 (Cast-iron vs. welded steel)
The film's first extended dramatized argument. Robert the engineer pitches welded steel to his Boss using a wheel-comparison case and a deflection demonstration. Layer 2 p6–p12 → layer 3 §2.p1–p7. No edits.

### §3 (Riveting vs. welding)
Continuation of the Robert/Boss dialogue, pivoting to the riveted-joint comparison. The Boss's confusion about soldering (§3.p3) is preserved as scripted; it's a dramatic device for the narrator to make the fusion-vs.-mechanical-joint distinction explicit.

### §4 (How the arc works)
The film's technical exposition section, narrator-only. Layer 2 p18–p25 → layer 3 §4.p1–p8. This is the most reference-useful section of the film for a welding course; the narration walks through circuit, electrode, arc strike, droplet transfer, slag formation, joint types, and positions.

### §5 (Repair and the average American)
Transition section. Narrator introduces a "man in the street" foil (§5.p2) who serves as the audience surrogate for the industrial-reach catalog that follows.

### §6 (Industrial reach)
The film's catalog of arc-welding applications: machine tools, autos, locomotives, aircraft. Narrator-only.

### §7 (Petroleum and the welded economy)
Petroleum-industry case. The film makes an explicit economic argument linking welded refineries to low-cost gasoline to the volume of the 1935 American automobile market.

### §8 (Buildings, dams, and the brave new world)
The peroration. Returns to the "man in the street" foil at §8.p1, then catalogs buildings, bridges, water towers, the Shasta Dam, and finally lands on the postwar utopian "brave new world" register characteristic of late-1940s industrial film.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3 (constitutional constraint)

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
