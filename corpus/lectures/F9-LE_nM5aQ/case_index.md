## Cases developed in this lecture

### America's Cup 4340 welding procedure development
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`, `§2.p5`, `§2.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `America's Cup 4340 welding procedure development`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to introduce the hardenability concept. A four-inch-thick keel beam couldn't be made from HY-130 (too soft, plastically deforming in trials) so they wanted 4340, but didn't know how to weld it in that section thickness. Tom showed them how.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-130, 4340 alloy steel, keel beam structural element
- **Era:** Unspecified (post-1960s based on HY-130 reference)

### Sub-Zero refrigerator hinge failure
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `PROPOSED: Sub-Zero refrigerator hinge failure (303 stainless misuse)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Illustrates the consequence of 303 vs. 304 mix-up. Manufacturer used 303 (sulfur-bearing, non-weldable) instead of 304 for a pin-to-plate weld. Cracked at the weld, plated over, doors fell off in service. ~$7M in repairs.
- **Materials/systems:** 303 vs. 304 austenitic stainless steel; brazing-welding application
- **Era:** Unspecified
- **Note:** Possibly maps to "Sub-Zero refrigerator hinge failure" in the canon — confirm cluster_id during reconciliation.

### Duplex stainless steel centrifuge brittle failure
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Duplex stainless steel centrifuge brittle failure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Physical demonstration. Cast duplex stainless centrifuge (~1 ton, 3000 RPM, 4 ft diameter) for separating starch in corn-methanol production. Embrittled by iron-chrome intermetallic phases. Shows orange-peel surface where large casting grains stretched before fracture.
- **Materials/systems:** Cast duplex stainless steel, ethanol production equipment

### 316 stainless steel heat exchanger tube pitting failure (Fenway Frank application)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Fenway Frank hot dog cooker stainless steel failure` (likely matches "Hot dog cooker corrosion failure" or "Fenway Frank hot dog cooker failure" in aggregate)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Dramatic 304-vs-316 substitution case. Original 316 cooker ran 10-20 years with no problems. Replacement parts mis-specified as 304 cracked and rusted within weeks in the high-salt steam environment.
- **Materials/systems:** 304 vs. 316 austenitic stainless steel; food processing
- **Era:** Unspecified (the cooker is "over here in Everett")

### New England Aquarium exterior panel corrosion
- **Anchor:** `§11.p8`, `§11.p9`, `§11.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `New England Aquarium exterior panel corrosion`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Passivation defeated by post-passivation mechanical abrasion. Mill-passivated 304 panels were ground to create fish-scale texture, which removed the protective chrome oxide. In marine environment (Boston Harbor chlorides), the oxide could not reform. Rust appeared along grinding marks and along bumper scrapes at the loading dock. Tom tested citric acid and Diet Coke (phosphoric acid) as environmentally acceptable cleaning agents.
- **Materials/systems:** 304 stainless steel panels, nitric acid passivation, marine chloride environment

### New York City building crack near George Washington Bridge
- **Anchor:** `§11.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `New York City building crack near George Washington Bridge`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief anecdote — 8-foot weld crack in a 20-story building. Tom uses it for the cocaine-capsule sidewalk joke connected to the Diet Coke punchline of the preceding aquarium case. The technical content is minimal in this lecture.
- **Materials/systems:** Weld crack (alloy unspecified in this telling)

### Argon oxygen decarbonization (AOD) process development
- **Anchor:** `§12.p2`, `§12.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Argon oxygen decarbonization (AOD) process development`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Closing case of the lecture. MIT doctoral student Krivsky, working under John Chipman in the Building 8 basement, doing basic carbon-monoxide-reaction research. Discovered low oxygen potential of argon would pull carbon and oxygen out of molten steel. Not patented at MIT; Krivsky commercialized it at a company. Now used for virtually all stainless steel and most nickel superalloys worldwide. Tom estimates ~$1B/year value to the world steel industry. The case Tom uses to illustrate "MIT lost the patent" lessons elsewhere in the corpus.
- **Materials/systems:** 304L stainless, nickel-base superalloys; argon bubbling, slag chemistry
- **Era:** Discovered late 1950s, commercialized ~1960

### Japanese steel soda cans
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Japanese steel soda cans`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Policy-protected industrial choice — Japanese government protected domestic steel by keeping vending-machine soda in steel cans rather than aluminum, since Japan has no indigenous aluminum industry. Aluminum has now displaced steel as the protection eased. Used as the lecture-opening illustration of how non-technical factors drive materials selection.

### Steel vs. aluminum automobiles (US vs. Europe market split)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `PROPOSED: Steel vs. aluminum auto markets US vs. Europe`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Fuel-price economics would favor aluminum cars in Europe, but European manufacturers must build for the US market where fuel prices favor steel. Illustrates how exporting drives materials selection.

### Hadfield's manganese steel (prison bars application)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `PROPOSED: Hadfield's manganese steel — prison bars`
- **Frame in this lecture:** 12-13% Mn steel that work-hardens during machining, making it nearly impossible to saw or file. Used historically for prison bars. Distinct alloy class from the 200-series stainless steels Tom is discussing.
