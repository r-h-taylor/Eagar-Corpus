## Cases referenced

### John Hancock Building
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`–`§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "John Hancock Building glass cracking failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom uses the building both as a structural-glass failure case (wind-induced suction popping windows out) and as a tuned-mass-damper case (whole floor with rotating weight to damp sway). Chemically tempered glass was the fix.
- **Materials/systems:** chemically tempered sodium aluminosilicate glass; tuned mass damper.
- **Era:** late 1960s–early 1970s (construction during Tom's student years).

### Aberdeen Proving Ground kinetic energy penetrator armor defeat (1984-1985)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`–`§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aberdeen Proving Ground kinetic energy penetrator armor defeat (1984-1985)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student in the Army at Watertown Arsenal tells him about a three-tank RPG test where the projectile penetrated all six layers of armor; general "tossed his cookies." Within a year, composite ceramic-metal armor defeated all known penetrators.
- **Materials/systems:** depleted uranium sabots; composite metal/ceramic armor; explosive reactive armor.
- **Era:** 1984–1986.

### 1985 RPG armor penetration demonstration
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1985 RPG armor penetration demonstration"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Same demonstration as above, viewed from the RPG side: "they shot an RPG and it went through six layers of armor, through both sides of all three tanks." Likely the same event as the Aberdeen test.
- **Era:** 1985.

### 1984-1986 composite armor development cycle
- **Anchor:** `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1984-1986 composite armor development cycle"
- **Frame in this lecture:** "In 1984 there was no armor that could not be penetrated; by 1986 there was no propellant that could penetrate the modern armor." The composite metal-ceramic armor solution.
- **Era:** 1984–1986.

### MRAP vehicle armor selection and development
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`–`§4.p7`, `§5.p1`–`§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MRAP vehicle armor selection and development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Secretary of the Army challenges Aberdeen materials group to solve Humvee IED vulnerability. Within eighteen months they deploy MRAPs with V-shaped underbody and six layers of glass armor. Tom can disclose this because soldiers can see it; the why-glass-works mechanism remains classified.
- **Materials/systems:** layered glass armor (six sheets, ~0.5–0.75 inch each); V-shaped hull; shock-absorber seats.
- **Era:** ca. 2005–2007.

### Iraq War Humvee IED vulnerability and MRAP armor development
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p4`–`§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Iraq War Humvee IED vulnerability and MRAP armor development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Four-inch black iron pipe IEDs escalating to eight-inch (1.6 megajoule) devices destroyed Humvees; MRAPs were the response.
- **Era:** 2003–2007.

### Four-inch and eight-inch IED escalation in Iraq
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p4`, `§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Four-inch and eight-inch IED escalation in Iraq"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom traces the technical escalation: four-inch pipe (~200 kJ) → eight-inch pipe (1.6 MJ), with foreign-machined copper liner discs. The pipe was Iraqi oilfield stock; the precision-machined copper liner came from a country Tom cannot name on tape (classified).
- **Era:** 2003–2007.

### Corning float glass dual-surface manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`–`§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Corning float glass dual-surface manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Touchscreen glass needs float-quality surfaces on both sides because the tin-bath side has nano-scale flatness defects that fail under humidity attack. Corning's solution (which Tom is under NDA about) involves welding two float-glass sheets together, burying the tin surfaces inside.
- **Materials/systems:** sodium aluminosilicate display glass; indium tin oxide transparent conductor.
- **Era:** ca. 2010s.

### Corian countertop thermal cracking
- **Anchor:** `§7.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Corian countertop thermal cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's own kitchen — DuPont Corian countertops, mid-1980s. Hot griddle without reflective trivet caused thermal-expansion compression; on cooling, the surface cracked. DuPont reformulated three years later.
- **Era:** mid-1980s adoption; failure shortly thereafter.

### Alcoa monopoly and price fixing with Dow Chemical
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`–`§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alcoa monopoly and price fixing with Dow Chemical"
- **Frame in this lecture:** George Kenney's doctoral thesis (under Joel Clark, 1975) showed Alcoa and Dow had matched aluminum/magnesium prices from 1945 to 1975, evidence of a market-division agreement: Alcoa stays out of magnesium, Dow stays out of aluminum.
- **Era:** 1945–1975 (the price-matching period); 1975 (thesis).
