This lecture is structurally a metallurgy lecture on nickel-based superalloys and liquation cracking, framed by two narrative interludes: the opening anecdote about precipitation-hardening precious-metal alloys (§1), and the Soviet Alpha-class titanium submarine story (§4). The technical material in §2, §5, and §6 references several recurring cases.

## Cases referenced

### Sterling silver lightweight dinnerware alloy
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`, `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sterling silver lightweight dinnerware alloy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's precipitation-hardening work using lithium and tin in silver-copper alloys. Commissioned by a gold company (now defunct) seeking lightweight sterling silver dinnerware; rejected by Reed and Barton (~1985) because consumers prefer heavy silver for status display. Application pivoted to sterling silver baby cups, where the alloy enabled twenty-percent weight reduction.
- **Materials/systems:** silver-copper alloy with Li-Sn additions; gamma-prime-analog precipitation.
- **Era:** 1984–1985.

### 1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom returning from his first European trip in 1980 and learning from the *International Herald Tribune* (front-page story) that the Soviets had fielded a titanium submarine — the Alpha class. This was simultaneously the public disclosure of the Alpha sub and the moment Tom's heavy-section titanium welding work (begun 1977 under ONR funding) became urgently relevant.
- **Era:** 1980.

### Soviet Titanium Aircraft Program (Docket 3) / Soviet Alpha-class submarine
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet Alpha-class submarine" (primary); also intersects "Soviet titanium submarine (Alpha-class) creep-fatigue failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The technical story: Soviets built titanium subs that outran U.S. destroyers and dove below depth-charge collapse depth. But they hadn't solved (or known about) creep-fatigue interaction — the phenomenon NRL had studied, where titanium under sustained compression develops rapidly-growing fatigue cracks. A submarine hull in deep water is exactly that compression case. The Soviet subs cracked and were noisy.
- **Materials/systems:** heavy-section titanium hull plate; creep-fatigue interaction under sustained compression.
- **Era:** 1977–1980s; Tom's involvement via ONR funding for heavy-section titanium welding research.

### U.S. Navy titanium creep-fatigue research (1982 Soviet discovery)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy titanium creep-fatigue research (1982 Soviet discovery)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** NRL had studied creep-fatigue interaction in titanium and identified it as a barrier to U.S. titanium submarine construction. The Soviets didn't know about it and built the Alpha class anyway, learning the hard way. Tom received NRL questioning at David Taylor / Carderock conferences in the early 1980s.
- **Era:** late 1970s through 1980s.

### Naval Research Laboratory fatigue-creep interaction cracking
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval Research Laboratory fatigue-creep interaction cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** NRL's prior identification of the creep-fatigue cracking phenomenon in titanium under compression; the technical knowledge that made the Soviet Alpha-class outcome predictable in retrospect.

### U.S. Navy heavy-section titanium submarine program / U.S. Navy titanium submarine program decision
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`, `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy titanium submarine program decision"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The cost reasoning: at $100/lb fabricated titanium, an eighteen-billion-dollar fleet buy yields one titanium sub vs. nine steel subs or three stainless steel subs. Combined with the creep-fatigue barrier (§4), the U.S. did not pursue titanium submarines.
- **Era:** 1977 onward.

### U.S. Navy all-stainless steel submarine program (AL-6X alloy) / U.S. Navy superaustenitic submarine material evaluation
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy all-stainless steel submarine program (AL-6X alloy)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** AL-6XN cited at ~$10/lb as the non-magnetic stainless option. Used as the intermediate cost comparator between HSLA steel (~$1/lb) and titanium (~$100/lb fabricated).

### Westinghouse Bettis inconel electron beam weld cracking specification
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Westinghouse Bettis inconel electron beam weld cracking specification"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The only welding code Tom knew of that allows cracks to pass spec — used to make the point that liquation cracking is so endemic to nickel-based alloys that the Navy nuclear parts code at Bettis tolerates millimeter-scale cracks within bounds. The thirty-percent-overlap electron beam weld procedure is cited as the context where the grain-boundary liquid film forms.
- **Materials/systems:** Inconel (possibly Monel — Tom corrects himself); electron beam welding with multi-pass overlap.

### Chrome-molybdenum tube cracking at Boston Navy Yard / Boston Navy Shipyard destroyer boiler pipe weld (stainless-to-chromemoly transition)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Chrome-molybdenum tube cracking at Boston Navy Yard"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief callback. The Navy insisted on Inconel rather than stainless steel for chrome-moly tube welds because, after ten-thousand-hour service, stainless steel forms brittle topologically-close-packed (sigma/mu/Laves) phases. Tom uses the case to illustrate that the TCP brittle-phase problem is not merely academic — it shaped a real Navy specification.

### Rensselaer Polytechnic transverse strength test (stainless/nickel alloy)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Rensselaer Polytechnic transverse strength test (stainless/nickel alloy)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Varestraint test as developed at RPI. Used to demonstrate liquation/solidification cracking by bending the plate during welding and exposing the partially-liquid grain boundaries.
- **Materials/systems:** stainless steels and high-alloy nickel-based alloys.
