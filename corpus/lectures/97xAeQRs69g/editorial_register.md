## Sectioning

The lecture (partial, opens mid-discussion) breaks naturally into six sections of 1.5–4 minutes each. The opener is unusual: Tom begins mid-anecdote about a precious-metal precipitation-hardening project (§1), then pivots to nickel superalloy metallurgy proper (§2), the cost/economics of submarine alloy selection (§3), the Soviet Alpha-class titanium submarine story (§4), the technical material on liquation cracking (§5), and finally weld geometry and solidification cracking (§6). The pivot at §4 is a substantial narrative interlude triggered by §3's submarine cost framing; treating it as its own section preserves the natural teaching arc.

## Flags for editor review

1. **§4.p3 "Rocky-class" submarine reference.** Captioner garble; could be a person's name, a misheard class designation, or another reference. Tom's anecdote works without resolving it, but the canonical case index should resolve it if possible.
2. **§5.p4 John Lippold address.** Tom directly addresses "John" — is Lippold present in the classroom, or is this a rhetorical aside to an absent colleague? Affects whether to add a `*[Tom addresses John Lippold, who is visiting.]*` stage direction.
3. **§2.p1 "tantalum" reconstruction.** Captioner gave "panel"; tantalum is the unambiguous alloying element in the listed set, but the silent correction should be confirmed against any extant slide.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
