## Primary case

### NASA X-33 space plane
- **Anchors:** `§7.p1`, `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`, `§10.p1`, `§10.p2`, `§10.p3`, `§11.p1`, `§11.p2`, `§11.p3`, `§11.p4`, `§11.p5`, `§12.p1`, `§12.p2`, `§12.p3`, `§12.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "NASA X-33 space plane"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's extended case study of the failed $1.3B Lockheed Martin Skunk Works program. Used to teach: (1) rapid prototyping as a buzzword that can override engineering judgment; (2) how a 2.0 safety factor degraded to 1.05 through autoclave delamination; (3) how NASA misread the 3M epoxy data sheet (10% remaining strength after 10 days, not 80–90%); (4) the unverified hydrogen permeability number off by three orders of magnitude that became the final Achilles heel.
- **Materials/systems:** 3D woven carbon fiber composite (rejected as too expensive); Nomex honeycomb sandwich with 0/45/90 carbon fiber plies; 3M epoxy adhesive (refrigerator-cured); Inconel and titanium internal struts; aerospike engines (NASA Stennis); aluminum LOX tank; multi-lobe composite LH2 tanks.
- **Era:** Post-first-Gulf-War (early-to-mid 1990s); contract awarded for 33-month design-to-flight.

## Related/cross-referenced cases

### Boeing 777 advanced composites program
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 777 advanced composites program"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as the "$20 billion bet-your-company" precedent. Drawing-board ambition was 100% composite; final delivery was 12% composite (in the nacelle).
- **Era:** ~1990 program start.

### Boeing 787 all-composite aircraft
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`, `§3.p4`, `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 787 all-composite aircraft"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Drawing-board ambition was 100% composite; final delivery was 50% composite by weight. Internal structure (ribs, keel beam) still aluminum and titanium because composites would cost a small fortune; skin is carbon fiber. The engineering reason composites stop at the skin.
- **Materials/systems:** 50% carbon fiber composite (royal blue on diagram), 20% aluminum, 15% titanium internal structure.

### Boeing 747 C-5A competition
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 747 C-5A competition"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Origin story of the 747. Boeing lost the four-or-five-billion-dollar C-5A military airlift contract to Lockheed (Marietta, Georgia); used their sunk investment to pitch the 747 as a commercial transport. Used to teach the magnitude of bet-your-company aerospace programs.

### BMW i3 carbon fiber automobile design
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "BMW i3 carbon fiber automobile design"
- **Frame in this lecture:** How BMW drove down the cost of carbon fiber for a premium small vehicle ($50K) by manufacturing fiber in the Pacific Northwest (cheap hydroelectric power for the 2,000°C carbonization furnace). Subject of one of the LGO students' presentations in this class.

### Space Shuttle disaster (referenced)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`, `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle Challenger"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The X-33 was supposed to replace the Space Shuttle. Tom uses the Shuttle's claimed-vs-actual reliability (1 in 10,000 claim, ~4% actual demonstrated by the Challenger failure at flight 25) to establish that NASA's reliability calculations are systematically overoptimistic — context for the X-33's similar failure mode.
- **Related:** Feynman's role on the Rogers Commission.

### Space Shuttle cost overrun
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle cost overrun"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Promised $1,000/lb in orbit; delivered $40,000/lb. The Shuttle was "never cost-effective."

### Aluminum-silicon carbide composite welding project
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum-silicon carbide composite welding project"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Late-1980s consulting inquiry Tom declined. He refused to electron-beam-weld Al-SiC composites because the aluminum carbide formed in the weld reacts with humidity and cracks. The company took the work to Ohio State; the doctoral student who did it later agreed it was a lousy project. Used to teach the trade-off principle in composite design.

### First Gulf War helicopter engine failures from sand ingestion
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "First Gulf War helicopter engine failures from sand ingestion"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Six-month Saudi mobilization revealed helicopters losing engines in 24 hours from sandblasting. Drove development of engine air particle separators (EAPS). Bridge into the EAPS-as-human-nose teaching analogy.
