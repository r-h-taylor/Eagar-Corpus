# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture 12 (Solid State Welding, Spring 2013). This register records every editorial judgment made in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition.*

## Sectioning

The 50-minute lecture divides into 11 sections at natural teaching transitions. The lecture has an unusually high case density in its middle (the recuperator, the salt-shaker gold theft) and a more discursive structure at the ends. Section breaks fall at:

- §1 → §2: pivot from packaging geometry to thermal expansion physics
- §2 → §3: pivot from cooling to solder ball formation
- §3 → §4: pivot from electronic packaging to brazing (Tom announces it explicitly)
- §4 → §5: pivot from strength/erosion discussion to volatilization
- §5 → §6: introduction of the recuperator case with physical artifact passed around
- §6 → §7: new case (turbine shroud seals) and new physical artifact
- §7 → §8: Tom closes the gold-theft case ("I've got stories") and resumes systematic survey of braze alloy families
- §8 → §9: pivot from cadmium in brazes (technical) to cadmium in light switches (consumer products)
- §9 → §10: student question reframes back to solder/braze terminology
- §10 → §11: student question on hot shortness opens new technical thread

- **Cut:** "Can't read it very well but it says goldwy — gull wing, right down there. That says gull wing." — self-correction on reading his own slide. The corrected reading is already in the paragraph.

### §1.p2
- **Cut:** "well, I showed you this before, but I forgot to bring in the piece" — pocket-patting / item-locating speech per convention §2. The Cisco Systems board reference is preserved.
- **Self-correction smoothed:** "Cisco Systems, and you put a bunch of uh packages on each one of these slots" → "PC board with a bunch of packages on each one of these slots." Tom's "Cisco Systems" reference is preserved as the manufacturer of the board he's holding.

### §2.p1

### §3.p4 — Student question synthesized
- Tom's layer 2 line: "anybody know what a focused ion [iron] beam is? It is — you know what it is? What is it?" This is Tom inviting an answer and getting none, then answering himself. **Editorial decision:** rendered as a `**Student:**` question for readability, because the rhetorical structure on the page reads as a Q&A. This is the most consequential editorial liberty in this lecture. The alternative — preserving Tom's self-Q&A — produces a paragraph that reads as confused. **Flag for editor review.**
- Captioner slip "focused ion [iron] beam" — Tom said "ion," captioner heard "iron" once. Corrected silently per convention §10.

## Flags for editor review

1. **Five synthesized student questions** (§3.p4, §7.p3, §10.p2, §11.p1, §11.p4). All inferred from Tom's responsive openers, none captured by the captioner. If the convention is unacceptable, fall back to preserving Tom's responsive openers as monologue.

## Decisions NOT permitted at layer 3 (constitutional constraint)

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
