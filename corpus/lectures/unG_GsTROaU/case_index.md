## Cases developed in this lecture

### Naval ship recuperator heat exchanger failure
- **Anchors:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval ship recuperator heat exchanger failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $100 million Navy program (early 1990s), Rolls-Royce-built room-sized brazed stainless steel heat exchanger for preheating destroyer/frigate combustion air. Designed for 100,000 hours; failed in two minutes. Cause: 2D finite element model assumed symmetric gas flow; asymmetric heating snapped six-inch hardened-steel tie bars. Tom served on the post-mortem team. Teaching point: "wishful thinking is not good engineering."
- **Materials/systems:** brazed stainless steel honeycomb; hardened steel tie bars
- **Era:** circa 1992

### Aerospace company gold-nickel braze powder inventory control failure
- **Anchors:** `§7.p8`–`§7.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aerospace company gold-nickel braze powder inventory control failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mid-1970s New England aerospace company. Turbine shroud honeycomb seals brazed with 82Au-18Ni alloy applied from a literal salt shaker (50-oz jars, ~$16,000/jar at $400/oz gold). Brazing supervisor doubled his requisition during a production scale-up, kept the rate after scale-down, and stole one jar/week for 16 months — $1.6M to $2.5M total. Caught by tip, not by controls. Tom hired by the insurance bonding company (not the aerospace contractor, which was on cost-plus) to audit losses. Connecticut thief got one-year sentence; New York middleman six months suspended; NYC fence walked.
- **Materials/systems:** 82Au-18Ni braze powder; nickel-base superalloy honeycomb; Krylon lacquer binder
- **Era:** mid-1970s; audit circa 1976

### Turbine engine shroud blade tip wear and repair
- **Anchors:** `§7.p1`–`§7.p2`, `§7.p4`–`§7.p5`, `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Turbine engine shroud blade tip wear and repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Physical artifact (used shroud) shown to class. Honeycomb seal mechanics: three-to-four-thousandths nickel superalloy sheet, designed to be cut into by creeping turbine blades to establish a self-fitting seal. Transition from gold-nickel braze (non-eroding) to nickel-phosphorus/-carbon/-boron brazes (require chromium/palladium inhibitors against base-metal erosion). Cost driver for transition: 1980 gold price spike from $200 to $800/oz.
- **Materials/systems:** nickel-base superalloy honeycomb; 82Au-18Ni → Ni-P/-C/-B brazes; Pd/Cr inhibitors
- **Era:** late 1970s–early 1980s transition

### Norton asphalt reclamation carbide-tip braze joint
- **Anchors:** `§4.p5`–`§4.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Norton asphalt reclamation carbide-tip braze joint"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student developed an in-situ composite braze alloy (Cu-Sn-TiH2-C) for brazing carbide bits to highway asphalt-reclamation tooling. Titanium hydride decomposes in vacuum brazing, releasing Ti metal which reacts with C to form TiC particles in the joint. Result: braze alloy CTE halved vs. Cu-base; tool life tripled in field test on actual road surfaces. Failure mode addressed: tip loss during overnight road work stopping the whole reclamation machine.
- **Materials/systems:** Cu-Sn-TiH2-C in-situ composite braze; WC tips; nickel-base or steel substrate
- **Era:** unspecified; Tom's student project

### Molybdenum-copper cross-field amplifier radar waveguide braze joint
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Molybdenum-copper cross-field amplifier radar waveguide braze joint"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference back to a prior lecture's case. 82Au-18Ni braze of Mo to Cu formed Ni3Mo intermetallic, snapping at five degrees of bend. Reformulated to a low-Ni transient-liquid-phase braze (3% Ni, mostly Cu) — eliminated intermetallics, allowed 180° hairpin bend.
- **Materials/systems:** Mo, Cu, 82Au-18Ni → low-Ni TLP braze
- **Era:** prior lecture; cross-reference

### San Diego vacuum brazing furnace zinc contamination disaster
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "San Diego vacuum brazing furnace zinc contamination disaster"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $2M brazing furnace contaminated when wrong (Zn-bearing) braze alloy was run. Attempted recovery by raising furnace temperature to vaporize Zn out; instead redistributed Zn from hot zones to cooler water-cooled walls. Recovery attempt destroyed the furnace.
- **Materials/systems:** vacuum brazing furnace; Zn-bearing braze alloy
- **Era:** unspecified

### Aluminum radiator field brazing development
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum radiator field brazing development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Pre-1980, automotive radiators were Cu, field-repairable by mechanics with propane torches. All-Al radiators (e.g., Corvette) required factory brazing only. Around 1980, Alcan Aluminum developed a fluoride-based field-brazable flux for Al. Result: industry-wide transition Cu → Al for radiators.
- **Materials/systems:** Cu (legacy) → Al brazed radiators; fluoride-based flux (HF chemistry)
- **Era:** ca. 1980 Alcan flux development

### Sweden cadmium electrical switch fire hazard
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sweden cadmium electrical switch fire hazard"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1970s-80s Swedish environmental policy outlawed Cd in electrical components. Within three years, house fires from inadequate arc-suppression in <15A switches forced reversal. Teaching point: 0.1% CdO in Ag contacts raises arc-suppression rating from ~7A to 15A.
- **Materials/systems:** Ag/CdO electrical switch contacts
- **Era:** 1970s–80s
