## Cases referenced

### Davenport Iowa Alcoa quench-line plugged-jet failure
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`–`§1.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alcoa Davenport quench-and-temper failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A plugged water jet on the world's largest aluminum rolling mill at Davenport produced under-quenched streaks in plate that had already been machined onto flying Boeing aircraft. Government-Alcoa-airlines partnership developed an electrical-resistivity NDT probe to find affected wings in service.
- **Materials/systems:** Quenched-and-tempered high-strength aluminum aerospace plate; NIST-developed resistivity probe.
- **Era:** 1970s.

### Amtrak / British Rail / Phelps Dodge copper-wire fraud
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`–`§2.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Amtrak catenary wire procurement fraud case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A British contractor on the Amtrak Northeast Corridor electrification project tried to install a sub-spec copper wire (Rome, NY) to capture margin on a fixed-cost contract, told the federal government no other supplier existed, and was caught when the FBI walked passports off the executive team after a mediation Tom helped resolve. The Providence test track shows the inferior wire arcing on the train pass.
- **Materials/systems:** Overhead catenary copper wire, very high ductility spec.
- **Era:** Tom's involvement "a few years ago" — late 2000s / early 2010s.
- **Related canonical clusters:** "Accel trolley wire for Boston-New Haven electrified rail upgrade"; "Amtrak Accela trolley wire quality investigation"; "Amtrak New Haven to Boston trolley wire"; "Northeast Corridor Amtrak copper wire contract fraud."

### Seawolf hydrogen cracking and blue jelly suits
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's full forensic case — he traced the cracking to lubricant contamination in the GMAW weld zone. To repair, Electric Boat used cooling-suited welders ("blue jelly suits") on wheeled carts to work inside egg-crate construction with 400°F preheat. Forced reuse of the heavy plate because four-inch plate was on a >1-year mill backlog.
- **Era:** Tom's investigation dated to the Seawolf construction crisis.

### USS Cole repair cost
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Cole"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as another case where the repair cost exceeded new-build cost, for strategic-signaling reasons — the US didn't want to concede the loss of a capital ship.

### Bay Bridge tie-rod corrosion-driven hydrogen cracking
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bay Bridge California corrosion-induced hydrogen cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The 46-inch-diameter steel tie rods cracked within weeks after corrosion-generated hydrogen diffused into the steel. Used to extend the hydrogen-cracking framework from welds to corrosion.

### Japan Manufacturing Study and HSLA technology transfer
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1984-1985 Japan Manufacturing Study"
- **Related:** "US Navy HSLA Steel Mill Investment", "US Navy Japan steel mill visit and HSLA technology transfer", "Japanese HSLA shipbuilding steel development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Office of Naval Research sent Tom to Japan in the mid-1980s because the Japanese had the only world-class technology for accelerated-cooled HSLA steel. The Navy was considering a $100M Title III investment in a domestic steel mill.

### Naval Research Lab HY-80 laser welding (clean-steel finding)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval Research Lab HY80 laser welding study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Early-1980s NRL finding that laser welding HY-80 vaporized inclusions and gave better toughness — used to argue that ultra-clean steel resists hydrogen cracking, but isn't producible at tonnage scale.
