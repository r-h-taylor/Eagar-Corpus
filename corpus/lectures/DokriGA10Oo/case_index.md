# Case Index — Welding Metallurgy Lecture 1 (Summer 2015)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§9.p1`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This is a first-day lecture. The substantive teaching arc is in §6–§11: why metals (toughness, ductility) rather than ceramics or polymers. Several cases are introduced as illustrations of that thesis, and a few are foreshadowed (Sea Wolf appears twice — once in §2 as a course-context anchor, once in §9 as a developed teaching case).

## Cases referenced

### Sea Wolf submarine weld cracking (SSN-21)
- **Anchors:** `§2.p5` (course-context introduction), `§9.p1`–`§9.p3` (developed case)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem` / `Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking` / `USS Sea Wolf submarine hull cracking` — three canonical entries appear to overlap; reconciliation needed.
- **Frame in this lecture:** The weld metal ran 30–40% higher strength than intended because of carbon and alloy content; toughness dropped; 1 mm microscopic flaws formed that the original inspection wasn't looking for; a grinder noticed magnetized swarf aligning with the cracks, an inspector was called, and every weld in the sub had to be cut out. 18% of the ship had been built. Cost the Navy $2 billion.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-100 weld metal; magnetic particle inspection; probability-of-detection curve.
- **Era:** 1980s (cancelled then reinstated by Congress).
- **Pairs with:** Air Force probability-of-detection studies (1980s); Boeing 0.010" inspection spec.

### Persian Gulf destroyer brass valve stress corrosion cracking (Second Gulf War)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Persian Gulf destroyer 3000 psi air system failure` (closely related: `US Navy Destroyer brass fitting failure (Second Gulf War)`, `USS cruiser brass airline failure (Second Gulf War)`)
- **Frame in this lecture:** A destroyer in the Persian Gulf lost its 3,000 psi air system mid-attack; root cause was stress corrosion cracking in a brass valve made of the wrong alloy. Discovered at the Naval Supply Center in Philadelphia that twenty-some percent of brass valves in inventory were the wrong alloy. Two students in the same cohort had worked on different parts of the failure.
- **Materials/systems:** Brass alloy; stress corrosion cracking; 3,000 psi air system.
- **Era:** Second Gulf War aftermath (early-to-mid 2000s).

### Dan Rather CBS building collapse
- **Anchors:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Dan Rather CBS building collapse`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Six-story Manhattan building under construction for CBS, early 1980s. Concrete beams with steel rebar; rebars were tack-welded where they crossed; high-carbon rebar at the tack-weld became brittle, fracture toughness dropped by a factor of eight, fifth-floor beam failed in bending tension, dominoed all the beams. $50 million loss, one workman's leg broken. The Steinway Piano factory tour (CBS-owned at the time) is a coda.
- **Materials/systems:** Merchant-quality steel rebar; concrete in tension; weldability and carbon equivalent.
- **Era:** Early 1980s.

### Boeing 747-400 titanium catalytic converter / air tubing
- **Anchors:** `§10.p1`–`§10.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding failure` (related: `Boeing 747-400 Titanium Air Tubing and Catalytic Converter`)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Air tubing switched from stainless steel to titanium to save weight for the extended-range 747-400; Engelhard built the catalytic converters for ozone scrubbing in the cabin air system. Introduced here mainly as a vehicle for the aircraft-range discussion (10,000 → 13,000 mile aircraft) rather than as a developed failure case. Tom flags it as a long story not fully told.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium tubing; catalytic converter; ozone scrubbing.
- **Era:** Late 1980s.

### Air Force probability-of-detection studies
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`

### Ceramics fever in Japan (1985 ceramic show)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: `Japan ceramics fever (mid-1980s)` (closely related: `1980s all-ceramic engine development initiative`, `1985-1986 ceramic engine development failure (anticipated, not realized)`, `Ceramic engine development (1990s ceramics hype)`)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom in Tokyo 1984–85 saw the ceramic show that drew a million visitors. Used as evidence that the failure of fine ceramics to take over jet engines was not yet obvious to scientists, industry, or the public — Tom's 1992 *Welding Journal* prediction was contrarian at the time. Critical flaw size from the Ashby plot is the technical hinge.
- **Era:** 1984–1985 in Japan; 1992 prediction.

### Cruise missile graphite engine
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Cruise missile graphite engine`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as the existence proof that brittle-material jet engines can be built — when the engine only has to last an hour, the inspection problem becomes tractable. Graphite is tougher than most ceramics; coatings prevent burn-up.
- **Materials/systems:** Graphite engine; surface coatings.

### Harvard football stadium (1910s reinforced concrete)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Harvard Stadium reinforced concrete (1910)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as the oldest reinforced concrete structure in the country, used to illustrate that concrete continues to harden over years and decades — "you have to have a hammer drill with a carbide tip" to drill it now.
- **Era:** Construction 1903–1910; observation contemporary.
