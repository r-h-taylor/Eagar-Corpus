## §5. World Trade Center, Lester Thurow, and the soundbite world [19:47]

## §6. Why steel: ceramics, toughness, and critical flaw size [27:02]

`§6.p1` Another article you'll get is *Materials Research Needs in the 21st Century*. I wrote this for the National Research Council of the National Academy. They had a committee of distinguished people trying to tell the Defense Department what high-tech materials they would be using in the 21st century. Copyright 2003, so we were doing this around 2001. Harvey Schadler was chair, General Electric Research. Alan Liss, General Dynamics retired. James Baskerville, Bath Iron Works — I think he was a grad of this program too. Millard Firebaugh I mentioned; he was at Electric Boat then, but has been Chief Engineer of the Navy.

`§6.p2` I didn't really fit in, because I tended to agree with this million-dollar US Army study. The US Army had done a study in the late 1990s of what material they would be using the most of in the next 30 or 40 years. Anybody know what it was? **Student:** Aluminum? A million dollars to figure this out. **Student:** Humans? Steel. I'm going to tell you why it's steel in a moment. Because I could convince them that it would be steel, and they needed me to write something, I sort of took the outline of my material selection course — which is one of the modules of 12 units — and I put it into this four-page summary, which they put into an appendix. It doesn't have my name associated with it. I'm not ashamed of it.

`§6.p3` In 1992 I wrote this article in the *Welding Journal* called "The Future of Metals." This was at a time when people thought fine ceramics were going to take over jet engines and everything else, and they didn't. I was predicting in 1992 that they wouldn't. How did I know? Because I was looking at the properties. What do you think about a ceramic as a structural material? **Student:** Hard, brittle. Hard, brittle, right. High temperature too, which is why they wanted it for jet engines. They actually built jet engines, but it turns out the critical flaw size for a brittle material is extremely small. The formula is simple: the toughness has to be greater than the stress times the square root of pi times the crack length. You can get a very high applied stress. The toughness of a ceramic is terrible. Anyone ever seen someone cut glass? They score it. They take a carbide or a diamond tool and scratch the surface, and then they hit it with the heel of their hand and it'll break right where they put the scratch — because the critical flaw size is less than a human hair for a brittle material like glass.

`§6.p4` So as long as you make a jet engine with no imperfections larger than half or one-tenth the size of a human hair, it'll run at great temperatures. We've already done that. It's called a cruise missile. The cruise missile engine is made out of graphite. Graphite's not particularly tough, but it only has to last for an hour, and the bigger problem is keeping it from burning up in an hour. They put coatings on it. But graphite's tougher than most of the ceramics.

`§6.p5` When I was in Tokyo in the mid '80s, they had what they called ceramics fever. They had a ceramic show in 1985, when I left — a million people showed up. Not just scientists; everybody thought fine ceramics were great and were going to take over the world. I came back and gave talks at NIST in Gaithersburg, Maryland. I got up and said, the problem with ceramics is they don't have any toughness. The only structural materials made out of ceramics are Portland cement and toilet bowls. I'd throw in the kitchen sink, but you've got to line it with cast iron to give fracture toughness. If you drop something in the sink and it was just made out of ceramic, you'd end up with a shattered sink. So they make it out of cast iron — and gray cast iron is the worst metal of all in terms of toughness. People forget this toughness requirement.

## §9. The Sea Wolf weld inspection problem [44:59]

`§9.p1` One of the points I'm trying to make — this is one of the two or three for today's lecture — is that we use metals because they have good toughness, and we don't use ceramics because they have lousy toughness. Their critical flaw size is so small that you can never inspect for those things. The Sea Wolf submarine problem, to get back to a Navy example, is that they ended up getting too high carbon content and alloy content. The weld metal was 30 or 40% higher strength than it was intended to be, because of the chemistry, and they got a less tough steel. It formed little microscopic flaws that the original inspectors weren't looking for, something that small. It's a different type of cracking that we hadn't seen before. They had built 18% of the ship before a guy found it.

`§9.p2` He found it by a non-destructive test technique that was not exactly authorized. The outside of the hull has to be very smooth. They grind off the weld reinforcement, because you want a hull so smooth that even the weld reinforcement would create enough of a ripple that a guy listening on the other side could hear it. So they grind the weld smooth on the outside surface. The grinder was grinding, and he saw some of the grinding swarf — what's grinding swarf? Grinding swarf is the little powder you get from grinding. He noticed some of it had been magnetized — the steel had been magnetized, and the magnetic field was aligning with these little cracks in the weld metal. He had never seen that before. He had enough sense to call over an inspector — what's that, I've never seen that before. They went in with their instruments and found these things that were about a millimeter in size. Typically all the requirements for any inspection anywhere — except some of Boeing's, which are tighter than anything the world ever needed — typically we're looking for eighth of an inch, 3 mm flaws. These were 1 mm flaws, all over the place but very small.

`§9.p3` They had to cut out every weld in the sub. **Student:** What testing did they usually do? Typically magnetic particle, but they're looking for large flaws. If your minimum detectable flaw size is an eighth of an inch reliably detectable — the Air Force did a bunch of studies in the 1980s on what they call probability of detection. If you plot the probability of detection by flaw size in inches, you'll be at 95% probability of detection by a manual inspection at an eighth of an inch. You'll be at about 50% at a sixteenth of an inch. These were down in the millimeter size, very low probability of detection. Aircraft engines, they're looking for things 0.015". We don't really have inspection technologies that can do it, and Boeing has specs of 0.010". No one can find it, but it's in the spec.

## §11. Demonstrations: ductile vs. brittle, and why metals [52:57]
