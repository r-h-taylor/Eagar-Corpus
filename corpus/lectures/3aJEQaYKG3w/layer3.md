## §2. Engine architecture and operating environment [00:54]

`§2.p1` A little background on critical airplane engine components. Everyone in here has been on a flight before, correct? One of the things you don't realize is just the harsh operating environment that the engines experience while you're in flight. You're dealing with high temperatures in the turbine section above the melting point of the alloys used, as well as high atmospheric pressures and issues related to velocity and vibration of the turbomachinery.

`§2.p2` What we see here is the general sections of the engine. You have the inlet section where the air comes in, and then you have your fan blades. You have the compressor section, which consists of both LPC, low-pressure compressor, and high-pressure compressor. From there you have a combustion chamber where ignition actually occurs. From the combustion chamber it goes into the turbine section, which is where the thrust in the engine comes from. From the turbine section it then goes from a high-pressure turbine to a low-pressure turbine and out the exhaust.

`§2.p3` The example shown here was for a two-spool engine, which deals with how the engine actually rotates. This isn't an aero-astro class, but there are elective classes on engine design. One of the things to be cognizant of is that the speed at which the engine rotates and the temperatures the turbine inlet reaches all deal with the efficiency of the engine and all have constraints. So for a two-spool engine you have constraints that the various spools have to operate independently but together at a fixed ratio. There are mass flow constraints, power and energy balance constraints between the compressor and turbine sections. If you don't have those, you can either get surge or stall conditions in operation, or you can get vibration.

`§2.p4` For the compressor section, it's the aerodynamics. You have the air come in, and you have to compress it through the various stages of the LPC and HPC. When you go into the turbine after combustion, it's the turbine inlet temperature that drives the efficiency of the engine. You want to get as high a turbine inlet temperature as possible so you can get the best efficiency overall. Now they're at the point where the turbine inlet temperature is above the melting point of the nickel-based superalloys, or whatever alloy they typically use in that section.

`§2.p5` If we look at this chart, from the various sections — as you have the inlet through the fan section, you're at low temperature and low pressure. As you go through the LPC, the total number of stages between the low-pressure compressor and high-pressure compressor is typically 12 to 19 stages. So if you had 12 LPC stages and 7 HPC stages, through each one of those stages you have both a temperature rise and a pressure rise, to the point where it then goes into the combustion chamber. Ignition occurs at about 40 atmospheres, and over 2500 Fahrenheit — that's where your ignition occurs. As it goes through the turbine section is when the work is done. Work is done on the engine in the form of thrust, which runs the engine. As it goes through the turbine section and out the exhaust, there is a drop in both temperature and pressure.

`§2.p6` This image here shows various material selection options in the various sections of the engine. In the turbine section a lot of times you'll have what's called nickel-based superalloys, because they're able to withstand the high operating temperatures and pressures, and they have good oxidation resistance in that environment. In the fan and compressor sections you'll either see stainless steel type alloys or titanium-based alloys, with Ti-6Al-4V being the most highly used alloy.

## §3. Critical parts, life-limited parts, and uncontained engine events [06:07]

`§3.p1` So what is a critical part? The FAA, Federal Aviation Administration, defines engine life-limited parts, LLPs, as rotor and major static structural parts whose failure is likely to result in a hazardous engine effect. A hazardous engine effect means an uncontained engine event — some part, whether it be a disc, a blade or vane, or another part, can actually pass through and be released from the engine in operation. They also have contained engine events, and Professor Eagar mentioned that in one of the previous lectures. Something like that can happen and you might not even know it happened during a flight, and it might just result in shutting down that particular engine. Whereas if you have an uncontained engine event, the NTSB will get called in and there'll be a large investigation, because oftentimes when you have an uncontained event, it results in catastrophic failure and more often than not fatalities and critical accidents are involved.

`§3.p2` One of the images here, I believe this was the nose of the plane after the Pensacola, Florida crash. A couple more images — a lot of these images are taken from the NTSB when they're on site to do their accident investigation and reconstruction. What you're seeing here would be a fan hub section that has complete fracture and was released. There was on the news a couple weeks ago, the cover to an engine came off during flight, and the news was showing the engine vibrating back and forth. When something like this happens, a lot of pieces in the engine can be released, and oftentimes they either have to scrap the entire engine, which is millions of dollars loss.

`§3.p3` Industry data has shown that manufacturing-induced anomalies — in the forging and casting processes themselves — account for about 40% of the rotor cracking events, for data up to 2010. The FAA every six years or so puts out a report on a whole list of uncontained engine events and what their cause, the location, and whether human factors were involved. About 40% of those deal with manufacturing-induced defects in the fabrication of the component.

`§3.p4` Uncontained engine rates — I don't know if it's gone up or down since then, but there were about three events per year from data from 1984 to 1989. Material issues have become less of a problem since they've gone to triple melt practices and vacuum arc remelting; manufacturing issues and maintenance issues — most of the plants have improved since the 80s on those issues.

`§3.p5` Because there were some catastrophic failures in the 80s, the FAA decided to set what's called a safe life approach to these critical parts, meaning that whether they still had useful life left or not, they would have to be retired from service based on a set number of cycles. So it wasn't retirement for cause, meaning the alloy had reached the end of its life due to the environment. It was retirement based on a preset number of cycles that the engine would reach. In their fracture mechanics calculations they use 0.03 inches long by 0.01 inches deep as their input to determine what number of cycles would be allowed.

`§3.p6` The lifing methodology requires coordinated engineering, manufacturing, service, and maintenance plans, because you have various numbers of life-limited parts. They're manufactured from different alloys, and based on cycles and on the mission profile — whether you're doing little jump flights, one-hour flights, or 16-hour flights halfway across the world — the mission profile and the environment the parts undergo will affect how often maintenance has to be performed on them.

In the turbine section, because you're operating at such a high temperature, the airplane companies and the OEMs — original equipment manufacturers — have had to design more intricate methods.

`§4.p2` This is a turbine blade here. What you'll see is they actually laser-drill in cooling holes. The other thing they do on the turbine blades is they put what's called a TBC, a thermal barrier coating, which is a ceramic-based coating, and that also has laser drills put through it because you're operating beyond the melting point of the nickel-base superalloy. You can pass that along.

`§4.p3` The other thing on here, this is a duct segment. When they do non-destructive testing on it, they'll do eddy current tests. `§4.p4` The processing steps are key. Each one of these blades is probably five thousand dollars. Because of the high alloy costs, you have hundreds of blades, you have multiple sections. They're high-value parts, so a high reject rate is unacceptable for the owners and operators.

## §5. Failure modes and disc design constraints [14:47]

`§5.p1` In terms of the failure modes for these types of engine components, very important failure modes to consider include high cycle fatigue and low cycle fatigue. Every time you go up on a plane and you have a takeoff, you have cruise, and it goes through its various mission profile up to landing. You have a pressure and depressure, which is a cycle. Every time you're on a plane, it undergoes cyclic stresses. These planes operate basically non-stop — it goes from one location to the next, it starts acquiring cycles. So both high cycle fatigue and low cycle fatigue are very important, as well as creep, because we're dealing with high temperature mechanical degradation. You have thermomechanical fatigue due to temperature gradients and load variations.

`§5.p2` As an example, on a disc, the temperature can vary from 300 degrees at the hub to over a thousand degrees at the rim. Even on a single disc, you have over 700 degrees temperature gradient in the radial direction in service. On these discs, the manufacturing is very critical. This is your dovetail attachment that the turbine blade actually has to fit in. Every turbine blade goes into one of these dovetail attachments. The tolerances are very tight, because if you have a manufacturing issue from the milling process, that will be a little stress concentrator, and you'll start getting cracks at the dovetail attachments.

`§5.p3` One of the things they design for in the disc sections is what's called the burst speed, which is the speed in your design input at which you have to prevent the disc from actually fracturing and bursting while it's running. That's typically about 1.3 to 1.4 — the factor that they use beyond the design speed. So you do have safety factors already accounted for, but the components operate at pretty aggressive temperatures and pressures, and they really push the limits of the design over the years.

## §6. Titanium alloys, Ti-6-4, and microstructural defects [17:32]

`§6.p1` Titanium alloys — this relates to the Sioux City, Iowa incident, which was a fracture of a titanium component. Titanium alloys are allotropic, which means they exist at more than one crystallographic phase depending on which temperature you're at. At room temperature they're hexagonal close-packed, and this is what's known as the alpha phase. Around 1620 Fahrenheit there's a transformation from the alpha to beta phase, and the beta phase is body-centered cubic. The titanium alloys actually change their structure depending on temperature, and this needs to be accounted for in your heat treatments and the forging and casting processes that they undergo.

`§6.p2` We'll talk about Ti-6-4 because it makes up about 60% of total titanium-based alloy production. You have to add stabilizers, because titanium alone won't give you the properties you need. You start adding what's called alpha stabilizers and beta stabilizers, to give you mechanical properties, oxidation and corrosion resistance, and other various properties needed in service. The alpha stabilizers include aluminum and oxygen, and the beta stabilizers are vanadium and molybdenum. As you start adding more and more alloying elements — and this is the same as true for nickel-based superalloys — it gives you more and more opportunities for localized areas of concentration where you can ultimately get brittle failure, inclusions, or other issues if you don't do your forging, casting, and heat treatment appropriately.

`§6.p3` This image down here is what's called alpha case. On Ti-6-4 alloy, you get oxygen diffusion into the alloy, and oftentimes it'll get this rust-looking appearance. They have to mill that off, so you can see a distinct difference between the alpha case region and the region that's been machined. All these things have to be taken into account, especially for people who work in the manufacturing facilities, because you don't want to leave material just laying around — you'll have oxidation reactions undergoing. That ties into people who do the supply chain and the movement of the material through the plant.

`§6.p4` One of the issues with Ti-6-4 is you have an inherent microstructural variability of the alloy itself, because you have two phases — alpha phase and beta phase. What we're seeing here in these micrographs are grains. The darker colored are beta grains, and the lighter colored are the alpha grains. They're distributed in various proportions depending on the chemical composition and the manufacturing process. You're always going to have variability from one batch to the next. These are things you can't really design against — you have to take into account when you're doing mechanical properties. You have a range, it's not just a single value. If you have isolated regions where there's either all beta grains or all alpha grains, that's not wanted. You want a random dispersion of the grains throughout the structure.

`§6.p5` One other thing to point out for the titanium alloys — it's what's called a Widmanstätten structure. It translates into a basket-weave structure. Just a little metallurgy since we are in a material science class.

## §7. Nickel-base superalloy segregation and titanium inclusion types [22:08]

`§7.p1` For the nickel-based superalloys, some issues — segregation is the main issue in the forging and melt processes that are critical for the nickel-based superalloys. As you have a large ingot or billet, as it cools the outer part of the billet will cool at a different gradient than the inside part. On a large structure, whether you're in the center or on the ends, you can have segregation that occurs if it's not cooled in a controlled manner. You can get segregation, it results in various grain sizes. So what they do typically with nickel-based billets is a process called hot topping. They know they'll get segregation based on the cooling, so they decide that if they can control where the segregation occurs they can then remove that, and that goes back in and they remelt it and reuse it. They take off about 7% from either end where they expect the segregation to be.

`§7.p2` Now if you have an upset condition in the plant and you have segregation somewhere else that you don't expect after you do your hot topping process, you can get other issues, which could include carbonitrides, which are slag stringers; white spots and freckles are various forms of segregation. What you see here is just some SEM images of white spots and freckles in a nickel-base superalloy. Porosity is another issue due to diffusion effects.

`§7.p3` For titanium alloys there are two main types of defect. The type one defect is called a high interstitial defect, and this is an alpha region that has a higher hardness than the surrounding material. When you have a localized area of high hardness, that's where your crack initiation will occur. You want to try to avoid having brittle regions in these components that are spinning at 20,000 rpm and operating at 2,200 to 2,500 Fahrenheit and high pressures. If you have a brittle region, it often will end in a catastrophic type fracture. The brittle region and the high interstitial defects are often due to localized nitrogen, oxygen, or carbon components in the alloy melts. This is also known as a hard alpha inclusion or a low-density inclusion.

`§7.p4` The second type is the high aluminum defect, which is an aluminum-rich alpha region that is over multiple beta grains. Depending on whether you have alpha grains or beta grains, because you have various stabilizing elements, you will have different types of defects when you look at it under a microscope based on the elements in each one of those defects. The third one is a high-density inclusion — this relates to refractory elements in the melt.

## §9. Non-destructive evaluation methods [32:08]

`§9.p1` What do they do to prevent against those types of degradation mechanisms and defects? Oftentimes when the parts come in they'll do a visual inspection. That's the cheapest way, it's the first line of defense. It has limitations, though. You can't detect internal flaws. If there was a flaw on the inside of the turbine blade rather than on the surface, you wouldn't be able to see something like that from a visual. If it was an issue in one of the cooling passages, you can't see that with just a visual. There's also a limit of detection based on the inspector. Basically they say it's a quarter of an inch. There's variability from inspector to inspector, so there's a range, but the limit of detection used as the input in the design for fracture mechanics and other calculations is about a quarter of an inch for a visual.

`§9.p2` In conjunction with the regular visual they often do a penetrant inspection. FPI is fluorescent penetrant; LPI is liquid penetrant. They will spray the component with the developer, it operates by capillary action, and then with fluorescent they'll put a black light over it, and if you have a crack it will light up — that's your indication. Fluorescent dyes render indications of cracks or other surface flaws under UV light. It's fairly inexpensive. You can perform the inspection on complex parts such as blades — you can do it in the root section, you can do it along the thickness of the leading edge, trailing edge.

`§9.p3` One of the things is, it takes time. If you're doing a penetrant inspection, you have to let it sit for 20 minutes to allow the capillary action to go into what may be a crack or not. The whole test takes about 30 minutes — the prep for the test, letting it sit, and then putting the black light over it. Time is one of the big disadvantages. Sensitivity and reliance on inspector is another big disadvantage because of the variability involved.

`§9.p4` The main point is there is no single bullet NDE technique that they can use. They try to use multiple techniques. They can do ultrasonic inspections for subsurface defects. When these things go in for maintenance in the plant, they have to perform these things before it'll be allowed back into service.

## §12. NTSB findings — inspection failures and process changes [45:56]

`§12.p1` Some key NTSB report findings. The hard alpha metallurgical defect was formed in the material during manufacture, from the ingot from which the disc was forged. The defect was missed not only by ultrasonic inspection — it was missed by a macro inspection that was performed at the GE facility. The reasoning in the NTSB report why they missed it was because the macro etch was performed upstream of the critical location where it should have been done. They did the macro at one part, and then the part progressed through the plant where most subsequent machining operations were done. The macro etch should have been done after those machining operations, and that's why it was missed — because of when they did the macro etch, not the process of the macro etch itself.

`§12.p2` The other key thing is that liquid penetrant was performed only 760 cycles before the catastrophic failure, and they allowed it to go in even though they noticed discoloration on the liquid penetrant. This is a key case study because the FAA, as a result of the number of fatalities and the criticality, really went and changed the processes — quality assurance, quality control, and the non-destructive evaluation processes that had to be performed in the plant.

`§12.p3` The hard alpha defect cracked with the application of stress during the first initial exposure to thrust. First takeoff is when the crack initiated. As I said, fluorescent penetrant was performed only 760 cycles to failure. It was still allowed to go back into service. One of the findings in the report, they noticed in the area around the inclusion there was discoloration, and the discoloration was attributed to the penetrant. That was actually a fairly damning conclusion that led to quite a few changes to the quality assurance and quality control in the plant.

## §13. Cost-benefit analysis of NDE failures [48:50]

`§13.p1` A quick cost-benefit. With a failure like this, you have insurance issues after the fact, and it's how do you quantify losses and things like that. In 2009 dollars: an in-flight engine shutdown is about half a million dollars. A lot of these things you can get from either FAA reports or advisory circulars, but some of it is from airline transport associations or other aviation industry groups that publish these values.

`§13.p2` What I did as part of my thesis was a cost-benefit related to NDE missing indications and non-destructive inspection alone. What I found was, in the period 1989 to 2009, there were three or four commercial accidents that could be attributed directly to non-destructive testing, either missing or improperly performing the inspection. Depending on what year it occurred, you try to put everything on an apples-to-apples basis. We're talking the same dollars — same net present value of money. You have past dollars, future dollars, you have to take into account interest rates, you have to take into account the Consumer Price Index, which is related to inflation. A lot of these numbers you can get from the Federal Reserve. They change every year, like inflation rate changes.

`§13.p3` This value of approximately 1.5 million dollars for a life — that's the value that the Department of Transportation estimated a human life was worth in 1996. That number can vary greatly depending on potentially who a person is and the potential loss of future earnings. For the Sioux City Iowa case, it was basically an order of magnitude — for one individual they placed fifteen million dollars, whereas the other average is about 1.5 million.

`§13.p4` If we look at the four incidents in that twenty-year period, Sioux City Iowa is by far the most catastrophic of them all, compared to Carrollton flight, ValuJet, and the Pensacola crash. 111 fatalities, 47 serious injuries. Taking into account all the costs associated with injury, loss of the aircraft, engines, and things like that, this number trumps far and away — it was over eighty percent of all the costs associated in that twenty-year period. And those four were due to non-destructive testing issues. There are a lot of other events that weren't included in this.

`§13.p5` The last slide is just on peripheral costs. Litigation costs can be ten to twenty percent of that final number, which is not taken into account. You have damage to the airline's reputation resulting in loss of revenue. It was estimated that in the ValuJet incident there was a fifty-five million dollar loss in sales in the seven months following that crash, just due to people not feeling comfortable flying with it. The public perception resulted in significant loss of sales revenue. Ultimately there have been catastrophic failures where the airline has rebranded and changed its name. You don't hear about ComAir anymore, or other certain companies, as a way to deal with the poor public perception — they'll change the company name.
