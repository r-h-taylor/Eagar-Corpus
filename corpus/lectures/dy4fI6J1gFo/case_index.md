## Cases referenced

### GE GTD-111 / GT-100 superalloy patent dispute
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "GTD-111 superalloy patent dispute" (also tracked as "GE GTD-111 nickel superalloy patent dispute" and "General Electric GT-100 patent litigation")
- **Frame in this lecture:** GE applies for heat-treatment patent on a public-domain alloy in the early 1980s; patent office grants it nearly twenty years later, in 1997-98, after competitors have already designed the alloy into their systems. Used to introduce HIP rejuvenation as a way for buyers to legally re-condition aged parts they had previously purchased.
- **Materials/systems:** GT-100/GTD-111 gamma-prime-strengthened nickel superalloy; land-based and aero turbine components.
- **Era:** Alloy developed early 1980s; patent allowed 1997-98.

### Chromalloy HIP rejuvenation with Greek Navy student
- **Anchor:** `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Turbine blade coarsening and remanufacturing with HIP treatment" (closely related to "Engine blade rejuvenation via hot isostatic pressing (HIP)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student work with Chromalloy: hot isostatic press treatment at 20,000 psi argon used to restore the fine gamma-prime structure of coarsened turbine blades without melting them, preserving the buyer's license to use the patented alloy.
- **Materials/systems:** Gamma-prime nickel superalloy turbine blades; hot isostatic pressing.
- **Era:** Post-patent-grant (late 1990s/2000s).

### MIT campus steam pipe weeping leak (1980s)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`–`§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT steam plant pipe cracking during hot-weld repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's recommendation thirty years ago to leave a crack (with a patch over it) on a winter-active steam line near 100 Memorial Drive, rather than continue trying to hot-weld it while moisture-driven hydrogen kept reopening the crack. Used to illustrate the rare case where the practical answer is "leave the crack."
- **Materials/systems:** Steam piping carbon steel; hydrogen cracking from moisture during hot-weld repair.
- **Era:** Circa mid-1980s.

### US Navy 5000-series aluminum superstructure cracking
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`–`§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval cruiser 5456 aluminum corrosion and fatigue cracking" (closely related to "Navy aluminum ship cracking" and "LCS (Littoral Combat Ship) aluminum alloy redesign")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Triggered by a Coast Guard / Navy student's report of severe corrosion and fatigue cracking on a 5456 cruiser superstructure. Tom traces the problem to residual stresses in heavy-thickness aluminum welds, plus pitting at 5456's higher alloy content acting as fatigue initiation sites. The Navy is now retreating to 5083 for LCS construction.
- **Materials/systems:** 5054, 5083, 5456 aluminum-magnesium alloys; David Taylor / Alcoa development.
- **Era:** Alloys developed 1970s-80s; cracking emergence 2000s-2014.

### Scandium-aluminum baseball bats
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Baseball bat aluminum alloy development" (also "Aluminum baseball bat")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom holds up the scandium-microalloyed aluminum bat case as the highest-strength aluminum alloy he knows of — 100 KSI yield from fine grain size achieved by extrusion-quenching. Limited to ~2 mm wall thickness; bats break and must be replaced; $300-400 retail.
- **Materials/systems:** Scandium-aluminum, 8000-series classification.

### Westinghouse Bettis crack-acceptance code exception
- **Anchor:** `§4.p10`, `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Westinghouse Bettis crack-acceptance welding code
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited twice as the only welding code Tom knows of that permits leaving small cracks in place, on the basis of detailed fracture mechanics analysis. Used as the exception that proves the rule "all codes say grind out cracks."
- **Materials/systems:** Naval nuclear welding code.

### Air Force acoustic emission flight test with intentional crack
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force acoustic emission crack-growth flight test"
- **Frame in this lecture:** General refused to authorize flying an instrumented aircraft with a known crack to monitor its growth by acoustic emission, despite Tom's point that all in-service aircraft already contain undetected cracks.
- **Materials/systems:** Aerospace structures; acoustic emission NDT.

### Coast Guard student question on Inconel pipe crack compression repair
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Inconel pipe crush-repair proposal (Coast Guard student case, 2014)
- **Frame in this lecture:** A Coast Guard student describes a proposed (but unused) repair scheme: mechanically compressing a cracked Inconel pipe to introduce compressive residual stresses at the crack and prevent growth. Tom has never heard of this approach; reasons through it on the spot, acknowledging the mechanics work but expressing skepticism about it as a critical-application solution.
- **Materials/systems:** Inconel piping; heat-affected-zone cracking; residual stress engineering.
- **Era:** 2014 (in-class report from a student's recent experience).
