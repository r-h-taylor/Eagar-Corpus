### Boston and New York cast-iron water main failures
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`, `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Boston and New York cast-iron water main failures`
- **Frame:** Cast-iron water mains in Boston are now 100 years old. Annual failures from age, backhoe strikes, and quality degradation. Inline concrete-trowel repair extends life.
- **Materials:** Cast iron (3% silicon), concrete lining, plastic lining (UV-cured).
- **Era:** Pipes installed early 1900s; repairs ongoing.

### Florida high-rise copper-plated stainless steel water piping failure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Florida high-rise copper-plated stainless steel water piping failure`
- **Frame:** Allegheny Ludlum's 1970s copper-plated stainless thin-wall pipe as a cheaper alternative to copper tubing. Failed by galvanic pitting through pinholes in the copper plate in a marine-humid Florida environment. Lab tests on internal water flow had missed the external-corrosion failure mode.
- **Materials:** Copper-plated stainless steel.
- **Era:** Installed ~1972; failures within one year; product withdrawn after class-action damages totaling tens to hundreds of millions.
- **Cross-reference:** Tom's thesis advisor was involved in the original investigation; Tom replicated the 24-hour perforation test as a student or junior faculty member.

### Delrin (polyformaldehyde) toilet valve failures and exploding toilets
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Delrin (polyformaldehyde) toilet valve failures and exploding toilets`
- **Frame:** DuPont's 1958 polyformaldehyde plumbing material, tested against 200+ solvents but not against water. 5 ppm chlorine in humid air causes stress corrosion cracking. Twenty years of failed water-closet valves, "exploding toilets." Hoechst Celanese paid $900 million class action; DuPont paid privately, estimated $1–1.5 billion.
- **Materials:** Delrin (polyformaldehyde / polyoxymethylene).
- **Era:** Introduced 1958; failures recognized through 1970s–'80s.

### Century-old carbon steel gas pipe fractures
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`, `§6.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Century-old carbon steel gas pipe fractures`

### Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing
- **Anchor:** `§7.p6`, `§7.p7`, `§7.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing`
- **Frame:** Gas-association product launched 1997. Flexible 10-mil stainless steel tubing under yellow polyethylene jacket, displaces black iron pipe for residential gas distribution. Cost $5/ft but installs in 1/3 the time. Tom holds the product.
- **Materials:** 10-mil stainless steel, polyethylene jacket.
- **Era:** 1997 launch.

### Corrugated stainless steel gas pipe lightning strike fires
- **Anchor:** `§7.p8`, `§7.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Corrugated stainless steel gas pipe lightning strike fires`
- **Frame:** Late 1998 onward — CSST perforates under lightning strikes. 1/10 coulomb perforates the 10-mil stainless; average lightning strike is 5 coulombs. Houses burn down at ~200/year; one Texas fatality. Tom shows a perforated sample.
- **Era:** Failures recognized 1998 onward.

### Improved lightning-resistant flexible gas tubing development
- **Anchor:** `§7.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Improved lightning-resistant flexible gas tubing development`
- **Frame:** Replacement product with two layers of carbon-black-filled conductive polyethylene plus an aluminum layer. Tolerates 80 coulombs versus the original yellow CSST's 0.1 coulomb. Tom holds the alternative product.
- **Materials:** Polyethylene with carbon black, aluminum.

### Arthroscopic knee surgery tool design and materials selection
- **Anchor:** `§10.p1`–`§10.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Arthroscopic knee surgery tool design and materials selection`
- **Frame:** Johnson & Johnson division consulting case (early 1980s, arthroscopic-surgery era). Rongeur tools needed to be longer and more slender but lacked stiffness. Tom proposed sapphire (or chromium-doped ruby) instruments — color-coded, corrosion-resistant, sharper than stainless. J&J declined. Tom's personal cartilage-tear anecdote (late 1960s) frames the contrast between open-knee surgery and modern arthroscopy.
- **Materials:** Stainless steel (existing); sapphire / ruby / emerald (proposed alumina).
- **Era:** ~1980s consulting engagement.

### Silk screws for pediatric jaw fractures
- **Anchor:** `§11.p1`, `§11.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Silk screws for pediatric jaw fractures`
- **Frame:** Tufts University research (Professor Kim Thurler [spelling to verify]) on silk-derived resorbable screws. Modulus matches bone better than metal screws, avoiding stress-concentration cracking at bone-metal interfaces. Resorbable in vivo so no removal surgery needed. First application: children's broken jaws.
- **Materials:** Silk fibroin.
- **Era:** Research announced March 2014 (lecture delivered Fall 2014).

### Food and Drug Act polymer restrictions (medical devices)
- **Anchor:** `§11.p2`, `§11.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Food and Drug Act polymer restrictions (medical devices)`
- **Frame:** Early-1970s Food and Drug Act grandfathered all pre-existing medical-implant materials but required full requalification (dog studies plus human trials) for any new material. Tom claims no new material has been qualified since. Result: medical material selection is locked to stainless steel, titanium, molybdenum, platinum, polylactic acid, and a small grandfathered list.
- **Era:** Early 1970s onward.

### Artificial hip replacement failures - early iterations
- **Anchor:** `§11.p4`, `§11.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** *not a direct match* — the closest aggregate cluster is "Metal plate and screw bone fixation stress concentration problem"
- **canonical_cluster_id (chosen):** `Metal plate and screw bone fixation stress concentration problem`
- **Frame:** General principle — bone has low modulus, metal plates and screws have high modulus. The stiffness mismatch creates stress concentrations even without notches, causing cracking. Used as the motivating problem for the silk-screw solution.
