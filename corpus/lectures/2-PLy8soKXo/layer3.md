## §1. Helicopter rotor mechanics: collective, cyclic, and the swashplate [00:01]

`§1.p1` Questions? We were talking yesterday about making measurements and the precision of parts per thousand, parts per million — that you can measure mass, length, and time more precisely than anything else.

`§1.p2` I'm going to start today by telling you what they'd call at Harvard Business School a case study, about a product that has some very precise manufacturing tolerances and what you have to do to achieve them; what happened when the company screwed up; why they likely screwed up; how someone else blamed the next failure that occurred — which killed a couple of people — on that same cause, even though it wasn't the same cause. And how, after the fact, you can make a measurement of something that's virtually completely destroyed and still get some idea of whether it really had the manufacturing dimensional defect or not.

`§1.p3` It has to do with the helicopter swashplate. Did anybody know what a helicopter swashplate does, or is? *[Tom shows an image of Igor Sikorsky's 1939 helicopter.]* Here's Igor Sikorsky in 1939 flying his first working helicopter, and up here there's a mechanism. Obviously you don't want the passenger compartment to rotate, you want the blades to rotate, so you have to have a stationary plate and a rotating plate that come together. It's called a swashplate.

`§1.p4` In the old days it basically was a spherical dish that was stationary and another spherical dish on top, and you just greased the whole thing up. Two saucers, one rotating inside the other with a bunch of grease. Now we use bearings rather than the early grease-and-dish design. If you think of the plate, it can be rotated in the x or y direction, and that gives you control for two things on the rotor blades.

`§1.p5` One of the things you want to do is take that rotating disc and change the angle of attack of each blade as it's going around. You want to tilt the blade up to a higher angle if you want to go up, tilt it down if you want to lose height, keep it at some intermediate level to maintain height. And you also want to go right or left. The first one is called *collective* — you collectively move all the blades with the same angular rotation to give you more lift up or less lift down.

`§1.p6` The other one is called *cyclic*. The pilot has a lever for the right hand and a lever for the left hand. If I remember, the left hand is the collective that allows you to go up or down, and the cyclic allows you to tilt the rotor disc. If you want to go forward it tilts forward, to go right that way, to go left that way, to go backwards you tilt it back.

`§1.p7` So the cyclic is the angular orientation of the rotor disc, and the collective is the actual angle of attack of each individual blade. You can have up to seven blades — there's actually a Soviet helicopter that has eight — and they collectively all get the same angle in collective. The pilot is controlling the main rotor with his two levers, collective and cyclic, and his two feet are controlling the rear.

`§1.p8` This one's a NOTAR — no tail rotor. Normally you have a little blade back here, because you've got friction in the system and the thing is going to start to spin if you don't have some restoring force to keep it heading in the same direction. In fact, if you lose that tail rotor, the thing will start spinning out of control and crash. A NOTAR has a great big fan — well, about this big — that blows air out the side as the counteracting force to keep the thing from rotating. So it simplifies the mechanics. The tail boom is just a tube, whereas a conventional tail boom has bearings and shafts and all kinds of things that can fail.

## §2. The Mach-1 blade-tip limit and the V-22 Osprey [05:35]

`§2.p1` **Student:** [Question about helicopter speed limits.]

`§2.p2` They didn't change the speed. The speed of the helicopter is limited by the speed of the advancing blade. Some blades are clockwise from the top, some are counterclockwise — you can design them both ways. But the limiting speed is when this blade tip hits Mach 1. You can't go any faster, because you have to break the sound barrier. At that point you start getting all kinds of instabilities and vibrations, and typically you'll rip your blades apart.

`§2.p3` Down in Hampton, Virginia, there's a NASA facility where they do test flights, and they have a helicopter out there. NASA once flew a helicopter that had more than Mach 1 blades. Typical helicopters might be limited to 200 miles an hour — they can't go 250 miles an hour because you'll rip off the blades from the vibrations of trying to break the sound barrier. You get a sonic boom and all kinds of vibrations.

`§2.p4` That's why the V-22 Osprey, the Marines' sixty-million-dollar tilt-rotor helicopter, can go 300 miles an hour — because it can tilt. The rotor blades are now in airplane mode, so you don't have the additional velocity of your forward motion, you just have the tip speed. The receding blade is always going less. One blade is going plus your forward motion, the other at the three o'clock position is minus your forward motion. No problem with the receding blade — it's the advancing blade that will set up instabilities and vibrations that'll crash.

`§2.p5` NASA once did it with a big jet engine off the back of an experimental helicopter. If you go down there, it's sitting out in the lawn in front of the administration building. It was unsafe to fly. They did it because someone wanted to see exactly what happens when you start breaking the sound barrier with your blades, plus all the noise — and helicopters are noisy enough.

## §5. The Reali-Slim bearing and closed conformity [14:22]

`§5.p1` *[Tom shows a cross-section of the bearing.]* The bearing is what's actually called a Reali-Slim bearing, made by Kaydon. These things go in not only helicopters, they go in CAT scan machines at hospitals. This particular one was 40 inches in diameter with a one-inch cross section and half-inch balls. If you looked at the cross section, it was one inch by one inch — outer race, inner race, and the ball was a half-inch ball.

`§5.p2` They've made thousands of these for lots of applications, and this was the largest one anyone in the world made — they were a sole source. There's a company in Germany that makes something similar, but not exactly like this. The prior history: yes, they'd had a failure of the swashplate on a Japanese helicopter. This was the second failure they had ever had, and they'd been producing this helicopter for twenty-five, thirty years. So this was sort of a new type of failure, and they didn't even know it was related to the Japanese failure until they started their board of inquiry. They said, "Oh, we had one of these helicopters go down in Japan, let's go over there." They flew over to Japan, saw the pieces, and said, "Oh, that was a bearing failure too."

`§5.p3` They started looking. At one time the company had manufactured six bearings — these bearings went for forty thousand dollars apiece, or a hundred thousand dollars apiece, they weren't cheap bearings — and one of them started creating some vibration. They took it off and looked at it and it had what's called a closed conformity.

`§5.p4` What's a closed conformity? *[Tom shows a generic bearing diagram.]* This is an open conformity. You want the balls to roll and ride at the bottom of the groove. The rounded groove has a larger radius than the ball. A closed conformity — I couldn't find one anywhere because no one ever makes these intentionally — is one where the ball has a larger radius than the curvature of the groove, and it's riding on the edges. Not a good idea. A lot of sharp contacts, stresses, and you wear out those edges. That's what caused the vibration.

`§5.p5` One of the six bearings that had been manufactured had a closed conformity. Someone in the military found it, wrote it up, and there's all kinds of documentation. The Navy went and started inspecting other things, but they decided it was just a one-of-a-kind. Why was there a closed conformity? When you've got something as flexible as a 40-inch diameter ring that's only one inch high and even less in thickness, that's pretty flexible. If you just had the outer ring or the inner ring, you can actually turn it into a potato chip shape with your own wrist.

`§5.p6` That bearing is pressed into an aluminum housing, which is the upper swashplate. That aluminum housing is milled on a vertical boring mill. The thing that gives the shape to the bearing is the swashplate aluminum housing. The helicopter manufacturer manufactured that big aluminum housing themselves, and they just never bothered to look into the shape of that housing — and in fact they destroyed it. Not only that, about six months after this whole inquiry started, they destroyed the three-year-old milling machine that had made it. There was no way to go back and prove what happened to this aluminum housing.

## §6. Precision tolerances and matched sets [19:01]

`§6.p1` The tolerances we're talking about: the diameter of that 40-inch bearing is supposed to be 40 inches plus or minus 2 thousandths. If it's four thousandths total variation out of forty inches, that's one part in ten thousand. And what did I tell you yesterday? A typical manufacturing tolerance is one part in two thousand for machining or measuring something. If you've ever worked in a machine shop trying to turn the diameter of something to one inch on a lathe, it's hard to hold anything better than half a thousandth. You can grind it and lap it and get down to a tenth of a thousandth, but if you're doing that you now have to start specifying the temperature at which you made the measurement. And the tools that you're measuring with, which also are steel, have to be at the same temperature.

`§6.p2` When they grind this bearing at Kaydon, the whole thing's in an automatic grinding machine. You set it up and jig it, and the grinder goes around and grinds the grooves on the diameter, making sure everything's as spherical as possible. They actually take the micrometer — the whole thing is flooded with oil while the machine's running, as lubricant and coolant — and put the micrometer right there on the bed of the vertical boring mill, bathed in the same bath of oil. When they measure it, they're measuring with the tool at exactly the same temperature. Who cares if it's 66 degrees or 72 degrees, as long as the two tools are calibrated and at the same temperature, you'll get a valid reading. They're trying to hold one part in ten thousand, which means you've got to be within about two degrees Fahrenheit of the same temperature.

`§6.p3` That wasn't how they caused the closed conformity. The balls are not just half-inch balls, they're 0.500 inch to 50 millionths of an inch. They've got to run true in this 40-inch bearing, and actually as you get to larger diameters, the need to run true gets greater and greater even though you're getting larger. You're heading in the wrong direction, which is one of the limitations on the ability to even manufacture bearings this thin and this small.

`§6.p4` So the ball diameter has a total tolerance of 50 millionths — plus or minus 25. Now this is tighter than one part in ten thousand. This is one part in twenty thousand.

`§6.p5` How do you do it? You can't, even by lapping — if you put a bunch of balls on the lapping table, they will not come out with that type of tolerance. And you had to have 192 balls with that tolerance — 192 is the number around this 40-inch circle. So how do you do it? There are many cases in manufacturing where your tolerance required is tighter than anything you can manufacture the individual part to. What they do is they make matched parts. You make a bunch of parts, pick this one and measure it, pick that one and measure it, and you put the two of them together in matched sets.

`§6.p6` They make 50,000 balls, then they sort them: which ones are half an inch exactly, which are fifty millionths under, which are twenty-five millionths under, which are fifty millionths over. They group them into sets.

`§6.p7` If you go into Kaydon's shop, or a Pratt Whitney shop — the two places I've been — they have micrometers that look like a lathe to measure a half-inch ball. It's sitting on the floor, the size of a small lathe. The room is temperature- and humidity-controlled within one or two degrees. There's a person sitting there putting the half-inch ball in the micrometer of this great big machine that's got all these compensation mechanisms to measure something that small reproducibly. Someone sitting there making minimum wage, measuring each ball, putting them in different bins. Here's a set of 192 — or they might actually make it a set of 200, in case someone lost one — that will go into the assembly room, where they have the two rings ground as a matched set to about four thousandths. Then they put the balls in based on a more precise measurement of the actual grooves and the shape of that groove. If I only had to do it to a tenth of a thousandth, no big deal, but when I have to do it to a half a tenth of a thousandth, I have to use matched sets of balls.

`§6.p8` So how did this closed conformity out of six bearings ever occur? Someone probably picked up or mislabeled a set of balls and assembled them in there. There were other inspectors who should have caught it before it went out, but they didn't, and it got out, started vibrating on a helicopter, and they caught it. No one got hurt.

`§6.p9` The inspectors for the government — we're going to talk later about inspectors — each individual employee is supposed to be their own inspector with a little "i", then the company has their own inspectors with another little "i", and then the Navy has someone full-time in that plant and they're the capital "I" inspectors. But they don't really do the inspecting, they're just checking on the quality control, doing quality assurance. They're not checking every part.

## §9. 188 witness marks: proving open conformity [32:36]

`§9.p1` I was brought in not as part of the criminal investigation, but at this point the helicopter manufacturer decided, "Well, we lost a 30- or 40-million-dollar helicopter, we're going to sue Kaydon." The dead pilots are going to sue Kaydon in criminal court. And the FBI has impounded all the evidence. And we're going to sue Kaydon in civil court for the loss of the helicopter, because the Navy didn't buy it, we still owned it.

`§9.p2` Kaydon settled the criminal case very quickly — criminal cases are not things you want to defend yourself against when the government's going after you. In a criminal case they sort of have the upper hand. They settled that one for about 15 million dollars with the families of the two pilots. But I was there to figure out: did this thing have a closed conformity?

`§9.p3` When it seized, it seized because it was generating too much heat. The aluminum housing — aluminum will absorb a lot of heat — when it crashed and landed, the heat started to soak back into the steel bearing. The aluminum housing's 20 or 50 times the mass of the steel, and it soaked back and just pressed the balls against the races. Everything was overheated, deformed, and we're supposed to go in there and figure out whether these things are accurate to 50 millionths of an inch. That's pretty much impossible.

`§9.p4` That's what the helicopter manufacturer was depending on — that no one could figure out whether this was a closed conformity. They're going to use the history that there had once been a closed conformity a couple years before — that never caused a big accident — to say, "Oh, they did it again, shame on them. Pay the pilots 15 million, we want another 15 or 20 or 30 million for our destroyed helicopter. And FBI, oh, it's terrible what this bearing manufacturer has done to us and to the government, you should consider further criminal action."

`§9.p5` What are you doing, helicopter company? They're the sole source. If the FBI leaves and says no longer a national emergency, and Kaydon says, "We don't want to do business with you" — you can't ever make another helicopter, because there's no one else in the world who knows how to make these bearings. This is the problem when the attorneys in the company start running the business. They don't worry about the long-term consequences of not working with your suppliers. They just want to show that for their annual bonus they collected a lot of money for you.

`§9.p6` So we went out to Muskegon, Michigan, for several days. There were a couple of bearing experts with me; I was the metallurgist. We couldn't look at it without an FBI agent in the room. We couldn't touch a thing unless he was there, because they were still considering criminal action.

`§9.p7` We looked at it for two days, trying to see whatever the patterns were. Finally at the end of the second day, I sort of looked at the forest for the trees, and I said, "There's no closed conformity." I had 188 measurements that proved there was no closed conformity on this bearing.

`§9.p8` The way I did it was: if you think about how a bearing ball sits in the races, it actually forms a little elliptical contact surface. When you embed the ball — it's called brinelling, when you embed the ball in the race — it forms an elliptical wear mark, an impression. It's just a hardness test. If it had been a closed conformity, what would you have had? An hourglass. The railroad tracks would be on the edges, and we had been looking at those edges for a day and a half, trying to say — but it was so beat up, some parts of it got to eight or nine hundred degrees centigrade and transformed.

`§9.p9` So we had been looking at the races, trying to figure out whether it's closed conformity, contacting in the bottom or on the edge. I started looking at the wear marks in the bottom. If it's an open conformity, you get an ellipse. If it's a closed conformity, you get an hourglass, because it's contacting at the edges. When the ball embeds into the piece you'd form an hourglass. We didn't. There were four of them you couldn't even tell, but of 192, 188 you could see a nice little ellipse. There's no hourglass there.

`§9.p10` From the shape of that ellipse we probably could have calculated exactly what the conformity was. I was all excited — we're going to take that helicopter company, we're going to stick this down their throat. That night, the big-shot name on the letterhead attorney from the law firm that hired me came in, and the brand-new president of Kaydon came in, and we all went down to dinner on the waterfront of Lake Michigan, about 20 people between all the experts and the attorneys.

## §10. The settlement and the potato-chipped swashplate [38:43]

`§10.p1` I'd explained to what I call the outhouse attorney — they have in-house attorneys who work for the company and outhouse attorneys who are private attorneys — that day, I had showed him how we could prove this was not a closed conformity. There's no doubt — I had 188 witness marks and they all said 100% this is open conformity. I figured he would explain this to the in-house attorney for Kaydon and the new CEO of Kaydon. They talked about the criminal case and everything, having their attorney dinner conversation.

`§10.p2` What happened is, Kaydon settled the case with the helicopter manufacturer for another 15 million. This is an important lesson: the new CEO — this problem had not occurred on his watch, and he was going to get rid of it at the beginning of his new watch. He didn't care what it cost to get rid of it, he didn't care whether he was right or wrong, he just wanted to get rid of it. So he bought his way out, because he could blame it on the previous CEO.

`§10.p3` So I spent three days in Muskegon, Michigan, for nothing, to figure out what happened. But in fact we did know what happened. We were looking — could we measure anything? This thing had been cut up into little pieces by the helicopter company. It was no longer circular.

`§10.p4` We said, well, why did it fail? We looked at those wear marks, and you could break the wear marks into four quadrants. Two quadrants had deep elliptical impressions, and two quadrants opposite each other had shallower witness marks. One of the other bearing engineers said, "Swashplate had to be potato-chipped." If you put a perfectly spherical bearing into a swashplate that has a potato chip shape, that potato chip ends up creating a minor axis and a major axis. It's effectively a little ellipse, and it's going to get deep impressions on the minor axis and smaller impressions on the major axis.

`§10.p5` So we started looking — where are the other swashplates that were machined just before and just after this one? In an aerospace fabrication operation they can tell you by serial numbers exactly which part was made on what day. We knew all the brothers and sisters of this swashplate. But mysteriously they had all seemed to have evaporated. The helicopter company had gone out over the previous two years and for various reasons determined that these swashplates needed to be replaced, kind of on a one-by-one basis, until they were all gone. They didn't exist. We said, "Well, where are they?" "It was government equipment." They'd taken them back and scrapped them.

`§10.p6` We said, "Well, we want to see the milling machine that produced these swashplates." "Oh, that was three years old, it was too old, we scrapped it three weeks ago." A three-year-old, quarter-million-dollar milling machine, and they scrapped it. You don't think the helicopter company knows what happened? They knew exactly what happened. They had a milling machine that was out of spec, machined a potato-chipped swashplate. The Reali-Slim bearings took the shape of the swashplate. They did their own investigation. The fox was watching the hen house.

`§10.p7` There should have been a criminal investigation, but not of Kaydon — of the helicopter manufacturer. That's why I haven't told you the name of the helicopter manufacturer. Did you tell the FBI about that? We didn't tell the FBI. It's not for me to start telling the attorneys how to manage the whole thing. The guy who's going to decide this at Kaydon, he wants to get rid of it. He wants it off his watch. The last thing he wants is a continuing investigation.

`§10.p8` Just as another point of fact, the military is not as stupid as the helicopter manufacturer. The helicopter manufacturer is willing to throw their monopoly bearing manufacturer under the bus. The U.S. Navy is not going to throw their monopoly helicopter manufacturer under the bus. They could run an investigation, and they would probably find, "Oh, the evidence is not available." Well, who made the evidence not available? Duh.

## §11. How investigations should work — and the V-22 Osprey court of inquiry [44:16]

`§11.p1` This little story has a number of parts about precision measurement, but mostly it's one about how the system works. Ordinarily, if this had been a military helicopter, there would have been some Navy captain or Marine major in charge of the investigation, and they would have found the problem, because they would have invited the bearing manufacturer to participate. `§11.p2` We could read those transcripts and see, well, someone might say, "We ought to look into this swashplate," and then they go off the record. But in a regular investigation, when you don't have people trying to cover something up, it would have been run properly, and maybe justice would have been done.

`§11.p3` I could tell you half a dozen stories in different industries. One, big forgings for ships, where a company got screwed by another company. ## §12. The word "engineer": from war machines to civil engineering at MIT [49:35]

`§12.p1` I've got a couple minutes, so I can give you my story — I don't know if you've ever heard it before, it may be on another tape, I give it all the time — of the history of the term "engineering." Anybody know what the word "engineer" comes from? Engineering in this country only goes back a couple hundred years. The word comes from the French — if you look in the Oxford English Dictionary, you'll see it comes from the French *ingénieur*, which begins with an "i," which, as Professor Sataway [Saadeh?] would say, comes from the Latin root for ingenuity. But an engineer before 1800 was basically a maker of war machines.

`§12.p2` The first engineering school in this country was West Point, 1790-something — 1797 or '92 — and they trained military engineers. In 1823, the second engineering school in this country was Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, in Troy, New York. They were creating engineers to help build what was the big construction project in New York in 1823 — the Erie Canal. They needed people to dig the canal and build bridges and dams and locks. They created a curriculum of engineering different from the military engineers who built breastworks and catapults. They called it civil engineering to distinguish it from the only type of engineering that existed before that, which was military engineering.

`§12.p3` There were other schools — Michigan claims to have engineering in the 1840s, but I don't know how many bears in Michigan in 1840 were going to university and studying engineering. In the 1860s, MIT actually started to differentiate. What's Course 1 at MIT? Civil engineering, because that was the only type in addition to military engineering. MIT decided to break it up into other disciplines. One was mechanical, that's number two. Number three is mining — not mining and metallurgy, that didn't enter until 1888 — it was mining engineering. Coal mines, copper mines. Four was architecture. Five, I think, was chemistry. Six, I don't know what the original six was. Electrical engineering didn't start until the 1880s, after Edison and Westinghouse gave us a ready source of electrons.

`§12.p4` MIT also defined the field of chemical engineering back in the 1880s, nuclear engineering in the late 1940s — we were the first. A number of the fields of engineering — aerospace engineering, we had the first aero department; aeronautics, there was no astro department originally. Hunsaker, in like 1917, started an aeronautical department. MIT has actually defined a number of the fields of engineering. Not all of them — we didn't define electrical.
