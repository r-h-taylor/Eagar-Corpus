## Cases developed in this lecture

### Japanese Maritime Self-Defense Force helicopter swashplate seizure
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`–`§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Japanese Maritime Self-Defense Force helicopter swashplate seizure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The first known failure of the Reali-Slim swashplate bearing — a heavy-lift Navy helicopter crashes in the Sea of Japan, late 1980s. Spends years on the ocean floor, recovery too corroded to diagnose definitively. Sets up the U.S. failure to come.
- **Materials/systems:** Kaydon Reali-Slim 40-inch bearing; aluminum swashplate housing.
- **Era:** Late 1980s.

### Kaydon bearing conformity failure and helicopter crashes
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§10.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Kaydon bearing conformity failure and helicopter crashes" (also: "Caydon bearing defect and helicopter crash", "Replacement Caydon bearing failure", "Manufacturing bearing closed-conformity defect discovery", "MH-53 bearing conformity lawsuit and criminal investigation", "Bearing conformity misidentification investigation", "Helicopter swashplate bearing failure", "Second swashplate failure blamed on wrong cause" — these aggregate-v2 entries appear to be different lectures' framings of the same underlying case)
- **Frame in this lecture:** The most fully developed version of this case in the corpus. Two U.S. test pilots killed during a two-hour acceptance hover; subsequent Cherry Point near-crash; fleet grounding; FBI takeover under the Emergency Powers Act; perfect new bearings still failing within two months; Tom's forensic discovery that 188 of 192 brinelling marks are ellipses (open conformity), not hourglasses (closed conformity); the potato-chipped aluminum swashplate as actual cause; the helicopter manufacturer's destruction of the milling machine and sibling swashplates; the new CEO's decision to settle anyway for $15M to clear his watch.
- **Materials/systems:** Kaydon Reali-Slim bearing, 40-inch diameter, one-inch cross-section, half-inch balls, 192 per bearing; tolerances ±0.002" on diameter and ±0.000025" on balls; aluminum swashplate housing milled on a vertical boring mill.
- **Era:** Late 1980s to early 1990s.

### USS aircraft carrier steam system weld failure during Grenada invasion
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`–`§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS aircraft carrier steam system weld failure during Grenada invasion"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Digression illustrating military urgency. The Atlantic carrier defending against Soviet preemptive attack develops steam leaks mid-invasion (October 1983); emergency repair at Jacksonville Naval Shipyard; technicians flown in by helicopter from their homes at 3 a.m.
- **Materials/systems:** Steam piping welds, naval steam plant.
- **Era:** October 1983.

### V-22 Osprey Ship Four crash and rigged court of inquiry
- **Anchor:** `§11.p3`–`§11.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "V-22 Osprey Ship Four crash and rigged court of inquiry"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Parallel case to Kaydon, invoked at the lecture's close to generalize the "rigged board of inquiry" pattern. The court was instructed to find a fixable cause to protect the $40 billion program from Congressional cancellation. Tom served as expert witness in the subsequent civil suit by widows; his hydraulic-leak-in-the-upper-nacelle theory (one miracle) displaced the Navy's nacelle-fire theory (six miracles); the JAG investigation eventually adopted Tom's theory.
- **Materials/systems:** V-22 Osprey nacelle hydraulics.
- **Era:** Crash date and trial in the late 1990s / early 2000s; lecture is Fall 2012.

## Cases referenced in passing

### NASA Langley supersonic helicopter blade-tip experiment
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Langley helicopter supersonic blade tip experiment"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited to explain why conventional helicopters are speed-limited around 200 mph — once the advancing blade tip approaches Mach 1, vibrations and instabilities will rip the blades off. NASA built and flew an experimental helicopter with a jet engine on the back to break the barrier deliberately; it's now an outdoor exhibit at the NASA Hampton facility.
- **Materials/systems:** Rotor blade structural dynamics at transonic tip speeds.
- **Era:** Historical NASA experiment; date not specified.
