## Cases referenced

### 1985 Ford Aerostar rear axle fatigue failures
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `1985 Ford Aerostar rear axle fatigue failures`
- **Frame in this lecture:** The shock to Ford when high-strength steel substitution for fuel-economy weight reduction failed in fatigue — Ford's design engineers didn't know what bridge engineers had known for forty years, that welded joints get no fatigue benefit from higher strength steels.
- **Materials/systems:** High-strength steel rear axles, fillet-welded
- **Era:** mid-1980s

### King Street Bridge failure
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`, referenced again `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `King Street Bridge failure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Classic case of a doubler plate welded all around its tip, creating a fatigue crack initiation site at the change in stress concentration. Used to motivate the AISC fatigue-class restrictions on wrap-around welds.
- **Materials/systems:** Welded steel girder with doubler plate
- **Era:** early 1970s

### Cement truck case
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Cement truck case` (one of several cement-truck cases in the corpus; this one is the water-tank-saddle stiffness-change fatigue case)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief mention. Aluminum water tanks on cement trucks with saddle arrangements — change in stiffness at the saddle creates a fatigue crack location from sloshing liquid.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum tank, saddle support
- **Note:** Tom says "I may have told you about the aluminum tanks on cement trucks" — likely overlaps with several aluminum cement truck cases in the canonical list. This appearance is a one-paragraph reference.

### Caterpillar earthmoving machinery fatigue study
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`, `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Caterpillar earthmoving machinery fatigue study`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Caterpillar's multi-decade, multi-hundred-million-dollar program to develop welding procedures (torch angle, weave pattern, pulse current, robotic consistency) that produce weld profiles good enough to gain fatigue benefit from higher-strength steels (50 ksi → 60–70 ksi HSLA). Tom consulted briefly; most work is proprietary.
- **Materials/systems:** Fillet welds on fatigue-loaded heavy machinery; EH-32, DH-32, HSLA steels
- **Era:** ~2005–2010

### Guthrie weld toe fatigue research
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Guthrie weld toe fatigue research`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Guthrie's career at the British Welding Institute developing TIG dressing, full-penetration welds, and machined toe profiles to recover the strength-fatigue benefit lost in as-welded fillet joints.
- **Materials/systems:** Fillet welds, TIG dressing
- **Era:** 1960s

### Coast Guard cutter bracket undermatching case
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Coast Guard cutter bracket undermatching case`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Undermatching filler metal acceptable here because the brackets had ample weld area, the substrate was high-strength for deflection (not strength) reasons, and undermatching gave better corrosion resistance and toughness.
- **Materials/systems:** High-strength steel brackets, undermatched filler

### Sub-Zero refrigerator hinge failure
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`, `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Sub-Zero refrigerator hinge failure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's consulting case. Sub-Zero (twelve-thousand-dollar refrigerators) had a TIG-welded hinge pin made by a subcontractor from free-machining sulfur-bearing steel; sulfur formed iron sulfide and the welds cracked. ~3000 units repaired in the field at ~$7 million. Used as the headline example of the design/fab/field 1:10:100:1000 cost-of-failure escalation, and the example that wealthy customers are litigious.
- **Materials/systems:** 303 free-machining stainless (mis-substituted for carbon steel), TIG weld, hinge pin
- **Era:** ~recent (Tom's consulting case)

### Egyptian armor plate tank weld cracking
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Egyptian armor plate tank weld cracking`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Austenitic filler metal welded to armor steel produced a martensite-austenite mixture in the diluted zone, which cracked. Used to illustrate the Fe-Cr-Ni phase diagram regions to avoid.
- **Materials/systems:** Armor steel, austenitic filler metal

### US Navy superaustenitic submarine material evaluation
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `US Navy superaustenitic submarine material evaluation`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Navy considered replacing HY-100 with super-austenitic stainless (zero ferrite, non-magnetic, no acoustic signature) ten or fifteen years prior. Rejected as overpriced and unnecessary given the threat environment.

- **Era:** ~early 2000s

### General Electric boiling water reactor stress corrosion cracking
- **Anchor:** `§10.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `General Electric boiling water reactor stress corrosion cracking`
- **Frame in this lecture:** GE's discovery thirty years prior that 304 stainless reactor components were cracking on solidification, traced to trace sulfur. Motivated the development of the Varestraint test at RPI.
- **Materials/systems:** 304 stainless, sulfur inclusions, austenite grain boundaries
- **Era:** ~1970s–1980s

### Rensselaer Polytechnic transverse strength test (stainless/nickel alloy)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Rensselaer Polytechnic transverse strength test (stainless/nickel alloy)` — note this cluster name appears imprecise; the test is the Varestraint test
- **Frame in this lecture:** RPI in the 1970s as the leading US producer of welding PhDs. Developed the Varestraint test (TIG weld over a radius block under bending strain, count cracks afterward). Showed that three percent ferrite in austenitic weld metal scavenges sulfur and prevents solidification cracking.
- **Materials/systems:** Austenitic stainless, sulfur, ferrite scavenging
- **Era:** 1970s

### Farberware cooking pot thermal warping / stainless steel pot cracking
- **Anchor:** `§10.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Either `Farberware cooking pot thermal warping` or `Farberware stainless steel cookware pot cracking` (one of several Farberware entries)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Tom's pot had varying ferrite content from bottom to top — 5–10% ferrite at the bottom, ~80–90% martensite (transformed by deformation) at the top, making the top magnetic. Used to illustrate that "zero ferrite" super-austenitic specifications can be misleading.
- **Materials/systems:** Austenitic stainless, deformation-induced martensite transformation
- **Note:** Multiple Farberware cases in the canonical list; aggregator should reconcile.
