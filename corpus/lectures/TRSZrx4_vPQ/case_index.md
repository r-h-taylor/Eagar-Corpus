*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `¬ß1.p3`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture's center of gravity is the **economics of structural-material processing**: why bolting trades off against welding, why cost-per-pound and cost-per-mole determine which materials reach structural use, and why vapor deposition cannot make structural materials. The dominant extended case is the **NASA X-33 space plane** as a parable of rapid-prototyping failure, with MP35N and bulk metallic glasses as bracketing examples of "wonderful materials you can't make big."

## Major case: NASA X-33 space plane

### NASA X-33 space plane
- **Anchor:** `¬ß5.p18`, `¬ß6.p1`‚Äì`¬ß6.p14`, `¬ß11.p21`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "NASA X-33 space plane"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom uses the X-33 as the central morality tale of post-Gulf-War rapid prototyping. NASA awarded Lockheed Martin Skunk Works a $1.3B contract on a ~33-month schedule for a single-stage-to-orbit replacement for the Space Shuttle. The team spent six months designing a 3D-woven carbon composite ring-stiffened liquid hydrogen tank, discovered the design would blow the budget 4‚Äì5√ó, then improvised a Nextel-honeycomb / carbon-tape sandwich design in one month at $50M per tank, ~2,000 lb each. Cryogenic fill test at Huntsville appeared successful until video showed frost popping off ‚Äî the carbon composite was permeable to hydrogen. Project cancelled; the tanks still sit at Palmdale. The X-33 piece passed around the class ($12,000/lb) anchors the cost-per-pound argument.
- **Materials/systems:** Nextel honeycomb, carbon-fiber/polymer tape, Inconel fastener inserts; liquid/gaseous hydrogen permeability
- **Era:** Post-1991 Gulf War through cancellation (mid-to-late 1990s)
- **Related clusters in canon:** NASA X-33 liquid hydrogen tank failure; X-33 hydrogen tank adhesive joint failures; X-33 space plane liquid nitrogen tank failure; Lockheed Skunk Works X-33 spaceplane composite tank (rapid prototyping redesign); Space Shuttle reusable spaceplane hydrogen tank failure
