### Mexican airline hot corrosion engine failure
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p3` (with thermodynamic setup at `§1.p3`–`§1.p4`)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Mexican airline hot corrosion engine failure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom uses the NiCrAlY coating corrosion-rate-vs-temperature diagram to explain why Mexican carriers experienced sulfidation failures on turbine blades. The operational cause: skipping the runway preheat warmup and instead climbing to altitude with engines passing slowly through the 800–1000°C hot-corrosion window at full power.
- **Materials/systems:** NiCrAlY overlay coatings on nickel-based turbine blades; jet engine high-temperature corrosion.
- **Era:** roughly 2006–2008 (Tom: "six or seven or eight years ago").
- **Related cluster:** *Mexican airline jet turbine blade sulfidation* — these two canonical entries may refer to the same incident; recommend reconciliation at next aggregate pass.

### Fenway Franks hot dog cooker (316/304 stainless substitution)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Fenway Frank hot dog cooker stainless steel failure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** The exemplar case for "wrong material" — one of the three legs of the Venn diagram of corrosion (material / stress / environment). A Fenway Franks steam-cooker tunnel in Everett, MA, specified as 316 stainless, had replacement parts fabricated in 304 instead. Within weeks, the 2% molybdenum difference produced visible chloride stress-corrosion cracking in the hot, salt-laden steam environment. Tom did the SEM analysis.
- **Materials/systems:** 316 vs. 304 austenitic stainless steel; chloride steam corrosion; hot food-processing environments.
- **Era:** undated in this telling; Tom presents as a personal consulting case.
- **Related clusters in aggregate:** also catalogued as `Ballpark Franks hot dog cooker failure`, `Hot dog cooker corrosion failure`, and `Tom Eagar's hot dog cooker stress corrosion cracking`. These appear to be the same case under different naming. Recommend collapsing at next aggregate pass.
