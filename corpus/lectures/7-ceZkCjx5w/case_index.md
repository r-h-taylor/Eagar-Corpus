## Cases developed in this lecture

### Pennsylvania roof collapses from 1980s snowstorm
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`, `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1988 nor'easter structural collapses (New Jersey/Pennsylvania)" — possibly the same event, possibly a different snowstorm
- **Frame in this lecture:** Computer-aided design that shaved safety factors via doubler-plate beams; battery plant burned to the ground, fire trucks unable to reach through six-foot snow drifts. No one hurt.
- **Era:** mid 1980s

### NASA X-33 space plane composite tank failure
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lockheed Skunk Works X-33 spaceplane composite tank (rapid prototyping redesign)" — also "NASA X-33 liquid hydrogen tank failure" / "X-33 hydrogen tank adhesive joint failures" / "X-33 space plane"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Knowing manufacturing defect; engineers shaved the factor-of-two safety factor down to 1.05 via fracture mechanics calculations and lost the tank to thermal stresses on liquid nitrogen fill. $1.3 billion project cancelled.

### Liberty ships and the *SS Schenectady* / *Esso* fracture
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`, `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady" + "ESSO Manhattan" / "T2 tanker fracture (1952)" / "WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The anchoring case for the entire bulking-up arc. 4,700 ships built in WWII; 970 (22%) had major fracture casualties; 24 sustained complete strength-deck fracture. All low-toughness plates: Charpy < 10 ft-lb.
- **Era:** 1942–1946 failures; 1946 Navy report

### Quincy Shipyard LNG tanker aluminum sphere skirts
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p2`, `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Quincy Shipyard liquid natural gas tanker construction (mid-1970s)" + "Quincy LNG tanker aluminum tank skirts" + "LNG tanker shipyard plate selection and testing circumvention" + "LNG vessel heat treatment coupon substitution and testing failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Double- and triple-tempered A537 steel barely meeting 20 ft-lb spec; shipyard cut Charpy samples from the rare high-toughness plates, used the rest in production, and the actual ship welds were tested only via runoff tabs that bore no relation to the shipboard joints.
- **Materials/systems:** A537 normalize-and-temper plate, aluminum spheres, –60°F service
- **Era:** mid 1970s

### Japanese low-sulfur A36 plate market disruption

### Alaska Pipeline brittle fracture risk assessment
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alaska Pipeline brittle fracture risk assessment" + "Alaskan Pipeline construction"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The economic case: $60M/day (1970s) to $200M/day (today) of oil flow means the operator has more skin in the game than any environmentalist. Cited to motivate the 80-ft-lb base-plate proposal that follows.

### Gas pipeline brittle fracture propagation / Alaska gas pipeline feasibility
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`, `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "30-mile pipeline brittle fracture" + "Gas pipeline brittle fracture propagation" + "Pipeline brittle fracture and crack propagation" + "Alaska gas pipeline nickel alloy feasibility" + "High-strength steel crack-stopper rings for pipelines"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Decompression velocity in gas < fracture velocity → crack tip always sees full pressure → 30-mile cracks. Two responses: A710 crack-arrestor rings; raise base-plate toughness to 80 ft-lb.
- **Materials:** A710 copper-bearing HSLA

### V-22 Osprey crash (Ship 4)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`, `§5.p6`, `§5.p7`, `§5.p8`, `§5.p9`, `§5.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "V-22 Osprey Ship Four crash and rigged court of inquiry" + "V-22 Osprey aircraft" + "V-22 Osprey prototype Ship 2 crash"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Carbon-fiber drive shaft between rotors burned through in hydraulic-fluid engine fire; the redundancy that was supposed to let the aircraft survive engine loss was defeated by the fire-vulnerability of the cross-shaft. Redesigned with titanium drive shafts.
- **Materials:** carbon fiber composite → titanium retrofit
- **Era:** late 1990s development testing

### Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding "stupid spec"
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`, `§6.p5`, `§6.p6`, `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding failure" + "Boeing 747-400 Titanium Air Tubing and Catalytic Converter" + "Boeing 747-400 fuselage welding porosity issue"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Engelhard making titanium catalytic-converter housings for cabin air ozone treatment. Boeing's x-ray spec called for no flaw > 10 thousandths — a limit chosen by NDT detectability, not by any analysis of the service application (low-pressure air). Half of all welds failed; with four welds per part and one allowed repair, virtually nothing could ship. Tom diagnosed the spec as bureaucratic CYA.
- **Materials:** ~40-thousandths titanium, gas tungsten arc weld
- **Era:** circa 1990

### MAP gas cylinder drop and bending incidents
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`, `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`, `§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MAPP gas cylinder drop and bending incidents" + "Plumber MAPP gas torch facial injury incident" + "Mount Shasta MAP and acetylene burn testing study" + "Southern California MAP cylinder misuse federal lawsuit"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 30-thousandths steel can't fail by overpressure (safety factor enormous, ~7×) — fails by abuse: torquing off with 80 ft-lb bar bends it; plumber-with-cigar incident; throw-it-on-the-ground crack initiation at 25–30° bend.
- **Materials:** ~30-thousandths steel cylinder
- **Era:** ongoing consulting

### Fire sprinkler head misuse as coat hook / Wood's metal creep
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Fire sprinkler head misuse as coat hook"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Precision device with Wood's metal melting sensor at 160°F; creep above 100°F storage temperature degrades the rating. NFPA forbids storage above 100°F; refrigerated trucks used for Texas-origin shipments.

### Corrugated stainless steel gas tubing lightning fires
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`, `§8.p4`, `§8.p5`, `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing" + "Corrugated stainless steel gas pipe lightning strike fires" + "Flexible gas piping lightning strike fires" + "Improved lightning-resistant flexible gas tubing development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Three-generation product evolution. Original yellow jacket: 0.1 coulomb capacity (typical strike is 3–5 coulombs). Black carbon-filled: ~5 coulombs. Perforated aluminum sheet: ~80 coulombs. 200–300 fires/year through the transition. Original product taken off North American market only September 2011.
- **Materials:** 10-thousandths corrugated stainless steel + plastic jacket
- **Era:** product introduced 1990s; failures recognized 1998–2001; redesign through 2011
