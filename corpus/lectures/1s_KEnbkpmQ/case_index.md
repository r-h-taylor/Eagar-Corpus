# Case Index — Lecture 1s_KEnbkpmQ (Welding Metallurgy, Summer 2014, 34/35)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§5.p3`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is structured around Tom's titanium-welding teaching, anchored by two extended industrial consulting narratives (Engelhard / Boeing 747-400; GE Lynn) and closed with an Alcoa Davenport rolling-mill anecdote. The teaching points throughout are about cleanliness, specifications, and the gap between engineering advice and industrial follow-through.

### IBM stainless steel high-purity semiconductor piping rejection
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "IBM stainless steel high-purity semiconductor piping rejection"
- **Frame in this lecture:** IBM would reject $15,000 stainless steel gas piping trains if any heat-tint colors appeared on weld surfaces — concerned about contamination reaching gallium arsenide semiconductor processes.
- **Materials/systems:** stainless steel, arsine, gallium arsenide
- **Era:** ~10–15 years before lecture (~2000)

### Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Engelhard contracted to weld thin-walled titanium ducting for ozone catalytic converters on the 747-400. Boeing's spec required no flaws larger than ten thousandths of an inch on x-ray. They were failing one out of two welds. Tom diagnosed contamination from commercial-grade acetone and fingerprint oils; reagent-grade acetone and white gloves got them to ninety percent. Problem recurred when they slipped back to old process. **Central case of §5.**
- **Materials/systems:** titanium tubing, catalytic converter, gas tungsten arc welding
- **Era:** late 1980s

### General Electric spot weld adaptive control system
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric spot weld adaptive control system"
- **Frame in this lecture:** GE Lynn contracted GE Schenectady Research at $1M/year for seven years to develop spot welding adaptive control for nickel superalloy combustor cans. Tom hired at $150K as "whipping boys" after Lynn cut Schenectady's funding. Tom and his students discovered the controller wasn't adapting until six cycles after the four-cycle weld was over.
- **Materials/systems:** nickel-based superalloy, resistance spot welding
- **Era:** mid 1980s

### General Electric adaptive weld control feedback timing failure
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`–`§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric adaptive weld control feedback timing failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Same engagement as above, but specifically the technical finding — the adaptive controller's response lag (six cycles) exceeded the weld duration (four cycles). Tom presents this finding in front of GE Lynn's boss and the GE Research engineer who admits "we fixed that last week."
- **Materials/systems:** 60-cycle current control, feedback loops
- **Era:** mid 1980s

### General Electric titanium compressor case electron beam welding porosity
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`–`§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric titanium compressor case electron beam welding porosity"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Ten-year-old titanium electron beam welding process at GE Lynn started failing x-ray inspection. Tom diagnosed contaminated commercial-grade acetone from a 55-gallon drum, and dust raining in a non-clean welding shop onto $25,000 titanium rings. Same fix as Engelhard: reagent-grade acetone. The technician got a letter of commendation; Tom got nothing. **Central case of §7.**
- **Materials/systems:** titanium, electron beam welding, $70,000 compressor part
- **Era:** mid 1980s

### Resistance spot welding (Elihu Thomson invention) — automotive
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Automobile spot welding quality"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to introduce resistance spot welding — Elihu Thomson, GE/MIT co-founder, ~1900. Tom's recurring "3,000 spot welds, 2,000 good ones" figure for automotive practice.
- **Materials/systems:** sheet steel, copper electrodes, 10,000-amp current
- **Era:** invented ~1900; ongoing automotive practice

### Alcoa Davenport aluminum rolling mill thickness variation
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alcoa Davenport aluminum rolling mill thickness variation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's LFM (Leaders for Manufacturing) student Julie diagnosed the world's largest rolling mill's biggest reject category — plate thickness variation — by ultrasonic survey instead of single-point micrometer measurement. Pattern showed thickening at plate ends; cause traced to water pooling at one end because the top roll ran slightly slower than the bottom roll. **Central case of §8.**
- **Materials/systems:** thick aluminum plate, hot rolling, water cooling
- **Era:** 1989–1990

## Figures referenced

### Welding process invention timeline
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`
- 199 welding/cutting processes in the welding handbook
- 75% developed 1900–1950
- 24.5% developed 1950–1975
- 1 (friction stir) developed 1975–2000

### Resistance spot welding economics
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- 10,000 amps for one-third of a second per weld
- 60 welds per minute on assembly line
- $0.05–$0.10 per weld
- 3,000 welds per automobile, with ~1,000 bad ones tolerated by design margin

### Titanium welding cleanliness thresholds
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§4.p2`
- Tank argon dew point: typically −20 to −30 °C; specifiable to −60
- Six-nines (99.9999%) argon corresponds to roughly −60 to −100 dew point
- Boeing 747-400 spec: no x-ray flaw larger than 0.25 mm (ten thousandths of an inch)
