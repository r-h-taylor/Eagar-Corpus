# Case Index — Lecture 15 (Welding Metallurgy, Summer 2015)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p1`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is structured around aluminum alloy metallurgy and temper designations, culminating in the America's Cup hydraulic cylinder consulting case as the worked fracture-mechanics example.

## Cases referenced in this lecture

### America's Cup hydraulic cylinder stress corrosion cracking (Navtech consulting case)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "America's Cup hydraulic cylinder stress corrosion cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Worked fracture-mechanics example. 7075-T6 piston cylinder in a yacht winch hydraulic system, operating at 7,500 psi, failed by stress corrosion cracking from saltwater exposure. Critical flaw size calculation showed ~1 mm — confirmed by examination of the fracture surface. Solution: switch to 7075-T73 (over-aged, lower yield, higher toughness and corrosion resistance) with slightly thicker wall, achieving leak-before-break.
- **Materials/systems:** 7075-T6 aluminum, 7075-T73 aluminum, hydraulic cylinder, saltwater service.
- **Era:** 2002 (case came to Tom).

### Wright brothers' aircraft engine precipitate coarsening (Smithsonian study)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Wright brothers' aircraft engine"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief teaching aside on natural aging. SEM study of the engine block in the Smithsonian showed aluminum-copper precipitates still coarsening after a hundred years at room temperature; data fell on a straight line on a log-time scale.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum-copper precipitation alloy.
- **Era:** Engine ~1903; study ~1990s–2000s.

### Aluminum-lithium development for Space Shuttle and Boeing 787
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`–`§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle Main Tank lithium-aluminum development" (primary); also touches "Boeing 787 aluminum-lithium components"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Thirty-year development arc. Soviets discovered aluminum-lithium alloys in the 1970s. Alcoa worked on it for thirty years through hot cracking and weldability problems. Used at the end of the Space Shuttle main tank lifetime and in Boeing 787 components. Lithium reduces density 10%, raises Young's modulus 10%, giving 20% improvement in E/ρ. Still difficult to weld, and lithium worsens corrosion.
- **Materials/systems:** 8000-series aluminum-lithium alloys.
- **Era:** 1970s discovery → 2000s deployment.

### Hall-Héroult aluminum refining discovery (1888)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Hall-Héroult aluminum refining discovery (1888)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Simultaneous independent invention by Charles Martin Hall (founder of Alcoa) and Paul Héroult of France, within two months of each other and with no communication. This is the foundational moment for cheap aluminum.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum smelting.
- **Era:** 1888.

### Alcan-Péchiney merger and Novelis Skooter co-cast Alclad
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`, `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Novelis Skooter co-cast Alclad process"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Modern Alclad manufacture. Traditional roll-bonding of pure-aluminum cladding onto a high-strength aluminum-alloy core had ~15% yield. Novelis (Alcan/Péchiney merger entity) developed co-casting that gives ~100% yield. Outside skin of Airbus aircraft is Alclad of this type.
- **Materials/systems:** Pure aluminum cladding on 2xxx/7xxx aluminum alloy cores.
- **Era:** Roll-bond process: 1930s–2000s. Co-cast process: ~2005–2010 adoption.

## Figures referenced
- **20-year drift of aluminum precipitates** (§3.p4): the Smithsonian Wright engine study showing room-temperature precipitate coarsening over a century falls on a log-linear plot. Used to motivate why "stable" heat-treated condition is a relative term.
- **Helicopter maintenance ratio** (§2.p4): two hours of maintenance for every hour of flight. Commercial aircraft: about one hour per hour. Used as a digression on why aluminum-lithium hasn't displaced more conventional alloys in corrosion-critical service.
