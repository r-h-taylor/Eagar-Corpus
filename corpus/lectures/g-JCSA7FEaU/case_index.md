- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boston Big Dig"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 2001 tour. Used to illustrate cable-stay tensioning precision — strands must match length within a quarter inch or stress concentration causes cascade failure.

### Artificial hip replacement failures — early iterations
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`, `§2.p4`, `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Artificial hip replacement failures - early iterations"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's MIT-bellwether argument. "Hip corroders" were the traditional biomedical engineering focus; Tom rejected them in favor of soft tissue engineering when he became department head in 1995. Used both as historical case and as the pivot to the rabbit impact-load research anecdote.
- **Materials/systems:** stainless steel and other metals in corrosive body environment
- **Era:** 1960s–1990s evolution from 6-month to 30-year service life

### Soft tissue engineering in biomedical materials
- **Anchor:** `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soft tissue engineering in biomedical materials"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 1995 strategic hire after meeting with Doug Lauffenburger. Now a third of the materials department. Example of MIT setting national field direction.
- **Era:** 1995–present

### Egyptian obelisk engineering mystery (granite fracture toughness)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`, `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Egyptian obelisk engineering mystery (Al Bakun / Wendell Wilkenning fracture analysis)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A 1970-era fracture mechanics calculation said obelisks couldn't be raised intact. Tom's office-mate (Wilkening, working under Backofen) discovered microcrack toughening at the crack tip in three-phase granite (silica, feldspar, third phase), giving 5-10× higher toughness than expected. Resolved the engineering paradox and seeded 1980s tough-ceramics research.
- **Materials/systems:** granite (three-phase composite)
- **Era:** ca. 1970 thesis work; obelisk-raising itself ancient Egyptian

### Washington Monument
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Washington Monument design safety factor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Contrast case to the Egyptian obelisks — the Washington Monument is brick assembly, not monolithic stone. Used to highlight that Egyptian obelisks really are single pieces.

### Tacoma Narrows Bridge ("Galloping Gertie")
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Tacoma Narrows Bridge"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Petroski's canonical failure example. Long slender suspension bridge lacking torsional stiffness; resonant or wind-coupled failure (cause still contested). Second iteration of the design failed. Solved by stiffening webs under the deck.
- **Era:** 1940s

### Chaparral Steel / Gordon Forward — mini-mill labor economics
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`, `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Chaparral Steel" (with overlap to "Gordon Forward mini mill competition strategy" and "Nucor/Chaparral mini-mill competitive challenge to integrated steel mills")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Gordon Forward (MIT alum, retired Chaparral CEO) on the $30/ton trans-ocean shipping cost as the threshold for U.S. mini-mill competitiveness against any overseas producer, regardless of foreign labor rates. Forward also promoting "micro mills" beyond the mini-mill scale.

### Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria"
- **Frame in this lecture:** LD firm in Austria, 1950s. Switched from air to pure oxygen blowing. Cycle time fell from 24 hours / 400 tons (open hearth) to 1 hour, now 20 minutes / 300 tons. Tom's productivity-revolution headline example.
- **Era:** 1950s development; 1970s–1980s U.S. adoption

### U.S. Steel's last large open hearth furnace
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Steel's last large open hearth furnace"
- **Frame in this lecture:** U.S. Steel built a 450-ton open hearth in the 1970s while the rest of the world was moving to BOF. Cited as case of competitive misreading.
- **Era:** 1970s

### Continuous casting of steel — Japanese development
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`, `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse" (with related "Copperweld continuous caster breakout")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Japanese-led commercialization of continuous casting. Ten-story-tall casters; copper molds; 10-foot-high mold zone; horizontal cut-off as the slab moves at centimeters/minute. Yield jumped from 65% (ingot) to 90%+ (continuous). Breakouts (shell rupture) catastrophic — hundreds of tons of liquid on a billion-dollar facility, weeks of shutdown.

### Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Last integrated steel mill built by a company (rather than a government). $5 billion in 1965 dollars — equivalent to $15–20 billion today. Nearly bankrupted Bethlehem. Profitable by 1974. Tom's bet-your-company example for capital intensity of fundamental industries.
- **Era:** built 1965, profitable by 1974

### POSCO (Korea) government-built steel mill surpassing Nippon Steel
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "POSCO (Korea) government-built steel mill surpassing Nippon Steel"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom was the POSCO professor; lectured there in 1999. Korean government financed the mill; bought the technology from U.S. Steel. POSCO surpassed U.S. Steel. Example of post-1965 integrated steel mills being national, not corporate, projects.
- **Era:** 1970s–1999

### Bao Steel (China)

### Lakshmi Mittal steel mill acquisition strategy
- **Anchor:** `§6.p8`, `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lakshmi Mittal steel mill acquisition strategy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mittal recognized in the 1980s that worldwide steel excess capacity was temporary; bought up cheap steel companies (ten cents on the dollar); by 2005 sitting on a fortune as capacity caught up with demand. ArcelorMittal as conglomerate of ~15 pre-1970 firms.
- **Era:** 1980s–2005

### Saugus Ironworks — energy-crisis emigration of British iron
- **Anchor:** `§6.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks" (with overlap to "British iron industry wood shortage crisis" and "Early American iron and glass industries—British energy crisis relocation")
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1600-era. British wood shortage forced iron production to North America. Tom's estimate: 2,000–4,000 person-hours per ton of steel at Saugus. Today: 15 person-minutes per ton. Tom's headline productivity-gain figure.
- **Era:** ca. 1600

### Japanese steel manufacturing technology — Tom's 1985 Navy-funded visit
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Japanese steel manufacturing technology visit" (overlap with "US Navy Japan steel mill visit and HSLA technology transfer")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 1985 sabbatical in Japan; U.S. Navy sent him to learn Japanese steel for better ship construction. Discovery: Japanese were using 1950s/1960s American technology that U.S. firms had never commercialized.
- **Era:** 1985

## Figures referenced (recurring numeric anchors, not cases)

- **Steel productivity 1980→1990:** U.S. steel employment halved (500,000 → 250,000) while consumption held at 100 million tons. Productivity doubled. (§6.p4)
- **Steel productivity per ton, historical:** Saugus 1600 ≈ 2,000–4,000 person-hours/ton → today 15 person-minutes/ton. (§6.p9)
- **Cost decomposition of steel production:** 1974 Bethlehem: 45% raw materials, 45% labor, 10% profit. 1980s: 80% raw materials, 30% labor, −10% profit. (§6.p3)
- **Ingot vs. continuous casting yield:** 65% → 90%+. (§6.p7)
- **Bet-your-company capital benchmark:** $15–20 billion to build a steel fab, a semiconductor fab, a new aircraft, or a new jet engine. (§7.p3)
- **Engineering as fraction of bachelor's degrees:** worldwide 13.8%; China 45%; U.S. 5%. (§9.p2)
- **U.S. share of world economy:** 30%. (§6.p3)
- **Trans-ocean shipping cost for steel:** $30/ton. (§6.p2)
