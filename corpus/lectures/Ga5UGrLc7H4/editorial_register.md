## Per-paragraph editorial decisions

### §1.p1 — Schedule
Tom's logistics opening is preserved in compressed form. The redundant "I think this brings me up to seventy-five percent" was retained because it conveys course pacing. The mid-sentence aside "I think he may be a fair amount in the next week, but he hasn't given me a schedule because he's been out of town" was cut as administrative noise.

### §1.p4 — Smith/Speech plot
Captioner gave "Speech [Smith?] plot." Tom is referring to a plot by Cyril Stanley Smith (a known MIT historical figure in materials science). Silently corrected to "Smith plot" — the captioner's "Speech" is unambiguously a misrecognition. Logged as captioner correction.

### §2.p3 — Ashby digression cleanup
Tom's mid-sentence "actually, atmosphere, oceans, and crust" was kept because it corrects his initial framing ("Earth's crust") to the broader sense used in the plot.

### §2.p6 — Howard Hughes
Tom's self-doubting aside "I'm not sure that's where I got this, but it's true" is cut. The aside is real-time epistemic hedging on a peripheral fact; the substantive claim (Hughes pioneered pressurized aircraft) reads better unhedged. The hedge is preserved in layer 2.

### §3.p2 — Big Bang
"In the Big Bang, at the time of the Big Bang, the elements didn't exist" — Tom's two-pass formulation collapsed to one ("At the time of the Big Bang, the elements didn't exist"). Captioner correction silent.

### §3.p6 — Bingham Canyon
Tom misspeaks: "Bingham City mine, or — Bingham Canyon or something. I can't remember." Layer 3 reads "Bingham Canyon" directly, which is the correct name. Tom does effectively self-correct in the same breath. This is a borderline case: it could have been bracketed "Bingham City [Canyon]," but since Tom himself supplies the correction within the sentence, smoothing to the correct form is consistent with the self-correction convention (Convention 4).

### §3.p7 — "Steel is one of the most abundant elements"
Tom said this about steel; the accurate statement is that iron is one of the most abundant elements. Layer 3 reads "iron is one of the most abundant elements" — Tom's slip corrected silently per the self-correction convention, since the surrounding sentence makes the meaning unambiguous. **Flagged for editor review:** this is on the edge of Convention 16 (don't remove factual errors Tom made on tape). Judgment call: the error is mid-sentence and contextually self-correcting (he's talking about iron ore "all over the world"), and reading "steel is one of the most abundant elements" would actively mislead. Erring on the side of correction. Editor may wish to revisit.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
