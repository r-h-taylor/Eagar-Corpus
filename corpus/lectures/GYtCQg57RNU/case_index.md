## Cases referenced

### Burns Harbor steel mill (Bethlehem Steel, 1965)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `post-1965 greenfield steel mills`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Burns Harbor as the last green-field steel mill built in the U.S., framing the magnitude of capital investment required for primary materials production and why only nation-states (not companies) now build them.
- **Era:** 1965, with present-day cost comparison

### Boeing 747 origin from C-5A military transport competition
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Boeing 747 C-5A competition`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's anecdote about the 747 being Boeing's losing C-5A military transport design, repurposed as a commercial jetliner — used to illustrate the bet-your-company scale of aerospace capital investment. Tokyo Narita anecdote of forty-seven 747s on the ground.
- **Era:** late 1960s origin; 1970s-80s as Boeing's cash cow

### Copper-bearing carbon steel for railroad corrosion resistance
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Copper-bearing carbon steel railroad application`
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1880s discovery that small copper additions (<0.2%) doubled atmospheric corrosion resistance in railroad steels. Used to motivate why "carbon steels" carry more than just carbon.
- **Era:** 1880s

### America's Cup yacht center board (Jerry Milgram consulting)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `America's Cup yacht keel beam`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Twenty-years-ago consulting call from MIT Professor Jerry Milgram (Course 13, now 2N): a six-inch-thick HY-80 (or HY-130) center board bent on first sea trials. Tom consulted on the move to 4340 and the 600°F preheat needed to weld it. Used to introduce both the high-end of alloy steel hardenability and the consequences (preheat protocols) for welding.
- **Materials:** HY-80, possibly HY-130, 4340

### Nimitz-class carrier weight creep / HSLA-65 study (1996)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1-p2`, `§5.p4-p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `HSLA 65 carrier weight-reduction study`
- **Frame in this lecture:** NAVSEA-funded million-dollar Newport News study finding 2,000-ton weight savings possible by substituting HSLA-65 for 50 ksi steel on Nimitz-class carriers. Tom's punchline: his "10× material cost" rule of thumb predicts the same answer. Also frames the broader Nimitz weight-creep problem (250 tons/year) and the $50M cost of designing a new hull.
- **Materials:** HSLA-65, A36, EH-32/DH-32, HY-100
- **Era:** 1996 study; ongoing weight problem since 1960s

### US Navy HSLA Steel Mill Investment / Japan visit
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `US Navy Japan steel mill visit and HSLA technology transfer`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's trip to Japan to study HSLA accelerated-cooling technology for Navy and Japanese steel companies. Background to the HSLA-65 story.

### Single-crystal turbine blade manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Single-crystal turbine blades (GE/Pratt & Whitney)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Student-driven aside on why we don't grow single-crystal structural metal. Tom explains creep resistance motivation, growth rate (~1-2 mm/hour), $7,000 per blade, Pratt & Whitney / GE Christmas-tree casting trees vs. Rolls-Royce's small-furnace approach.
- **Materials:** Ni-base superalloy turbine blades

### Liberty ship brittle fractures
- **Anchor:** `§2.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Liberty ships and SS Schenectady`

### Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1-p6`, `§8.p1-p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking` / `SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem` / `USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking`
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecture's centerpiece case. Tom's consulting role on the Seawolf HY-100 cracking investigation. Key elements: high-side wire from Lincoln Electric meeting spec but at the upper alloy bound (producing 130-140 ksi weld metal); 5-15 ppm hydrogen vs. the 2-3 ppm tolerable at that strength; Tom's drawing-lubricant hypothesis based on hydrogen scaling with wire surface-to-volume ratio (1/R); the Crystal City meeting with two Navy captains; in-his-head fracture mechanics calculation showing critical flaw size >> existing sub-eighth-inch flaws, leading to "don't derate, do more inspection" recommendation.
- **Materials:** HY-100 base, HY-130-equivalent weld deposit, GMAW with argon
- **Era:** Tom's involvement c. 1990 (he says "almost twenty-five years later" in 2015)
- **Sub-cases / cross-references:**
  - John Galligan (Tom's first student) at Electric Boat on HY-100 modules in HY-80 hulls
  - Lincoln Electric wire-drawing lubricant and skiving practice
  - The "Tom Eagar table" of restraint × strength × hydrogen tolerance

### Aloha Airlines Flight 243
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1-p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Aloha Airlines Flight 243`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Parallel case to Seawolf. The early-1990s 737 fuselage roof failure attributed to Aloha being the fatigue-leader in the worldwide 737 fleet — 40,000 cycles in 30,000 hours due to 40-minute interisland flights. Boeing's fleet-average inspection schedule missed the outlier. One fatality (unbelted flight attendant). Used to teach: do more inspection on the fatigue leader, the same lesson as Seawolf.
- **Materials:** 2024 aluminum fuselage skin around rivet holes
- **Era:** April 1988 incident; Tom's framing as "early 1990s"

### de Havilland Comet (mentioned as contrast)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `de Havilland Comet`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief contrast to Aloha — the 1950s British Comet's square windows caused stress-concentration fatigue cracks leading to mid-Atlantic disappearances. Reason airliners no longer have square windows.
