# Case Index — Lecture 10/14, Codes and Standards, Fall 2012

*Lecturer is a guest, not Tom Eagar. Cases below are introduced by the guest lecturer from his own consulting work and from class examples carried forward from Prof. Eagar's sessions.*

## Cases referenced

### I-35W Minneapolis Bridge collapse
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "I-35W Minneapolis Bridge collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecturer's own consulting work on the collapse. Used to illustrate (a) how factor of safety degrades over decades when maintenance assumptions are violated (locked bearings), (b) the dead-load fraction of bridge loading (80–85%), and (c) the coincidence of construction load, thermal expansion, and design omission that drove the failure. NTSB findings cited.
- **Era:** 2007 collapse; lecture 2012.
- **Materials/systems:** Steel bridge gusset plates, bearings, dead load vs. live load.

### Helicopter blade intellectual-property litigation
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`–`§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Helicopter blade intellectual property dispute"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecturer's own consulting case. One party reverse-engineered another's rotor blade design; the question was whether the substituted aluminum alloy was "as good" by aerospace certification standards. Used to walk through fatigue testing methodology, S-N curve interpretation, confidence intervals, and the damage-tolerant design approach.
- **Era:** Unspecified, evidently within the lecturer's career.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum spar (extrusion, leading-edge load-bearing); honeycomb core with aluminum skin on trailing edge; fatigue, stress-corrosion cracking, fatigue crack propagation.

### Rubber-sealed pressure vessel crack-bridging
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Rubber-sealed pressure vessel crack-bridging failure
- **Frame in this lecture:** A pressure vessel designed with factor-of-safety four; an internal rubber liner intended to protect the bottom welds and reduce corrosion exposure ended up bridging a circumferential crack so that it did not leak. The crack grew all the way around before the operators knew. Used to introduce leak-before-break as a design philosophy and to illustrate the limits of in-service visual inspection.
- **Era:** Unspecified.
- **Materials/systems:** Pressurized cylindrical vessel; internal rubber seal; circumferential weld; fracture toughness (K = σ·√(πa)); leak-before-break.

### Copper pipe pitting corrosion self-sealing
- **Anchor:** `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Copper pipe pitting corrosion self-sealing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief aside within the rubber-vessel discussion. Hot-water copper pipes pit through wall thickness, then the dissolved salts and organics in the water deposit at the pit and seal it; failure presents as a white stain rather than as a leak. Used to extend the lesson that self-sealing defects can defeat inspection.
- **Era:** Ongoing.
- **Materials/systems:** Copper plumbing; pitting corrosion; mineral deposition.

### Honda ATV market-entry overdesign
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Honda ATV design optimization history"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate that statistical design is an after-the-fact discipline. Honda's initial ATVs were overdesigned for lack of failure-rate history; later iterations were optimized against accumulated field data. Connects to the previous lecture's ATV example (welds-not-needed-all-around).
- **Era:** Early ATV market, 1970s–1980s; lecture 2012.

## Figures referenced

- **80–85% dead-load fraction** for a bridge (`§5.p2`). Recurring civil-engineering ratio cited by the lecturer in support of "fatigue was not the main driver" of I-35W.
- **Factor of safety 2 → 1.2 over service life** (`§4.p5`). The static-vs-real safety factor relationship that organizes §4.
- **Aerospace landing gear factor of safety ≈ 1.1–1.2** (`§4.p7`, `§7.p6`). Cited as the floor end of the safety-factor range, achievable only because the underlying fatigue curves have been knocked down by full statistical characterization.
- **10⁷ cycles, 25 ksi nominal → 18 ksi after confidence-interval correction** (`§7.p5`). Worked numerical example of how confidence intervals shift design allowables.
