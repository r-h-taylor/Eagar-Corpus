## §1. Inspection and the weld-angle criteria [00:02]

`§1.p1` We'll still talk about the factor of safety, but we'll use a new idea — statistics — to try to explain how we do things. The first time we talked about the definition and discussed that a safety factor of four was not really what we were doing. Yesterday we looked more closely at demand and capacity, and I gave some examples on designing for the worst-case load when I was talking about my trailer — that you want to be able to lift it up with the wind pressure from driving and still have everything strong enough. And then we talked about the little ATV, discussing that you don't have to put welds all around. It's still going to be strong enough in most cases.

`§1.p2` We didn't talk a whole lot about inspection, and one of the reasons is that Professor Eagar talked about it and will continue to talk about it. The inspection requirements are different from one standard to the other. When we're looking at ISO, for example, some of their requirements for welds are more specific than what we have with AWS.

`§1.p3` If I'm an inspector and I inspect with ISO, let's say I have a T connection between two members. The angle between the two pieces — we're going to measure it from the outside, because that's how the standard calls for it. So let's call this TA: it's got to be greater than — and they have three different levels. If it's the lowest quality inspection, they say it's going to be greater than 90 or 100. I'll put a question mark, but it's either 90 or 100. It's not very precise. Then you have 110, and you have 120.

`§1.p4` The idea is, by looking at it visually, you can determine whether you had enough heat when they welded to fuse and heat up enough of the metal. So if we have a flat surface with a bead of metal on it, and the bead is looking like this, that's a cold weld. This is a situation of a cold piece of metal in which you put liquid metal, and there's a high surface tension between them, and that's why it's bulging up a little bit. Paraffin would do this behavior. Oil and water would do this behavior. It just doesn't want to have too much interface between the liquid and the solid. When you heat it up more, the bead's going to start looking flatter. And you're also going to have melting of your base metal underneath. And that's what we want for welding.

`§1.p5` So ISO is very specific about asking for a specific angle from a visual standpoint. It's still not perfect because most of the time this is too small to really put a measuring device on. So I have to evaluate visually whether I think that it's a straight-out type of weld. The other standard we talked about, AWS, will allow you to stick out like this, as long as overall this convexity is not more than a certain amount. From my perspective, this is even harder to evaluate — how much convexity you've got by looking at it visually. But if you hire an inspector — say, "come and look at my welds, these are to be shipped to Europe for this construction project" — the inspector should be informed as to whether he's going to inspect this way or this way. And maybe he's trying to do only one of them, by experience.

`§1.p6` So inspection ties into our factor of safety. In the work I've done going around various structures, I prefer looking at the angle, maybe because I can figure it out personally better. The issue is if this weld is made out of multiple passes. Somebody came, did a first weld, another, another — you don't have a whole lot of convexity. But if you have something that's exactly like a gun pushing this way, that's also unacceptable by AWS. You can't have the weld with an angle less than 90 by AWS either.

`§1.p7` If you're exactly at 90, all you can do with AWS is use the subjective criterion — that there's a lack of fusion. And people will do it. They'll say, "this is not a good weld. It's not acceptable, because I believe you don't have the fusion." But it's a more qualitative description than to say, "you're on the edge, you got 110° or you got 100°," or whatever the number is from your estimate. So the angle criterion allows you to quantify, and that's another topic that Professor Eagar taught us in this class. If you can't quantify lack of fusion, how can you really control it? Somebody is going to say, well, it's borderline lack of fusion, or it is lack of fusion. Typically, in a quality control inspection, it's a go/no-go criterion.

`§1.p8` But if you're building a lot of a certain type of structure, you can also go back and figure out in general what your welds look like. If you came out and said, "ninety percent of the time they're 120 degrees, but ten percent of the time they're 90°" — that tells you you can't rely on a single connection to be fully reliable, because when you go to 90°, you don't have ductility, typically, when you're not fused. Fusion will be very limited under these beads. There'll be a little bit, maybe, in the middle of the bead, whereas on a properly heated weld, you expect to melt this whole area to make the connection.

`§1.p9` So in terms of capacity, I'll mention inspection as a specific aspect to call for when you're making a design. If you just ask for a weld, you get a weld. But sometimes people will say, "I want a half-inch weld." This is a half-inch weld, this is also a half-inch weld, maybe this is a one-inch weld. Is it okay also to have a one-inch weld that's not fused? So this is where it comes in to say, you've got to do it by ISO, or by AWS. The Joist Institute also has welding requirements. ASME has welding requirements. And they're all different.

`§1.p10` AWS is the only one where you don't have to qualify a single weld. If you say, "I want this very typical type of welding detail," as long as you have a qualified welder and a material that is listed in AWS, you can go and weld it. So that's a big advantage as opposed to the other codes. They will require you, even though your joint is very simple, to do your own qualification. Qualification is just part of the specification. When I was talking yesterday about the ATV — whether it's okay to have welds that are not good all around — that comes into qualification testing. You hope that in qualification they do it all well. And then you may say, "because I'm using some robots and the setup may vary a little bit every once in a while, maybe I have one weld out of the four all around that's not going to be fully fused." So now you know what "not fully fused" means: a bead that's going to be more like a caulking joint in the corner, as opposed to flatter. And it's all based on the fusion.

`§1.p11` So the inspector needs to be told, or needs to know, what's acceptable and not acceptable, in addition to being there. A lot of different things tie into the factor of safety. Design — with the trailer specification, that's what Professor Eagar is talking a lot about in this class. Manufacturing — there's variation when we're talking about welds, and that needs to come into account in the design. We talked about the trailer where we wanted to put the welds away from the highest-loaded locations, specifically to rule out manufacturing as affecting the strength. There's still the manufacturing of the wrought steel itself, but that really tends to be consistent, as opposed to making bolted connections and welded connections.

## §2. The rubber-sealed pressure vessel and leak-before-break [11:30]

`§2.p1` Another example of things that are not accounted for in the design: a pressure vessel with a factor of safety of four. On the inside, they had a rubber seal in the bottom — the rubber was at the bottom, working its way up a certain amount. We don't know the intent from the designer, but one advantage of doing this is you can reduce the exposure to oxygen. You can reduce the temperature of exposure for the metal. You can hide a welded connection that may not be as corrosion resistant as the cylinder itself. And it's the region where you have the most hydrostatic pressure, just from gravity.

`§2.p2` They had an overpressure, and they found at the very end that the crack was unzipping around the circumference — which in principle is the direction where you only have half the stress. What happened is the crack somehow knew that it was being sealed. The crack grew all around with the rubber bridging it, so it didn't leak.

`§2.p3` That's a concept I remember when I was a student — the professor said, "leak before break." So I said fine, how exactly do you do that? Because I was not exposed to it. Everybody knows what leak before break means? You want to be able to sustain a crack that grows through the thickness, and allow for a leak before you reach the fracture toughness where the crack just unravels. So you allow for not critical crack growth — subcritical. It's growing by fatigue or creep to a point where you go through the thickness and you start leaking, and people know there's a problem, and they have to come and fix it. As opposed to growing a crack up to ninety percent — and I've seen it.

`§2.p4` So say we have a vessel and we're looking at it from the top down. From the inside, because of some chemicals, there's a crack that grew like this — a semi-elliptical crack that creates this stress intensity at the front of the crack. What you want is, given your thickness, you require the material to be tough enough so the crack can actually grow all the way through. Leak, and people react to it and fix it. So the main requirement is not based on the fatigue resistance. It's only based on the fracture toughness of the material. That's the K value, related to the stress times the square root of pi A. A is the length of the crack. And then we can add a little fudge factor in front. So the idea is, for a given application, you want A to reach T, given your level of stress, and all of this to be inferior to the toughness of the material.

`§2.p5` Now when you come up with all of this, you're assuming that the crack is not going to seal itself. And that wasn't the case with this vessel, because of that rubber membrane. We have projects where we see copper pipes that have holes that go all the way through the thickness by pitting corrosion, and they seal themselves. If it's hot water, you have a little bit of flow. All the salts, and maybe some organics from the water, just come through the crack and seal it. So what are you going to do about this? Are you going to also be inspecting for stain? Because most of the time it is visible, but as a stain — a white stain, as opposed to a leak.

`§2.p6` In this example, that's the idea: if you're looking carefully at the bottom of this pressure vessel, you'll see the rubber. You were probably able to see the rubber before it cracked. But was that type of structure subject to an in-service inspection? Most of the time it's not. There's no necessarily visual inspection schedule. It depends on the type of factory, but in some factories they wouldn't have anything very specific to look for — little bulges of rubber, or stain coming from a leak that's opening and sealing. So it comes back to this idea that you don't design with a factor of safety that's minimal, thinking, "it can break, it'll be fine." You have redundancy in your safety. You don't want it to get into this leaking situation. If it happens, you hope that inspection's going to catch it, or that it will actually leak and relieve the pressure.

`§2.p7` There's nothing perfect. It becomes a question of acceptable risk. The reason they were able to do this for a while is they thought, "it's going to improve our design." When they started getting feedback like this, I'm sure they had to step back and say, "we'll let it corrode and leak. It's just safer." That's where the history comes in. So I think you have the idea as far as design and manufacturing — how you can affect your factor of safety in different ways.

## §3. Statistics, demand and capacity [18:46]

`§3.p1` So we're going to go ahead with the statistics for today. Statistics typically work backward. If you try to come up with a new design and you want to do a statistical design, I'm sure you can — is that going to be very helpful to you? Probably not. It would only be helpful if you look back to a similar application and find out how they consumed the factor of safety by experience — how they designed it very precisely, what's their failure rate, and what is it attributed to. As opposed to just saying, "I'm going to assume that the material properties are going to be plus or minus twenty percent of typical value." Where did that come from? You need to have a basis for what you're putting in.

`§3.p2` That's why it's easier to do it after the fact, and that's why it's very hard to start from scratch with a very optimized design. When Honda started to do these little ATVs, they were clearly overdesigned. They were much stronger and much more long-lasting than they probably wanted them to be, but they wanted to penetrate the market, and they didn't have the history of all of this being used everywhere, and they were somehow able to afford it. The exhaust system, everything — things would just last forever. And they changed. They went back and started to optimize based on history. So I don't want to create the false idea that you'll start from scratch, take some notes, go to some handbooks and come up with a statistical design for your new product. You can do it in an existing product. And that's more what we're going to be doing for the next twenty minutes or so.

`§3.p3` This is just a normal distribution for failure where things will fail after a mean amount of time, with things that will fail prematurely for various reasons or at a lower load, and things that will last longer. It's very easy when you think about a simple parameter. So here we're looking at it in 2D. One standard deviation, two standard deviations — if you go away by one standard deviation, sixty-something percent of your failures are going to be within that range, plus or minus one standard deviation.

`§3.p4` This is the reference I'm using for the statistical discussion. It's not a book that you're going to design something new with, but it's helpful to understand why people do the things the way they do. We talked about demand and capacity in the last class, and here we have it in an X and Y plot. There's some uncertainty on each. That's why our overall design — or manufactured structure or product — is going to behave in a certain way where most of the time it's going to be away from that failure boundary at 45 degrees.

`§3.p5` But as you change your conditions, what it's being exposed to outside the precise value that you've put on your design — that's where your safety factor goes down, and you get close to that failure boundary. In this case they overlap, here on the last circle. There'll be a given combination of load and strength that will result in failure. The probability of failure is constant around that circle. That's just assuming that each parameter is working on the positive side and the negative side in the same way. We have a circle because we're assuming that we have a twenty percent variation in capacity and a twenty percent variation in demand. It's not the general case.

`§3.p6` If we look at this, one thing to keep in mind is, in a worst-case scenario, if I go pretty bad just on the design, I'll still be okay on the capacity side. If I'm changing the load applied, same thing. But when there's something that was not what I intended as a nominal value both ways, that's where we have the failure here at an angle in the chart.

`§3.p7` And it doesn't have to be straight. That's why now we look at it in 3D. It's still the same thing, where we have the demand and the capacity, and this cut is at an angle here for the failure. This is more or less saying — and I think it's probably fair — that you can break just about anything. Everything that's being built and is being sold can be broken, one way or another. Sometimes because that specific one was not exactly as strong as the others. Sometimes because you just put too much load on it. So that concept is almost universal. And what we're going to go through is see how, after the fact, we can determine this probability of failure.

## §4. Time, social risk, and the bridge case [25:33]

`§4.p1` The criterion for being in that zone — this looks big, but it depends. It depends on what period of time you're looking at. If you look at it when you get a product right off the factory, this looks big. But if you look at it at a probability of failure over 10 years — maybe for an aircraft 20 years — every day there's a different load, every month or year they do significant repairs on your structure. The cut in the pie would probably start very close to zero when it's coming out of the factory, and then as time goes, it's just going to creep up on you. That's taken into account in this basic criterion, where you have a multiple factor. So you have time coming in, or frequency coming in. And then you have, for various structures, this social criterion factor.

`§4.p2` What it says is, if you're looking at something that a lot of people would be exposed to a failure of, like a dam, you want to minimize even further your risk. If you have 2,000 people on a bridge, it's not like my tire blowing up because it's a hot day and I was in park at the right location. Now these factors — when we're looking down here for towers, masts, and offshore structures — I think the offshore structure is really true. When Hurricane Cino [?] or some of the Gulf storms come in, it's not atypical to have a whole platform just collapsing. You hope that before they do, they're able to shut the well. But the basic idea is that it's out there — there are very few people exposed to it. It's worth a lot of money, but it becomes more of an economical decision rather than a people-safety decision.

`§4.p3` When we're looking at the Longfellow Bridge, just across here — it's more than a hundred years old. If there was a risk for it to really fail, we'd have to shut the Red Line and get it fixed, because there are a lot of people that count on it every single day. Just the train going on is different than a few cars and bicycles every once in a while. It's creating the highest loads and exposing a lot of people at the same time.

`§4.p4` So that's how, in the book, they go about figuring out why people do things a certain way. If we're looking at a bridge — I thought, when I looked at this, that the factor for a bridge was not that high. But it really depends on how many people are going to be exposed, how many cycles you're going to be having, and whether we're looking at short-term risk of failure or long-term risk of failure.

`§4.p5` So here we are with the question of time, because this shows a different probability. I would think of this as — if I start here, let's say I want a factor of two to begin with, and I'm only going to be loading it as it gets off the factory. I do some pressure test. I think it's really good. I'm going to load it 100 times or 50 times, whatever the number is, for the first few months. As time goes by, these little probabilities are going to become more and more important. I specifically picked the factor of two as the initial safety factor. This is what they call a static safety factor. So the real factor of safety comes down to about 1.2. That's taking into account some maintenance, some inspections, some variation in loads that were not expected. In the case of a bridge — bigger cars, smaller cars, more salt on the road, more lanes — you don't really know what's coming up with a bridge.

`§4.p6` That's why you put a safety factor to begin with. But that safety factor becomes significant when we have this distribution — the normal probability distribution. I'm looking at it for 5 years first. Say this is three times the standard deviation, where we put the six sigma. You've heard of six sigma?

`§4.p7` This is something I could probably test for, if I really wanted to, with my design. This is something I would very hardly test for in practice. The way I get there is by inferring that I've done my job up to a certain interval that I was able to. And that's where probability and risk and your factor of safety are changing. When you change between something you can measure — you can predict this by testing, or maybe you just do one sigma, but a certain value here you can test — there's a certain point where really it's not practical, and the only way you can do it is by looking at the history. If I start with a factor of safety of two based on what I know, I end up with 1.2.

`§4.p8` Keep that in mind if you just go on the drawing and try to start something from scratch. If I'm looking at my trailer, I'm not planning to sell it. As far as user and knowledge, it kind of stays with me, and it's going to go for recycling one day, probably. If we're looking at a bridge, the designer is not even going to be there 30 years down the road. And whoever is going to start making decisions may or may not know everything that came into the original design. They may make some decisions like throwing salt.

## §6. Helicopter rotor blades — reverse engineering and the spar [38:23]

`§6.p1` Helicopter blades. This is something I worked on. They were arguing essentially intellectual properties. Somebody was trying to copy somebody else, and the question was, was their design as good? Because there's a lot in reverse engineering. `§6.p2` So we start with the rotor in metal, and it comes out. It's got a front leading edge, tail. When you get to the rotor, you have a bunch of stiffeners, because that's where your highest load is. The reason it's the highest load is all the centrifugal force that's pulling on the end of it this way.

`§6.p3` We're not going all the way to the end. Let's say we're doing it relatively close to the rotor. It's a tapered section. Say we've identified one of the regions of highest load. The centrifugal force — well, it's the rotation speed. So we can get a good estimate from that, based on everything we know about the design, including the aerodynamic force of the engine. So say we come up with a maximum value for that. Now we also have the wind pressure and the rotation direction. That's really strong, and it's one of the reasons why this blade is deeper in this direction than in this other direction. But you do have the uplift force also. That's what keeps the helicopter up, and that's fighting against the tension direction.

`§6.p4` So by looking at this — we were talking in the trailer example about a soft point. I know my highest tension is going to be right here under the plane. It's going to be in this direction, coming from both the traction on the blade and the bending from the uplift. `§6.p5` So if we decide, "I'm going to change the aluminum from this front section" — what I want to do is make the front out of an aluminum alloy, because it's more reliable. Let's be very clear on that. And then the tail is going to be some form of a composite structure — honeycomb, anything you want. Silver foam. It's actually the direction where you have the highest strength from extrusion. ## §7. Fatigue data, confidence intervals, and the damage-tolerant approach [43:17]

`§7.p1` So we can start looking at the material properties — specifically, in this case, fatigue properties — although this is essentially exposed to the environment. If there's any risk of an effect from salts into stress cracking, we also need to test for that. So if we have two alloys and we want to verify that the new one is as good, we're looking for stress corrosion cracking. I'm going to look for high-cycle fatigue. That's the plot we had yesterday — the S-N curve, where over time the strength's going down. So that's also a factor in why we know our safety factor is going down with time. And we may also be looking at fatigue crack propagation. That's the rate, by cycle, that the crack's going to move in, if it starts penetrating.

`§7.p2` I should say right now that some designs will allow for this to happen. That's the damage-tolerant approach. If you say, "I'm going to have a crack to begin with," you're not so concerned about the time it takes to start your crack, in this kind of framework. You just say, "I have one to begin with. I don't want it to grow more than a certain amount." So we call it a damage-tolerant approach.

`§7.p3` Going back to this example, when I have data — say I'm doing it twice. I'll call it aluminum one. And then I have aluminum two. Hey, looks good, doesn't it? It's a higher curve. No problem. Now this didn't come from a single data point, and that's where the real business starts. If aluminum one came out and, for a given stress level, I had a failure here, another there, another here, another there, and then there — that was my typical curve. And that's something I want to introduce to you today. The other came out — say we were at higher stress, and yes, we ended up further, but we had some kind of variation like this, and we don't really know why it went out. It was more distributed this way.

`§7.p4` Now qualitatively this is not as good. You have too much variation. You have too much range in your levels that it's possible. Say I take the worst-case scenario here. My curve would be something like this. I'm more or less showing that it could go under when you take into account the variability.

`§7.p5` And that's one of the things we were looking at here. Same type of plot. We have two colors. Two materials. And the dotted lines are what we call the confidence intervals. They are very helpful for fatigue. And whether you take them into account or not in your design makes a huge difference. If I come here and say, "this curve looks really good, maybe 10 to the 7 cycles, 25 ksi, that's what I can take" — if you take into account the risk of variation from your measurements, you'll be down to 18. So if you say it's 18, and the other is 17, well, now we have a change. It's different from one to the other.

`§7.p6` That's really one thing I'd like you to take away — in aerospace, you don't take these curves for granted. You have to go a step further and look at how they were generated, and the variability associated with them. When we talk about the landing gear having a safety factor of 1.1, 1.2, it's because they've already knocked it down based on actual data. They went with more testing than you'll do in, you know, building up a trailer, just to be able to know that this safety factor is just ten percent or whatever.

`§7.p7` So think about the effect of time, and try to think about the risk being different depending on your application.
