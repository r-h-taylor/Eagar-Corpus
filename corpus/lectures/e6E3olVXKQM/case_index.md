# Case Index — Lecture 16

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§7.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is primarily a technical exposition on hydrogen cracking — diffusion physics, restraint tests, and the stress/material/environment framework. Tom illustrates the principles with several recurring cases.

## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Consulting engagement with Bruce Sylvester (metallurgical services)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Consulting engagement with Bruce Sylvester (metallurgical services)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Origin story — how Tom became a consulting metallurgist circa 1979, replacing David Holt, doing 500 failure analyses a year. Used to motivate "failure analysis is how you learn."
- **Era:** 1979

### Steel mill wet scrap incident
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Steel mill wet scrap incident"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Magnetic crane lifting rain-soaked scrap into the steel furnace, illustrating the everyday source of 1–3 ppm hydrogen in commercial steel heats.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon steel scrap, electric arc / hot furnace charge

### Sea wall bolt failures
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sea wall bolt failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Three or four consulting cases where steel bolt heads broke off after six months in salt water due to corrosion-generated hydrogen. Used to make the point that hydrogen embrittlement does not require welding — corrosion alone can charge the steel.
- **Materials/systems:** High-strength steel bolts, salt water, galvanic coupling

### Evanston Wyoming hydrogen sulfide gas field operations
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`, `§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Evanston Wyoming hydrogen sulfide gas field operations"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's visit to the sour-gas dome at Evanston, including the "Danger, toxic gases" roadside signs and the two competing approaches (Chevron heated sulfur pipeline vs. Amoco's 10-mile sulfur-trucking road) to handling the H2S byproduct. Sets up sulfide stress corrosion cracking as a hydrogen-cracking variant.
- **Materials/systems:** Sour gas, H2S, sulfur transport
- **Era:** ~1980s (drilling started "about 30 years ago")

### Nine-month delayed hydrogen crack (High Sierra mountains)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Nine-month delayed hydrogen crack (High Sierras)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Forward-referenced as Monday's story — steel component buried in snow that finally cracked nine months later because the cold Sierra environment slowed hydrogen diffusion to a near-stop. Not developed in this lecture.
- **Era:** unspecified

### Hydrogen cracking in welded steel (Bethlehem Steel restraint test)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Hydrogen cracking in welded steel"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's own work at Bethlehem Steel using a three-inch plate with a window cutout to fully restrain a test weld with strain gauges. Pedagogical illustration of how restraint is simulated in the lab.
- **Materials/systems:** Three-inch plate steel, strain gauges
- **Era:** late 1970s (Tom's Bethlehem Steel years)

### Farberware stainless steel cookware pot cracking
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`, `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Farberware stainless steel cookware pot cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** ILP consulting case ~20 years prior. Cooking pots formed from 304 stainless steel shipped from Brooklyn factory to Japan, arriving with side cracks. Tom's magnet test revealed deformation-induced martensite at the formed sidewall — the case continues into the next session.
- **Materials/systems:** 304 austenitic stainless steel, deformation-induced martensite
- **Era:** ~1994 (lecture is 2014, "about 20 years ago")
- **Note:** This case continues into Lecture 17.
