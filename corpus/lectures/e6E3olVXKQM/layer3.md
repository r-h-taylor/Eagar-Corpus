`§4.p3` The distance that heat or mass travels — since you're taking math courses, you might have an exponential solution, e to the minus x over — always called alpha t — or x squared over alpha t. ## §6. Notched tensile strength and reversibility [17:46]

`§6.p1` If I look at this out of Coe's book — temperature versus a hydrogen-free specimen, what they call a notched tensile strength. The notched tensile strength is, am I ripping the piece of paper? If I have a notch or a welding defect, like in those Liberty ships, I had a notch which weakened the ship in a brittle material. A hydrogen-free specimen — no brittleness, no hydrogen embrittlement. If I have a hydrogen-containing specimen, it turns out the worst condition is right somewhere down here around room temperature. Higher temperatures, it diffuses out just like the CO2 coming out of my Sprite or the hydrogen bubbling out of the weld that you saw the video of. If I get colder, everything slows down. I get down to this type of situation, and start looking at the diffusivities down here, 10 to the minus 10, and start calculating how long it's going to take in seconds for the hydrogen to move around to get to that crack tip to weaken the steel to make it brittle.

`§6.p2` The nice thing about hydrogen cracking is it's reversible. You can get the hydrogen in the steel, but if you get it out before a crack forms, no harm done. What do you have to do? For example, the ASTM spec for electroplating — because there's different ways to get hydrogen into steel. You can get it from welding. You can get it from corrosion. Corrosion breaks down — if you have 1.1 volts, you can break down the water into hydrogen and oxygen, and that stuff can go right in the bolts. I've had plenty of steel bolts and high strength steel bolts in sea walls, and people want to know why the heads are breaking off the bolts. Well, you're in salt water, and if you have it galvanically coupled to something else — one of you had the titanium pipe with the galvanic coupling. You're going to be generating hydrogen, and that hydrogen will get into the steel and can then embrittle it. Now you were looking at corrosion, but if you had stresses in there you could have cracked it.

`§6.p3` I've had three or four times someone come to me with a sea wall they built at some port, and the heads of the bolts are coming off after six months. What can they say? "Oh, must be defective bolts." No, it's corrosion, and it's generating hydrogen. There wasn't any hydrogen in these bolts when they were made, but you generated it from corrosion. So you can get it from welding, you can get it from corrosion, you can get it from electroplating. The welders all know about the hydrogen from welding, the corrosion guys all know about the hydrogen from corrosion, and the electroplaters know about it from electroplating.

`§6.p4` The spec from ASTM says that you must put it in the furnace within one to four hours for electroplating to get rid of the hydrogen. Why? Well I just went through the calculation. If you don't get it in there within an hour, it's liable to migrate and diffuse to those crack tips, a millimeter or whatever to whatever the imperfection is, and the thing could crack. Now if I have a bigger section — if I'm building a 100 ksi strength bridge which has 120 ksi — I am not allowed for the D1.1, the bridge welding code of AWS, to do my non-destructive testing first for 48 hours, or 72. I think it's 48. Because I might have had a crack form, but in 48 hours most of it will have diffused out, and so I'll be okay. I shouldn't expect cracks after that at that strength level.

`§6.p5` If I have a higher strength part, the ASME standard — it's not a code — says, you've got to put it in within one to four hours, and you either heat treat it for four hours at 375 degrees Fahrenheit. Where they get 375 degrees Fahrenheit, I don't know. Calculate it in centigrade and see if it came from Europe or the United States. But 375 sort of works as a high enough temperature. From a plot like this, 375 is probably something like 200, and you're getting up here to almost as good as unnotched. You either do it for four hours at 375 within an hour to four hours, so that you get it into the furnace and then you hold it for four hours, or you do it for 23 hours.

`§6.p6` Now where did 23 hours come from? In a heat-treating shop, think about it. Think about the next shift. You've got one hour to take one load out of the furnace and put another one in. Whoever wrote this standard realized you have to load and unload your furnace, so they said 23 hours is the standard. They could have said 24 hours, and you would just creep up into the next shift after eight days or something. When you see something like that, be curious. Ask yourself the question, why would someone pick 23 hours? Anyway, that's why they picked 23 hours.

## §8. Restraint tests: CTS, Tekken, and the implant [28:22]

`§8.p1` Okay, so where are we now? We've covered all that. What do we do when we're trying to understand all three of these plots? I lost my plot. By the way, Dustin is going to start helping me organize some of these things. *[Dustin raises his hand.]* He's also the one putting things on YouTube. `§8.p2` One of the things I have to get rid of is some of the stress. But how do I even simulate it? If you go back to Stout and Doty's book, which is where this thing came from, there's lots of ways to try to simulate it. When I worked at Bethlehem Steel and one of the other engineers had a hydrogen cracking project, we had some three-inch-thick plate, and we cut a little window in it, and we put two-inch plates up against that. We would put the steel we wanted to test as a vertical piece, and we'd weld it into this three-inch plate, so it's completely restrained. They had strain gauges on it to try to quantify everything.

`§8.p3` There are simpler tests. This is called the CTS, controlled thermal severity test. I think it was actually invented by the Navy at one time. You basically put one little plate on another plate, you bolt them together, you put a fillet weld on two sides, and then you actually measure your tri-thermal weld. By tri-thermal, you have welded these three sides. This is like putting the fourth side on a four-sided box. I talked about a six-sided box and restraint before, for a three-dimensional box. This is the test weld, and you've got this other plate sticking off here to put lots of restraint on this. There's nowhere for this weld to shrink. So they call that controlled thermal severity.

`§8.p4` There's the Prooftstform [Prüfstand?] test. You basically put two plates on top of another plate, you tack weld all three of them together on these end welds, and then you put in one, two, three, and four. The fourth one is the one you're really testing, because that's the one where there's nowhere for it to bend. You're putting in now hopefully full-level residual stresses.

`§8.p5` The Japanese had one they called the Tekken test, T-E-K-K-E-N. Tekken means railroad in Japan, so guess what industry this came out of. They used to call it the Y-groove test, and the reason they liked it is because it was easy to flame cut. You basically take your two plates and make a Y with the joint preparation, and then you put a weld right in here. They found that was a terrible geometry in terms of residual stresses, and they could get hydrogen cracking. So in Japan they'll talk about Y-groove tests or Tekken tests. That's their test. There are literally dozens of these little laboratory tests to try to simulate the restraint that you get in a real welding situation. They all sort of work, but they also all sort of don't work, right?

`§8.p6` **Student:** *[inaudible question]*

`§8.p7` What are you measuring? You're just looking for a crack. You put a weld in, and then you cross-section and say, is there a crack there? You can do a dye penetrant, and the crack might be under-bead, so you may have to section it to find the crack. But you just weld these things and you look for a crack. `§8.p8` There is another test that the French developed which is a little more scientific. It's called the implant test. You take a plate, and you put a hole in it. You take the steel plate that you want to test and you make it into a screw with a thread on it. You put the screw up through the hole, and you weld the end of that screw to the plate. `§8.p9` Hydrogen cracking is also known as delayed cracking. It takes time. It doesn't happen immediately while you're welding. It doesn't just pop seconds behind you. They want to find the crack when they inspect, not inspect and then have a crack occur that they don't know is there. So we have these little tests, but it's called delayed cracking.
