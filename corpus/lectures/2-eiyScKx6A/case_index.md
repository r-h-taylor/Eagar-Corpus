### Davenport Iowa plate stress relief by hydraulic jacking
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Davenport Iowa plate stress relief by hydraulic jacking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Alcoa's Davenport, Iowa rolling mill — "the world's largest" — rolls aluminum plate up to four inches thick for 747 wings, then mechanically stress-relieves it by 1–3% stretch in a large hydraulic machine. Used to introduce the T651 / T63 temper designations as mechanical-stress-relief codes.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum aerospace plate; T651, T63 tempers

### Aluminum washer stress-corrosion cracking failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum washer stress-corrosion cracking failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Customer bought aluminum in T651 (stress-relieved) condition, machined washers, reheat-treated them, and did not re-relieve the residual stresses. Resulting 8 ksi residual stress exceeded the 5 ksi SCC threshold for the alloy; washers cracked. Resolved by going to overage temper with higher SCC tolerance.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum, T651 → overage temper

### Welded crane component failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Welded steel crane component failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A two-inch-thick steel crane component with un-stress-relieved welds failed in brittle fracture under bending, dropping a seventy-ton water bag onto a worker (back injury). Boiler-and-pressure-vessel code requires post-weld heat treatment above 1.5 inches; this code was not followed.
- **Materials/systems:** structural steel, two-inch thick weldment

### US Navy submarine first-dive stress relief
- **Anchor:** `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Submarine first deep dive stress relief mechanism"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Submarines cannot be furnace-stress-relieved after outfitting (rubber, plastics, O-rings inside). The Navy uses hydrostatic compression on the first deep dive to mechanically relieve weld residual stresses — and requires shipyard top management to ride that first dive. Tom's "favorite quality-control story."
- **Materials/systems:** HY-80 / HY-100 submarine pressure hull steel

### Trolley wire (Conform process)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Trolley wire manufacturing by Conform process"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Phelps Dodge produced trolley wire by the Conform process (invented at a UK national lab), which knead-deforms powder/rod through a circular cavity to ~1,000% equivalent plastic strain. Output had near-superplastic elongation. British purchasers attempted to reject the wire on a too-much-elongation specification reading — Tom alludes to legal consequences (lost passports).
- **Materials/systems:** copper trolley wire; Conform process
- **Linked cluster:** "Conform process development"; "Amtrak catenary wire procurement fraud case"

### 50,000-ton Wyman-Gordon forging press structural crack
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "50,000-ton Wyman-Gordon forging press structural crack"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom visited Wyman-Gordon (North Grafton, MA; later acquired by Krupp) in the early 1980s to investigate a crack in a forged I-beam at the base of the Air-Force-built 50,000-ton press. Six 300-ton I-beams at top and bottom; one had cracked at a weld over thirty years of service. Replacement estimated at two billion dollars in 1983.
- **Materials/systems:** large structural steel I-beam forgings
- **Era:** crack investigated early 1980s; press built early 1950s

### 50,000-ton forging press development (Air Force/Alcoa/Wyman Gordon)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "50,000-ton forging press development (Air Force/Alcoa/Wyman Gordon)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Air Force built two 50,000-ton presses in the early 1950s for large bomber forgings because welding was not yet trusted for structural components. Wyman Gordon (Grafton MA) and Alcoa (Cleveland) operate them; titanium/nickel-superalloy forgings vs. aluminum forgings respectively.

### Air Force 100,000-ton forging press project
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force 100,000-ton forging press project"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Air Force had 1950s plans to site a 100,000-ton press in a Colorado granite mountain (mountain itself as foundation). Never built — electron beam welding emerged as a more economical solution for the swept-wing fighter center-box parts that motivated the press. Plans would have cost ~$2B in 1980s dollars.
- **Linked cluster:** "Swept-wing fighter aircraft center box fabrication"

### Swept-wing fighter center-box fabrication
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Swept-wing fighter aircraft center box fabrication"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Late-1950s swept-wing fighters (wings move from sonic to supersonic configuration) required a large structural center box. Initially the motivating part for the 100,000-ton press; instead solved by electron beam welding.

### 7,000-ton forging press weld repair (aluminum truck wheels)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "7,000-ton forging press weld repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's welding-course teaching specimen — a 20-inch-thick weld repair on the head of a 7,000-ton press that stamps eighteen-wheeler aluminum truck wheels. Head dome ~10 feet high, ~350 tons, would fill a third of the classroom volume.
- **Materials/systems:** large steel forging-press head; aluminum truck wheel forgings produced

## Figures referenced

- **Dislocation velocity ~ 1/3 speed of sound** (§4.p3, §4.p5). Used to bound when strain-rate effects actually appear in cold deformation.
- **Speed of sound ~ 5,000 m/s in metal** (§4.p3).
- **T651 = 1% stretch; T63 = 3% compression** (§5.p3, §5.p7). Aluminum temper designations as mechanical-stress-relief codes.
- **5 ksi SCC threshold for the washer alloy; 8 ksi residual; overage gives 20–25 ksi tolerance** (§5.p6).
- **Boiler/pressure vessel code requires PWHT above 1.5 inches of steel** (§5.p8).
- **Frictional shear factor 0.18–0.53 in ring test across forging alloys** (§9.p6).
- **Wire-drawing temperature rise: 86°F entry → 130°C exit, 212°F rise** (§10.p4).
- **High-volume automotive die set cost: $100M–several-hundred-M for 100,000 units over three years** (§10.p3).
