# Case Index — Lecture 10 (3.371 Deformation Processing, Spring 2012)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§5.p3`). This lecture is unusual: Tom announced it was not the lecture he'd planned, and it became a sustained meditation on materials hype cycles, decision-making in industry, and the MIT-vs-HBS divide. Several recurring corpus cases get extended treatment.*

## Cases referenced

### Egon Orowan dislocation discovery (rug anecdote)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Egon Orowan dislocation discovery (rug anecdote)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Origin of the dislocation concept in 1930s Hungary; Orowan watching a maid kick a dislocation across an oriental rug. Used to introduce the concept of dislocations as one-atom-row-at-a-time deformation.
- **Era:** 1930s.

### Iron whiskers (1950s screw dislocation studies)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Iron whiskers (1950s screw dislocation studies)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1940s iron whiskers with axial screw dislocations measured at 2 million PSI when pulled along the screw axis. Used to establish that theoretical strengths are achievable only in idealized geometries.
- **Era:** 1940s.

### Carbon nanotubes structural applications (overpromised research)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`, `§2.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Carbon nanotubes structural applications (overpromised research)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Modern nanotube strength claims (3 million PSI) treated as a recapitulation of 125-year-old theoretical bond-strength calculations, with the same fatal flaw — vacancies at any non-zero temperature destroy the strength.

### Sprague's law on material properties
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sprague's law on material properties"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Bob Sprague (GE Aircraft Engines, 1970s): "When you first hear about the properties of a new material, write it down — those are the best properties the material will ever have."

### Williams's law on material costs
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Williams's law on material costs"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Jim Williams's corollary to Sprague's law: the first quoted cost of a new material is the lowest it will ever be.

### High-temperature superconductors hype and failure to solve power transmission
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`–`§3.p5`, `§4.p3`, `§5.p1`–`§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "High-temperature superconductors hype and failure to solve power transmission"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecture's central extended case. 1988-89 discovery; NSF policy that all grants required HTSC keywords for eighteen months; 75% of the MIT materials department pivoting; the four tenured holdouts who knew critical current would not scale.

### High-temperature superconductivity for high-field magnets and levitated trains
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`, `§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "High-temperature superconductivity for high-field magnets and levitated trains"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's "we won't see magnetically levitated trains in our grandchildren's lifetimes" prediction made in 1995 at Kresge Little Theater.

### High-temperature superconductor critical current density problem
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`, `§3.p5`, `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "High-temperature superconductor critical current density problem"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The scaling argument: pinning-site size scales inversely with critical temperature, so 40-70 K superconductors need pinning sites smaller than an electron — impossible.

### High-temperature superconductor critical current measurement error
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "High-temperature superconductor critical current measurement error"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Papers in *Science* magazine around 1990 measuring critical current with field parallel to current (zero J-cross-B force), reporting fantastic values that would never apply to a wound magnet where J and B are perpendicular. Seven orders of magnitude difference.

### Niobium-titanium superconductor development for high-energy physics
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Niobium-titanium superconductor development for high-energy physics"
- **Frame in this lecture:** John Wulff's work; critical temperature ~9 K, fields ~6 Tesla. The 1970s workhorse superconductor.

### Niobium-3.5tin superconductor with zirconium oxide pinning precipitates
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Niobium-3.5tin superconductor with zirconium oxide pinning precipitates"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mark Benz at GE Research; 1% zirconium internally oxidized to form 20-30 nm zirconium oxide precipitates spaced ~100 nm apart, providing the pinning sites that gave good critical field. The case Tom was originally going to lecture on.

### Wire drawing thesis project on superconducting wire production
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Wire drawing thesis project on superconducting wire production"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's house tutor's doctoral thesis on severely deformed niobium as a superconductor; the wavy-slip picture in Backofen.

### American Superconductor commercial viability (1995–present)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`, `§5.p6`, `§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "American Superconductor commercial viability (1995–present)"

Twenty-some years on: limping, Chinese theft of wind-turbine software, some New York City transmission lines installed. The downstream consequence Tom predicted.

### Ford Taurus aluminum body cost analysis
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford Taurus aluminum body cost analysis"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Joel Clark's student's doctoral thesis (early 1990s, later Harvard Business School assistant professor): $500 steel unibody vs $1,000 aluminum unibody, before fabrication. Tom's critique: the thesis ignored that aluminum can't be resistance spot welded like steel.

### Automobile spot welding quality
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Automobile spot welding quality"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 3,000 spot welds per car / 2,000 good ones; ~$0.05 per weld; aggregate worldwide volume ~90 billion welds per year (Tom's live arithmetic). Used to demonstrate that joining cost equals material cost in automotive bodies.
