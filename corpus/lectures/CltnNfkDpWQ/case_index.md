# Case Index — Lecture 7 (CltnNfkDpWQ)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§5.p8`). The lecture's spine is the history of steel productivity gains plus a closing arc on turbine-blade temperature limits and the Air Force Mach 17 NRC study.*

## Cases referenced

### Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1956 Austrian innovation after Allied bombing destroyed open-hearth capacity. Origin story of the BOF.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon steel; pure-oxygen blowing on Bessemer-derived vessel.
- **Era:** 1956 onward.

### Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`–`§4.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Technical exposition of the continuous caster — tundish, water-cooled copper mold, breakout risk, copper-check defect on startup.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon steel; water-cooled copper molds.
- **Era:** 1960s onward.

### Copper checks from continuous caster startup
- **Anchor:** `§4.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Copper checks from continuous caster startup"
- **Frame in this lecture:** First fifty tons of a startup cast are returned for remelting because copper contamination causes surface checks before slag layer develops.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon steel; copper-mold interface.

### Continuous caster breakout
- **Anchor:** `§4.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Continuous caster breakout"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Catastrophic failure mode: skin breaks, fifty tons of molten steel dump out, machine down for weeks, millions in losses.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon steel; continuous caster.

### U.S. Steel's last large open hearth furnace
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Steel's last large open hearth furnace"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Early 1970s — U.S. Steel builds the world's largest open hearth at 450 tons while every other producer is installing BOFs. Within a few years they have to convert anyway. Emblematic of management failure.
- **Era:** early 1970s.

### Gordon Forward mini mill competition strategy
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`–`§5.p11`, `§5.p17`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Gordon Forward mini mill competition strategy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Gordon Forward (1975 MIT 3-department graduate) founds Chaparral Steel in Texas. Tom recurringly slips and calls him "Gordon Forward."

### Chaparral Steel
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`–`§5.p21`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Chaparral Steel"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Net-shape casting of structural shapes (near-I-beam), 1 person-hour/ton by 1990, exporting to Japan, $450,000 sales per employee. Management culture: blame for not innovating rather than for failed experiments; production employees spend a week/year with sales visiting customers.
- **Materials/systems:** electric-arc-furnace carbon steel; structural shapes; rebar.
- **Era:** 1975 onward.

### 3/8 inch rebar rolling mill productivity case
- **Anchor:** `§5.p20`–`§5.p21`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "3/8 inch rebar rolling mill productivity case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Chaparral cannot run rolling mill faster than 60 mph (instabilities at 70). Solution: run slower, split into two strands, then four. Doubles productivity; takes over the 3/8" rebar premium market.

### Clayton Christensen innovator's dilemma research—steel mill cost data
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`–`§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Clayton Christensen innovator's dilemma research—steel mill cost data"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's daughter, working summer research for Christensen, can't find published steel mill construction costs. Tom supplies a slide with his Burns-Harbor billion-dollar estimate, which appears as a reference in *The Innovator's Dilemma*.

### Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1965–1975 construction at a cost of ~$1 billion. The last integrated steel mill built by a private US company. Tom invokes it to make the country-vs-company-investment point.

### Steel mill construction cost analysis (disruptive technology case study)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Steel mill construction cost analysis (disruptive technology case study)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $10–20B greenfield integrated mill cost today; no private builder since 1975. Parallels drawn with Boeing/Airbus airframes and Intel silicon foundries — all country-scale subsidies.

### Boeing door product center cost accounting failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p12`–`§5.p15`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing door product center cost accounting failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student thesis. 250 employees, 750 doors/year, no per-door cost tracked because the unit operated as a Boeing cost center. Tom estimates ~$50,000/door using sales-per-employee heuristic; manager confirms.

### General Electric/Pratt & Whitney directional solidification and single crystal turbine blades
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`–`§6.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pratt & Whitney single-crystal turbine blade"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mert Shank (formerly MIT 3 faculty) drives directional solidification at Pratt & Whitney. Grain-boundary creep failure mitigated first by columnar growth, then by single crystals. Air-cooled internal channels then enable firing temperature above the alloy melting point (3000°F firing vs 2400°F alloy melt).
- **Era:** 1970s–1990s.

### High-temperature turbine blade rhenium content escalation
- **Anchor:** `§6.p12`–`§6.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "High-temperature turbine blade rhenium content escalation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** CMSX-4 (~3% Re), CMSX-5 (~6% Re). Rhenium is a platinum-group metal; $1000–$2000 per ~1 lb pigtail sample. Tom recalls Nick Grant's class declaring platinum the ideal jet engine material — laughable then, no longer.

### CMSX4/CMSX5 rhenium alloy pigtails in MIT lab
- **Anchor:** `§6.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "CMSX4/CMSX5 rhenium alloy pigtails in MIT lab"
- **Frame in this lecture:** ~20 pigtails discovered during a lab cleanout, each containing ~$1000 of platinum-group metal. Tom collected two and instructed others not to scrap them. They typically get recycled.

### Pratt & Whitney niobium alloy development failure
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`–`§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pratt & Whitney niobium alloy development failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $18M of United Technologies internal R&D spent on oxidation-resistant niobium alloys at the urging of an 85-year-old Pratt & Whitney engine designer on the NRC committee. Predictable failure given niobium-oxygen reactivity.

### Air Force Mach 17 air-breathing aircraft program
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`–`§7.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force Mach 17 air-breathing aircraft program"
- **Frame in this lecture:** NAS committee (~2005). Air Force wants a Mach 17 non-man-rated engine for cruise-missile-style strikes. Frictional heating to 3000–4000°C against materials limits (W ~3300°C, Ir ~3100°C). Justified by need to strike anywhere in the world in 20 minutes (bin Laden window). Tom: dreamed up by people who believe Star Trek is real.

### National Aerospace Plane
- **Anchor:** `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "National Aerospace Plane"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mid-1980s. 3mm copper skin, water-cooled, with liquid hydrogen fuel tank inside and 3000–4000°C frictional heating outside. Hydrogen-air contact at those temperatures = kaboom. Billions spent. Antecedent to the Air Force Mach 17 program.

### Titanium combustor fires in jet engines
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Titanium combustor fires in jet engines"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Compressor blades are titanium. Spike to 900°C ignites titanium like a flare, melts the engine core.

## Figures referenced

- **Steel productivity timeline (1600 → 2013):** 3000 person-hours/ton in 1600 → tens of hours/ton by WWII → ~10 hours/ton in 1975 → ~0.33 hours/ton in 2013. Source: Tom's own graph. `§1.p2`, `§1.p5`.

- **Liquid vs solid-state diffusivity:** 10⁻⁵–10⁻⁶ cm²/s liquid vs 10⁻¹² cm²/s solid near melting point — seven orders of magnitude. `§2.p4`.
- **BOF cycle:** 300 tons in ~40 minutes (20 min burn + 20 min analyze and pour). `§4.p5`, `§5.p1`.
- **Turbine engine fuel savings:** ~$5B/year to commercial airlines per 50°F firing-temperature increase. `§6.p1`.
- **Turbine alloy temperatures:** 3000°F firing, 2400°F alloy melt. `§6.p9`.
