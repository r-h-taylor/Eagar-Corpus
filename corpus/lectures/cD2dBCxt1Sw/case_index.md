## Cases referenced

### Naval diver thermal protection using magnesium alloy
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`–`§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval diver thermal protection using magnesium alloy" (also relates to "Navy diver salvage gas generation system (magnesium composite)" and "Fort Fundy magnesium-graphite composite diving application")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Port Hueneme (~1980) graphite-magnesium composite developed for two diver applications — a hydrogen-generating lifting bag for underwater salvage, and a heat-generating belt-pack to keep divers warm in cold water. Used to motivate the magnitude of the galvanic potential between the most-noble and least-noble entries on the series.
- **Materials/systems:** graphite-magnesium metal matrix composite; seawater electrolyte.
- **Era:** ~1980 (Tom: "about thirty-five years ago").

### NYC titanium heat exchanger fire
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "NYC titanium heat exchanger fire" (also relates to "New York City titanium heat exchanger corrosion failure")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Post-9/11 skyscraper in NYC; titanium heat exchanger took on East River silt through a misopened valve; under-deposit corrosion; non-English-speaking workers brought in with oxy-acetylene torches. Used to demonstrate that titanium *can* corrode and *can* burn when its protective oxide is breached.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium heat exchanger; East River silt; oxy-acetylene flame cutting.
- **Era:** shortly after September 2001.

### Titanium anhydrous ammonia explosions
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Titanium anhydrous ammonia explosions"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stress-corrosion-cracking failures in early titanium rocket-program applications. Titanium + anhydrous NH3 (less than ~100 ppm moisture) → titanium nitride and titanium hydride; loss of protective oxide → "exciting explosions" on launch pads.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium; anhydrous ammonia; early rocket program.

- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`
- Magnesium at -1.6 V; platinum/graphite at +0.2 V. Max practical aqueous voltage ~1.5 V (battery limit); ~1.1 V is the sea-water electrolysis threshold for cathodic protection.
