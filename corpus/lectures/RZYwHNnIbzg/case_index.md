The teaching arc of Lecture 15 runs from generic pressure-vessel stress relief through the specific Seawolf hydrogen-cracking forensic investigation, returns to mechanical stress relief via the Davenport aluminum-plate stretcher and the first-dive submarine practice, and closes with Tom's account of how he learned failure analysis at JG Sylvester and Associates in the late 1970s.

## Cases developed in this lecture

### USS Seawolf hydrogen cracking
- **Anchors:** `§5.p1` – `§5.p11`, `§6.p1` – `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking" (also matches "USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking" and "Groton Connecticut submarine welding hydrogen embrittlement failures")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's role as one of five outside experts brought in by the Navy in 1991–92 to advise on the Electric Boat HY-100 weld failures. His report uniquely concluded the cause was dirty welding wire (drawing-lubricant contamination, worse on smaller-diameter wire due to higher surface-to-volume ratio). Eighteen percent of the hull was riddled with sixteenth-inch under-bead cracks in weld metal, discovered when a grinder noticed swarf aligning magnetically.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-100 hull steel; 7018-class basic electrodes; gas metal arc welding wire (3/32, 1/8 in.)
- **Era:** 1991–1992

### Existing 688-class HY-100 hull sections: derating decision
- **Anchors:** `§6.p1` – `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "688-class submarine HY-100 test section derating decision"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Two prior 688-class attack submarines had 20–40 ft HY-100 test sections built into them as a precursor to Seawolf. After the Seawolf cracks were found, the Navy asked Tom whether those existing ships had to be derated. His answer: no — increase inspection budget, the sixteenth-inch cracks won't grow fast enough to threaten ship life.
- **Era:** 1992

### Davenport Iowa aluminum plate mechanical stress relief
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`

### JG Sylvester and Associates consulting apprenticeship
- **Anchors:** `§8.p7` – `§8.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Consulting engagement with Bruce Sylvester (metallurgical services)" (also "Sylvester and Associates embezzlement fraud")
- **Frame in this lecture:** How Tom learned failure analysis. Returning to MIT from Bethlehem Steel in 1976 with a one-third pay cut and a growing family, Tom needed consulting income. Bruce Sylvester (who ran his late father's metallurgical/NDT firm south of Boston) hired Tom on the side. Sylvester wanted to expand into diving and nuclear failure analysis; his prior staff metallurgist had been Dave Holt, a former MIT junior faculty member who'd gone into the ministry after being denied tenure. Bruce Sylvester turned out to be embezzling from his mother's company via fake invoices, caught ~1983; he embezzled half a million dollars and disappeared, leaving his mother to mortgage her home.
- **Era:** 1976–1983

## Figures referenced

- **Pressure-vessel stress relief: 1100–1250°F for one hour per inch of thickness, per Stout and Doty** (§2.p1).
- **ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code: stress-relief required above 1.5 inches thickness** (§1.p2, §2.p1).
- **Hydrogen cracking thresholds:** 30 ppm to crack 35 ksi yield (A36-class); 10 ppm at 120 ksi (HY-80); 5–6 ppm at HY-100; 2–3 ppm at 150 ksi yield (Seawolf weld metal) (§3.p5–§3.p6, §5.p1).
- **Air Force NDT detection threshold:** >90% probability of finding a flaw greater than 1/8 inch (§5.p3).
- **Seawolf weld cracks:** 1/16 inch, in weld metal (not heat-affected zone), 18 percent of hull affected (§5.p2–§5.p4).
- **Davenport aluminum stretcher:** 3M to 10M lb force, 3% plastic stretch (§7.p4).
