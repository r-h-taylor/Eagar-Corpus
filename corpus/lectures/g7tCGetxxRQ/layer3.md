## §2. Submerged arc, electroslag, and the first electroslag titanium weld outside the Soviet Union [03:55]

`§2.p1` Some people were asking me about submerged arc welding. They said, "is that submerged underwater?" Submerged arc welding is a process where you weld two plates in the flat position. You have a wire, and instead of a hundred or two hundred amperes you might have five hundred or a thousand amperes of current. You put down a really big weld. This is one of the ways they make great big pipeline steels, like the Keystone pipeline. It will be a steel plate that's been turned into a U-shape and then cold-forged into an O-shape, and then they'll run a submerged arc weld in the center. It might be three-quarters of an inch thick, and you can make a great big weld. It's submerged underneath a bed of sand or flux — steel welding flux. The sand might cost you two or three dollars a pound. You've submerged the arc underneath the sand, so you have this arc that otherwise would be blowing sparks all over and would be too bright to see, but it's submerged beneath this sand which melts and forms a glassy layer on top, which is the slag.

`§2.p2` My first research contract on titanium at MIT was on submerged arc welding of titanium to get a good weld. They didn't have the best shape, but they had chemistry that was excellent. I had to use optical-quality calcium fluoride crystals, which I was buying as scrap from a company in Western Massachusetts that was making these optical windows out of calcium fluoride. I was paying $100 a pound for the scrap. It turns out it would cost about $1,000 a foot just to pay for the flux for submerged arc welding. Technical success, economic failure. That was one of the first things I helped the US Navy learn about.

`§2.p3` There are a number of welding processes that were used for welding titanium, one of which I didn't realize until 1980, in the Alpha sub. I was at a meeting down at David Taylor Ship Research Center in Annapolis, Maryland, and all of a sudden I realized Gurevich had been publishing in the early 70s on electroslag welding. Electroslag welding is a process where you have a wire feed, you have two vertical plates, you put a water-cooled copper dam on either side, and you put a little bit of flux in the center — you don't cover the whole length of the weld with flux, you put a handful of flux in there. You run the wire in, you melt the flux, and now it's the resistive heating of the flux that melts the wire and you just make a casting going up. *[Tom passes around a sectioned titanium electroslag weld.]* This is a two-inch thick titanium plate with a gap here. You can see where the water-cooled copper mold was, and the weld is this thing that's about two inches by two inches. Very efficient process.

`§2.p4` I came back from that meeting in 1980 in Annapolis and had one of my students make what I think is probably the first electroslag titanium weld outside of the Soviet Union or France. I later found a paper in France where they tried it, but right on the other side of that wall we made the first electroslag weld. They classified it, so that work was done — I couldn't do any more of that. The reason they classified it is — to the best of our knowledge, that's one of the ways the Soviets built the Alpha subs. They didn't use submerged arc welding, they didn't use what the US Navy had spent millions of dollars trying to do, which is gas metal arc welding. The bulk of steel welding and most of the aluminum welding done in the world today is gas metal arc welding. You just feed a wire in, you have some argon shielding gas, and it works great. The US Navy had been working on it, and they never really got it to work very well. I was doing research on it and I couldn't get it to work either.

`§2.p5` In 1980 I went over to the Soviet Union, and I got to meet with Gurevich in front of Mariinsky, the KGB agent. He was sitting right there in the room, and I talked to Gurevich for two hours. He was a wonderful man. He answered every question I asked, and he answered them all honestly. I said, "what about gas metal arc?" "Oh, we don't use that." The US Navy had been working on a process that the Soviets had determined a long time ago didn't work. I won't go into the reasons why it doesn't work, but the Soviets used electroslag, and another process they used was gas tungsten arc welding, GTA — you just use a tungsten electrode, you use some argon shielding gas, and you make a weld. Here are some titanium welds made by gas tungsten arc. Usually gas tungsten arc is for little welds in eighth-inch or quarter-inch thick material.

## §3. Semi-submerged GTA and the Marangoni flux trick [09:55]

`§3.p1` It turns out Gurevich had done a lot of work in the late 60s and early 70s on a process which he called semi-submerged gas tungsten arc weld. It wasn't submerged under a bed of flux. If you made something that was, let's say, two inches thick, just like that electroslag weld, but you had it in the horizontal position — just like these two tables. If I wanted to weld these two tables together I could prepare the joint edges, machine them like this, and this could be two inches thick. If I put just a little thin layer of flux under — remember, this flux could cost you a hundred bucks a pound — put a little thin layer, just like painting on some slurry about a quarter or half millimeter thick, and then you come in with a great big tungsten electrode, a quarter-inch diameter, and use a thousand amps, you can make a weld bead that looks like that, half an inch deep. Then you can put another one on the top with some filler metal, and then you can flip the plate over, make another one over here, make another. In four passes you could weld a two-inch plate. More efficient than welding steel.

`§3.p2` I can't tell you how I know they did this, but I know they did this. They would have to shoot me if I told you. Okay, they're gonna shoot me, go ahead.

`§3.p3` **Student:** [question about titanium filler metal]

`§3.p4` The flux is actually the key. They were calcium fluoride, strontium fluoride, they might have some other fluorides — chlorides in there. It was just a little paste of these optical fluorides. That piece I'm handing around, the quarter-inch piece, is actually an example of one that had a flux on one side and another weld on the other side. One of them only goes about a millimeter deep, the other one goes all the way through, or nearly all the way through, about five millimeters. Same welding current. One of them had a flux and the other one didn't. What happens is the flux causes what we call Marangoni surface-tension-driven flow. You get the hot metal from right in the center of the molten weld pool driving down and digging a deep penetration. If you didn't have the flux there, the Marangoni flow would be out from the hot center to the sides, giving you a very wide, very shallow weld pool. In titanium, with these fluxes, you can change the depth-to-width ratio of your weld zone by a factor of five or even ten, by painting this little flux on there.

`§3.p5` So in the mid-1980s you will see coming out of Ohio State all these projects on trying to do Marangoni flow for gas tungsten arc. They wanted to make it work for steel. Doesn't work so well for steel; works great for titanium. The Soviets were doing it in the 60s. I was the only person outside of the US Navy welding heavy-section titanium — that's another story. In 1980 when the Alpha sub came out, and then I tried the electroslag, kind of a light went on at this meeting. I thought, that's why Gurevich was doing this — like I had been trying to do submerged arc — and then I started thinking of his semi-submerged arc. So I came back and I did electroslag welding, semi-submerged arc with this little flux for gas tungsten arc, and then the Navy classified everything, so I quit doing any of that work. You can't do classified work on campus. I didn't want to even touch it. I basically sort of quit doing most of my titanium work at that point.

`§3.p6` But Oregon Graduate Center, where they made the electroslag weld — they allowed classified contracts. The guys at Ohio State in their welding department weren't working on titanium, they were working on steel or aluminum, and that way they kind of got around the classification. But the thing that really worked, and what the Navy was working on, was semi-submerged arc with the little thin layer of flux, or electroslag with a little thin layer of flux. A whole chapter or two in this book is how to make flux and purify it.

## §4. Titanium metallurgy: alpha-beta phases and Widmanstätten structure [14:55]

`§4.p1` There are other ways to weld titanium. One of the problems with titanium — I can show you one of my slides from the welding handbook. Titanium is allotropic. At low temperatures it's hexagonal close-packed; at high temperatures it's body-centered cubic. Here's a schematic phase diagram. If you have an alpha-beta alloy and an alpha stabilizer like aluminum in your titanium, at higher temperatures with more aluminum you stabilize the alpha phase. Titanium 6-aluminum 4-vanadium is an alpha-beta alloy that has both crystal structures. I'll show you more about that later.

`§4.p2` It turns out if you start looking at impurity elements, which is important in titanium — oxygen, nitrogen, and carbon — the strength goes up from about 40 ksi up to a hundred ksi with 2000 parts per million oxygen. The elongation goes down, but you can still have ten percent or fifteen percent, which is plenty of elongation in these alloys. So we actually add oxygen to strengthen titanium alloys, and we lose a little bit in elongation, but it's still got enough ductility.

`§4.p3` **Student:** [question about thermal expansion]

`§4.p4` The thermal expansion is about the same in both crystal structures. It's anisotropic — it's not the same in both directions — but that transformation temperature is about 900 centigrade. Everything's plastic enough at those temperatures that you don't run into trouble. They sort of have a little bit of micro strain to accommodate each other.

`§4.p5` What you get is what we call Widmanstätten structure. This is slowly cooled titanium 6-aluminum 4-vanadium. At the grain boundaries you'll get some alpha, and you'll get some beta and alpha going in different directions. If you cool faster — this is furnace cooled, this is air cooled — this is basically what you'll get in a weld, and it's ultrafine compared to this. If you water-quench you'll get something even finer. This is super strong because of the fine structure compared to the furnace-cooled, but you actually get what we call basket-weave morphology. You get grains crossing each other because you nucleate and you get anisotropic crystal growth. That gives you a very favorable, very fine-grain, very mixed-up crystal structure, which is one of the things that gives you good strength.

`§4.p6` It turns out the welds in titanium have better fatigue strength than the base metal because they have a finer structure. I had a student in the early 80s working on helicopter rotors over here at Watertown Arsenal. The Army wanted to use castings. You cast titanium or anything else, you're gonna have defects in the casting. You can't throw out a fifty-thousand-dollar casting, you've got to weld-repair it. The question was, would the weld repair be any good. Gordon proved the welds were better than the base metal, because you've got a better cooling rate, gave you a finer structure. We don't have time to go through all the metallurgy, but the point is, titanium is relatively easy to get a good structure in the weld.

## §5. Titanium market size and the TIMET steel-mill rental [18:45]

`§5.p1` There are not a lot of titanium alloys out there. There are dozens, but it's not like steels where you have thousands, or aluminum where you have hundreds of alloys, because the market size is just too small. There's not even a titanium mill. If a steel mill produces two million tons of steel a year and there's a billion tons of steel made in the world each year, that means there have got to be five hundred steel mills in the world. That's a lot. Aluminum — there's 45 million tons, and if an aluminum mill produces half a million tons, that means there might be a hundred aluminum mills in the world. They're smaller than steel mills. Titanium — twenty-six thousand tons. You can't justify building one mill.

`§5.p2` So what do they do? TIMET — Titanium Metals — is one of the largest producers of titanium plate and sheet in the United States. They rent time one day a week on a steel mill. I'm not kidding. One of the reasons it takes so long to get something out of TIMET is because they only do processing one day a month on someone else's mill. It's a billion-dollar facility, and with twenty-six thousand tons of structural titanium in the world, you can't afford to build a billion-dollar mill. But you can afford to go to some steel company that's not doing too well and rent time on their mill. There's lots of changeover — you've got to get rid of all the iron oxide on the surface, you've got to go in there and clean that mill. You can't just do titanium on a steel mill with all the steel dirt. They have to clean things off. That's why it costs more. It's more than just the rolling time, it's the cleaning time of the mill. A little titanium in the steel doesn't hurt it, so it's not a problem for the steel, but it is a problem for the titanium to get a bunch of iron in there that you didn't want, or iron oxide. There's lots of processing things that go into all these things, and market size.

`§5.p3` **Student:** [question about melting temperatures]

`§5.p4` Titanium melts at like 1700 C, steel at about fifteen or sixteen hundred C — roughly a hundred degrees C apart — and both have a transformation temperature around 900 C. So all your processing equipment, your controls, your furnaces, they're all the same. What happens in the furnace is — they're just air furnaces in a steel mill, and you form a little oxide skin on the steel. It's a scale. You roll it, it just falls off. Or you take a sandblaster and blast it off. Or you put it in a hydrochloric acid bath and pickle it off — we call it pickling, when you dissolve it off. Titanium — you get a little oxide scale, the diffusion layer might be a quarter of an inch — it might be a millimeter thick. You're gonna go in there and machine that off the titanium because it's what they call the alpha case. Oxygen is an alpha stabilizer, and your surface could be ten percent oxygen, almost going back to titanium dioxide. Just machine it off. When they make titanium castings they put them in a hydrofluoric acid bath and just dissolve the surface, because you can't machine a complex surface so readily. That just ups the cost again. You're talking about material that might sell for $100 a pound, and your yield — the scrap is greater because you've got to machine off the surfaces.

`§5.p5` But there are some things steel can't do, like fly light. When you need to build an SR-71 Blackbird, titanium skin — can't do it with aluminum, can't do it with steel, too heavy to fly. Aluminum basically over-ages at that surface temperature, doesn't quite melt but almost, because of the melting temperature of aluminum. Material choice — titanium. You could make it out of beryllium except you can't afford it. The price of beryllium is another factor of 10 to 50 above the price of titanium.

## §6. The Alvin, the Glomar Explorer, and titanium submersibles [23:31]

`§6.p1` The reason we never built a titanium submarine — well, we did build titanium submarines. The Alvin, the nice little Alvin submarine, the one that goes down and finds the Titanic and such. Originally built out of steel in the 1960s and 1970s. Who owned the Alvin originally? Actually, who still owns the Alvin? Originally it was owned by the Office of Naval Research. The US Navy built it for deep-sea work, and they were interested in deep-sea work because in the early sixties an American plane not too far from Spain dropped a nuclear warhead in the ocean by accident. It wasn't open — soon as a big oops, and the Soviets, all their ships, converged in that area of the Atlantic. Fortunately we found it before the Soviets found it, so we got our weapon back. There have been times the Soviets lost some things in the ocean, and we actually got some of those things back.

`§6.p2` If you ever read about Howard Hughes and the Glomar Explorer — the CIA built a full-size ship that was sort of a plant. It just kind of went out to that part of the Pacific, and it had all this high technology stuff, and it lifted a Soviet submarine off the floor. It was a spy ship, and it was supposed to look like a freighter. There are books written about the Glomar Explorer. It was a multibillion-dollar CIA project to get Soviet warheads. We got our warhead back from off the coast of Spain, but it was a big problem.

`§6.p3` So they started working on how to get to the bottom of the ocean. They built the Alvin out of steel in the 60s. The US Navy turned it into a titanium hull in the 70s, and that titanium hull kept on going until about the last five or ten years. I was on a study commissioned of what it would cost to build a new titanium hull and submarine. That was in the 90s, and we determined in the 90s you could probably still afford it, but you didn't have anybody who could build it anymore. In the 1970s we had this big military-industrial complex, they could make these things. By the 1990s a lot of those companies were out of business — that's another story. In any case, they finally decided they could build it, and then they found the price had jumped by a factor of five or six. If you remember, about ten years ago the Chinese started buying up all kinds of metals, and the price of everything jumped. We were supposed to try to build the new Alvin for — NSF had twenty-five million dollars in their budget. Woods Hole, who was pushing this because they maintained the Alvin, said we can build a new one for six million dollars. I said, bull, it'll cost you fifteen. Well, I was closer to the actual cost. The one they're building right now, I don't even know if they finished it — it's 50 million, to build a new little six-foot or seven-foot diameter titanium sub.

`§6.p4` In the meantime, in the 1980s the US Navy built the Sea Cliff, a little bit bigger titanium mini-sub, supposed to be like eight feet in diameter. They tried their gas metal arc welding technology that they'd spent millions of dollars on. They were building it at Mare Island Naval Shipyard. The Navy wanted to develop their own technology. They tried, and they finally gave up and went back to gas tungsten arc — not deep TIG gas tungsten arc, but just gas tungsten arc. Super slow gas tungsten arc: you can lay down two pounds an hour of weld metal. And you've got to really shield it.

## §7. Shielding, color, and dew point [27:35]

`§7.p1` One of the problems with titanium — it's not that it hates hydrogen, you can actually tolerate a fair amount of hydrogen. This is dew point of the argon shielding gas versus hydrogen content. Here's 60 parts per million. If your dew point gets very high, all of a sudden your hydrogen goes up, your hardness goes up, your minimum bend radius — you lose mechanical properties. It doesn't crack, but you lose mechanical properties. You've got to do a lot of things to shield the titanium when you weld it.

`§7.p2` In Gurevich's book, you'll see one of the things the Soviets used to do — they used to put the welders in spacesuits and put them in an argon-filled room so the titanium was welded in an argon atmosphere. That's sort of an extreme. If you look in the welding handbook today, they'll show you glove bags. Here's a welder, and the glove bag is filled with argon. In 1969 when I worked at the Naval Air Rework Facility in Norfolk, Virginia, we were repairing titanium compressor cases for aircraft. This is what we'd use. We'd put the whole diffuser case inside one of these bags, fill it with high-purity argon, and the guy would go in there and weld through the gloves. He'd be on the outside welding through the gloves. This is standard technology — it was in 1969.

`§7.p3` You might put a plate across here, you have a diffuser, you bring in backing gas — they call it backing gas. You bring in your high-purity argon, you diffuse it in here, it floods the area. You might end up using ten times as much shielding gas when welding titanium. You can look at the color of the titanium when you're all done. You can say, oh, this is nice and shiny like the ones I passed around — that's a good weld, it's silver-color titanium. Here's a straw color. There's different colors. It gets worse and worse — this is more and more air contamination.

`§7.p4` Here's another thing I found off the web, or maybe this one's out of the welding journal. That's supposed to be a good weld, and it gets progressively worse. You see, it's just oxidizing all the way over. The problem is it kind of goes through darker, darker, cleaner, but dirtier further out. Right in here you've gotten so dirty that the colors you're getting are because of the thickness of the oxide on the surface. Once your oxide gets very thick, you get these interference colors, and it just goes back to its normal gray color. People have tried for years — you look at the color and you find out whether the quality of the weld is acceptable, and they go through all these colors and tell you what's unacceptable. White is when you've gone back to titanium dioxide. Gray, unacceptable. What's the difference between gray and silver? Well, you know what it is. But when you actually look at a piece of titanium it's not so clear. The US Navy had determined you can't use color to determine quality, because all it takes is someone like me that's colorblind, and they're all good, or they're all bad — who knows what they are.

## §9. Draper Lab titanium submarines and federal consulting rates [33:29]

`§9.p2` The rest of the story is when they came to me, this was a rapid-prototyping thing in the early 90s, and rapid prototyping was a big deal in the US Navy then.
