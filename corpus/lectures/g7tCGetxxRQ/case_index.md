# Case Index — Lecture 28

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p4`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is a sustained treatment of titanium welding, framed by the 1980 Soviet exchange. Many of its threads connect to recurring corpus cases (the Alpha sub, the Alvin, GE turbine work, Watertown Arsenal). Several sub-anecdotes — Medovar, Mariinsky, Flemings's name-confusion, the Glomar Explorer, the Draper consulting dispute — are either historical color or come up only here.

## Cases referenced

### 1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`, `§1.p2`, `§1.p3`, `§1.p4`, `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The framing case for the whole lecture. Tom's two-hour interview with Gurevich at the Soviet welding institute, conducted in front of Mariinsky the KGB minder, established that the Soviets had abandoned gas metal arc welding for titanium and were using electroslag and semi-submerged GTA — technology the US Navy was still chasing.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium, electroslag welding, gas tungsten arc, gas metal arc
- **Era:** 1980 (Tom's visit); 1960s–1970s (Gurevich's work)

### Soviet Alpha-class submarine
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p4`, `§3.p5`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet Alpha-class submarine"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Alpha sub revelation on the *International Herald Tribune* front page in 1980 is the moment Tom realized why Gurevich had been publishing on electroslag — the Soviets had built a titanium-hulled submarine. Two years later the Alpha subs were mothballed: noisy, fatigue-cracked.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium submarine hull, electroslag welds
- **Era:** 1980 (revelation); early 1980s (Soviet mothballing)

### Soviet titanium electroslag welding technology
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p4`, `§2.p5`, `§3.p1`, `§3.p4`, `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet titanium electro-slag welding technology"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Two-pass treatment. First Tom recounts realizing in 1980 that Gurevich's group had used electroslag for the Alpha sub hull. Then he describes returning to MIT and making "probably the first electroslag titanium weld outside of the Soviet Union or France." The Navy classified the result.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium, calcium fluoride flux, water-cooled copper dam
- **Era:** late 1960s–early 1970s (Soviet); 1980 (MIT replication)

### Titanium electroslag weld at Oregon Graduate Center
- **Anchor:** `§3.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Titanium electroslag weld at Oregon Graduate Center"
- **Frame in this lecture:** OGC accepted classified contracts that MIT couldn't, so after Tom's electroslag work was classified, OGC became the venue for continued development.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium electroslag welds
- **Era:** 1980s

### U.S. Navy heavy-section titanium welding (1980s)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`, `§3.p6`, `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy heavy-section titanium welding (1980s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Navy spent millions on gas metal arc welding of titanium that the Soviets had already determined didn't work. After Tom's electroslag and semi-submerged GTA findings, the Navy classified the research. The Sea Cliff submersible (§6.p4) was the Navy's own attempt — they ultimately gave up on GMAW and went back to slow GTA.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium, GTAW, GMAW, electroslag
- **Era:** 1970s–1980s

### Army helicopter rotor casting weld repair (early 1980s)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Army helicopter rotor casting weld repair (early 1980s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student "Gordon" worked at Watertown Arsenal on whether weld repairs of titanium helicopter rotor castings would be acceptable. Result: welds were better than the base metal because of faster cooling and finer Widmanstätten structure. Used to make the point that in titanium, welds can outperform base material in fatigue strength.
- **Materials/systems:** Ti-6Al-4V castings, weld repair
- **Era:** early 1980s

### Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Full historical arc — Alvin built in steel in the 1960s by the Office of Naval Research; converted to titanium hull in the 1970s; ran until roughly 2005-2010; replacement build came in at $50M against Woods Hole's $6M estimate (Tom predicted $15M, the closest of the three). Sea Cliff treated as a parallel Navy effort that failed at GMAW and reverted to slow GTAW.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium submersible hull
- **Era:** 1960s–2010s

### Atomic bomb recovery off Spain
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Atomic bomb recovery off Spain"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Briefly cited to explain why the Office of Naval Research wanted deep-sea capability in the early 1960s — an American plane dropped a nuclear warhead in the Atlantic near Spain, and the recovery race against the Soviets drove the Alvin program.
- **Era:** early 1960s

### Glomar Explorer (Soviet submarine recovery)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Glomar Explorer (Soviet submarine recovery)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Howard Hughes's cover for the CIA Glomar Explorer — a multibillion-dollar disguised freighter that lifted a Soviet submarine off the Pacific floor — invoked as the most dramatic example of US deep-sea retrieval capability built up in the same period as the Alvin work.
- **Era:** 1970s

### SR-71 Blackbird
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "SR-71 Blackbird"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The exemplar of when only titanium will do: aluminum over-ages at the skin temperatures, steel is too heavy, beryllium too expensive. Used to close the market-size argument by showing why a tiny-market material gets built at all.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium skin
- **Era:** 1960s

### Naval Air Rework Facility titanium engine case repair
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval Air Rework Facility titanium engine case repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 1969 work at NARF Norfolk repairing titanium compressor diffuser cases inside argon-filled glove bags — cited as the origin of his familiarity with titanium shielding practice.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium diffuser case, glove-bag argon shielding
- **Era:** 1969

### Draper Autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) prototype welding
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`, `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Draper Autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) prototype welding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's early-1990s rapid-prototyping consultancy for Draper Lab on two 4-foot-diameter, 20-foot-long titanium submarines (mission classified). Material problem solved by using aerospace jet-engine-grade titanium tubing from Pratt & Whitney. The case then pivots into the consulting-rate dispute, which becomes the second half of §9.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium, GTAW
- **Era:** early 1990s
