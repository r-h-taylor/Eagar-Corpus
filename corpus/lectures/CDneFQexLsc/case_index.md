# Case Index — Lecture 1 (CDneFQexLsc)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§6.p7`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

Lecture 1 is a course-introduction and externalities-framing lecture. It surfaces several cases in passing — Pennzoil is the only one Tom develops as a teaching unit; the rest are illustrative one-paragraph mentions.

## Cases referenced

### Pennzoil Oil City tank explosion and facility
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`, `§6.p7`, `§6.p8`, `§6.p9`, `§6.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pennzoil Oil City tank explosion and facility"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecture's central externality example. A woman welder killed by a 1920s riveted-tank explosion at a Pennzoil refinery in Oil City, PA (downstream of Titusville). Tom's argument: Pennzoil couldn't afford to *shut down* the small money-losing refinery because environmental cleanup liability for ninety years of soil oil contamination would have cost more than continued operation. Frames the regulatory/environmental externality concept.
- **Materials/systems:** 1920s riveted steel storage tank, corroded base; oil-contaminated tank-farm gravel.
- **Era:** Refinery built ~1900; tank ~1920s; incident ~early 1990s.
- **Related clusters in corpus:** "Tank explosion at Pennzoil Oil City refinery," "Pennzoil tank farm contamination," "Pennzoil plant environmental regulation case," "Oil City refinery storage tank explosion," "Oil City refinery explosion," "Pennzoil Oil City tank explosion and facility," "Oil City river oil transport and pollution" — these may be the same underlying incident clustered under different names in aggregate_v2.

### 1972 Environmental Protection Act / Pittsburgh air quality
- **Anchor:** `§8.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pittsburgh air pollution and steel mills"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's recollection that as a child, Pittsburghers couldn't wear a white shirt outside for a full day because of steel mill emissions. Cited as the political origin of the 1972 EPA legislation.
- **Era:** 1950s observations; 1972 legislation.

### TATA Steel / J. J. Irani (India iron ore externality)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`, `§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: TATA Steel J.J. Irani India iron ore externality (World Economic Forum at MIT, early 1990s)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Irani at the MIT World Economic Forum panel argues India makes steel in environmentally unfriendly ways because (a) iron ore is cheap (~$5/ton), (b) India needs foreign currency, (c) the technology to make it clean has to be paid for. Used by Tom to illustrate Maslow's-hierarchy-of-needs framing of why poor countries pollute.
- **Era:** Early 1990s.

### Soviet Siberian oil pipeline leaks
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet oil pipelines in Siberia"
- **Frame in this lecture:** At the same MIT World Economic Forum panel, the Russian environment minister (probably Viktor Danilov-Danilyan, identification uncertain) explains that interest in fixing oil-spilling Siberian pipelines collapsed after glasnost because by ~1991 Russians were just trying to eat. Same Maslow-hierarchy point as the Irani case.
- **Era:** Early 1990s.

### Mesabi Range iron ore depletion
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`, `§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Mesabi Range iron ore depletion" (or possibly the duplicate "Massabi Range iron ore depletion" / "U.S. iron ore depletion—Mesabi Range")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as comparison to India's cheap ore — the US once had crushed-stone-grade ore in Minnesota that could go straight into the blast furnace, but it was depleted in WWII.

### China rare earth embargo on Japan
- **Anchor:** `§8.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "China rare earth embargo on Japan" (also: "2010 China rare earth embargo on Japan")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Closing exemplar for the political externality category. China's territorial dispute with Japan over the Senkaku/Diaoyu islands led to a rare-earth supply cutoff that threatened the billion-dollar Hitachi-and-others rare-earth-magnet industry overnight.
- **Materials/systems:** Neodymium-iron-boron magnets; rare-earth supply chain.
- **Era:** ~2010 (Tom says "five, six, seven years ago" in 2014).

### Neodymium-iron-boron magnet development (GM Research) and automotive starter motors
- **Anchor:** `§8.p10`, `§8.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Neodymium-iron-boron magnet (GM Research) and starter motor miniaturization
- **Frame in this lecture:** GM Research invented NdFeB magnets (~40,000 gauss vs. 10,000 for ceramic, 7,000 for Alnico). The energy-squared scaling let starter motors shrink from football-sized (15–20 lb of copper) to fist-sized.
- **Note:** Related to canonical "Sony Walkman and neodymium-iron-boron magnets" and "GE samarium cobalt magnet development" — distinct development but same physical class. Proposed as its own cluster since the GM-Research-invention claim and starter-motor-miniaturization framing are specific.

### Blood diamonds (Angola civil war)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Blood diamonds civil war"
- **Frame in this lecture:** One-sentence example of political externality. Angolan civil war separatists controlling diamond mines.

### Rhodesian chrome embargo
- **Anchor:** `§8.p14`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Rhodesian chrome embargo" (also: "Rhodesian chromite embargo and laundering")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Forty-year-old example of political externality. Rhodesian chrome ore is the world's best; embargo on it could be defeated by laundering through other countries, but quality was distinctive enough that buyers knew the source.

### General Motors carpet adhesive failure (waterjet contamination)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p7`, `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Motors carpet adhesive failure in assembly plant" (or possibly "Carpeting adhesion failure at unspecified facility")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Story about the GM Ohio shop steward / philosophy major who took Tom's video course and diagnosed a hundred-thousand-dollar-a-year carpet-bonding failure as waterjet-cutter mist contaminating the bonding surface ten feet away. Used by Tom to argue that fundamental principles ("cleanliness is next to godliness") beat consulting expertise.
- **Era:** Early 1990s.

### World Trade Center collapse paper
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "World Trade Center collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Not a forensic case as discussed here — rather, the paper itself ("Why the World Trade Center Collapsed," Eagar & Musso) as an example of writing-for-non-specialists. Tom contrasts the three hours he spent on it (and its outsized citation impact) with the hundreds of obscure peer-reviewed papers he wrote that nobody read. The paper's substantive content (the steel-melting misconception) is mentioned but not developed in this lecture.
