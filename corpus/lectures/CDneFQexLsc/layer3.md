## §2. How MIT teaches, and how Tom learned to learn [04:16]

`§2.p1` I am an MIT product, and I have some strong opinions about MIT. My opinions about teaching — and it's not just at MIT, it's just the way we teach in general in this country — we teach students to take tests. Your goal is doing tests. The problem is, you're MIT students, you wouldn't be here if you weren't very good at taking tests. Unfortunately, there are no tests in this class. So everything you've learned about classes, forget. I hated taking tests. I used to look at undergraduate courses and determine which ones I would take to see if they didn't have a three-hour final. I just couldn't stand three-hour finals.

`§2.p2` What happened to me was, in my junior year, which was forty-four years ago, I was taking introduction to quantum mechanics in the physics department, and I was doing terrible. I was getting 15 out of 100 on the homeworks where average would be 85 in the class. It was actually an elective for me. I was in Course Three, I didn't have to take quantum mechanics. But I took it, and I wasn't doing very well.

`§2.p3` I should tell you a little bit more. I came from a southern high school where they didn't even know how to spell MIT. We didn't send anybody to the Ivy League schools or places like that. It was in Virginia Beach, Virginia, and to them, making it to the University of Virginia was a big deal. They didn't know about Princeton or Harvard or Stanford.

`§2.p4` When I first came to MIT, I found out in the first week I was in the bottom third of my entering class. They had put us in Kresge Auditorium and they used to say, look to your right, look to your left, a year from now one of the three of you won't be here. Not a great way to introduce you to MIT. They said we don't say that anymore. Then they put up the SAT scores, and I was smart enough, good enough in math, I could figure out from the statistics that I was not near the top of my class.

`§2.p5` There's a serious problem you have at MIT as an undergraduate: 45% of you were valedictorians in your high school. I wasn't even the valedictorian. I was number eight out of two hundred in my high school. You're very good at taking tests, and you're used to comparing yourself with others on academics, because you would stand out. You come to MIT and on average you find your average. It's sort of a shock.

`§2.p6` So I went through that, and by junior year I was muddling along. I'd done okay — it was the first year freshman pass-fail, so I got passes freshman year. I take this quantum mechanics course, I'm doing terrible. The night before the three-hour final, I decided I'll just take the textbook and study the highlights. This was a transformation for me in my undergraduate and graduate career, and to a certain extent, the way I learned to study and learn on my own. I went into that three-hour final, finished it in an hour and twenty minutes, checked it over. They wouldn't let us leave until you'd stayed there at least two hours — another story. I got an A in the course. I probably got close to 100 on that final. All of a sudden I realized all this fluff they teach you, it's all fluff.

`§2.p7` Typically in a lecture there are one or two themes, maybe three, that a professor can get across in an hour. Beyond that, all this other stuff is part of the firehose, and you don't have to learn it, particularly if you're just trying to take a quiz. So from then on out, I quit taking notes in class. I see you're ready to take notes — you don't have to take any notes. It's not going to be quizzes. For the rest of my undergraduate and graduate days at MIT, I never took notes. I quit studying. It was just like high school and elementary school where I never took books home. I did my homework, but I used to try to sit there and listen to the professor and figure out what his two or three themes were. I used to call it "guess my outline." Most people actually do have some idea of what they're going to talk about. So I would try to figure out — what are the two or three themes in the lecture? At that point I did pretty well with grades, because it was a big transformation to realize that most of this other stuff was fluff.

`§2.p8` So then I became a faculty member, and in the early 80s I was teaching all undergraduates, sophomores in thermo. That's when I really started to learn — well, the students don't really care that much about learning the material, they just want to pass the quiz. I won't tell you all the stories about how I learned that. I thought, this is terrible, they should be interested in learning things. Then I realized it's partly because of the way we structure the courses. You've got to pass the quiz and you're competitive with everybody else.

`§2.p9` I continued to teach along that line through the 1980s, the traditional way. But I wasn't very happy about it. In the 90s I became an administrator, department head. I still taught because I enjoyed teaching, but I was the only department head in the School of Engineering who actually taught a course. Most department heads say, oh I don't have to teach anymore, and I used to think, well why are you here on the faculty if you don't want to teach? A lot of faculty have that opinion. They're bothered by having to teach, and part of it is because they're doing it in the drudgery way — well, I've got to prepare the students to take the quiz.

`§2.p10` When I stepped down as department head fourteen years ago, I decided, I'm now free. I don't have to do administrative work, I don't have to do anything I don't want to do for the rest of my career. So I started teaching this course for fun. Then Professor Shu came to me four or five years ago — it had been a welding course — he said, well Tom, we want you to talk about something broader than that. I said fine. So here's the syllabus of what we're going to teach this semester.

## §6. Externalities: the Pennzoil refinery [30:40]

`§6.p2` We're going to talk about structural materials here, and today I'm going to talk about externalities. Anybody know what externalities are? Anybody had an economics course? `§6.p4` There's an article here that I'll pass out. You have some homework reading if you want to do it. Hopefully they're going to be fun things to read. `§6.p5` This is an address he gave — it was published in fall of 1994. `§6.p6` I'm going to give you a sort of homework assignment — you don't have to turn it in. One time, in the early 90s, I was asked to go look at an explosion that occurred and killed a woman welder, threw her across the river when this tank blew up that she was welding on, at an oil refinery in Oil City, Pennsylvania. `§6.p7` This refinery had been built about 1900. I go there, and it was an old riveted steel tank from the 1920s that had blown up, and it was corroded around the bottom. `§6.p9` Since then it has been shut down. Sort of an extra tax on having polluted in the past. The rules for pollution have changed over the years.

`§6.p10` Do you know how they used to get the oil from Titusville down to Oil City to the refinery? They just floated it on the river. Just pour it down the river, they had a weir at the other end, skim it off the water. Not a lot of fish back then. Now if you did that today, what would happen? Bang, you're going to jail. So the rules have changed.
