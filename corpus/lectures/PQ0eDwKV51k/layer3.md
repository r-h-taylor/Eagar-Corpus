## §3. Why steel: melting temperatures and the blast furnace [05:08]

`§3.p1` I want to talk primarily about steel today, which gets back to the melting of metals. Putting a little science in this course: the adiabatic flame temperature in air of a hydrocarbon, or a carbon like coal or coke or charcoal, is about a thousand degrees C, 1800 degrees F. In pure oxygen it might be 3,000 degrees Fahrenheit, but in fact we've got all that excess nitrogen we have to heat up along with it, so we're limited to about a thousand degrees C. Copper melts at 1083, so we can just barely do some melting of copper with a little preheated air. Steel we can't melt, and we're going to talk about that. Cast iron melts at 1150, so you have to do some other things to help melt cast iron.

`§3.p2` This is another view of the Saugus Iron Works, an old picture of a blast furnace where people are using carbon reduction. At the top they're going to pour in limestone, iron ore, and charcoal. The guy will come and dump a wheelbarrow load of each one in succession, and you layer this thing up. You have a bellows over here. A lot of times it wasn't a person holding the bellows — they would have a paddle wheel or water wheel that would turn a bellows, so that you would have a blast of air. With the blast of air you can pump that temperature up a little bit, plus you're in a confined insulated space. Here's the brickwork at Saugus. They built it on a hill so they could be at different levels, and the casting floor is right down here, which is basically just a sandy floor. They cast the cast iron when they tap the furnace about once or twice a day, and it typically has to run continuously.

`§3.p3` Steel is an important material today, because it's ninety-five percent of all metal produced. Metals have high ductility and toughness, and steel doesn't have the highest strength or the highest toughness, although it's close to it, but it has the lowest cost by far. We've got superalloys that are tougher and stronger than steel, but they might cost a hundred times as much, or thirty times as much. Steel is the low cost, and that's why ninety-five percent of all metal produced is steel. It can be made everywhere. Iron is the fourth or fifth or sixth most abundant element in the Earth's crust, so there's iron ore all over the world. There's limestone all over the world, because that's just left over from the oceans. And there are trees to make charcoal, or coal to dig out of the ground. You can't just use any coal — you've got to use high-quality low-sulfur coal, because iron sulfide melts at low temperatures.

`§3.p4` The early iron industry could not make good steel until they found a certain ore in Czechoslovakia that had high manganese in it. Of all the elements in the periodic table, the only element that will tie up the sulfur impurity in the steel is manganese. Manganese sulfide melts at a higher temperature than the melting point of iron, just barely. Otherwise you end up getting liquid iron sulfide at the grain boundaries. You try to heat it up to roll it and it's like trying to roll a Slurpee — it's got a liquid grain boundary with solid grains, it doesn't work. But steel can be made, and is made, in virtually any part of the world.

`§3.p5` It's essential to industrial economy. Countries decide they have to have their own independent steelmaking capacity — they can't afford to just keep buying steel from somebody else. And they all want to sell it to somebody else, and of course the richest single market is the United States, so they all want to sell it in the United States. Generally we've allowed people, if they can compete, to do that, and we'll talk about how we fight back on some of that.

`§3.p6` If you're going to build bridges and buildings other than concrete and stone, you're going to have to have steel. To build modern skyscrapers — people attribute the fact that Bethlehem Steel, or Charles Schwab — he was running US Steel for Andrew Carnegie, had a fight with him, went off and took this little iron foundry in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania and created Bethlehem Steel, and became the second-largest steel company in the world after US Steel. Charles Schwab and Bethlehem Steel learned how to roll I-beams, and people point out that's when New York City was allowed to grow up. Before that the tallest building was probably ten stories tall. Some people say it wasn't just that, it was also Otis with his elevator, because not everybody wanted to climb up more than ten flights and back down to go to lunch. So between elevators and I-beams, the big-city skyscrapers came into being.

## §4. Steel as the most technologically advanced material [10:58]

`§4.p1` I've made this point a number of times, probably never in front of some faculty around here who were in the silicon business, but I would maintain that steel is the most technologically advanced material. There's a certain fraction of the industry that is devoted to research and development, and today in the United States the steel companies don't do much R&D, but in the past they did. The Taj Mahal research lab in the mid-1960s was Bethlehem Steel's Homer research lab. That's where I went to work. It was a 600-million-dollar research lab — it'd cost you three or four billion dollars to build today — and it was just a fantastic facility, because the steel companies were the big fat cats in industry at the time.

`§4.p2` We know more scientifically about steel than we do about silicon or any other material, and I'd be happy to debate anybody on that. It's one of the easiest materials to fabricate — easy to form, easy to weld, easy to machine, easy to repair in part because it's easy to weld. Iron is a very malleable, very easily worked material. It has this wonderful property — I did a junior paper in my humanities class on the history of the hardening of steel — you can form it in the soft shape, it has a crystal-structure transformation with temperature, and that allows you to heat it up and quench it down and harden it to five to ten times its original hardness. It can be brittle, you have to learn to temper it, but that ability to form tools in the soft condition and then harden them so they don't wear out is a fantastic property.

`§4.p3` Some people say that was the second innovation of Bethlehem Steel. They came up with hot-work tool steels using molybdenum and molybdenum carbides, and that allowed a lot of the growth of the automotive industry and other things when they came up with these tool steels in the 1890s and 1910s.

`§4.p4` The Achilles heel of steel: it corrodes. Now, corrosion engineers don't think I said Achilles heel, they think it's job satisfaction. There are estimates that out of a 15- or 16-trillion-dollar economy in the United States, corrosion costs us five hundred billion dollars, so two or three percent of the GDP is lost to corrosion each year. So it's great — you've got to keep making steel and replacing that stuff that corrodes away. Hydrogen damage can cause steel to fracture — we understand it, but we still let it happen all the time. I put a number of children through college on hydrogen and steel, okay. And it has bad low-temperature toughness, we've talked about that. Stop me if you have a question.

## §5. Wrought iron, cast iron, and the iron-carbon phase diagram [14:27]

`§5.p1` The history of steelmaking is that, because we couldn't melt it until Henry Bessemer came along and taught us how to melt steel at 1500 degrees centigrade, we actually made two types of steel, called wrought iron and cast iron. If you heated the iron ore up to below 700 degrees centigrade with charcoal, the charcoal would form carbon monoxide, and the carbon monoxide would react with the iron oxide — or the carbon would react to form carbon monoxide and iron — and you would end up with this solid sponge. It was just like a steel sponge: it had bits of charcoal, bits of limestone, and a blacksmith would take that chunk and forge it. He'd put it in the heat, heat it up to a red heat, and he'd hammer it and weld that sponge together. As he did, the charcoal would burn or oxidize away, or flake off if it was a big chunk, and the limestone would come out. You'd end up with something that had quite a few limestone inclusions, but you'd end up with a wrought iron that was bendable, that was ductile — the same type of wrought iron we know today.

`§5.p2` Cast iron: if they heated it up above 750 degrees centigrade, you'd end up with two or three percent carbon in the steel, and you could melt it in a blast furnace. With the confined space and blast of air — that's why it's called a blast furnace — you could melt it and cast it, but it was too brittle to mechanically work it to shape. So you had wrought iron and cast iron. This is all explained in terms of the iron-carbon phase diagram. Have people seen this before, an iron phase diagram? Do you know a phase diagram? Okay — that's a lot easier when there are only three students in class.

`§5.p3` Here's temperature versus composition. You have this face-centered cubic phase that you have to heat it up to if you're going to harden it, and down here you have the body-centered cubic phase, and this is the wrought iron below 700. This is the eutectoid temperature between body-centered cubic and face-centered cubic. If you're only heating this thing up to 700 or below, you're going to form wrought iron, which is very low in carbon. That's the equilibrium, so you get very low carbon, almost pure iron — easy to work, can't be hardened too much, but relatively easy to work. If you heat it up to higher temperatures, all of a sudden you get to cast iron. You can form a liquid at this composition with over four percent carbon, and that's cast iron — easy to cast and make things out of, but somewhat brittle. You can make pots or lumps of whatever, depending on your casting furnace.

`§5.p4` Going back to this: this is coming out of the blast furnace, and this is the cast iron. You can see they would just dig a trough in the sand. This is more modern — an old facility doing more modern stuff — but they dig a trough, and then off that they would have other things, and these little castings, just dug troughs in sand, are called pig iron. Do you know why it's called pig iron? Because these larger pieces are called sow bars. And what's a sow? A female pig. And these are the little piglets coming off the sow. That's no kidding, that's where it came from, folks. The big sow bars were heavy, hard to work, because it needed multiple people — you didn't have cranes and stuff — and they would forge those into cannons. The little pig iron they would make into other cast iron things, and then re-melt the pig iron and make little utensils and cups and bowls and porches and things.

## §6. Bessemer, Carnegie, and the integrated mill [19:09]

`§6.p1` Henry Bessemer comes along around 1856, and then we entered the Iron Age. This is actually Bessemer's first converter. It took him about ten years to really perfect the process. There's a door down here so you can see — this is several stories tall, it's fairly large. And there's little Henry. We actually owe a lot to Henry. What people did before Henry to make wrought iron, they would take cast iron and basically puddle it — they'd have a shallow trough of molten cast iron and blow air through it to burn off the carbon, so they could make wrought iron. But they were making it a hundred pounds at a time. Henry comes along and shows them how to build a converter that will have 10 or 20 or 30 or 50 tons of cast iron in it, and he blows oxygen in, and he gets essentially puddling on a larger scale — tons of steel rather than hundreds of pounds.

`§6.p2` Then a guy came along to become the richest man in the world ever — well maybe not ever, there might be some people in ancient times, it's kind of hard to convert — but Andrew Carnegie came along. If you read Andrew Carnegie's life story, some people find he was a very ethical person, and other people say he pulled some shady deals to get where he got. You can say the same thing about Bill Gates or Warren Buffett or whatever when you get that wealthy. But he was worth about a hundred billion dollars in today's dollars. He sold the steel company, US Steel, to JP Morgan for 480 million. Doesn't sound like much today, but back in those days a day's salary could be ten cents.

`§6.p3` Here's Andrew. If you look on the web you'll find all kinds of quotes from him. There's one I like, where he realized that the people were more important than the factories. He treated people well — he treated them better than the slaves that other people were treated as. And I'm not talking about blacks in slavery in the South; there was indentured servitude in the North, which is just a different name for slavery in the North. They didn't own them, they just possessed them — what's the difference? He had these beautiful steel plants that would pollute all the atmosphere and everything else, but people did earn what was a living wage, particularly for immigrants at the time — a lot of people from Czechoslovakia.

`§6.p4` An integrated steel mill, what we call it today — the last one ever to be built by a private company was Burns Harbor in Indiana, started in 1964 by Bethlehem Steel Corporation. It cost them about 5 billion dollars. They were the second-largest steel company in the world, and it almost bankrupted them. I went to work for them in 1974. It turns out 1973 was the most profitable year they ever had, and I try to say I wasn't the one who made them go down — it wasn't just me. `§6.p5` Most steel mills last for a hundred years. At the time, you almost had to build two blast furnaces, because when you do a blast-furnace reline — which means you shut it down for about three months — you can't stop the whole plant and not have any hot metal for three months. You need two blast furnaces in order to shut one down and keep the other running. You have a real problem if your one non-scheduled shutdown happens to coincide with an unscheduled shutdown of the other. Typically one blast furnace will turn out about 10,000 tons of cast iron a day, and that works out to about 5 million tons a year. ## §7. Open hearth, basic oxygen furnace, and the missed innovation [27:17]

`§7.p1` This is an open hearth. It kind of replaced the Bessemer converter, but basically it's just a shallow pool about one foot deep of steel, about half the size of a football field in terms of this whole thing. Here are your stoves, if you will — they call them checker blocks of bricks, or recuperators in this case, but it's the same thing. You have a switching valve so that you preheat one set of bricks and you extract the heat from the hot bricks on the other thing, and then switch it over. You have charging doors where you throw in the cast iron and the scrap. You can only use about ten or twenty percent scrap in this process — most of it has to be virgin cast iron. It takes about 24 hours. You're basically puddling the iron, bringing air over the top and burning off the carbon in the steel.

`§7.p2` US Steel built the last open hearth in the early 70s. They were about 450-ton capacity, huge. In this whole thing, you tap it by the whole thing lifts up, so you're lifting up this thousand-ton vessel that has 450 tons of steel in it. What replaced it — we're going to talk productivity here — was the basic oxygen furnace, after World War Two.

`§7.p3` Some people in Linz, Austria — remember, I told you seventy-five percent of the world's capacity was in the United States, and US Steel and Bethlehem had forty percent of the world's capacity between themselves. They thought they were fantastic managers, just the best in the world. The real reason they were so good is because they had bombed out the competition — well, they hadn't done that, someone had done it for them. Some people in Austria wanted to find a way to get into the steel business, but they didn't have the equivalent of 20 billion dollars in today's dollars. Frankly, 20 billion today is sort of a magic number — that's what it takes for a huge corporation to be able to invest without some help. Bethlehem Steel almost went bankrupt, and they were the second largest steel company in the world. If Boeing wants to design a new aircraft, we're talking 20 billion dollars, and when Boeing designs a 787 or 777, it's a bet-the-company proposition. Intel, if they want to build a new fab, is twenty billion dollars. People were projecting fifteen years ago that a new fab was going to cost 30 or 40 billion dollars, and no company in the world, not even Intel, could have afforded it. They were talking about how anybody could build the next-generation fab. Well, people innovated and came up with more efficient ways to do it, and that's essentially what happened in the steel industry.

`§7.p4` The Austrians came up with the basic oxygen furnace. The process for liquefying air had been developed in Germany back in the World War One era, and they decided that rather than burning the carbon out by puddling the steel with air, they'd use pure oxygen. They blew oxygen in — this is a water-cooled copper trough, the same kind of size, about five or six stories tall. You only put a little bit of steel in the bottom, because when you blow this liquid oxygen in, as it's coming out and expanding supersonically, you form a froth, and you get lots of surface area with all these oxygen bubbles burning off the carbon, forming CO. You have a huge flame shooting out — carbon monoxide just burning off in the air. You throw some flux and other stuff in, and you can make 300 tons of steel in 20 minutes. It doesn't take you 24 hours to make 450 tons — you can make 300 tons in 20 minutes.

`§7.p5` Guess what? This technology was proven in the mid-60s, but US Steel management — these wonderful intelligent people — decided to put in open hearths at the cost of about a billion dollars in the early 1970s. So they built billion-dollar dinosaurs, because that's what they grew up on, and they weren't willing to innovate. There's been a study done that the top 20 innovations in the steel industry were all done by someone outside the steel industry. None of the big integrated steel producers did the innovation — it was always someone like this little firm in Austria, and I'll tell you some others.

## §9. Argon oxygen decarburization: the MIT basement innovation [41:04]

`§9.p1` One technology that was innovated — the seeds of it were right in the basement of Building 8 here. It's called argon oxygen decarburization, or AOD. The professor who worked on it was John Chipman [Chapman]. He had a student named William Krivsky. Krivsky got his PhD blowing argon bubbles through a bath of steel to look at the carbon monoxide reaction at different partial pressures of carbon monoxide. He went to a small steel company — not a big integrated steel company in the United States — and they tried to use this to decarburize high-quality steel.
