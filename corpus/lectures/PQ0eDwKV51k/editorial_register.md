# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture 9 (PQ0eDwKV51k). This register records the editorial judgments made in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition. Mechanical rules (filler cuts, captioner-error fixes) are captured in the transformation log; this register captures decisions where an editor chose what Tom meant on the page.*

## Sectioning

The lecture divides into ten teaching units of 1.5–5 minutes each. Section breaks fall at natural pivots:
- §1 → §2: from course-roadmap framing to the first material case (shelter)
- §2 → §3: shelter pivots to "I want to talk primarily about steel today"
- §3 → §4: from melting / blast-furnace mechanics to the rhetorical claim that steel is the most advanced material
- §4 → §5: from the corrosion / Achilles-heel summary to the wrought-vs-cast history
- §5 → §6: from the phase-diagram / pig-iron explanation to the Bessemer narrative
- §6 → §7: from blast-furnace operation to the open-hearth slide
- §7 → §8: from BOF and the missed-innovation indictment to ingot/continuous casting
- §8 → §9: from continuous casting plus class-business interlude to the AOD case
- §9 → §10: from AOD to the productivity-curve summary slide

§3 is long (six paragraphs) but coheres around a single thread: why steel dominates as a structural material. Splitting it further would fragment Tom's argument.

## Per-paragraph decisions

### §1.p1 — Opening framing
- **Cut:** "Was in some class, you ready? Okay, so we had been talking about, we're now starting to talk about strength of materials, and I actually gave a little review yesterday and I'll repeat it here." This is class-management and a backward-reference to yesterday's session; cut per convention 5.
- **Cut:** trailing "and so that's just a review of yesterday. And then I told you that for the rest of this class..." — the "I told you" is dropped, the actual content (the list of materials) is kept.

### §2.p3 — Steel two-by-fours
- **Self-correction smoothed:** "well, yeah, they don't make them. The two-by-fours that go into industrial buildings..." Tom says "they don't make them" and then immediately gives a counter-example (commercial galvanized-steel studs). Layer 3 reads "well, they do" so the paragraph makes sense on the page. **This is a meaningful edit** — Tom's spoken answer was the opposite of what he then described. Reading the literal transcript would mislead. Flagged for human review.

### §3.p3 — Iron abundance
- **Smoothed:** "Iron is the fifth-most, fourth or fifth or sixth most abundant element" → "Iron is the fourth or fifth or sixth most abundant element." Tom's first-attempt "fifth-most" dropped; the corrected hedged form retained.
- **Cut:** "we'll talk about that a little bit later" — forward-reference, cut.

### §4.p4 — Corrosion cost figure
- **Bracketed correction:** "five hundred million dollars" → "five hundred billion dollars." Tom said "million" but the figure only makes sense as "billion" given his framing ("two or three percent" of a "15 or 16 trillion dollar economy"). Convention 9: a genuine spoken slip, corrected in brackets in layer 2; layer 3 reads the corrected form silently because the slip would actively mislead a reader (convention 16 exception).

### §5.p2 — Phase diagram aside
- **Cut:** "I've seen it before." Tom mutters this to himself after asking the class about phase diagrams; it doesn't function as teaching content.

### §5.p4 — Pig iron / sow bars
- **Smoothed:** "wrought iron tonight, oh I did go, okay" — a Tom self-interruption transitioning back to the cast-iron slide. Cut entirely; the slide-transition signal is not needed on the page.

### §6.p1 — Bessemer converter
- **Cut:** "blows the oxygen in" → "blows oxygen in." Bessemer actually blew **air**, not oxygen — but Tom says "oxygen" twice in this paragraph (and corrects it to "air puddling" in §7.p4). Convention 16 says preserve factual errors unless actively misleading. Here the error is recoverable from later context and from the surrounding "puddling on a larger scale" framing, so it's preserved. Flagged for human review.

### §7.p3 — Austria / BOF
- **Cut:** "We're going to talk productivity here" (forward-reference fragment from the prior paragraph; the productivity arc is set up later in §10).
- **Cut:** "Well, people innovated and they came up with more efficient ways to do it" — preserved (it closes the Intel/Boeing rhetorical excursion before returning to steel).

### §8.p2 — Casting cost stack
- **Bracketed correction:** Tom says "one basic oxygen at a billion dollars," but in context he's describing what US Steel actually built, which were open hearths. Preserved as "basic oxygen furnace [open hearth]" — convention 9 applied with the bracket because the next paragraph confirms he meant open hearths.

### §8.p4 — Burns Harbor sale
- **Bracketed correction kept from layer 2:** "ISIL" — Tom's spoken acronym for ISG / Mittal Steel, the actual purchaser of Bethlehem Steel assets. Bracket kept visible because the spoken form is wrong and unrecoverable from context.

### §8.p5 — Continuous caster
- **Smoothed:** "about 20 to 50 [200 to 300] tons" — Tom misspeaks the ladle capacity by a factor of ten. Bracketed correction kept from layer 2; layer 3 reads the corrected figure silently because the slip is unambiguous from context (a BOF heat is 300 tons, established in §7.p4).

### §8.p11 — Industry size figure
- **Bracketed but preserved:** Tom says "1.6 trillion tons times four hundred dollars a ton, it's a six trillion dollar industry." World steel production is about 1.6 **billion** tons, not trillion; the resulting figure is wrong by three orders of magnitude. Convention 16: preserve genuine misstatements unless actively misleading. Here the figure IS misleading. The unit is corrected to "billion" and the dollar figure footnoted "[actually about $640 billion]." Flagged for human review — this is the most consequential factual fix in the lecture.

### §9.p1, §9.p2 — Chipman name
- **Bracketed:** "John Chipman [Chapman]" on first occurrence per section. Convention 9. Note: the historically correct name for the MIT process metallurgist is John Chipman, and Tom may actually be saying "Chipman" correctly with the captioner garbling it to "Chapman." Pending verification; treated as Tom's slip until disambiguated. Flagged for human review.

### §9.p2 — VAR / VOD
- **Bracketed correction kept:** Tom says "vacuum arc oxygen decarburization." VOD is **vacuum oxygen decarburization** (no "arc"); VAR is vacuum arc remelting, a different process. Cleaned silently in layer 3 since Tom's intent is unambiguous (he expands the V correctly as "vacuum").

### §10.p3 — Productivity figure
- **Smoothed:** "less than point six" → "less than 0.6," matching the decimal convention.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
