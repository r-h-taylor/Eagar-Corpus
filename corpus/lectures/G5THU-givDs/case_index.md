# Case Index — Lecture 26, Welding Metallurgy Spring 2014

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p1`). Click-through reveals the layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

The lecture is built around two consulting cases — the recent (January 2014) aluminum crankcase 4043 filler metal problem, and the 1980s Johnson & Johnson anodized aluminum laser surgical instruments — that together set up the titanium material that opens at §8. The aviation industry repair structure (§1–§2) frames the first case; the patent liability lesson (§7) is the punchline of the second.

## Primary cases

### Aluminum casting aircraft engine repair consulting case
- **Anchors:** `§1.p1`–`§5.p9` (the case is the through-line of sections 1, 3, 4, and 5; section 2 is the related jet-engine repair industry context)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum casting aircraft engine repair consulting case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Recent (Jan 24, 2014) consulting question from "Chuck," chief engineer at an FAA-approved aircraft piston engine crankcase repair shop. Customer's 3/8" stud stripped at 204 inch-pounds in a 4043-filler weld repair. Tom walks through SAE/AMS specifications, Aluminum Association filler metal recommendations (4043, 4145, 4009, 4010, 4011, 4047), and the reasoning for matching vs. over-matching vs. under-matching filler metal selection.
- **Materials/systems:** AMS 4280 / aluminum 355-T71 casting, 4043 / 4145 / 4009 filler metals, helicoil and double-helicoil thread inserts.
- **Era:** 2014 (case), 1976–2014 (Chuck's company's filler metal history)

### Canadian Transportation Board filler metal composition criticism
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Canadian Transportation Board filler metal composition criticism"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Prior (fall 2013) accident investigation in Canada where the Canadian Transportation Safety Board concluded the under-matching 4043 filler metal caused a fatigue crack. Tom helped the client respond. Reused in §5 as an example of how failure analysts mistake difference for cause.
- **Materials/systems:** 4043 filler in aluminum casting alloy
- **Era:** Fall 2013

### Johnson & Johnson laser surgery instrument coating
- **Anchors:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Johnson & Johnson laser surgery instrument coating"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Early-1980s consulting case. J&J's surgical instruments division wanted laser-cutting hand tools (scissors, drills, reamers) that wouldn't reflect the laser onto unintended tissue. Tom recommended anodized aluminum (oxide layer for absorption + aluminum substrate for thermal diffusion), helped them design the experiment with 4/6/8/10-micron anodized discs, and confirmed the 6–10 micron optimum.
- **Materials/systems:** Anodized aluminum (Al₂O₃ surface, 6–10 µm), YAG laser
- **Era:** Early 1980s

### Anodized aluminum medical laser instrument design
- **Anchors:** `§6.p4`–`§6.p5`, `§7.p1`–`§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Anodized aluminum medical laser instrument design"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The patentable result of the J&J consulting case. Two patents issued with Tom's name on them; the patents form the subject of the indemnification lesson in §7.
- **Materials/systems:** Same as J&J case
- **Era:** Early 1980s (work); ~1985 (patent issuance); patent has since expired
- **Note:** This is the technical result; the J&J case is the narrative frame. Listed separately because the cluster id in the canon distinguishes them.

### Independent consultant patent liability and indemnification
