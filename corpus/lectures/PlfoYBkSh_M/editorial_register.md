# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture `PlfoYBkSh_M` (High Energy Density Beam Welding, Fusion Welding, Summer 2013). This register records the editorial judgments made in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition.*

## Provenance note (constitutional)

This recording is not a Tom Eagar lecture. It is a narrated Japanese technical film — almost certainly a JWES/Osaka University production from the early-to-mid 1980s — shown by Prof. Eagar in his Fusion Welding course. The narration is by an uncredited English-language voice-over artist describing x-ray and high-speed optical footage of EB and laser welding.

Consequences for layer 3:

- No `**Student:**` turns appear; this is a single-voice narrated film.
- No stage directions appear; there is no physical-object teaching by Tom.
- The "filler vs teaching" principle does not really apply, because the narration is a pre-written script. There are no false starts, no pocket-patting, no self-corrections. The text was already prose.
- Tom's voice is absent from the file. The bracketed-slip convention does not engage.

The editorial work at layer 3 is therefore primarily **sectioning** and **light copy-editing of captioner artifacts** that survived layer 2. The text on the page closely tracks layer 2.

## Sectioning decisions

The narration is internally chaptered — it announces its own section breaks ("Mechanism.", "Beam hole behavior during electron beam welding.", "Dynamic behavior during laser beam welding.", "Suppression of defects and tandem electron beam welding.", "Cutting."). Section breaks were placed at these announced transitions. Two additional breaks were inserted at natural topic pivots that the narration itself implies but does not label:

- **§5 (Front wall dynamics and the spiking mechanism)** begins at the pivot from descriptive observation of laser welding (§4) into the analytical model of front-wall behavior (`p20`–`p30`). The narration signals this with "This behavior will now be explained" at the end of `p19`.
- **§8 (Summary and outlook)** begins at "Now we understand what happens…" (`p47`), the narration's own recapitulation.

The resulting eight sections range from ~35 seconds (§1, three paragraphs of framing) to ~6 minutes (§5, the technical core). This is at the upper end of the recommended section count, justified by the film's distinct internal chapter structure.

## Captioner artifacts carried forward

Two captioner errors were caught and corrected in layer 2; they appear corrected in layer 3 without further annotation:

- `p38`: "argon [argon] jet" — layer 2 retained a redundant bracket. **Layer 3 silently drops the bracket**; the captioner's mishearing was already fully corrected in layer 2's main text. Logged here so the discrepancy between L2 and L3 is recorded.
- `p39`, `p44`: "kerf [curve]" — layer 2 retained the bracketed captioner reading. **Layer 3 silently drops "[curve]"**; "kerf" is the correct technical term and the captioner garbling adds nothing for the reader.

These two silent drops are the only places layer 3 diverges from layer 2's main-line text.

## No soft reordering

None performed. The narration is sequential and tightly scripted; there is no fragmentary first attempt that needs to be moved later, and no summary line that arrived too early.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)

This constraint is the project's safeguard against drift into reconstruction.
