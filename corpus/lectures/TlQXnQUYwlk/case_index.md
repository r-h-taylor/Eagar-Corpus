## Cases developed in this lecture

### ALVIN submarine pressure hull cost overrun
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`–`§3.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "ALVIN submarine pressure hull cost overrun"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's role on the 2003–04 National Academy of Sciences study estimating the cost of a new Alvin submersible. Woods Hole's $6.5M estimate; Tom's $16M revised estimate; final actual cost ~$40M. Pressure hull fabrication identified as the dominant cost risk.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium pressure hull, syntactic foam buoyancy, Ti-6211, heavy section titanium welding.
- **Era:** 2003–2009 study and procurement; references back to 1970s Alvin and Sea Cliff construction.

### Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`, `§3.p5`, `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Referenced as the prior generation of US titanium-hulled submersibles whose fabrication base no longer exists. Sea Cliff completion at Mare Island with gas tungsten arc welding cited as the cautionary precedent for the new Alvin cost estimate.
- **Materials/systems:** Two-inch titanium plate; gas metal arc (failed) and gas tungsten arc (completed) welding.
- **Era:** 1970s construction; Mare Island fabrication.

### Soviet Alpha-class submarine
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§7.p5`, `§9.p1`–`§9.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet Alpha-class submarine" (also covered by "Soviet titanium submarine (Alpha-class) creep-fatigue failures" and "Soviet Titanium Aircraft Program (Docket 3)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Alfa sub becoming public knowledge ~1980; leapfrog of US submarine technology by ~2 years; creep-fatigue cracking failures; Congressional alarm and the David Taylor conferences. Tom's eventual inference that Gurevich used electroslag welding to build the heavy-section titanium hull.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-section titanium hull; electroslag welding.
- **Era:** circa 1980 revelation; subsequent US response through mid-1980s.

### 1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p5`, `§9.p1`–`§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's trip to the Soviet Union with Professor Szekely, late in the Carter-era NSF/State Department scientific exchange that Reagan was shutting down. Visit to the Paton Institute in Kiev; two-hour interview with S.M. Gurevich; Gurevich's gift of the titanium welding book.
- **Materials/systems:** GTAW, GMAW, submerged arc, electroslag welding of titanium; high-purity calcium fluoride fluxes.
- **Era:** circa 1980, end of the Carter-era exchange.

### Submerged arc welding of titanium flux economics
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`–`§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Submerged arc welding of titanium flux economics"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first research contract as a young professor, ~1977–78. Calcium fluoride flux at $100/lb, hygroscopic and contamination-prone, with 1.5 lb of flux per lb of titanium weld metal — an order of magnitude worse than steel submerged arc economics.
- **Materials/systems:** Single-crystal calcium fluoride flux; titanium plate.
- **Era:** late 1970s research contract with the Navy via Union Carbide.

### Watertown Arsenal titanium development
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Watertown Arsenal titanium development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief historical anchor — titanium 6-4 alloy developed at Watertown Arsenal around 1945–47 as titanium first became available in larger quantities.
- **Era:** mid-to-late 1940s.

### B1 bomber program titanium surplus repurposing
- **Anchor:** `§9.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "B1 bomber program titanium surplus repurposing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Air Force cancellation of the B-1 left thousands of tons of titanium 6-4 in pre-ordered shapes. Navy convinced the Air Force to transfer the stockpile to David Taylor, which used four-inch plate to fabricate welding tables in lieu of buying new ones.
- **Era:** post-cancellation, late 1970s into 1980s use at Carderock.

### NYC titanium heat exchanger fire
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "NYC titanium heat exchanger fire"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Backward-reference to a previously-discussed case. Cited to make the point that water spray on burning titanium generates hydrogen and amplifies the fire.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium tubing, water/hydrogen reaction.

### Titanium heat exchanger corrosion failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`–`§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Titanium heat exchanger corrosion failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Under-deposit corrosion attack on titanium heat exchanger tubes where river silt accumulated; replacement of the entire heat exchanger. Cautionary case against the assumption that "titanium won't corrode."
- **Materials/systems:** Grade-2 titanium heat exchanger tubing; silt-induced crevice corrosion.
