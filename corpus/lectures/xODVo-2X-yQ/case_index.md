- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Accel trolley wire for Boston-New Haven electrified rail upgrade"

### LNG tanker tail shaft fatigue failure, Philippine Sea (1985)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "LNG carrier tail shaft fracture" (probable match) or "SS Libra LNG vessel tail shaft failure" (possible match — confirm)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Illustration of an internal forging defect originating in the as-cast structure of a great-big forging blank: solidification weakness at the centerline gets carried through forging, then propagates as a fatigue crack. Ship stopped dead with a full cargo of LNG in the Philippine Sea.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-section forged steel shaft from cast ingot; centerline defect from solidification; fatigue propagation in service.
- **Era:** 1985 (Tom dates the failure observation).
- **Flag:** which LNG-tail-shaft cluster this matches needs confirmation. Tom's account here (cast → forged shaft, centerline defect, Philippine Sea, full of LNG, fatigue) is specific enough to disambiguate against existing canon entries.

### Aluminum plate thickness variation, Alcoa Davenport rolling mill
- **Anchor:** `§10.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alcoa Davenport aluminum rolling mill thickness variation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Closing anecdote on the limits of deformation-process tolerance for thick aluminum plate (2–4 inch). Tom's LFM student "Julie" diagnosed a 5–10% thickness variation that was causing wing-plate scrap rejection; Alcoa never implemented the fix despite a one-day payback. Used to set up the larger theme: deformation processing can hold thousandth-of-an-inch tolerances, but only within geometric limits, and industrial uptake of academic findings is patchy.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy aluminum plate for aircraft wing skins (production rolling mill at Davenport, Iowa).
- **Era:** Tom's LFM student era — roughly 1990s–2000s.
- **Cross-references in canon:** also overlaps with "Davenport Iowa plate stress relief by hydraulic jacking," "Davenport Works plate cooling problem," and "Boeing aluminum plate residual stress relief (Davenport Iowa)" — these may be the same physical mill in different consulting contexts.

## Biographical sketch (not a case but indexed)

### Percy Williams Bridgman (1882–1961)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Percy Bridgeman high-pressure materials research"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended sketch — Harvard physics professor, Nobel laureate, suicide in Randolph NH 1961. Invented the tetrahedral anvil press, opened up high-pressure materials science (artificial diamond synthesis). Used as the lead-in to the central teaching point: hydrostatic stress suppresses brittle fracture, and at sufficient pressure even steel necks like plasticine. Tom anchors the rock-creep / deep-mine point in Bridgman's framework.
- **Era:** Career roughly 1905–1960.
