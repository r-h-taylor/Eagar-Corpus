### Bulletproof glass / Protective Armor Systems (PAS)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Protective Armor Systems bulletproof glass (Western Massachusetts)

- **Era:** contemporary

### 1985 RPG armor penetration demonstration
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** 1985 RPG armor penetration demonstration
- **Frame in this section:** Aberdeen Proving Ground three-tank lineup. Shaped charge penetrated all six layers of armor; armored general "tossed his cookies." Within a year ceramic armor defeated all then-current shaped charges.

### Ceramic armor development against shaped charges
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Ceramic armor development against shaped charges
- **Frame in this section:** Copper jet doesn't react with ceramic, so ceramic defeats the shaped charge. Now "all kinds of different armors," modeled on supercomputers.
- **Materials/systems:** ceramic composite armor
- **Era:** mid-1980s onward

### Depleted uranium armor penetrators
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`, `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Depleted uranium armor penetrators and Gulf War environmental contamination
- **Frame in this section:** Half-inch by 36-inch DU rod generates 300–400 ksi stresses at impact. Counter-strategy: reactive armor with explosive surface layer to multiply stress to 500 ksi and shatter the penetrator. Twin-projectile design follows the first hole.
- **Materials/systems:** depleted uranium, reactive armor
- **Era:** late 1980s

### Diamond-coated asphalt tool development
- **Anchor:** `§1.p5`, `§1.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Diamond-coated asphalt tool development
- **Frame in this section:** Tungsten carbide cutter with double-step brazed sintered-diamond tip. Lasts significantly longer than eight-hour shift, but sintered diamond cost too high to be economical. Worked technically, failed economically. Consulting evaluation for acquiring company.
- **Materials/systems:** tungsten carbide, sintered polycrystalline diamond, brazing
- **Era:** consulting case, undated

### US Navy aircraft carrier weight gain problem
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`, `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** US Navy aircraft carrier weight gain problem
- **Frame in this section:** Nimitz-class gains 250 tons/year, mostly in superstructure (stability problem). Navy evaluated HSLA 60 plate; Newport News study showed as-fabricated cost ten times raw plate cost — confirming the ten-percent rule.
- **Materials/systems:** HSLA 60 plate steel
- **Era:** ~50-year span ending recent

### Molten Metal Technologies
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`, `§8.p3`, `§8.p4`, `§8.p5`, `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Molten Metal Technologies
- **Frame in this section:** Extended cautionary tale. Chris Nagel's universal-solvent steel-bath waste disposal patent (US Steel passed). Marketed via MIT TLO (John Preston); raised $130M; founders worth $25M each. Tom and Sadoway raised conflict-of-interest concerns to VP Research (rebuffed because "MIT has a piece of this action"). Technical fatal flaws: sodium destroys refractories; chlorine produces phosgene; $30M DoE radioactive-waste contract claimed transmutation. Indicted ~3 years later. Used to illustrate (a) Sprague's-law overpromising, (b) institutional conflicts of interest, (c) "don't invest from the *Wall Street Journal*."
- **Materials/systems:** molten iron baths, refractories
- **Era:** mid-1990s

## Recurring figures referenced

- **The ten-percent rule** (`§3.p1`, `§3.p3`, `§5.p1`): material cost ≈ 10% of fabricated product cost. Tom's "general rule of thumb worked out over the years."
- **Material cost by industry tier** (`§2.p1`, `§5.p1`): ships/railroads $0.20/lb; automobiles $2/lb; aircraft; spacecraft $20,000/lb in orbit.
- **Aircraft carrier weight gain** (`§3.p2`): 250 tons/year, Nimitz-class baseline.
- **Sprague's law** (`§7.p1`): "Whenever you first hear about the properties of a new material, write it down — those are the best they will ever have." (Robert Sprague, GE Aircraft Engines.)
- **Williams's law** (`§7.p1`): "Whenever you first hear about the cost of a new material, write it down — that's the lowest it will ever have." (Jim Williams, successor to Sprague.)
- **Twenty-year new-materials lag** (`§7.p10`): time-to-market for a new material; from Tom's 1995 *Technology Review* paper.
- **One-in-twenty startup success rate** (`§7.p10`): general figure, not specific to materials.
