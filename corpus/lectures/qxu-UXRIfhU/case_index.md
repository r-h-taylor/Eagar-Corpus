# Case Index — Lecture 10 (Solid State Welding, Spring 2013)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor. Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage.*

This lecture is primarily theoretical (fluxes, wetting, surface energy) with three named industrial-historical cases at the close. Most of the lecture handles material categories (aluminum silicon carbide composites, gallium arsenide packaging, lead-tin solders) generically rather than via named cases.

## Cases referenced

### Motorola Boynton Beach pager manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`–`§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Motorola Boynton Beach pager manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Motorola's late-1980s decision to reshore pager assembly from Asia to Florida, combined with eliminating the rework area, drove process improvement and pioneered six sigma practice. Used to teach that built-in rework capacity becomes a self-fulfilling defect rate.
- **Era:** ~1988–1990
- **Materials/systems:** electronic assembly, solder joints, automated assembly line.

### Ford Mustang assembly line non-start defect
- **Anchor:** `§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford Mustang assembly line non-start defect"
- **Frame in this lecture:** New plant manager replaces tow motors with push-the-car-250-yards rule; defect rate collapses. Paired with the Motorola pager story as a second instance of incentive structure driving quality.
- **Materials/systems:** automotive assembly.

### Massachusetts lead solder ban (1978)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Massachusetts lead solder ban"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Massachusetts Commonwealth plumbing code change forced transition from lead-tin to tin-silver and then tin-antimony solders for potable water. Sets up Tom's argument that the tin-base replacements don't bridge plumbing-clearance gaps the way lead-tin did.
- **Era:** 1978 onward.
- **Related:** "Lead-tin solder ban and plumbing failures (1978)", "Lead-tin solder plumbing toxicity".

### Lead-acid car battery terminal corrosion
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`–`§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lead-acid battery terminal corrosion from road salt"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as a real-world illustration of how trace chloride catalyzes a self-regenerating corrosion cycle on lead — Tom's mechanism for why halogen-activated flux residue destroys printed circuit boards.
- **Materials/systems:** lead terminals, road salt (NaCl), CO₂/moisture chemistry.
- **Related:** "Car battery terminal corrosion (road salt environment)".

### Tom's aluminum soldering demonstration failure
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Tom Eagar's aluminum soldering demonstration failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Personal anecdote — Tom's assistant-professor-era aluminum soldering demo using tin/zinc chloride flux fell apart in his hands a year later because retained flux on blind joints was hygroscopic. Illustrates why reaction fluxes for aluminum are limited in practice.
- **Era:** late 1970s / early 1980s ("a few years ago, before you were born").

### Bismuth-tin solder steel recycling contamination
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`–`§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bismuth-tin solder replacement causing steel scrap contamination"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The $100M/year US research program on tin-bismuth as a lead-free solder replacement, which Tom argues was systems-blind because bismuth segregates to steel grain boundaries during automotive recycling and cannot be oxidized out.
- **Related:** "Tin-bismuth solder steel recycling contamination", "Steel contamination from bismuth and antimony solders".
