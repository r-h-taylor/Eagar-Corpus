# Case Index — Lecture 2, Welding Metallurgy Spring 2014

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§6.p3`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is primarily a historical-metallurgical overview rather than a case-driven session. Cases appear as brief illustrations of broader points about steel composition, cost, and history.

## Cases referenced

### 1930s all-aluminum Duesenberg automobiles
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1930s all-aluminum Duesenberg automobiles"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to make the point that aluminum cars existed long before mass-market aluminum vehicles. JP Morgan's all-aluminum Pierce Arrow is mentioned in the same breath.
- **Materials/systems:** all-aluminum automobile body
- **Era:** 1930s

### Wright brothers' aircraft engine
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Wright brothers' aircraft engine"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Aluminum-copper precipitation-hardened engine block at Kitty Hawk. Used to make the point that "nanotechnology" is older than the term — and that the engine is still in the Smithsonian.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum-copper precipitation-hardened alloy
- **Era:** ~1903

### Saugus Ironworks
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§8.p1`, `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used twice — first to anchor the historical practice of mixing charcoal with iron ore, then as the physical site Tom walks the class through (the shaft furnace, the sand-floor pig-iron casting, the bellows and water wheel). The Saugus furnace is the central physical referent of §8.
- **Era:** 1600s
- **Materials/systems:** charcoal + bog iron ore + limestone/sand flux → cast iron + slag

### 19th-century European steel phosphorus and sulfur brittleness
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`, `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "19th-century European steel phosphorus and sulfur brittleness"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Czechoslovakian steelmakers as among the best in Europe, but limited by high-phosphorus and high-sulfur iron ore. Used to introduce the role of manganese and basic refractories.
- **Materials/systems:** iron + phosphorus + sulfur, manganese sulfide remediation
- **Era:** pre-1880s through Chipman era (1940s)

### Czechoslovak high-phosphorus ore steel cracking

## Figures referenced (numeric anchors, not cases)

- **Steel productivity, 1600s vs. today** (`§6.p3`): "2,000 to 3,000 person-hours per ton in the 1600s; about twenty minutes a ton today." This is a recurring Tom figure across the corpus, paired in other lectures with the 1990 → 0.3 person-hours/ton modern-productivity statistic. Treated here as a figure rather than a case.
- **Bessemer 1856** (`§6.p3`): Tom's anchor date for "the beginning of the Iron Age."
- **Steel tonnage hierarchy** (`§2.p2`): 1.5 billion tons steel/year; 45 million tons next-most-common metal; 6 billion tons stone; 2.2 billion tons cement; 300 million tons all plastics.
- **Steel price tiers, 2014** (`§4.p2`, `§4.p3`): $400/ton carbon bar stock; $1,000/ton automotive sheet; $800–$10,000/ton low-alloy; $16,000–$40,000/ton high-alloy and high-nickel.
- **Sulfur content evolution** (`§7.p6`): 0.05% (1920s ASM spec) → 0.04% (1940s) → 0.01% (1975 US) → 0.005% (1975 Japan) → 0.002–0.001% (today).
