# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture 2, Welding Metallurgy Spring 2014. This register records the editorial decisions made in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition.*

## Sectioning

The lecture was divided into eight sections at natural teaching transitions:
- **§1** (00:02) — opens with class logistics, ends with the Sprite/hydrogen demo
- **§2** (04:55) — opens with "Does anybody have any questions?" pivot to overview; covers why steel matters
- **§3** (09:13) — pivot to "we're going to start out weldability talking about steels"; introduces the iron-carbon phase diagram
- **§4** (13:17) — Tom names the diagram explicitly and moves to taxonomy and cost

## Per-section decisions

### §1 — Logistics and the demo
- **§1.p1.** Tom's logistical opening preserved nearly intact; light filler removal ("just sort of an introduction as far as that goes" → "It was just an introduction"). "Are we on now, Nicholas?" cut from §1.p2 — this is a studio-tech check, not teaching content.
- **§1.p3.** Stage direction `*[Tom pours Sprite Zero into a beaker.]*` substituted for the "want to take some Sprite Zero and basically all I wanted" hedging. Tom's verbal search for the right framing of the demonstration is the kind of real-time speech the layer-3 convention cuts; the action of pouring is what the reader needs.
- **§1.p4.** Self-correction smoothed: "the carbon is coming out, or CO2 came out" reads cleanly as "CO2 — came out and bubbled away." Tom's mid-sentence correction ("hydrogen — or CO2") preserved in §1.p4 because it carries the analogy itself — that's the teaching point.

### §2 — Why steel
- **§2.p1.** Backward reference to last semester's article preserved, since it contains content (the article is being handed out). Tom's apology ("and I apologize to the people who took my class last semester") cut — pure throat-clearing.
- **§2.p3 / §2.p4.** Tom's F-150 anecdote spans a natural rhetorical break: the setup ("they haven't announced the price") and the bet ("anybody want to guess?"). Split into two paragraphs at the natural pause. No reordering.
- "Twenty-five years ago I said it will be twenty-five years before we see a high-volume all-aluminum vehicle" preserved as Tom's framing — this is the punch line of the anecdote.

### §3 — Phase diagram
- **§3.p2.** Stage direction `*[Tom passes around an iron-carbon phase diagram.]*` substituted for "I just happen to have somewhere here an iron-carbon phase diagram for you, so you can pass that around. But I got to figure out the exposure on this thing." The exposure remark preserved as a parenthetical because it conveys that the diagram he's showing is a slide/photograph, not the diagram itself — this matters for understanding what students see.
- **Bracketed correction: "Mike Bonforth [Bibber]"** at §4.p1. Tom appears to slip on the name of the stainless steels guest lecturer. Per convention, first occurrence in a section gets the bracketed correction. (Name flagged for editor review — confirm Mike Bibber is the intended reference; if uncertain, this should be raised as a transcription question.)

### §4 — Cost
- **§4.p2.** Self-correction smoothed: "80 cents a pound. Right, no, 20 cents a pound, sorry, it's 20 cents a pound" → "about 20 cents a pound." Tom catches his own error mid-stream; layer 3 reads the corrected figure only.
- **§4.p3.** "Now you're in your $4 a pound range per ton — $16,000, right? Or $8,000 is $4 a pound" preserved as Tom's working-it-out aloud. This is teaching, not stumbling — he's showing how to convert units. Cleaned only of "right?" and "okay" filler.
- **§4.p4.** Tom's nuclear-reactor digression preserved. The interlocutor's reply ("Billion?") preserved as a `Billion?` echo — Tom is doing call-and-response with an unnamed student. The exchange is short enough that adding a `**Student:**` label would over-formalize it. Flagged for editor review: if convention is to label even one-word student interjections, this would change.

### §5 — Why steel is attractive
- Tom's "since the dawn of creation" rhetorical flourish preserved.
- §5.p3 lands the Wright Brothers anecdote cleanly. No reordering; minor filler removed.

### §6 — Cast iron and Bessemer
- **§6.p1.** "Huh?" cut — this is Tom registering that no one answered his rhetorical question. Reads as static on the page.
- **§6.p3.** The productivity statistic ("2,000 person-hours per ton in the 1600s, twenty minutes today") preserved as a key Tom figure. This is the same figure that anchors the corpus-wide productivity theme.
- **Bessemer** date "1856" preserved as Tom's chosen anchor date (the figure for the Bessemer patent is variously given; Tom's date is on tape and stays on the page).

### §7 — Slags

- **§7.p6.** Sulfur progression preserved verbatim as Tom's chronological figure-walk. The "Are you kidding me?" preserved as the rhetorical landing of the ASM-spec critique.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3 (constitutional constraint)

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
