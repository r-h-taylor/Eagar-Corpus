## Cases / anecdotes referenced

### Wang Computer 4K laboratory minicomputer
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Wang Laboratories 4K minicomputer (early-1970s MIT lab purchase)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Opening anecdote about the cost of memory in the early 1970s — $4,000 for 4K of storage, used to set up the contrast with modern computing.
- **Era:** early 1970s

### Belmar startup: portable hardness tester (frictional sliding)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Belmar portable hardness tester startup (frictional-sliding yield-strength field instrument)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Belmar's own NSF-supported startup, used as the live case material for his portion of the course. Revenue grew from $200K (2016) to $500K (2017) to projected $1-2M (2018).
- **Materials/systems:** portable yield-strength field instrument for bridges and pipelines
- **Era:** 2015–present

### SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research) program
- **Anchor:** `§4.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: SBIR small-business research mandate (10% federal R&D set-aside, ~1990–present)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom frames SBIR as the institutional backdrop for Belmar's NSF-supported startup. Notes Congressional finding that new jobs come from small companies, hence the 10% set-aside.
- **Era:** late 1980s–present

### West Point and the Corps of Engineers

- **Frame in this lecture:** Until 1845, the commandant of West Point had to come from the Corps of Engineers.

### Rensselaer Polytechnic and the Erie Canal
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Rensselaer Polytechnic (1823) — coining of "civil engineering" to distinguish from military, driven by Erie Canal construction
- **Frame in this lecture:** Origin of the term "civil engineering" and why Course One at MIT carries that designation.

### MIT founding and Course numbering origins
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: MIT founding (1861/1863/1865) and Course One/Two/Three numbering origin
- **Frame in this lecture:** Morrill Act, land-grant status, doors opening 1865. Course One = Civil (only non-military engineering known), Course Two = Mechanical (William Barton Rogers's "mechanical arts"), Course Three = geology and mining (Metallurgy added 1883).

### Harvard-MIT merger attempts and Gordon McKay trust litigation
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Harvard attempts to acquire MIT (1873–1917) and Gordon McKay trust Supreme Court ruling
- **Frame in this lecture:** Three Harvard attempts; 1914–1917 students received dual degrees; Massachusetts Supreme Court blocked the merger ruling McKay trust funds could not be used to purchase MIT.

### George Eastman anonymous donation to MIT
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: George Eastman anonymous donation rescuing MIT from post-1917 bankruptcy (Building Six dedication)
- **Frame in this lecture:** MIT bankrupt after building this building (1917); rescued by Eastman's anonymous gift; Building Six is the Eastman Building, plaque nose-touching tradition.

### Harvard Business School founding and Frederick W. Taylor
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Harvard Business School founding (1921, Allston) — Carnegie land gift, Frederick W. Taylor faculty
- **Frame in this lecture:** Carnegie's $100K land purchase, 1921 opening, Taylor as industrial-management/industrial-engineering founder. Used to argue Harvard's engineering ambitions are old, not new.

### Paulson School of Engineering at Harvard
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Paulson donation to Harvard engineering school (Allston engineering campus across from HBS)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Harvard's contemporary strategy to compete with MIT — Paulson gave $400-600M to strengthen HBS, money routed to the new engineering school across the street, signaling Harvard's recognition that top business schools require engineering proximity.
- **Era:** 2000s–2010s

### Harvard-MIT School of Public Health (and the name removal)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Harvard-MIT School of Public Health — 1920s donor-driven name removal
- **Frame in this lecture:** A 1920s Harvard alum donor conditioned a major gift on removing MIT's name from the joint school. Used to establish the long history of Harvard-MIT institutional competition.

### Chuck Vest's decision to accept MIT presidency (Dave Ragone advice)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Chuck Vest / Dave Ragone "no football team, no medical school" advice
- **Frame in this lecture:** Closing anecdote. Ragone's two reasons to accept the MIT presidency: no football team, no medical school. Frames why MIT created the Division of Health Sciences and Technology (HST) jointly with Harvard rather than starting its own medical school.
- **Era:** Vest's 1990 hire
