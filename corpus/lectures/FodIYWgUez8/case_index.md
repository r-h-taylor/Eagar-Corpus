*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g., `§5.p3`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture's spine is the codes-and-safety-factor argument: how engineering profession has historically encoded uncertainty into design through factors of safety, what those factors look like across industries, and how the field has transitioned in recent decades from allowable stress design (ASD) to load and resistance factor design (LRFD). The teaching cases serve that argument.

### Boston Big Dig
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boston Big Dig"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's consulting role on the galvanized bolt corrosion question, before the ceiling collapse that killed a motorist. Used to demonstrate the engineering-judgment clause of the code: a non-conformance (out-of-sequence galvanizing) didn't have to mean uninstalling all the bolts; comparative analysis of zinc throwing power between hot-dip and electro-galvanized coatings showed the disputed bolts were safer than the as-built electrical hardware adjacent to them.
- **Materials/systems:** 1.5-inch diameter steel bolts, hot-dip vs. electro-galvanized zinc coating
- **Era:** 1990s–2000s construction phase

### Alberta transmission tower steel contract dispute
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p2`, `§4.p5`, `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alberta transmission tower steel contract dispute"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A $50M contract dispute between buyer (Canadian utility) and seller (Indian steel mill) over 100,000 tons of steel for 111 towers (~1000 tons each at ~$1.5M steel cost per tower). Tom's quoting code section 6.5 (inspection responsibilities). Used to ground the abstract code-citation discussion in current consulting work, and at §9.p5 to illustrate the financial stakes of weight savings from LRFD design.
- **Materials/systems:** structural steel, 500 kV transmission towers
- **Era:** 2015 (recent consulting)

### Private Mach 2.5 aircraft crash and wing flutter failure
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Private Mach 2.5 aircraft crash and wing flutter failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Reno-based homebuilt-loophole aircraft program: $10M deposit aircraft, second test pilot (the owner) killed at Mach 2 / 40,000 ft over Nevada desert. Tom's forensic finding: inadequate adhesive joint on the lead-weight damper at wingtip — wing flutter went unstable and ripped the wing off. Wreckage spread over 50 miles. Tom's signature observation: "Why they didn't put a screw in there to join the lead to the aluminum is beyond me."
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum airframe, adhesive-bonded lead vibration damper at wing flap
- **Era:** consulting case, date unspecified

### NASA X-33 space plane
- **Anchor:** `§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "NASA X-33 space plane"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A $50M structure with manufacturing defects; NASA reduced the safety factor to 1.05 to keep the program alive; the tank passed at room temperature but failed on liquid-hydrogen thermal cycling. Used to illustrate that safety factors are not magic — they exist to absorb the manufacturing imperfections of real fabrication.
- **Materials/systems:** liquid hydrogen / liquid oxygen propellant tank, composite/aluminum structure
- **Era:** late 1990s (program canceled 2001)

- **Era:** 2005 (failure); 1880s (Tom's family connection to original construction)
