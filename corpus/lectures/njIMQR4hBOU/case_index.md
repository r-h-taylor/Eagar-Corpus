# Case Index — Lecture 1 (njIMQR4hBOU)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§5.p10`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

Lecture 1 of Fall 2016 is a course-opening lecture and is structured around the concept of *externalities* rather than around developed forensic cases. Most case mentions are illustrative rather than developed. The Pennzoil Oil City refinery is the only fully-developed case unit.

## Cases referenced

### Bell Labs transistor development
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2` through `§2.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bell Labs transistor development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to make the point that what is remembered as foresighted basic research at Bell Labs was actually applied research forced by the failure-rate math of mechanical switches under a regulated-monopoly six-percent-profit incentive structure. Tom uses this case as his lead example for the "industry doesn't do basic research" thesis.
- **Era:** 1925 onward (Bell Labs); regulated-monopoly era circa 1908 onward.

### AT&T telephone network mechanical switch failure risk
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`–`§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "AT&T telephone network mechanical switch failure risk"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The failure-rate calculation (one in 10,000 hours) that drove the Bell Labs search for an electronic switch. Tom references the AT&T Reliability Handbook as a primary data source.
- **Era:** 1920s–1950s.

### World Trade Center collapse
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`–`§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "World Trade Center collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 2001 paper, written in three hours for a non-technical audience, is given to students as both a content reference (steel does not melt in ordinary fires) and a methodology example (writing for the lay reader). The paper has generated ongoing correspondence including continuing emails from conspiracy theorists fifteen years later.
- **Era:** September 2001; paper written shortly after.

### China rare earth embargo on Japan
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`–`§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "China rare earth embargo on Japan" (alias: "2010 China rare earth embargo on Japan", "US-Japan rare earth metals embargo")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Lead example of a *political externality*. Tom uses the case to make the point that monopolistic positions in commodity inputs trigger five- to ten-year diversification responses from the rest of the world, citing the 1972 Arab oil embargo as the structural parallel.
- **Materials/systems:** Rare earths (neodymium, others) for permanent magnets in electronics.
- **Era:** 2010–2011 (embargo); referenced 2016.

### 1973 Arab oil embargo
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`, `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1973 Arab oil embargo"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Structural parallel to the rare earth embargo. Tom cites the case to demonstrate that within five years of the embargo, U.S. utilities had retooled boilers for fuel-switching between coal, gas, and oil — his recurring thesis that monopoly power is self-limiting on a five-to-ten-year horizon. Tom dates the embargo to 1972 in the lecture; the historical canon dates it October 1973. Preserved per the no-correction-of-misstatement convention.
- **Era:** 1972–1973 (Tom dates 1972).

### GE/General Electric aerospace rhenium supply constraint
- **Anchor:** `§5.p7`–`§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric/GE aerospace rhenium supply constraint" (alias: "High-temperature turbine blade rhenium content escalation")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Raised by a student as an extension of the rare-earths-as-political-leverage point. Tom acknowledges the case without prior familiarity, then connects it to his own turbine-blade work (six-percent-rhenium superalloys at roughly forty dollars per ounce).
- **Materials/systems:** Rhenium in nickel-base superalloys for turbine blades.
- **Era:** ~2010s.

### Pennzoil Oil City refinery explosion
- **Anchor:** `§5.p10`–`§5.p11`, `§5.p13`–`§5.p14`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pennzoil Oil City tank explosion and facility" (also overlaps with "Tank explosion at Pennzoil Oil City refinery" and "Oil City refinery explosion" / "Oil City refinery storage tank explosion" / "Pennzoil plant environmental regulation case" / "Pennzoil tank farm contamination")
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecture's central developed case. A welder was killed when a tank in the tank yard exploded. The forensic visit revealed that the ground was saturated with oil to a depth of two feet from ninety years of routine spills. Tom's insight, arrived at over breakfast the morning after, was that Pennzoil's continued operation of the dilapidated refinery was rational under externality pressure: closing it would convert it to a Superfund site and trigger taxpayer-then-stockholder cleanup liability. The refinery was eventually closed.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon steel tanks; welding-on-tank fatality.
- **Era:** Refinery dates from circa 1900; Tom's consulting visit was 1996; closure post-dates the visit.
- **Related historical anchor:** Edwin Drake's 1856 Titusville oil well, referenced via the 1864 Oil City photograph.

### Belmont elementary school oil tank leak
- **Anchor:** `§5.p14`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Belmont elementary school oil tank leak"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Contrast case to Pennzoil — same hazard class (heating oil leak) under modern environmental regulation cost the town of Belmont two million dollars in cleanup. Used to make the point that the rules have changed since 1900.
- **Era:** ~2001 ("about fifteen years ago" relative to 2016).

### Blood diamonds / conflict diamonds
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Blood diamonds civil war"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Social externality example. Tom extends the case to ISIS-era antiquities trafficking as a parallel structure (rebel-finance via tradable extractive goods).
- **Era:** Angolan and Congolese civil wars through ~2005–2010.

### Roman lead plumbing
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lead pipe creep in historic aqueducts" (closest aggregate match; lecture frames this as Roman lead-mine production volume, not aqueduct failure)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Environmental externality. Roman lead production exceeded 10,000 tons per year for plumbing applications that would be impermissible today.
- **Era:** Roman period.

### Lead paint poisoning case (Cambridge apartment)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`–`§6.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lead paint poisoning case (Cambridge apartment)" (lecture references the general phenomenon rather than the specific Cambridge case; tentative match)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Why children eat lead paint — lead oxide is sweet to the taste. Tom uses this as an aside on environmental externalities of historical materials choices.
- **Era:** general / 20th century.

### Japan-US Oil and Steel Embargo (World War II)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p11`–`§6.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Japan-US Oil and Steel Embargo (World War II)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Military externality. The 1941 embargo of oil (Long Beach) and scrap iron to Japan in response to atrocities in China is presented as the proximate cause of Pearl Harbor. Tom develops the case through Japanese carrier strategy (four-carrier strike force aimed at U.S. carriers, which were at sea).
- **Era:** 1937 (Nanking) through December 1941 (Pearl Harbor).

### Pearl Harbor attack
- **Anchor:** `§6.p12`–`§6.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pearl Harbor attack"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Linked to the embargo case above. Tom emphasizes the strategic miss — the Japanese hoped to destroy American carriers but found only battleships at Pearl.
- **Era:** December 1941.

### Indian gold-shirt murder
- **Anchor:** `§6.p17`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Indian businessman murdered for gold shark shirt"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cultural externality. The real estate tycoon's quarter-million-dollar gold shirt and subsequent murder used to illustrate the cultural valuation of gold in India (which holds half the world's mined gold).
- **Era:** 2015–2016 (recent at time of lecture).

### Wind turbine ring vortex / aircraft encounters
- **Anchor:** `§7.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Wind turbine ring vortex aircraft encounters"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mentioned by Tom as an unexpected externality of wind-power siting: ring vortices from large-blade turbines have entrained small aircraft. Used jokingly in the context of Martha's Vineyard wind-farm opposition.
- **Era:** ~2015–2016.

### American Electric Power Texas transmission line investment
- **Anchor:** `§7.p9`, `§7.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "American Electric Power Texas transmission line investment"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Infrastructure-externality case. AEP is spending $1.2 billion on West-to-East Texas transmission lines so that wind generators have a load to sell to. Tom's framing: no one builds wind towers until transmission exists, and no one builds transmission until towers exist — AEP's tax-credit-supported investment breaks the deadlock.
- **Era:** ~2014–2016.

## Figures and recurring framing statistics

- **Twenty years of oil reserves for the last hundred years (§2.p8):** Tom's recurring framing statistic for industry investment horizons. The eight-percent-interest twenty-times-payback calculation is the analytic core of his "no one looks past twenty years" claim.
- **Internal rate of return horizon — twenty years to twentyfold payback at 8% (§2.p8):** the numeric framing statistic that backs the oil-reserves anchor.
- **130 million tons of global rare earth reserves; 30,000 tons/year shipped (§5.p2):** twenty-years-supply framing applied to rare earths.
- **Saudi production cost: $1/bbl (historical), $5/bbl (current); Alberta tar sands: $80/bbl; Nigeria: $10/bbl (§5.p5):** marginal-cost-of-production table for global oil.
- **705 → 3,000 metric tons gold mined per year (§6.p15):** fourfold growth in gold production over ~150 years.
- **All gold ever mined fits in a 100-foot cube (§6.p15):** Tom's *National Geographic* anchor for the scale of total gold extraction.
- **18% of Americans are 9/11 conspiracy theorists (§3.p5):** framing statistic for the audience-reach of his WTC paper.
