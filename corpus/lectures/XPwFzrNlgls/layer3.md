## §2. Early glass manufacturing and the move to plate glass [03:16]

`§2.p1` The main thing today is glass manufacturing. Only relatively recently have we developed the manufacturing methods to produce float glass and plate glass — that was around the 1950s, when it became an industrialized process. Before that, if you've ever been down to the basement in building four, we have a glassblowing lab and a foundry, and what we do there is very similar to how glass used to be made. You'd have a man using centripetal force, twirling the glass, blowing on it to get different features. The main limitation was the size of the glass panel or piece you could make.

`§2.p2` If you've ever been to some historical buildings, you'll see what they call a bull's-eye pattern on the glass. That's indicative of old glass manufactured by early processes. You don't see that anymore. Glassblowing was a trade — apprenticeships, passed down like woodworking. Here you see an individual blowing the glass and cutting off the edge.

`§2.p3` Now we have not only float glass processes but glass composites — fiberglass in particular. The fiberglass process is very different. The liquid glass gets poured in, and the design of the spinner system — these are typically made of cobalt alloys because of the high-temperature properties — the liquid glass comes out almost like yarn or fiber, and they can spin it into insulating materials. Now we're putting fiberglass into composites for aerospace applications. So fiberglass is very different from the traditional glass processes.

`§2.p4` One of the main breakthroughs was being able to perform a plate-glass manufacturing process — to shape flat glass. The early panes were not smooth; they had to be polished by hand. If you look at old buildings, the glass has waviness and curvature, very non-homogeneous, with variability from one pane to the next. The plate-glass process really revolutionized the industry. It was a rolling procedure — you start the glass at a temperature where it's softened, a plastic condition. Similar to how steel has an annealing temperature where you can form it, you can do the same with glass. Two forming rolls pull it through, almost a continuous process. There was still some additional grinding and polishing on the back end, but it was much better than the traditional ways.

## §3. The Pilkington float glass process [08:12]

`§3.p1` Then the float glass process came around. This is an image from Pilkington. This really revolutionized the industry. The name comes from Sir Alastair Pilkington — between 1953 and 1957 he and Kenneth Bickerstaff invented the float glass process. It created high-volume applications and much better uniformity. What happens is you float the glass over a bath of molten tin. You have your molten tin, and it's almost like oil and water, okay. You have your molten glass and raw materials on top, and because of surface tension it spreads out and fills the whole region where your panels come off the line.

`§3.p2` Pilkington licensed this to numerous companies and became wealthy — there was some family lawsuits later on. Professor Eagar talked the other day about the billion-tons-per-year club. Float glass itself is about 53 million tons per year, on the same range as aluminum alloys. We're nowhere near steel — steel is 1.5 billion, this is 0.05. But this is only float glass. It doesn't include recycled glass, bottles, solar panels, other applications. If you take all glass applications together, we're probably talking about a hundred and fifty million tons per year. Not billions, but still significant.

`§3.p3` After you float it on the tin bath, you still have to strengthen the glass, especially if it's going into a building as a safety curtain wall. There are three ways to strengthen glass. You can temper it. You can do a mechanical process, which is to laminate it. Or you can do a chemical process — an ion exchange. The raw material for glass is basically sand, so you have sodium ions and other materials. The ion exchange strengthens the glass by exchanging small sodium ions for larger ions. One way is a potassium chloride bath — you exchange sodium for potassium, and that puts residual compressive stresses on the surface. We'll talk about tempered glass later — that's a thermal process, and there are issues if you don't leave it in the temper condition long enough.

`§3.p4` Glass strength. Professor Eagar has a story about his thesis advisor Bob Rose. When Rose taught this class he would go to a light bulb manufacturing site on the North Shore of Boston, early in the morning, and get a freshly manufactured light bulb. Right after manufacture, you can bounce it and it won't break. Because right after manufacture you don't have the attack of the air at the surface. What happens over time is the oxygen creates small micro-cracks, micro-pits on the surface as the air diffuses in. So at the beginning of class he could bounce the light bulb, but at the end of class if he tossed it, it would shatter. After a certain time, diffusion and kinetics take over and you lose the ductility. One of the Achilles heels of glass is that it's brittle — but that's because of the diffusion of oxygen onto the surface over time. What you're seeing here is duration of stress versus stiffness and density. Over time you can put less stress on it before it breaks.

`§3.p5` Thin glass sheet. This is from Corning's website, one of their products for cell phones. You can make glass very thin — this is Willow Glass. What differentiates Willow Glass is they were able to invent an alkali-free manufacturing process. Normally your raw materials have a lot of alkali, and those are the materials that, if you don't do the chemical ion exchange, will diffuse over time and cause brittleness. An alkali-free process gives you more impact resistance. We'll come back at the end to cell phones and how much of a business broken screens are — the numbers are astonishing.

## §6. Fractographic analysis — terminology and ASTM standards [26:05]

`§6.p1` In the next few slides I want to show you different examples from different types of glass. We talked about bulletproof glass previously, we talked about float glass and flat panel glass. They all have slight nuances that make them different in how they're manufactured and how they fracture. We're going to talk about a few failure case studies, and then what's the proper equipment if you want to inspect glass. The first thing is where to look for information — specifications, technical organization documents. There's an ASTM standard, 1678, for fractographic analysis in ceramics and glasses. The main thing is to know the terminology. Very similar to how if you were dealing with a steel that had fatigue, glass fracture surfaces have similar features. They mean different things, but you need to know the features to look for: the origin, the mirror, the mist, and the hackle.

`§6.p2` At the origin — if you have a singular area where they didn't get out an inclusion, or something foreign fell in at the glass plant, that's where the defect can start. Normally around this inclusion you have a very smooth area, and that's why they call it the mirror — it's smooth, with a reflective appearance. Surrounding that, you have a radial outward appearance, with transition areas between mirror and mist, and between mist and hackle. If you've seen broken glass, you see how the shards branch out. The mirror is very smooth and reflective, and you have discrete boundary interfaces — the mirror-mist boundary, the mist-hackle boundary. This is just an example on a piece of glass that fractured.

`§6.p3` Additional ASTM standards. There's a standard for how to interpret what the fracture surface looks like, and one on determining load resistance. For any construction company building large skyscrapers — I was just down in Southie at the Seaport area this weekend, and all you see is high-rises going up left and right, it looks a lot different than it did ten years ago. If you're building that type of high-rise, you have to follow this E1300 standard practice for determining load resistance. You have wind loads, all of these things to take into account when you're using glass as your curtain wall material. If you don't, you won't get a certificate of occupancy from the Department of Buildings.

`§6.p4` Here's the E1300 practice. It's an 80-page document. I was on the committee that put this together for a couple of years. You have people from different industries — glass manufacturers, end users, companies that do coatings. Now you have LEED certifications — you can get actual tax credits by making a building more energy-efficient. There's a lot of money that can be made when you're designing buildings and thinking about how to use glass.

`§6.p5` We talked about thermal stress. Often with thermal stress the fracture will start on the edge, because it's a more highly stressed area from two directions. Glass has decent compressive properties — you can stand on the glass like we saw on the previous slide — but it doesn't have good properties in tension. At the edge, where you have both tension and compressive forces, that's where the crack can start. One of the issues with glass is it's not good in tension. That's why we put residual compressive stresses into it through the manufacturing process.

`§6.p6` Sun lighting. There was a famous building in London a couple of years ago. The designer who designed it has had this issue on two or three buildings — he was known for conical buildings with glass curtain wall facades. What happened was, at one area across the street, two blocks down, they were getting temperatures so hot it was melting car hoods. Super hot. The design of the building acted like a lens or a mirror and focused the sunlight at one spot. There's a famous picture, Google it, somewhere in London. Now people know not to park in that area if they want to leave with the car later that day operating. The sun's angle affects your thermal stresses. People say, why do I have 50 broken panels on this side of the building and none on the back side? More often than not, when the sun comes up and hits it, you're getting thermal stress breakage. So sunlight can actually fracture glass panels.

## §7. Nickel sulfide inclusions and spontaneous fracture [33:31]

`§7.p1` The next type of inclusion is nickel sulfide inclusions. This is typically only found in tempered glass. Tempered glass — similar to how you temper or anneal steel — you keep the glass at a certain temperature that removes stresses, and then you can strengthen it afterward. If you don't leave the glass tempered at a high enough temperature or for long enough, you can get inclusions that don't diffuse out of the molten glass. When the glass solidifies, these inclusions get caught about midway through the thickness. For float glass there's a thickness limitation — float glass can only make glass about 1/4 to 3/8 of an inch thick. Anything more, you have to use multiple panels. You can't make a one-inch-thick piece of float glass, because of the physics — the surface tension on the tin bath. Most glass is a standard quarter-inch. If you have an inclusion it's typically about midway through, because those are the inclusions that had the farthest to diffuse, and if you haven't left it in long enough, that's where they get trapped.

`§7.p2` Glass with a nickel sulfide inclusion has a characteristic figure-8 or butterfly breakage pattern. The reason nickel sulfide breakage is so scary is that it can happen spontaneously. You can have glass in service for 10 years and it can be fine, and one day it just breaks. The cause of that spontaneous breakage is the volume expansion between phase changes. Nickel sulfide has an alpha phase and a beta phase. When it transforms between those phases there's a volume change, and if the inclusion is at mid-thickness, that puts tensile stresses into the glass as it expands. Glass doesn't work well under tensile stress — it can accommodate it to some point, but at some stress it just breaks.

`§7.p3` Here's a piece of glass — when you look at it, nothing really jumps out. You have to see what techniques you can use to find the root cause. If we zoom in on this area, you see how smooth it is. Typical equipment would be a stereo microscope for higher magnification visual. Then you can use an SEM or EDS to confirm whether the inclusion is nickel sulfide. There are other types of inclusions, but EDS tells you the approximate chemistry.

`§7.p4` So when we looked at that glass under the stereo microscope, this is what we see. Nickel sulfide inclusions are known to have a more spherical appearance compared to other types. Looking at the scale bar — it's not nanomaterials, but it's also generally something you can't see visually. So you put the glass in the scanning electron microscope. There are two types of SEM — some don't need any coating and can do non-metallics. In this case there was gold sputtered on the surface so the SEM could image it. This is the higher-mag image — you see the spherical appearance, and you have peaks for nickel and peaks for sulfur to confirm.

## §8. Laminated safety glass and delamination [38:28]

`§8.p1` Another type of failure is delamination. This is what you see in an automobile. They make the glass a different way, called safety glass. If you've ever been in or seen a car crash, you don't have glass where the windows just break into a thousand pieces and go everywhere. They use safety glass to try to keep it in as many pieces as possible. They put a plastic layer between two glass layers — a sandwich structure. That plastic is typically PVB, polyvinyl butyl [polyvinyl butyral]. It adheres to the glass to keep it from falling off in shards, for safety. With delamination, at the edges you have multiple layers, and if the adhesion isn't done properly, one layer will start peeling away — you get a frosted or glazed appearance. Over time it gets worse and you have to replace the glass.

`§8.p2` One of the main tests for safety glass is called the pummel test. Similar to the Charpy test for steels, it's not very exotic. You take a hammer, hit the glass a certain number of times, and then look at visual comparison to see how much glass adhered or fell off. So the pummel test determines adhesion properties between the glass and the PVB inner layer. Generally the manufacturer does this in-house. You can't really find an ASTM spec for the pummel test — it's done in-house, not before the glass goes out. This is after a pummel test — they pounded the glass pretty good, and the ratings show whether you had well-adhered or poorly-adhered PVB.

`§8.p3` Equipment and techniques. You can use the techniques we talked about, but the real issue is adhesion between glass and PVB. To test adhesion properties, or to see if there are contaminants in the PVB, you use other tests: Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy, dynamic mechanical analysis, or DSC. FTIR measures the frequency and intensity of vibrations in the molecule — it shoots a laser through the sample, and you get a spectrum that compares to a library of hundreds of thousands of different organics. You can see if there's anything in the PVB that's a contaminant. An example would be a silicon-type caulk — when you're putting glass panels together you sometimes caulk the edges, and if that material gets onto the inner layer it can act as a contaminant.

`§8.p4` Here's a typical FTIR image. This is ATR equipment — you put your sample in, tighten it down, there's a crystal that shoots up and measures your spectrum versus wave number. PerkinElmer is a big company; there are hundreds of thousands of spectra for different chemical organics. They compare to known spectra to see if you have a contaminant.

`§8.p5` DMA is a mechanical test where you measure the mechanical properties of the inner layer. Similar to a fatigue test with a CT specimen for a metal — you vibrate the sample, impose an oscillating strain, and measure both elastic and viscous response. One thing you can use DMA for is to determine glass transition temperature. That's one of the properties the manufacturer gives a range on, and you check it's within range. You can run it at different temperatures in an environmental chamber, and measure the mechanical properties of the adhesion.

`§8.p6` DSC is a thermal characterization technique as a function of temperature. You put your sample in, it heats up, and at temperatures where you have phase transitions you'll see drops in the curves. You can do glass transitions, crystallization temperatures, melting temperatures, and use this to determine if the glass conforms to what the materials cut sheet states. A lot of times we get involved after the fact — people want to know, was the glass manufactured properly, was it installed properly, why did it break? It's an iterative process: if there's no smoking gun, you have to do additional tests to determine its properties. With a DSC you have a control sample on one end and your sample under test on the other. You'll see your glass transition temperature, and the peaks and valleys correspond to different phases — recrystallization, melting — that's what you compare against the manufacturer's literature.
