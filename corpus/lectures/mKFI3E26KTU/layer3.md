## §2. Dirty metals and the rare earth problem [07:34]

`§2.p1` **Adam Powell:** When I was an undergrad and grad student here, Tom was well known for having early morning classes. That was the only 8 a.m. class I ever took, so it's not necessarily the best time for an undergraduate. I will talk about some things that I find very exciting, and so hopefully that will keep you engaged and interested as well. Clean metal production for clean energy. Most of the slides are really about the technology of this company Infinium, which I started five years ago with a colleague. I'm in Materials Science, as Tom mentioned. My colleague's name is Steve Derezinski, he was in mechanical engineering — so that's between the two of us at most of the class, except for the nuclear engineers, though he did work in the nuclear industry.

`§2.p2` So, clean metal production for clean energy. I'll begin by motivating the problem, talking about clean technology and dirty metals. Metal production is a relatively dirty process. Generally it goes: metal oxide plus coal to metal plus CO2 and everything else that comes along with coal. That's sulfur, heavy metals, soot, all of that stuff. For renewable energy and energy efficiency it's especially dirty, and I'll get into some particulars.

`§2.p3` Neodymium and dysprosium are very important for the very high strength magnets that are used in high power-to-weight motors and generators. Most of the growth is in wind turbines, where you need high power-to-weight because you're supporting the weight on a long steel tower. Hybrid and electric vehicles as well — power-to-weight is important. Interestingly, the Tesla uses an induction motor, as you may know — it's rare-earth-free, but heavier and a little less efficient because you have to have a copper coil generate the magnet instead of permanent magnets. So you take a little bit of energy to run that magnet coil, and you add some extra weight.

`§2.p4` Close to 100% of the rare earth metals in the world are made in China. It's about 95% of the rare earth elements, but the metal production generally happens in China for reasons I'll get to in a moment. If you look at the technology changes in terms of magnet sizes required to generate a certain magnetic field, neodymium iron boron is an amazing magnetic material. Just the volume required for a permanent magnetic field, and with it the mass, has gone down quite a bit.

`§2.p5` However, the Chinese rare earth industry is notorious for a lot of pollution. You Google China rare earth pollution and you find things like this. And you find — this is our competition. This is a Chinese neodymium plant. They use very small molten salt electrolysis cells. This is basically the Hall-Héroult cell. The way it works: you have a bath with a molten salt, generally fluoride salt. You add metal oxide — in this case neodymium oxide; in the aluminum case it's aluminum oxide. You put in a cathode — they use tungsten, you can see the tungsten cathodes down the middle — and an anode; here they actually use the entire graphite vessel. They make a little bit of neodymium metal in the bottom, and then harvest that with a molybdenum ladle.

`§2.p6` The only anode technology that people can get to work with this is graphite, and carbon is a contaminant in neodymium. It's a diluent for the magnetic performance of the magnet. So you're making this in a graphite vessel with graphite anodes — you're going to get some carbon in it. What they found is that workers can be trained to visually and by feel figure out how to minimize the carbon content. They ladle these out in one-kilogram batches because the carbon content is very variable, and then they analyze each one-kilogram batch and sort them for carbon. Extremely labor-intensive, and that's why it's only done in China and somewhat in Vietnam.

`§2.p7` This is one of the reasons Chinese production has come to dominate the world's rare earths, and there are other market reasons as well. Very famously, Deng Xiaoping is quoted to have said in 1982: Saudi Arabia has oil, China has rare earths. China certainly has a very large rare earth supply. For heavy rare earth metals — if you look at the bottom of the periodic table, everything to the right of samarium — China has by far the best deposits in the world. Neodymium is a light rare earth, found all over the world, but dysprosium, which raises the Curie point of these magnets, is found pretty much only in China except in tiny concentrations elsewhere.

`§2.p8` China has exported at very low prices for a very long time, making it difficult for the rest of the world to produce. One of the best mines in the world, in southern California, for light rare earths actually shut down in 2002 due to Chinese competition. Then in 2010 — does anyone know what happened to the rare earth market then? Yes, they stopped selling to Japan, because of a border dispute. So suddenly there was panic throughout the world. Fortunately Japan was well prepared because they had the analysis of the critical materials. Toru Okabe, who was a postdoc in Don Sadoway's lab years ago — he's a professor at the University of Tokyo — sounded the alarm well in advance. In 2006, when I visited his lab, he said: we're especially concerned about dysprosium, we also have a neodymium issue. They stockpiled a lot of it and had about two-to-three years' worth of supply. So for the two-month embargo they were okay.

`§2.p9` Rare earth prices skyrocketed — factor of 10 — and they've since come down to about twice what they were before. China is now imposing export quotas. The suspicion is that other projects are starting throughout the world, and that China at some point is going to drop prices again, run them out of business, buy them up cheap, and then have even more control over the worldwide supply. But that's a suspicion, or conspiracy theory.

## §3. Magnesium for lightweight vehicles [15:40]

`§3.p1` Moving on: Tom talked about magnesium for lightweight vehicles. It's the lowest density structural material — density about 1.7 versus aluminum at 2.7. In stiffness-limited parts and applications, which is most of the car body and a lot of different parts, you can save about 20 to 30% in weight versus aluminum. And you cut the weight in half versus steel, or a steel and aluminum mixture. This shows a bunch of different parts made of magnesium by one particular die-casting company in southern Canada. The largest of which — you can see on the left — is the Lincoln MKT rear hatch door. Magnesium is expensive enough that in all of these applications there is some secondary savings in the car. It's not worth it at current prices and fuel savings motivations to substitute magnesium except where there are other advantages.

`§3.p2` For example, in the liftgate application, the secondary savings is that with a lighter liftgate you can use a smaller and cheaper motor. The most common application is the instrument panel beam that runs across the dashboard, holds the steering wheel instruments, glove compartment, etc. In steel that's about 30 different steel stampings spot-welded together. In magnesium it's one die-casting. The Corvette engine cradle — Corvette is a sports car so it's a little more weight-sensitive; it's had a fiberglass body for a long time for that reason — again, you go from about 10 steel stampings to a single magnesium die-casting. I like talking about this because magnesium's problems have generally been in corrosion and creep. But here it is in an application right next to the engine, getting salt water thrown at it from the road, and it doesn't corrode. Engines aren't falling out of Corvettes since 2006. It works quite well.

`§3.p3` There are projects involving the front end — the front end is important because it's the hard part. It has to support the engine, crush in an accident, all of that. And beyond that, the full vehicle. Some of the advantages: it's easier to die-cast than aluminum because it has half the solidification shrinkage. It machines faster. Dent resistance. It has this interesting elastic hysteresis — I saw a lecture once and I understood it then, but five minutes later I couldn't explain it to you if I tried — just the physical metallurgy of how this elastic hysteresis works. But it ends up absorbing energy in the course of elastic deformation, so it has very unique vibration damping. You can achieve very high vehicle weight savings versus replacements of steel and aluminum. It's a key weight reduction strategy.

`§3.p4` Magnesium is produced by one company in the US. Chinese competition shut down all other production in North America. They've survived by two things. One is a wonderful connection to their congressional delegation that won them, among other things, a 111% tariff on Chinese magnesium, but also a Clean Air Act exemption. They've been called the nation's worst polluter. They're on the Superfund list. Don Sadoway was actually involved in a lawsuit to try and shut them down. You can see the green chlorine emissions plumes. Back at the time they didn't capture just about any of their chlorine — they just vented it. They have a ferric chloride tailing pond that is poorly enough protected that when there's heavy rain every 10 years or so, some of it washes into the Salt Lake. Major problem.

`§3.p5` So magnesium is done by chloride electrolysis in the US. In China it's done by a more polluting process: silicothermic reduction. If you know your chemistry, using silicon to reduce magnesium oxide is backwards — magnesium should reduce SiO2. But silicon has extremely low vapor pressure, and magnesium has very high vapor pressure. So if you put silicon and magnesium oxide in a vessel, pull a vacuum, you'll get a little magnesium vapor pressure; heat one end, cool the other, and you get magnesium transport, and it condenses as a solid in the other end. But it takes a lot of energy to drive this reaction. It's a very long carbon payback time — it takes 275,000 km of driving before the fuel savings pays back all the carbon you put into producing it on the front end. Not really practical.

## §4. The Infinium process: solid oxide electrolysis [21:06]

`§4.p1` I co-founded Infinium five years ago with co-founder Steve Derezinski, mechanical engineering, class of '90. I was '92. We now have 22 employees in a 12,000 ft² facility out in Natick. As Tom mentioned, this is a metal extraction breakthrough invented and patented by U.D. Pal. It uses a zirconia solid electrolyte between the molten salts and the anode. There is no carbon, no chlorine in the process. Zero direct emissions. Our tagline has been clean metal production for clean energy.

`§4.p2` In a typical aluminum cell, the anode just sits in the molten salt, and you generate CO2 right there. You apply a potential, and so you produce liquid aluminum at the cathode. At the anode, the oxygen from the aluminum oxide reacts with the carbon, you produce CO2. In addition, the alumina is not completely dry and picks up some moisture from the atmosphere. When you have moisture at high temperature and fluoride salts, you make some HF. What comes off in terms of water and HF is about 10:1 to 20:1 water-to-HF ratio. But nonetheless, it's enough HF that you really cannot build a heat exchanger that can withstand that kind of acid corrosion.

`§4.p3` In its place we put a zirconium oxide solid oxygen ion conductor between the molten salts and the anode. We use a liquid metal anode. You can use the cathode materials of solid oxide fuel cells, perovskites, but it's very complicated. Liquid metals are very simple and robust. You can use liquid silver to make pure oxygen, or you can pipe in natural gas to create a reducing environment, and then you make some CO2 and some water. It has about the same energy content per mole of oxygen you pull as graphite. So it's pretty much equivalent to graphite anode energetically — except you make half the CO2 because it's CH4. And you don't have to make the graphite anode, so you save all the energy and CO2 emissions involved in graphite anode production, which is a very messy process in the aluminum industry. Furthermore, natural gas is cheap these days, as you all know.

`§4.p4` This has a lot of advantages. It eliminates something called the anode effect, where if you get CO2 bubbles forming, you get very high current density at the anode, and then you don't just have oxygen reacting with the oxide ions, but fluoride ions, and you form perfluorocarbons. CF4 has about 6,500 times the global warming potential of CO2. C2F6 is about 9,000 times. This eliminates that because you don't have any fluorine in contact with the anode. The zirconia is very selective — it only allows oxygen through. It eliminates HF in the anode gas because the anode is in a separate chamber from the rest of this. It eliminates the graphite anode plant. You can capture the energy from this CO2 and water, or from this oxygen, where you really can't from the anode gas in a Hall cell plant because of the HF.

`§4.p5` Furthermore, the only way to contain liquid cryolite in the traditional Hall cell is in solid cryolite. In our cell, because you've separated out the anode gas, you have a reducing environment in here. There's no CO2, there's no oxygen, and we can contain this in a metal. We can use steel — in fact, just cheap welded steel vessels. For that reason we can insulate around it and have very low energy losses. If you contain a liquid in its own solid, it takes a lot of cooling to maintain that solid. Imagine a long-term glass of water made out of ice — if you're heating the water, you have to cool the ice. Very inefficient.

`§4.p6` It also cuts the cell footprint. Now, instead of a flat anode and a flat cathode, you can go into the third dimension — you can have these zirconia tubes extending deep into the molten salt, and the cathodes as well. There's some limit to that; we can get between three and 10 times the productivity per unit cross-section area versus the Hall cell. So your overall footprint of the plant can be about half, we think, of the footprint of a traditional aluminum plant. Furthermore, as I mentioned, you can produce oxygen byproduct or use natural gas fuel, steel vessel. This also enables direct electrolysis of magnesium oxide.

`§4.p7` Magnesium oxide electrolysis has been a goal of the industry for a very long time, since Charles Hall in the US and Héroult in France showed that it's possible for aluminum oxide. People have been trying to do this. For both aluminum and magnesium it's very expensive and very difficult to make anhydrous high-purity chloride material. You have to do it either by carbochlorination — where you take magnesium oxide plus chlorine in the presence of carbon to produce CO2 and MgCl2. It also produces dioxins and furans, right? Carbon, oxygen, chlorine. Or you can add hydrochloric acid, then you have magnesium chloride solution, and then you dehydrate that. But that's very difficult to do — more difficult than it sounds, because you have to manipulate the pressure to avoid hydrochloric acid evaporation from your cell. It's the most capital-intensive operation in production of magnesium.

`§4.p8` The magnesium Hall cell has never worked. It's complicated why carbothermic reduction doesn't work. Silicothermic reduction leads to very high energy use, as I mentioned. This is why magnesium chloride is very expensive. Trust me, read up on it if you're interested. Our technology for magnesium is similar to what I just mentioned for aluminum, except that magnesium boils at 1090 °C. This is right in the heart of zirconia's high conductivity temperature range. We can run it above 1100 °C and make magnesium vapor. That's really neat because whereas the Hall cell for aluminum has to have very high purity aluminum oxide — any impurities go straight into the product — we can take 98% pure magnesium oxide and make 99.9% pure magnesium through this sort of inline distillation. So it's not only magnesium oxide electrolysis, but high purity magnesium from moderate purity oxide. Very high current energy efficiency.

## §5. Zirconia tubes: the enabling breakthrough [29:36]

`§5.p1` The main issue is the zirconia tubes. This idea has been around since the late '60s. If you look up in the patent literature, you see Swiss Aluminium has a couple of patents granted in the early '70s on basically this technology, because of all the advantages I mentioned. However, if your zirconia tube doesn't last very long, then your tube costs basically outweigh the advantages. So U.D. Pal's real breakthrough was a combination of chemical metallurgy thinking and stubborn persistence — he finally found molten salt combinations that don't destroy the zirconia. I can talk about it because he's at BU; he's published this. It turns out magnesium fluoride-calcium fluoride eutectic, unlike cryolite, does not attack the zirconia. In 500-hour tests you get about 100 microns of zirconia corrosion. So if we run for 5,000 hours as our target lifetime — that's a little more than 6 months — that's 1 millimeter of corrosion. If they last that long, the cost of zirconia tubes per kilogram of magnesium ends up being a very small fraction of our total cost. That's the enabling breakthrough.

`§5.p2` This is how it'll look in industry. You have your crucible producing this. You continuously feed magnesium oxide in, produce oxygen at the zirconia tubes. We use argon to both stir the molten salt and dilute the magnesium. Because if you know your electrochemistry series, magnesium plus zirconium oxide becomes zirconium plus magnesium oxide. But if you dilute magnesium to an activity of 1/10th or lower — 0.1 atmospheres or below 0.16 atmospheres — then it's thermodynamically stable, no reaction. We dilute it to about 10%, and then condense liquid magnesium in the condenser. We circulate the argon. We get over 99.99% argon recovery, so we use very little argon. And then just tap that out.

`§5.p3` One other advantage: we produce liquid magnesium. And because we have no emissions, a nice thing about this process is that it scales down well. There's no treatment of the hydrofluoric acid-containing anode gases. The only emissions are basically oxygen — which, as Sadoway likes to say, you're even allowed to emit oxygen in New Jersey, sorry if there are any New Jersey residents here — or, if you use natural gas, then it's natural gas combustion products, which, well, MIT emits a lot of that. So you don't have to have a lot of expensive equipment to contain that. You don't have to apply for any EPA permits, which is often the biggest time delay. You're getting ready to build a plant, you apply for the permit and you wait — your plant's already ready, but you have to wait another year because all the federal, state, and local permits take that long to come in. If you don't have emissions, you don't have to get permits.

`§5.p4` So suddenly, instead of having to build 60,000 ton or 200,000 ton plants, you can just build small 2,000 ton or 5,000 ton plants. You can build them right next to the consumers — right next to a die-casting plant, or twin roll strip casting to make sheet — and just sell the hot liquid metal. That cuts your customers' energy cost in half because they don't have to heat and melt the material — just contain it, put it into their machines, and use it. So you can charge a premium. It's a nice side benefit to the economics of scaling down this process.

## §6. Energy, emissions, and impurities [34:01]

`§6.p1` In terms of other externalities — energy use and emissions: Pidgeon is the name of the professor in Canada who invented the silicothermic process for magnesium, and that takes a huge amount of energy, 100 kilowatt-hours per kilogram. What the Chinese do is put their magnesium plant next to a coal mine, and then the energy is almost free. Coal energy is a lot cheaper than electrical energy. Western Electrolytic is US Magnesium. There's a company called Dead Sea Magnesium in Israel — I think they're still in production. Live used to be called Metal Oxygen Separation Technologies. This is a third-party energy and emissions model, showing that we use about half the energy and have somewhat less CO2 emissions.

`§6.p2` I should mention also — they actually emit some chlorine. This is all per kilogram of magnesium production. This assumes that we're using magnesium carbonate as a raw material, so CO2 is 44 versus 24 — that's about two kilograms of CO2 for every kilogram of magnesium right there. Take that away and this goes to something like 3 kg/kg instead. So quite a bit less energy and CO2 compared to aluminum. Aluminum primary production is about 24 kilowatt-hours per kilogram and 12 kg of CO2 per kilogram of metal.

`§6.p3` This is our design concept. You see the crucible with all the tubes, the top left, condenser and tank for the metal. We have a two-stage condenser. An intern from the Materials Science Department helped to invent it a few years ago, so he's on our patent application, which is about to be granted. Tom is also an inventor there. Argon recycling. And I didn't mention this, but because of impurities in the molten salt, you have to continuously remove some of the molten salt. About a six-month residence time in the cell there. This is about 40 ton per year cell concept, and fits into a relatively small box. We put it in a box with the heating inside to have an inert atmosphere, so we can just use steel without having to use very expensive stainless steels. We're now into our early production prototypes. This is an old picture; it's running now.

`§6.p4` Just in terms of what happens to the impurities — the basic reaction is magnesium oxide to magnesium plus oxygen. The feedstock has a lot of impurities, particularly more electronegative metals like aluminum, iron, silicon — or rather their oxides — and those plate out on the steel cathodes. For every metric ton of magnesium we produce, we get about 5 kg of these impurities plating out on the cathodes. We have to replace those every so often. We think we can sell them — or if not sell them, then give them to the steel industry — because aluminum and silicon are good at reducing porosity of large steel ingot castings.

`§6.p5` Zinc and sodium are the only impurities that, if they're present in the raw material, will get in, because they're both very high vapor pressure and their oxides are a little less stable than magnesium, which is counterintuitive. You think of sodium as being an extremely reactive metal, but actually in the oxide series, it's a little less stable than magnesium oxide. Chlorides evaporate at these temperatures very slowly, to the point where we end up with a steady state where it's built up in the crucible enough that it's evaporating off at the same rate you're feeding it in. That gets into the condenser. We actually haven't seen it in the condenser yet because we haven't run for long enough, I guess. About 5 kg will density-separate in the condenser for every metric ton.

`§6.p6` If aluminum sulfates are present in the raw material, that's our only emission. In the course of heating the raw material you get 100 to 200 grams of SO2 emission per ton of magnesium — low enough that we should not need any permits. Calcium and potassium will accumulate if we do nothing, and eventually cause a fine precipitate which messes things up. For that reason we just add some magnesium fluoride; some of it forms magnesium oxide and calcium fluoride. It's all just ion soup in the molten salt. Magnesium oxide we just make magnesium and oxygen with. But we also form this calcium fluoride-magnesium fluoride salt in situ. By adding the magnesium fluoride, we accumulate more salt, and it about doubles in volume every six months, which is why we have to drain it.

## §7. Q&A: feedstock, zirconia, and price [39:44]

`§7.p3` Canada, particularly British Columbia, has some of the best deposits. There's a new mine in Ontario with 1 million ton per year production of magnesite and talc. `§7.p4` **Student:** *[question about zirconia supply]*

**Adam Powell:** The supply of zirconia is not such a big issue. We use yttria-stabilized zirconia, and yttria is a bigger issue. The powder cost is the biggest component of the cost of the zirconia tubes. It's made from something called zircon sand, which is a relatively high zirconia concentrate in sand. Then you have to concentrate that further. But we're only going to run our tubes until they're about 1/3 corroded, so then we have a very high concentrate zirconia source for about 2/3 of our powder materials. That step is down the road after we solve a bunch of other problems. When we scale up, then we could place a significant amount of demand on the world zirconia market, and not such a large fraction of the yttria market, because yttria is used for lots of other things, like fluorescent bulbs.

`§7.p5` **Student:** Why can't you use calcium- or magnesia-stabilized zirconia?

**Adam Powell:** We are working on that as well. The issue is that they have about half the ionic conductivity of yttria-stabilized zirconia. It's very difficult to get powders of calcium- or magnesia-stabilized zirconia that are fully stabilized and fine enough quality to make the tubes last as long as we want. As part of our DOE project, we are making our own powders. We've made a couple of tubes — they don't — we're still working on that right now. Eventually the cost model will make that decision for us. Is the savings in yttria and in the yttrium fluoride that we have to put in the molten salt so it doesn't leach out of the tubes — is that worth the higher energy? Furthermore, if you have half the conductivity and you try to pump the same current through it, you get a lot more Joule heating and thermal stresses. Zirconia is terrible for thermal stresses — very low thermal conductivity, thermal expansion 12 parts per million per degree, comparable to a lot of metals. So the cost model will make that decision for us once we've done that. We have some other ways of improving the zirconia lifetime and performance, which I'll be able to talk about in a couple years.

`§7.p6` **Student:** What do you think you get as a price point if everything's successful, compared to aluminum? Right now magnesium is about twice the price.

**Adam Powell:** It's about $2.25 a pound. It fluctuates versus aluminum by a dollar. What I can say now is that we think we will be very competitive on world markets, which without the US tariff is about $1.40 a pound. At that price you're density-equivalent to aluminum, so you can put magnesium into the same die-casting volume as aluminum and pay the same amount for the metal. Now, it's not quite fair because aluminum is a little stronger and higher modulus. If you're stiffness-limited, you can do 20 to 40% less weight in magnesium than aluminum — 40% if you're a plate that you're trying to optimize, 20% for other geometries.

`§7.p7` If you're strength-limited, you can do about 10% better in magnesium than aluminum, a little better than that in plate bending, and not as well in pure tension — you're pretty comparable to aluminum in pure tension. So there's no real weight savings in a pure tension application; you have to use more volume of magnesium anyway. Our original cost models showed us a little better than the world price of magnesium. Based on what we've learned experimentally now, our new cost models are showing a lot lower, and I'm viewing that right now as some slack — all of the unexpected costs in running a magnesium plant that we don't know because we haven't run a plant, we'll make up some of that. That's about what I can say now on video.
