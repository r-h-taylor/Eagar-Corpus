# Case Index — Lecture 3 (`v-PMrqbjY1A`)

This lecture is structured around Tom's price-per-pound framework: two dollars (auto), two hundred dollars (aircraft), twenty thousand dollars (spacecraft). Cases are deployed to illustrate that framework rather than as standalone forensic units. The X-33 (§3) is the lecture's extended worked case; the rest are illustrations.

### NASA X-33 space plane
- **Anchors:** `§3.p1` through `§3.p13` (extended worked case)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "NASA X-33 space plane" (also: "X-33 hydrogen tank adhesive joint failures"; "X-33 Space Plane"; "X-33 space plane liquid nitrogen tank failure")
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecture's flagship case study. Three failure modes braided: (1) the 3D woven graphite-fiber composite cost overrun that ate the budget before tanks were built; (2) the 10-day-shelf-life adhesive bullet point that hid a 100%→20% strength curve; (3) the post-test leak from CTE mismatch between graphite-epoxy, Nomex, and adhesive joints under cryogenic-to-room-temperature cycling. Outcome: $50M tanks scrapped, $1.3B program cancelled.
- **Materials/systems:** Nomex hexel foam core, graphite-epoxy composite skin, structural adhesive; liquid hydrogen tanks; aluminum oxygen tank.
- **Era:** ca. late 1990s (Tom says "10 or 15 years ago" from 2013).
- **Note:** Tom held a piece of the X-33 tank composite in class (stage direction at §3.p10, inferred — see editorial register).

### Air Force One friction welding blade-to-disc program

### Polyethylene manufacturing and hula hoop market development
- **Anchor:** `§4.p7`, `§4.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Polyethylene manufacturing and hula hoop market development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mid-fifties chemical-company gamble on a 50-million-pound polyethylene plant with no application, rescued by the hula hoop craze. The variable-quality reject inventory finds a market and funds the learning curve that eventually delivers refrigerator-liner quality. Illustrates the "if you build it they will come" market-creation dynamic for new materials.
- **Materials/systems:** polyethylene; polypropylene (comparison); painted steel refrigerator panels (predecessor).
- **Era:** mid-1950s.

### Sony Walkman and neodymium-iron-boron magnets
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`, `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sony Walkman and neodymium-iron-boron magnets" (also: "GE samarium cobalt magnet development"; "General Electric/General Motors neodymium supply shortage threat")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Nd-Fe-B invented at GM research lab. GM's threat to vertically integrate into Brazilian neodymium mining triggered third-party suppliers to enter the market. Illustrates "availability" as a constraint distinct from properties or cost.
- **Materials/systems:** Nd-Fe-B permanent magnets; rare earths.
- **Era:** 1984 (GM Magnequench).
- **Note:** Tom's delivery oscillates between General Electric and General Motors; L3 reads General Motors throughout per the canonical answer. See editorial register.
