# Case Index — Lecture `kF4wXqPHw-o`

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p3`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture works through the hierarchy of engineering design — conceptual, architectural, detailed, shop, erection, and as-built drawings — illustrated by a sequence of forensic failures where the as-built diverged from the as-designed. Eight teaching cases anchor the discussion.

## Cases in this lecture

### Nova Scotia sailboat trailer weld failure
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Nova Scotia sailboat trailer weld failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's forensic case. A farmer-built sailboat trailer with a slugged weld (bolt buried in the joint, then welded over) fractured on Route 24 south of Boston on the return trip from a Newport race, destroying the 40-foot sailboat. Used to illustrate why standards prohibit welding on tractor-trailer frames and what "slugging a weld" means.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon steel angle/C-channel; three-quarter-inch bolt; manual arc weld.
- **Era:** Unspecified — likely 1980s consulting case.

### WWII ship construction slugging welds
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "WWII ship construction slugging welds"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Historical precedent for the Nova Scotia case. Welders on 16-inch armor plate, bored with 600-pass joints, would throw in pieces of pipe or bundles of electrodes and bury them in the weld groove.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-section armor plate, manual arc welding.
- **Era:** World War Two.

### Jet engine removal bolt design failure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Jet engine removal bolt design failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stage-business case. Tom passes around a hardened steel bolt assembly used to dismount jet engines from aircraft wings. The bolt had two diameters with flats on the center shoulder for restraint; mechanics torqued only the outer nut, ignoring the shoulder, which over-torqued the assembly and initiated a crack. A 5-million-dollar engine dropped on the tarmac. Used to introduce load-path reasoning.
- **Materials/systems:** Hardened steel bolt, jet engine wing mount.
- **Era:** Unspecified.

### Stata Center (MIT Building 32 / Gehry crumpled-paper concept)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`–`§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Stata Center (Frank Gehry conceptual design)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stage-business case. Tom crumples four sheets of paper to demonstrate how Frank Gehry presented the conceptual design of the Stata Center to MIT physical-plant head Vicki Sirianni. Budget went from $115M to $430M; MIT froze salaries for two years. Used to illustrate how informal conceptual designs become consequential.
- **Materials/systems:** Architectural concrete, steel framing.
- **Era:** Late 1990s–early 2000s construction.

### Hyatt Regency walkway collapse
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`–`§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Hyatt Regency walkway collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lecture's centerpiece forensic case. July 17, 1981, Kansas City. The original detail design called for solid hanger rods, threaded only at the ends; field fabricators, unable to thread a nut 15 feet down a solid rod, substituted a two-rod stagger that doubled the load on the upper rod's C-channel connection. The change was approved (or missed) under the high-volume change-order process. 114 dead, 200+ injured. Used to illustrate the as-designed vs. as-built distinction and to introduce the engineer-of-record concept. Cites Petroski's *To Engineer is Human*.
- **Materials/systems:** Steel hanger rods, welded C-channel walkway support.
- **Era:** 1981.

### Chevy Vega engine removal for spark plug service
- **Anchor:** `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Chevrolet Vega engine removal for spark plug service"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief design-foible aside in the Hyatt section. The 1978 Chevy Vega had to have its engine removed to change spark plugs because surrounding components blocked wrench access. Used as a low-stakes illustration of the same as-designed / as-serviceable gap.
- **Materials/systems:** Engine block, spark plugs.
- **Era:** 1978.

### Ford Taurus air conditioner compressor
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p3`–`§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford Taurus air conditioner compressor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Personal forensic case. Tom bought a 1990 or 1991 Taurus in February; the AC failed when first switched on in May. Ford's $100M production line had been built with clamping fixtures matching the engineering drawings, but the prototype machinist had built his own (correct) clamping fixture and never told the engineers. The as-built of the prototype was lost. Tom invokes the Massachusetts lemon law and his Ford VP connections to get a replacement AC. Used to illustrate prototype-to-production handoff failure. The Harvard Business School story attribution is preserved.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum air conditioner compressor; sliding shuttle / tight-tolerance machining.
- **Era:** 1990–1991.

### General Motors sliding-door van lemon-law case
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Motors sliding door van lemon-law case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Personal forensic case, 1985. The sliding side door of Tom's GM van fell off five times. After a nasty letter from the dealership owner, Tom filed lemon-law demand; GM offered an executive vehicle, then offered cash instead when the executive's loaner wasn't ready. Used as a narrative bridge into the Taurus case and to illustrate manufacturer response patterns.
- **Materials/systems:** Sliding door mechanism, sheet metal hinge.
- **Era:** 1985.
