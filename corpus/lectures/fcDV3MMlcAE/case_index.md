### Quincy LNG tanker aluminum tank skirts (Bethlehem Steel A537 development)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`–`§3.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Quincy LNG tanker aluminum tank skirts"
- **Related clusters:** "LNG tanker aluminum tank welds—Coast Guard Charpy toughness specification"; "LNG vessel heat treatment coupon substitution and testing failure"; "Quincy Shipyard liquid natural gas tanker construction (mid-1970s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's tenure as a Bethlehem Steel engineer developing weldable steel for the 150-foot-diameter cylindrical skirt supporting the aluminum LNG sphere. Coast Guard required Charpy testing at weld centerline, toe, and 1/3/5 mm into HAZ at 20 ft-lb / −20°F. Sparrows Point steel-making struggled to meet the spec on base plate; shipyard would hunt down high-toughness plates (30 ft-lb outliers) to cherry-pick for runoff tab testing — gaming the Coast Guard reporting.
- **Materials/systems:** ASTM A537 steel; aluminum LNG sphere on steel pedestal; Charpy V-notch impact testing
- **Era:** mid-1970s

## Figures referenced

- **Welding productivity table by process** (§5.p2–p5):
  - SMAW (stick): ~7 lb/hr; arc-on time 25–30%, often 5–10%
  - GTAW: ≤ 7 lb/hr
  - GMAW: 10–15 lb/hr
  - Submerged arc, single electrode: 40–50 lb/hr
  - Submerged arc, five electrodes (panel line): up to 160 lb/hr, ~10× manual rate
- **Charpy specification history** (§4.p1):
  - 1946 Liberty ship failures: < 10 ft-lb
  - 1950s–60s spec: 15 ft-lb minimum
  - 1970s Coast Guard: 20 ft-lb
  - Alaska pipeline era: 30 ft-lb
  - Proposed peak: 80 ft-lb
- **A537 LNG skirt toughness data** (§3.p5–p7):
  - Required: 20 ft-lb @ −20°F
  - Base plate first-normalize: 15–19 ft-lb (typical)
  - Average shipped: just under 21 ft-lb
  - Outlier high plates: 30 ft-lb (cherry-picked for runoff tabs)
