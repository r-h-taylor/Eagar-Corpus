# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture 9 (3.371 Welding Metallurgy, Spring 2014). This register records every editorial judgment made in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition.*

## Sectioning

The lecture is short (~13:30 of audio) but moves through six distinct teaching units: a technical preamble on hydrogen levels by consumable type, two helicopter case studies (Bell mast / peashooter), a thick-section forging press repair, a battleship turret historical case, and a wrap-up. Each gets its own section. Cases that Tom flagged as separate (Bell mast vs. peashooter) are kept separate.

## Per-section decisions

### §1 — Hydrogen levels by process and consumable

**§1.p1.**
- Cut: "I can get away from electro— these stick electrodes all together" — Tom self-corrects mid-word ("electro—"); layer 3 reads the completed form "I can get away from these stick electrodes altogether."
- Cut filler "okay" mid-clause: "use a rutile electrode okay. It's like a 7014" → "use a rutile electrode. A 7014, I think, is rutile."
- Numerals: "point two to point one" → "0.2 to 0.1" (technical datum context).
- Smoothed "Basic electrodes like the 7018 that have been dried at 100 degrees C, or up here I can get down to 350 to 400, the equivalent moisture in the electrode coating" — Tom's gesturing at a chart ("up here"). Removed the deictic reference; reads as a continuous technical statement.

**§1.p2.**
- "If you had on Groton, Connecticut" — captioner garbling for "If you go down to Groton, Connecticut" or similar. Smoothed to "In Groton, Connecticut" — the locative meaning is clear and the preposition is the only thing in question.
- Added "submarine" before "hull" — Tom is clearly referring to submarine hull welding at Groton (Electric Boat); the context from p1 makes this implicit, but on the page a reader needs the noun. **This is the most aggressive edit in §1** — strictly speaking it's a single-word addition. Justification: "the hull" without antecedent reads as ungrammatical; "submarine hull" matches what Tom means by Groton.
- Cut: "the three circles that we have there" → "the three circles in the Venn diagram." Tom is pointing at a slide; the Venn diagram is named explicitly in §6.

**§1.p3.**
- Smoothed self-correction: "and there's no cracks that form in the meantime because I had no stress let's say" → "and as long as no cracks formed in the meantime — say I had no stress." Tom's hedge ("let's say") is preserved as the dash-clause.

### §5 — Battleship turret repair

This is the canonical *USS Battleship turret hydrogen cracking repair (Puerto Rico, ~1980)* case. Tom's framing is a single rhetorical arc; layer 3 splits at the "We lost the technology" pivot to a second paragraph.

**§5.p1.**
- Cut filler "okay" five times. Preserved "Boom" as rhetorical sound effect.
- Cut "you know" twice.
- Note on factual content: Tom's account ("a sailor … set off one of the 16-inch shells inside the turret … to spite him") is his version of the 1989 USS Iowa turret explosion. The historical record is contested; the Navy's initial finding of intentional sabotage was later retracted. **Preserved verbatim** per the constitutional constraint against removing factual errors Tom made on tape. Flagged here for editor review but not footnoted in layer 3 — this is Tom's recollection used to make a metallurgical point about lost welding capability, and a correction would derail that point.

**§5.p2.**
- Smoothed: "everyone died who knew how to do it" → "everyone who knew how to do it died." Word order corrected; meaning unchanged.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
