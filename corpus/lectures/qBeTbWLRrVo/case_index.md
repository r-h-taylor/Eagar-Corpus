## Cases developed in this lecture

### Helms Project pumped-storage penstock failure
- **Anchor:** §3.p2–§3.p13 (primary), referenced again at §3.p13 as recap
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Helms Project penstock"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's most-developed case in the session. Eight-and-a-half-month delayed hydrogen crack on a 22-foot-diameter PG&E penstock at 6,000 ft elevation. Used to illustrate (a) that hydrogen crack delay can extend far beyond the standard "first couple of weeks" rule if the weld is frozen, (b) that boiler-and-pressure-vessel-code exceptions for inch-and-a-quarter material with fillet welds can be load-bearing in failure analysis, and (c) the litigation politics of expert witnesses ("the guy whose wife drove around in a Rolls-Royce").
- **Materials/systems:** Inch-and-a-quarter pipe steel, fillet weld on man-way flange, 188 PSI static head.
- **Era:** Early 1990s (Tom's involvement); construction Thanksgiving 1989-or-thereabouts (Tom's reconstruction); failure 18 hours after spring pressurization.
- **Linked cases:** "PG&E v. American Bridge lawsuit (Fresno, California)" — almost certainly the same litigation, viewed from the defendants' side. Reconciliation needed.

### Boston Navy Yard destroyer boiler weld
- **Anchor:** §4.p1–§4.p7
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boston Navy Yard destroyer boiler pipe weld (stainless-to-chromemoly transition)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first big consulting case as a young MIT faculty member, ~1978. Stainless-to-2.25Cr-1Mo transition weld with persistent root cracking; NAVSEA procedure specified Inconel filler, contractor wanted to substitute stainless steel. Tom's solution: change the joint geometry (J-prep with a long land) to reduce restraint, leaving filler-metal specification intact. Used to illustrate (a) the Venn-diagram methodology applied under constraint and (b) that solutions sometimes come from the only variable left when the obvious ones are fixed.
- **Materials/systems:** 2.25Cr-1Mo steel, stainless steel boiler pipe, Inconel filler, GTAW.
- **Era:** ~1978; shipyard subsequently closed.
- **Linked cases:** "Chrome-molybdenum tube cracking at Boston Navy Yard" — same case under a different cluster name.

### 7,000-ton forging press top-head weld repair
- **Anchor:** §5.p1–§5.p4
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "7,000-ton forging press weld repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 17-inch-thick weld on a top casting of a 1948-vintage 7,000-ton forging press, repaired by skilled Georgia-based welders without adequate preheat-through-section or post-weld heat treatment. Cracked twice (first from residual stress alone, second on the first forging stroke). Used to illustrate that excellent welder craft is not a substitute for engineering oversight on heavy sections. Cost: ~$5M.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy steel casting, ~400–600 weld passes over a day and a half.
- **Era:** Tom's consulting involvement; repair attempt date not specified in the lecture (the press itself dates to 1948).
- **Linked cases:** "Forging press repair weld failure," "Forging press casting repair," "Pennsylvania forging press repair," "7000-ton forging press casting weld failure" — multiple aggregate entries may all be this same case. Reconciliation needed.

### Bell Helicopter mast hydrogen baking procedure
- **Anchor:** §1.p4
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bell Helicopter mast hydrogen cracking prevention"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief but specific. 4340 steel mast at 240 KSI, electroplating-induced hydrogen baked out at 375°F for 23 hours, transferred from plating bath to furnace in under 5 minutes. Used to illustrate (a) that hydrogen sources are not only welding and (b) that procedural rigor matters in proportion to consequence of failure ("you've only got one mast on a helicopter").
- **Materials/systems:** 4340 steel, electroplating bath, post-plating hydrogen bake.
- **Era:** Not specified; presented as current Bell Helicopter Fort Worth practice.

### Nautilus submarine austenitic filler metal foundations
- **Anchor:** §2.p2
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Nautilus submarine hydrogen cracking during construction" / "Nautilus submarine hull material (HY-80)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Early Nautilus foundations welded with austenitic filler (half the strength of HY80) as a deliberate under-matching strategy to absorb hydrogen and limit residual stress, until higher-strength procedures were qualified.
- **Materials/systems:** HY80 base, austenitic filler.

### Navy under-matching filler metal studies (multiple rounds)
- **Anchor:** §2.p3–§2.p4
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Navy undermatching filler metal research program (multiple rounds)" / "Undermatching weld metal field trials"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's "about every twenty-five years" lament. The Navy spent millions in the 1960s on near-full-scale under-matched test panels with explosive loading; spent millions again about 25 years later; reached the same conclusion both times. Used to make the fracture-mechanics point: severe undermatching concentrates strain in the weld metal.
- **Era:** 1960s and ~late 1980s (Tom's "twenty-five years later" reckoning).
