# Case Index — Lecture 7 (Solid State Welding, Spring 2013)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§2.p4`).*

## Cases referenced

### Ford plastic bumper painting failure (PEO)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford plastic bumper painting failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Low-surface-energy plastic (PEO) wouldn't accept paint; Ford had to introduce sandblasting to convert to type-two mechanical interlocking. Used to set up the type-one vs. type-two adhesive bonding distinction.
- **Materials/systems:** Polyethylene oxide bumpers, automotive paint.
- **Era:** ~1990s (Tom: "fifteen or twenty years ago," from 2013).

### Millennium cookware hydrogen cracking and Teflon coating
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Millennium cookware hydrogen cracking" (also overlaps "Farberware Millennium cookware hydrogen cracking" and "Faberware Millennium cookware hydrogen cracking")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Two-part teaching unit using the pot as prop. (1) The aluminum/stainless cold-bonded base illustrates that a deliberately weak bond (10% bonded area) is correct design for dissimilar-CTE joints — porosity accommodates thermal strain. (2) The plasma-sprayed inner surface for Teflon adhesion introduced hydrogen (95% argon / 5% hydrogen gas), which caused delayed cracking after shipping from Brooklyn to Japan. Martensite transformation in deformed 304 stainless was the susceptible microstructure.
- **Materials/systems:** 304 stainless steel, aluminum, plasma-sprayed stainless powder, Teflon, hydrogen plasma gas.
- **Era:** Tom: "twenty years ago" from 2013, so ~1993.

### Ceramic-metal joint porosity optimization (doctoral thesis)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Doctoral research on ceramic-metal joint porosity optimization"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Thesis showed ~10 volume percent porosity in the proper pattern at the interface produces the strongest ceramic-metal joint. Used as theoretical follow-up to the cookware insight about porous interfaces accommodating CTE mismatch.
- **Era:** 1990s.

### Adhesive-bonded automotive door panels failure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`–`§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Adhesive-bonded automotive door panels failure (1980s–1990s)"

- **Materials/systems:** Steel door panels, water-soluble adhesives.
- **Era:** Early-to-mid 1980s.

### Boeing aircraft skin and wing adhesive bonding
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing aircraft skin and wing adhesive bonding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Boeing was proud of using adhesive bonding to join skins and wings to last 30 years — but they put rivets in anyway, because over 30 years interface corrosion will weaken the bond. - **Era:** Tom: "thirty years ago" from 2013, so ~1983.

### Presidential limousine glass armor
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`

### Aviation carburetor icing failures
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`–`§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aviation carburetor icing failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's consulting work in aviation failures from carburetor icing. Drove the question of whether Teflon coating would prevent ice — answer depends on icing mechanism: water droplets in rain (non-wetting, Teflon helps) vs. vapor deposition from humid air (vapor-deposited frost adheres to Teflon and cannot be removed). Real solution is the cockpit deicing switch.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum carburetors, Teflon coating, water droplets vs. water vapor.

### Boston Logan Airport deicing fluid transition
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`–`§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boston Logan Airport deicing fluid transition"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Historical transition from polyethylene glycol (toxic — metabolizes to methanol, causes blindness, EPA-controlled with collection dams) to polypropylene glycol (food-additive grade, metabolizes to ethanol, used at the gate now). Used as biochemistry digression off the carburetor-icing teaching beat.

### Tom Eagar's Teflon frying pan frost adhesion test
- **Anchor:** `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Teflon frying pan frost adhesion test" (also "Tom Eagar's aluminum soldering demonstration failure" — but that's a different demo; this is the frost test)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's own bench test. Cut out a Teflon pan bottom, soldered a cooling pad underneath, deposited water droplets and steam frost. Drops pop off easily; vapor-deposited frost cannot be removed without scraping off the Teflon. Empirically confirms the vapor-deposition-fills-asperities argument.

### Post-WWII cold heading machine phosphate-stearate surface treatment
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Post-WWII cold heading machine phosphate-stearate surface treatment" (also "Cold heading for furniture fasteners" and "Cold heading tool wear and surface cracking (pre-WWII)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's standing demo of a zinc-plated furniture-fastening insert. Pre-WWII these parts cost ~$1.50 (automatic screw machine); post-WWII phosphate-and-stearate surface treatment enabled cold heading them at ~$0.05. The porous iron phosphate surface anchors a calcium stearate "grass" lubricant (polyethylene-chain rooted by polar bond), enabling repeated deformation steps without die galling. Decibel anecdote (125 dB, 130 dB threshold of pain) from his plant visit.
- **Materials/systems:** Zinc-plated steel, iron phosphate conversion coating, calcium stearate lubricant.
- **Era:** Process developed late 1940s; Tom's plant visit ~1988.

### Titanium combustor fires in jet engines
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Titanium combustor fires in jet engines"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Above 900°C, titanium consumes its own protective oxide, exposing fresh metal to hot compressed air; engine becomes its own fuel. Several Air Force incidents. Used to motivate the metallurgy of why titanium diffusion-bonds so well (the oxide *will* dissolve into the substrate at temperature).
