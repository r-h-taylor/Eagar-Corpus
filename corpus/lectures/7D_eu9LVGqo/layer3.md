## §2. Why glass: transparency and ancient origins [06:17]

`§2.p1` Today I was going to start talking about another structural material, which is sort of the antithesis, at least in terms of toughness, and that's glass. What is the property of glass that makes it so important that we want to use it even though it's brittle? It's transparent. You won't find any metal that's transparent. If you want to know why, go back to your solid state physics. It's because metals have free electrons. By the very nature of metallic bonding you have free electrons, and free electrons can absorb virtually any wavelength of electromagnetic energy, including light. Whereas in glass, there are certain bands — the conduction band and the valence band — and forbidden regions in between, quantum mechanical forbidden regions, that electrons cannot have those energies in the solid. Because of that, you can come up with materials that are transparent to visible light. Metals will never be one of those, in spite of what Scotty said in *Star Trek IV: The Voyage Home*, where they had transparent aluminum. It wasn't metallic aluminum, because metals are not transparent.

`§2.p2` Because we'd like something transparent to keep the weather out, keep the wind out, and let the light in, we've had glasses since about 3500 BC. The first glasses were in Syria, and it was probably the result of refining metals — they had slags, and the slags in many cases were molten oxide, and they were glassy. Initially they took these molten slags and probably made some little shape out of it, and eventually they started making art objects out of glass. They were very expensive, and the glass wasn't controlled composition. A lot of it was black. Does anybody know the name of the stone that is basically a form of glass? It's called obsidian. Usually black. It's basically a mixture of molten oxide, and it's not very useful for transmitting light, but it has an amorphous structure. A glass essentially has no crystalline structure if you want to define it in modern physics terms.

`§2.p3` In the olden times, people learned that they could melt certain types of sand. Most beach sand is high in silica, and if you have the right composition, or you throw some limestone in with it, or some sodium oxide, you can lower the temperature and get something that melts around eight hundred to a thousand degrees Fahrenheit, and it's very viscous. It turns out it's a Newtonian liquid, which means you can stretch it just like silly putty — it gets longer and it doesn't neck down. It has very interesting properties. The Romans had glass — apparently they would cast glass melts onto a flat plate, whether a piece of polished stone or whatever, and they would roll it and make glass. From the 4th century, people found Roman glass. Not very transparent, had lots of impurities, but they did make glass, and it was for the wealthy people, to let some light into the homes when the sun was out.

## §5. Thermal conductivity, residual stress, and the black light bulb [25:37]

`§5.p1` I want to talk about glasses mostly as structural materials. Glass is still a brittle material. It has lousy thermal conductivity compared to diamond, which has the best. Looking at thermal conductivity versus linear coefficient of expansion: diamond is out here by itself, with fantastic thermal conductivity, very strong bonds. The metals are up in here, some of the ceramics are back here underneath the metals, the glasses are way down here. Some of the engineering ceramics, your woods, your plastics are in here. The glasses have a fairly low thermal conductivity compared to metals — lower than any metals by a substantial amount, which means you have to be careful. You can't form glass too quickly and cool it very quickly, otherwise because of the thermal expansion it will set up residual stresses, and it doesn't take very much of a residual stress to break glass.

`§5.p2` I had a case once where some kid — his parents gave him a black light, which is just a light bulb made out of a special type of glass, gives off ultraviolet rays. He was playing around with this; he'd just come out of the shower, and he wanted to see what his body looked like, whether he fluoresced — yeah, it's the soap, with fluorescent stuff. A drop of water fell down on this hot incandescent light bulb. The bulb exploded, took out his eye. They wanted to know why the glass exploded.

`§5.p3` When you make an incandescent light bulb — we don't have very many incandescent light bulbs anymore — Corning had a process to make very uniform light bulbs, and then they sold that process to someone else and went into the television picture tube. Remember the old great big picture tubes for televisions? Then they got out of that business, sold it to someone else. Now they're making the screens for your computers, and we'll get into that. In any case, I got the light bulb, and you could see where the soapy water had dripped on this black light incandescent bulb. I looked at it, and the glass was really thin on one side. It was blown glass, just like that stuff in the 1600s where guys had a blowpipe. This was made in Korea for General Electric. Instead of being made on the automated Corning process, where they made 60-watt light bulbs for people's homes on regular glass with uniform thickness and everything was controlled, they had somebody from Korea, less than minimum wage, sitting there blowing these specialty glasses. So it wasn't uniform in thickness.

`§5.p4` I got to thinking, well, they may not have tempered this properly. Ordinarily, if you form glass like that — how many people have ever watched a glass blower forming things? He takes his little glass tube, he's got a torch and he melts it, and he can make these little giraffes and rhinoceroses by dabbing the molten glass. Did you ever watch him after he had formed the glass with this kind of honey-type glass — he would just take his torch and heat it for a while. What was he doing? He's relieving the residual stresses. Because if he gave it to you just after he formed it and let it cool down, and you flicked it with your finger, it would shatter. Glass is brittle, and if it has severe residual stresses because of low thermal conductivity, it would just shatter. So you keep it hot for a while, and you let the residual stresses relieve themselves while it's cooling down. That's critical, otherwise you're going to make a brittle glass.

`§5.p5` I thought, maybe they didn't properly anneal the glass after making this little light bulb. I wanted to run the test, and the attorney didn't want me to know the results if it didn't come out the way I thought it would. So he hired one of the students to do the test, and we had a high-speed camera, and they videotaped a regular light bulb. You could drip water on a hot regular light bulb all day long, as long as you didn't electrocute yourself, and it wouldn't shatter, because it had been properly annealed on the Corning line. You go to one of these hand-blown glasses, and apparently — to this day I never got to see the video — but you take a little dropper, put one drop of water on it, and wow, blows up, because of the residual stresses. That's what happened to the kid, that's why he lost his eye. They had not properly annealed it, they had not properly blown it, it wasn't uniform thickness. You've got to control a lot of things in the manufacture of something like this.

`§5.p6` The important thing is, if you want to use glass as a structural material, you can't have simple little things — someone comes along and scratches it, or drops some water on a piece of hot glass. How many times have you heard, as a kid growing up, you're not supposed to take a piece of ceramic cookware or glass cookware directly from the oven into the sink and quench it in water, because it will shatter on you? That's because of the residual stresses and the lousy thermal conductivity.

## §6. Strengthening glass: mechanical, thermal, and composite approaches [32:06]

`§6.p1` To strengthen glass, we've come up with all kinds of things over the centuries. Pilkington, around 1900, came up with a way: they would pull out two sheets of plate glass they were going to roll, and they would put a wire mesh in between, and just squeeze it like a peanut-butter sandwich between two pieces of glass bread. You have the wire mesh in between — you've seen wire-mesh-reinforced glass. That's mechanically strengthened.

`§6.p2` Corningware originally used a glass ceramic that had very low coefficient of thermal expansion, but that was fairly expensive. Modern Corningware — and Corningware has sold this process to another company; Corning likes to get out of businesses after a while — has three different layers of glass that they bring in and roll together. The two outer layers have a different coefficient of expansion than the center one, and when it cools down, the center contracts more than the two on the top, and you end up with a tensile residual stress in the center and a compressive residual stress on the surface. So modern Corningware — you go buy a little white Corningware dish — is actually made by putting three layers of glass together, the one in the center having a larger coefficient of thermal expansion based on its composition, and you basically come in with a little forging press and stamp out the product, whether a little ramekin or a casserole bowl. It now has scratch resistance — if you scratch it, it's not going to shatter, because it's got compressive residual stresses. You can go through the fracture mechanics, but if the compressive residual stresses exceed the stresses that would be on it, you never get to tensile stresses at your crack tip, and the Griffith criterion for brittle fracture isn't satisfied. If you take it and put it in cold water after it's been hot, it won't shatter, because you've got compressive residual stresses, even though you've got microscopic flaws on the surface.

`§6.p3` When the glass is first formed, it has very perfect surfaces and extremely high strength. When we make fiberglass — if we make the fiberglass and then within minutes coat it with plastic and keep the moisture off — it turns out the humidity in the air will slightly attack the glass on the atomic scale, and create flaws that will embrittle the glass per the Griffith criterion. I've never done it, but my thesis advisor lives up on the North Shore, he's retired now. Sylvania had a glass factory up on the North Shore where they made light bulbs, and Bob would stop in the morning — he'd worked out a deal with the guy there — and he'd get a freshly made light bulb, and he'd bring it in when he was lecturing 3.091. He could take that light bulb and throw it across and hit the wall, and it would bounce, because it was freshly made. If he did that two days later, after it sat in the humid Cambridge atmosphere, it would shatter. But freshly made, it had no flaws on the surface, it had not been corroded by the moisture in the air, it had tremendous strength.

`§6.p4` So whether you're making fiberglass, you coat it with plastic — those fiberglass fibers could have strengths of 200,000 pounds per square inch. You don't think of glass as having that kind of strength, because glass has little imperfections, and so the Griffith criterion and the poor fracture toughness apply. But if you can keep the scratches from forming and the corrosion from the moisture in the air from occurring, glass has tremendous strength. So we make fiberglass boats, but we have to coat the fibers immediately after forming them to protect them from the humidity. That doesn't mean the glass continues to corrode — maybe it does, but it slows down so much that glass has been there for a hundred years now, some of it, and it's weaker than when it was first put in, but it's not that much weaker.

## §8. Tempered, laminated, and chemically tempered glass [43:30]

`§8.p1` So you have regular glass, you have laminated glass — this is what you have on the front windshield of your car, laminated glass. And you have tempered glass. What's tempered glass? It's thermally tempered. You take it out of the furnace in the sheet and you actually blow air on it, to get something like the Corningware, where you contract and cool down the outside surface more quickly than the inside. If you do this heating and cooling at the right rate, you'll get compressive residual stresses on the outside surfaces and tensile residual stresses on the inside. It will have fantastic scratch resistance and strength. It will also have greater strength than the untempered glass. And when it breaks, it shatters into little pieces.
