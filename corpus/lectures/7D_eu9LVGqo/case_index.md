### Saugus Ironworks

- **Frame in this lecture:** Time-marker — "the days of Saugus Ironworks" — to date the bullseye-glass era to the early 17th century.

### Saint-Gobain (Sango Bath)
- **Anchors:** `§3.p6`, `§3.p7`, `§3.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sango Bath (Saint-Gobain) founding and long-term strategy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Origin (1688) as a mirror-glass maker pouring molten glass onto plates and polishing with corundum. Used to make the long-time-horizon company-strategy point; contrasted with U.S. firms of which only five of the 1900 top 100 survived to 2000. Tom's former student is among Saint-Gobain's top 100 managers.
- **Materials/systems:** soda-lime glass, polished mirrors, corundum (Al₂O₃) abrasive
- **Era:** 1688–present

### Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long factory
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long factory"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Pre-Ford plate-glass production: cast onto a metal table, rolled and polished along a mile-long building. Used to set up the productivity contrast with the later float process (50-yard building).
- **Era:** 1800s

### Ford Motor Company and Libby-Owens-Ford continuous plate glass
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford Motor Company and Libby Owens Ford float glass development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1920s continuous-pour plate-glass process developed by Henry Ford with Libby-Owens-Ford for mass automobile production. Intermediate step between Pittsburgh Plate Glass and the Pilkington float process.
- **Materials/systems:** continuous plate glass, roller forming
- **Era:** 1920s

### Pilkington float glass process
- **Anchors:** `§4.p3`, `§4.p5`, `§4.p6`, `§4.p7`, `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pilkington float glass process"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Sir Alastair Pilkington, 1953–1957: continuous pour onto molten tin bath, natural-gas atmosphere, replaces the mile-long polishing line. The defining modern process. Also: Pilkington's earlier (~1900) wire-mesh laminated glass.
- **Materials/systems:** soda-lime glass, molten tin bath, natural gas atmosphere; nickel sulfide inclusions as defect mode
- **Era:** 1953–present

### Black light bulb eye injury case
- **Anchors:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`, `§5.p4`, `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Black light bulb eye injury case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Forensic consulting case. Hand-blown black-light incandescent bulb made in Korea for GE, not properly annealed; water drop after a shower caused the bulb to explode and blind the user. Compared to properly-annealed Corning-line bulbs that withstand water drops indefinitely. The teaching example for residual stress from poor thermal annealing of blown glass.
- **Materials/systems:** UV incandescent glass, blown vs. machine-formed
- **Era:** consulting case (date not specified)

### Corning Slim Glass / Eagle XG (display glass)
- **Anchors:** `§5.p3`, `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Corning float glass dual-surface manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom under NDA. Corning's display glass uses both float surfaces as air-side (no tin-side imperfections) plus a protective coating, enabling thin display glass for liquid crystal displays. Layered with indium tin oxide electrodes, polarizing filters, nematic crystals. Closes with Corning's "*Innovator's Dilemma*–anticipating" strategy of selling off mature product lines.
- **Materials/systems:** alkali-aluminosilicate glass, indium tin oxide, twisted nematic liquid crystal
- **Era:** late 1990s–present

### Sylvania North Shore light bulb plant (Bob's freshly-made bulb)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Freshly made light bulb strength demonstration"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's thesis advisor Bob (Uhlmann/Rose — see editorial register) would obtain freshly-made light bulbs from the Sylvania North Shore plant and demonstrate in 3.091 that a bulb thrown against the wall would bounce when fresh and shatter two days later, illustrating humidity-driven Griffith-criterion surface flaw formation.
- **Materials/systems:** soda-lime light bulb glass, atmospheric moisture corrosion
- **Era:** Tom's grad-school era (1970s)

### MRAP armor development / RPG (shaped-charge) penetration
- **Anchors:** `§7.p2`, `§7.p5`, `§7.p6`, `§7.p7`, `§7.p8`, `§7.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MRAP armor development for Iraq"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Shaped-charge history (Brooklyn Navy Yard, 1880s) → conical copper liners produce a three-foot molten-copper jet that penetrates three feet of steel → IEDs in Iraq/Afghanistan defeating Humvees → Army study of titanium, steel, aluminum, and six materials → glass selected as MRAP belly armor (six layers of two-inch glass). Textron Systems (Wilmington, MA) makes the copper liners. Aberdeen Proving Ground Army lab demonstration.
- **Materials/systems:** conical copper liner, glass-stack composite armor, MRAP vehicle
- **Era:** 1880s discovery; 2003–2008 IED/MRAP cycle

### Brooklyn Navy Yard shaped-charge discovery
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Brooklyn Navy Yard shaped-charge discovery"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1880s discovery of the shaped-charge effect with conical copper. Origin story for the RPG/IED case.
- **Era:** 1880s

### Protective Armor Systems (PAS) bullet-resistant glass
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Protective Armor Systems (PAS) bullet-resistant glass"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Western Massachusetts company. Four-layer glass-polycarbonate-glass laminate stops .38 and .45 rounds; thicker versions (4.5") in presidential limousine stop RPGs.
- **Materials/systems:** glass-polycarbonate laminate
- **Era:** current

### John Hancock Building chemically tempered glass
- **Anchors:** `§8.p4`, `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "John Hancock Building glass cracking failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Originally specified plate glass that cracked and fell to the street under wind loading. Required three years of total U.S. chemically-tempered-glass production (lithium-for-sodium/potassium ion exchange) to re-glaze. The classic structural-glass failure case.
- **Materials/systems:** lithium-aluminosilicate glass, chemical (ion-exchange) tempering
- **Era:** early 1970s building, glazing problem mid-1970s

### Corningware three-layer glass-ceramic (residual-stress strengthening)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Corningware three-layer glass-ceramic"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Modern Corningware: three glass layers rolled together with the center having higher coefficient of thermal expansion than the skins, producing compressive surface stress on cooling. Stamp-forged into ramekins and casseroles. Replaces the earlier low-CTE glass-ceramic body.
- **Materials/systems:** layered glass with engineered CTE mismatch
- **Era:** modern

### Professor Uhlmann's chemically tempered glass demonstration
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Professor Omen's chemically tempered glass demonstration"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's teacher (D. R. Uhlmann; cluster name in aggregate uses captioner mishearing "Omen") demonstrated chemically tempered glass by hand-flattening a curved 18″ × 3″ piece without breaking it. The pedagogical demonstration of ion-exchange surface compression.
- **Materials/systems:** lithium-aluminosilicate glass, ion exchange
- **Era:** Tom's student days
