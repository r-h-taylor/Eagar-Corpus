## §2. Steel productivity and the 1980s crisis [04:52]

`§2.p1` We finished up talking about productivity of steel. Even in my lifetime there's been a change from about six person-hours per ton to about a third of a person-hour per ton. That's a factor of about 20 in productivity. As a result of that change in productivity, all through the 1980s — about 1980 it was 6 person-hours a ton, by the end of the '80s about 3 person-hours per ton — because they had already put in continuous casting and basic oxygen furnaces, and the labor rates went down because of those things, all of a sudden there was a worldwide overcapacity, and the steel companies all started losing money.

`§2.p2` There was only one steel company I know of in the United States that never had an unprofitable quarter all through the 1980s. It was Allegheny Steel. Anybody know why they never had an unprofitable quarter? In my opinion it's because they had a CEO, a guy named Richard Simmons — not the guy who does the exercises, but Dick Simmons of Simmons Hall. Dick Simmons was an undergraduate at MIT. He had a hard time getting through MIT. He learned to work hard at MIT, he said. He went off, became CEO of Allegheny, and he understood the industry he was in. He didn't go to some business school to learn how to manage a business. He kind of figured it out on his own. He knew when they should invest in a new technology, not because it made economic sense to all the bean counters and the financial people, but because he knew this is going to revolutionize the industry. It helps to have a CEO who's not just a business-school-trained automaton.

`§2.p3` A 20-fold increase in 30 years is a pretty dramatic change. As a result of all this increase in productivity, which continued on with the mini-mills, Wall Street became very disenchanted with the steel industry because they weren't making money, and they said steel was dying. That's why in the early '90s I wrote that article I gave you on the future of metals, where I basically tried to argue that steel is still an important material today.

Their productivity had come on top of an already huge industrial base, and therefore everybody was cutting each other's throat to cut price to try to get the market. Aluminum was all new markets; steel in the 1980s was all old markets. The amount of steel used was increasing, but profitability was going down because there was overcapacity. It took about 25 years to demolish the old inefficient steel plants. At the same time, countries were building steel mills because steel was so fundamental to their economy, so they were building new facilities.

## §7. Bonding potential and the limits of materials [41:00]

`§7.p1` What I really want to point out is there are limits to materials, and superconductors are an excellent example. You have to know not just one property. This is fracture toughness and density. If you look at these Ashby plots there's a limit. Things only get so light. Polymer foams or cork or bolts of wood are very light, but they don't have very much strength. This is toughness and strength — you'd love to be super light and super strong. You'd love to be over here, super light and super strong. But there's nothing that exists over there.

`§7.p2` Fundamentally it's because everything we know about materials is related to their bonding. Dr. Belmar put up a plot like this, which is the energy versus distance between two atoms. If I bring two atoms together in space, just two atoms alone, they will find an equilibrium distance of separation, which we often call a₀, because this is the energy potential well. It's an electrostatic energy that goes as 1 over R² — you learned about electrostatic energy in freshman physics. This is a nuclear repulsive term, and it goes as something like R to the 8th or R to the 10th, and the two compete with each other. If you try to squeeze them any tighter, they just don't want to squeeze together. He pointed out that this curve is actually steeper than that curve. If you blow up this region right here, it's actually asymmetric, not a nice symmetric parabola. He talked about how you can explain Poisson ratio because of the energy potential. In the old days, the first ones in the 1920s that the quantum-mechanics people generated were called the Lennard-Jones potential. They've got all kinds of names nowadays.

`§7.p3` The curvature in here is nothing more than the modulus. So if we're talking about modulus of elasticity — let me do a force plot. The force is equal to dE/dR. The force plot looks something like this, where a₀ is where it crosses the zero force. That's the equilibrium distance of separation. If I look at this thing coming down here, that's a zero, and so you've got a force. And if you differentiate the force you get the modulus, which is going to be proportional to ∂²E/∂R². There's a math, to prove I'm an MIT professor.

`§7.p4` Most of what we know about strength of materials is contained right here, in the bonding potential between those atoms. That's for structural materials. And everything you need to know — magnetic properties, optical properties, piezoelectric properties — is contained, if you know everything about where the electrons are and how free they are in the material. So I'm not teaching a course on structural materials or on functional materials — in structural materials you need to know the Lennard-Jones bonding potential, and from this fundamentally you could generate everything else in the world. That's what computational material science today is all about.

`§7.p5` They were able to do this for very simple systems in 1925 out of quantum mechanics, but they didn't have the computing horsepower to do it for more complex systems until somewhere in the 1990s, when the computers got powerful enough that they could start doing computational material science. Now a third of the faculty in this department are working on computational material science. Why? Well, it's a new tool, the computer, to be able to do this. The other is it's a lot cheaper and a lot easier than going to the laboratory and actually doing something with your hands. You can sit at a computer terminal. And not only that, most people can't check your work yet — it's even better. No one knows whether you're right or wrong. You can just produce junk, and if you convince people you're right, good for you.

`§7.p6` There are limits to materials, whether it's optical transmission, magnetic fields, fracture toughness, or strength. We would love to be way out of here, but nature limits us to certain materials that have certain bonding strength. The bonding strength is basically how deep is this energy well, and how much force does it take to pull the things apart. If you take my soldering and brazing module, I'll go through and talk to you about how the fundamental strength of two atoms when you bring them together is about 2 or 3 million pounds per square inch, but we don't get that strength — we only get about a tenth of that. That's another story for another module.

`§7.p7` So we'd like to improve the properties. We have not just one property. Ashby did it in two dimensions in his book. He will sell you a $50,000 program that can do it in 10 dimensions. You tell it all the properties you want to optimize, and it will go through and try to select a material for you. If you've got $50,000, you don't have to take this course. ## §8. Composites: from concrete to the X-33 [47:50]

`§8.p1` One of the things we do to solve this problem is we make composites. As much as — you know that I now think steel is a wonderful material, but remember, your homework is to figure out what the Achilles' heel of steel is. Next week when I'm lecturing again I'm going to ask you if you figured out what the Achilles' heel of steel is. It's got fantastic strength, toughness, and cost — just those three multifactors — it's better than any other material. But it doesn't have all the properties we want. No material has all the properties. If it did, that's the material we would be using for everything.

`§8.p2` So one thing that gives material scientists a job is they get to try to figure out what the best material is. Composites are wonderful materials. I've shown you my $12,000-a-pound piece of the X-33 space plane. I've shown you this thing of the V-22 Osprey. They could not have built this without composites — graphite-epoxy composites. If they had made it out of something heavy like aluminum, they never could have gotten it to fly. It's the first large all-composite aircraft. They had made smaller little single- or two-person jets out of all composites, but the Osprey was the first one that was all composites.

`§8.p3` The 777 was supposed to be 80, 90% composite until Boeing started to design it in the early '90s, and they found they couldn't afford the composites for commercial aircraft. The Osprey was a military aircraft, and you start figuring out what the V-22 Osprey costs on a dollars-per-pound basis, it's probably up in the thousands of dollars a pound range, which you could not afford for a commercial aircraft.

`§8.p4` I sometimes in my pejorative way say composites are the materials of the future — they always have been and they always will be the material of the future. They are starting to find their own. The 787 is like 80% composites by weight. However, it was also about three years late on a three-year development plan, so it took six years to develop it, in part because of all the problems of developing a manufacturing base for making composites reliably.

`§8.p5` In spite of my negative comments about composites, composites are some of the most important structural materials we have in the world, and in the largest volume. Anybody know what I'm talking about? Concrete is a composite. In concrete, they put stone in as just an extender. Mortar is just the cement, but if you put stone in it and make it concrete, you also put something else in concrete — rebar. *[Tom holds up a piece of rebar.]* Just happened to have a piece of rebar — I didn't bring a piece of stone, but you can find one outside. You put steel in because one of concrete's problems is it's not very good in tension. It's a ceramic, it's got lousy fracture toughness. So you put rebar in it. You put stone in it to make it cheaper. Here's this old plot that we've been putting up — what's cheaper than cement and used in higher quantities? Stone.

`§8.p6` What's another composite that you drive on? Asphalt. It's just tar, but you put stone in it as an extender. You put silicon dioxide in your toothpaste as an extender.

`§8.p7` What are the problems with high-volume, very-low-cost materials like stone and cement? What are the limiting factors in use of these materials? It has nothing to do with production of the materials. It's weight — could be failure, but we usually use them and just lay them down on the ground, concrete or steel or asphalt. It's transportation cost. At something that's only worth $40 a ton, you can't ship it very far. There are stone quarries all over the world, in part because there's stone all over the world, but not necessarily the best stone. You will often use whatever is locally available, even if it's not the best stone, because it costs too much to ship stone. There are not big ocean-going vessels that are just taking crushed stone from one continent to another. It's transportation cost when you get way down on this end of this plot.

`§8.p8` Cement, it turns out — there's limestone in most parts of the world. No limestone in Hawaii, because limestone came up from the ocean, and all of Hawaii is volcanic. So there are no little sea urchins or sea animals that died and gave their life to make limestone for your garden. You have to ship cement to Hawaii. It might be cheaper to ship steel, which is 10 times stronger — it might be twice as heavy, but it's 10 times stronger, so on a per-pound basis you might want to build parking garages in Hawaii out of steel rather than concrete.

`§8.p9` We can ship concrete around the world. Nigeria one time, in their corrupt government of 25 years ago — which is different than the corrupt government of today — they had a guy who ordered 200 shiploads of cement. He was going to rebuild Nigeria with all this oil money, before he got deposed. At one time about 20 or 25 years ago there were 200 ships sitting in the Nigerian harbor full of cement waiting to unload, because they had a strike at the loading docks, or they ordered all this stuff and never figured out how to unload it. I don't know what all the problems were. The ships were sitting there waiting to unload, and in the meantime all the humidity was causing the concrete to set up. All these ships became junk. This was a major crisis in the shipping industry 25 years ago, because a lot of the world's shipping capacity was tied up outside Lagos.

`§8.p10` Cement, concrete, and asphalt are very important composite materials used in very high volume. At the other end of the spectrum, there are some very important composite materials that have fantastic properties, but they tend to be boutique materials. That X-33 space plane — they built two liquid hydrogen tanks, and they weighed a total of 8,000 pounds. I don't care if you get $12,000 a pound for it, it's only a $100 million market. So if you look at market size — large markets up in this corner, these are the boutique materials, may have fantastic properties.

`§8.p11` When we started the manufacturing program at MIT in '89, one faculty member of mechanical engineering, who's no longer in composites but got his tenure in composite materials, was supervising students at Boeing. This was back in the days when they were looking at the 777 and were going to make it all composite. The students went off and found that the value of a pound saved was $188, on the confidential Boeing information. We all knew it was $200 a pound — Boeing knew it was $188, so they had a 6% advantage on all the rest of us, big deal. The students came back after doing their thesis, and this faculty member who had spent eight or ten years of his life getting tenure working on better composites came back and said, I've been working on the wrong thing — I should have been working on cheaper composites.

`§8.p12` Anybody can do it, just like all-aluminum automobiles in the 1930s. You can build a spacecraft out of composites, but can you build a Ford Taurus out of composites? The answer is no. I will predict that we will not be riding around in all-composite Ford Tauruses 20 years from now. 25 years ago I predicted we would not be riding around in all-aluminum Ford Tauruses, at that point 20 years ago. About 10 years later I quit predicting that, because we might be riding around in all-aluminum Ford Tauruses in the next 10 or 15 years. I still don't think we will, but I'm not going to predict it. I don't like to gamble.
