# Case Index — Lecture 8

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p5`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is built around a productivity-and-economics arc that mirrors lecture 11's opening, with extended teaching units on aluminum's price collapse, the optical-fiber rescue of the copper industry, the failure of high-temperature superconductivity, the limits of materials from bonding theory, and composites as the engineering response.

### US copper penny material transition
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US copper penny material transition"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Early-1980s switch from copper to zinc-with-copper-plating, driven by copper-price threshold at which melting pennies becomes profitable. Now repeating with zinc.
- **Era:** early 1980s–present

### High-temperature superconductors hype and failure to solve power transmission
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`, `§6.p5`, `§6.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "High-temperature superconductors hype and failure to solve power transmission"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Central teaching case of §6. The 1986–1989 high-Tc superconductor boom that promised to solve copper-bottleneck problems under New York City; failed because critical temperature alone is insufficient — you also need critical current density, which requires pinning centers smaller than an electron. Used to demonstrate the "limits of materials" principle developed in §7.
- **Era:** 1986–present

### American Superconductor commercial viability (1995–present)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "American Superconductor commercial viability (1995–present)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Greg Yurek's 1989 departure from MIT to found American Superconductor; company has never made money but draws hundreds of millions in research grants on the promise.
- **Era:** 1989–present

### MIT School of Engineering strategy retreat (circa 1990) — NSF superconductor funding directive
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "NSF 1989 superconductor-only funding directive"
- **Frame in this lecture:** NSF announced in 1989 that only high-Tc superconductor proposals would be funded for a year; within one year, half the MIT Materials Science faculty pivoted into the field. Four of the five faculty who had prior superconductor experience stayed out, knowing the critical-current problem was unsolvable.
- **Era:** 1989
- **Note:** Possibly subsumed under the "High-temperature superconductors hype" cluster; flagged for reconciliation.

### Niobium-titanium superconductor development for high-energy physics

- **Frame in this lecture:** GE niobium-tin and other manufacturers' niobium-titanium magnets, ~1960, for physics. Establishes the pre-1986 commercial baseline against which high-Tc was hyped.

### V-22 Osprey aircraft
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`, `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "V-22 Osprey aircraft"
- **Frame in this lecture:** First large all-composite aircraft. Graphite-epoxy structure that would have been impossible in aluminum. Used as the high-end ("boutique," thousands of dollars per pound) composite case.

### X-33 space plane
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`, `§8.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "X-33 space plane"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $12,000-per-pound composite hydrogen tanks, 8,000 lb total mass, $100 million total market — the limiting case for boutique-composite economics.

### Boeing 777 advanced composites program
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`, `§8.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 777 advanced composites program"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Originally planned for 80–90% composite; reduced because the V-22-style economics didn't scale to commercial aviation. The $188-per-pound-saved Boeing internal figure (vs. the industry guess of $200) is the cost-analysis anchor.

### Boeing 787 all-composite aircraft
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 787 all-composite aircraft"
- **Frame in this lecture:** ~80% composite by weight; three years late on a three-year plan because of manufacturing-base maturation challenges. Used as the eventual realization of what the 777 program had to back away from.

### Nigerian cement shipping crisis
- **Anchor:** `§8.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Nigerian cement shipping crisis"
- **Frame in this lecture:** ~25 years before lecture, 200 ships sat full of cement outside Lagos, unloaded for so long that humidity set the concrete in the holds — converted the cargo into junk and consumed a significant fraction of world shipping capacity. Used as the punctuation example for "cement is heavy, transportation cost dominates."
- **Era:** late 1980s

### All-aluminum automobile body cost analysis (early 1990s) / Ford Taurus aluminum body cost analysis
- **Anchor:** `§8.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford Taurus aluminum body cost analysis" (closely related: "All-aluminum automobile body cost analysis (early 1990s)")

- **Era:** 1990s–present
