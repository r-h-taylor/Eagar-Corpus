# Case Index — Lecture 3 (Spring 2016, 3/12)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p5`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage.*

This lecture frames the externalities unit of the course. It opens with class-management material on presentations, summarizes the previous day's sound bites on material-property limits, finishes the 3D-printing-of-metals discussion with several industrial examples, and then pivots into the political, economic, and social externalities that will structure the next several lectures.

## Cases referenced

### Aerogel as structural material (New York Times article case)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p5`, `§1.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aerogel as structural material (New York Times article case)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Example of a student presentation that paraphrased press coverage of a "wonder material" without checking whether it had ever been deployed. Used as a cautionary tale about presentation scope.
- **Materials/systems:** Aerogel (density half that of water; soap-bubble structural integrity)
- **Era:** ~2000 article; lecture 2016

### Aluminum silicon carbide brake calipers
- **Anchor:** `§1.p8`, `§1.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum silicon carbide brake calipers"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Example of a sufficiently narrow presentation topic, and substantive failure mode (brake fade from aluminum creep under high temperature).
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum-silicon-carbide composite, disc brake calipers
- **Era:** ~1990

### Carbon nanotubes structural applications (overpromised research)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p4`, `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Carbon nanotubes structural applications (overpromised research)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to make the point that the 3-million-psi theoretical strength of carbon nanotubes is unrealizable because of vacancy defects, and that the calculation was done in the 1930s. Buckyball Nobel laureate (presumably Richard Smalley at Rice) gets credit for rediscovery.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon nanotubes, buckyballs (C60)
- **Era:** 1930s theory; 1985 buckyball discovery; 1990s–present hype

### Iron whiskers (1950s screw dislocation studies)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Iron whiskers (1950s screw dislocation studies)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Counter-example to the "defects destroy strength" rule — iron whiskers grown as single crystals with one axial screw dislocation achieved 2.2 million psi because the dislocation was not loaded.
- **Materials/systems:** Iron whiskers, screw dislocations
- **Era:** 1940s measurement

### 3D metal printing propeller test for US Navy
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "3D metal printing propeller test for US Navy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stainless steel propeller deposition trial. Surface tension drove a defect on the edge to propagate and grow. Used to illustrate why metal surface tension (10× higher than other materials) makes 3D printing of metals hard.
- **Materials/systems:** Stainless steel, electron-beam/laser deposition
- **Era:** Recent (Tom's research)

### Manganese aluminum bronze electron-beam 3D printing trial
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`, `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Manganese aluminum bronze electron-beam 3D printing trial"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Better-wetting alloy, deposited in circles with electron beam. Shows acceleration humps at scan reversals (Newton's laws) and residual-stress problems despite better surface-tension balance.
- **Materials/systems:** Manganese-aluminum-bronze wire/tube, electron beam additive
- **Era:** Recent (Tom's research)

### US Air Force turbine vane repair program (DAABER)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`, `§3.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Air Force turbine vane repair program (DAABER)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The "dabber" — arc welding machine to rebuild worn turbine vane tips. Five-hundred-dollar vane, couple hundred dollars repair, three thousand more hours of life. Ten-million-dollar automated machine in Oklahoma City repair facility making fifty million on the blades. Used to argue that we have been doing additive manufacturing for eighty years on high-value-added parts.
- **Materials/systems:** Nickel-based superalloy turbine vanes
- **Era:** ~1990 (Tom's "twenty-five years ago")

### POSCO continuous caster roll refurbishment
- **Anchor:** `§3.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "POSCO continuous caster roll refurbishment"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Twelve-foot, foot-diameter caster rolls refurbished by lathe-cutting damaged surface, preheating to 600–800°F with flames, and laying down weld metal. Stress relief happens in-process from flame temperature. Used as another long-standing example of industrial additive manufacturing.
- **Materials/systems:** Steel caster rolls, weld-metal buildup
- **Era:** Tom's visit ~when POSCO was the world's largest steel plant

### Turbine blade electron-beam repair and preheating
- **Anchor:** `§3.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Turbine blade electron-beam repair and preheating"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Hot-section turbine blade tip repair by electron beam, with preheat to 1000°C / 1800°F to prevent nickel-superalloy cracking under thermal stress.
- **Materials/systems:** Nickel-base superalloy turbine blades
- **Era:** Recent

### MIT Commission on Industrial Productivity - Steel Industry Study
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT Commission on Industrial Productivity - Steel Industry Study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom was a commission member, did the steel industry section. Dertouzos chaired (CSAIL head), Lester, Solow (Nobel laureate). Book *Made in America* — opening line: "to live well a nation must produce well." Frames the productivity-vs-competitiveness teaching unit.
- **Materials/systems:** N/A (policy)
- **Era:** Mid-1980s commission; 1989 book

### 1984-1985 Japan Manufacturing Study
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1984-1985 Japan Manufacturing Study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's sabbatical in Japan, walking through factories and research labs. Concluded they weren't more productive — they were more competitive because of the 240 yen/dollar exchange rate.
- **Materials/systems:** N/A
- **Era:** 1984–85

### General Motors 1980s automation and competitive decline
- **Anchor:** `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Motors 1980s automation and competitive decline"
- **Frame in this lecture:** GM VP big on robotics, spent fifty billion in the 1980s automating — same fifty billion that could have purchased Toyota if Japanese law had allowed it.
- **Materials/systems:** Automotive manufacturing automation
- **Era:** 1980s

### China rare earth embargo on Japan
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`, `§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "China rare earth embargo on Japan"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Canonical political-externality case. Chinese de facto monopoly (90% of world production), embargo on Japan, MIT faculty response funded to develop clean rare-earth extraction.
- **Materials/systems:** Neodymium-iron-boron magnets, samarium-cobalt magnets, rare earth oxides
- **Era:** ~2010

### 1973 Arab oil embargo
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`, `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1973 Arab oil embargo"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Two-dollar-a-barrel oil, embargo, screeching halt, four-or-five-hour gas lines. Industry responded by 1978 with fuel-switching capability, so the 1978 and 1982 embargo attempts failed. Frames political externalities and infrastructure response.
- **Materials/systems:** Crude oil, dual-fuel power plants
- **Era:** 1973 (also 1978, 1982)

### Tank explosion at Pennzoil Oil City refinery
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`, `§8.p3`, `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Tank explosion at Pennzoil Oil City refinery"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Forensic consulting case ca. 1995. 1920s riveted storage tanks, oil-saturated tank farm ground. Tom figures out at breakfast that Pennzoil keeps the refinery running not for production economics but because shutting it would trigger EPA cleanup assessment. Used to illustrate economic externalities.
- **Materials/systems:** Riveted carbon steel storage tanks, refinery infrastructure
- **Era:** 1995 consulting; facility 1890s–1990s

### Flint Michigan lead in drinking water
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Flint Michigan lead in drinking water"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mentioned in passing as current news (Spring 2016) to introduce lead as a social externality. Pivots to Tom's own house renovation to remove lead paint.
- **Materials/systems:** Lead service pipes, lead paint
- **Era:** 2014–16 crisis

## Figures referenced (not cases)
- **Carbon nanotube theoretical strength:** 3 million psi. The fundamental bond-energy limit on material strength. (§2.p2, §2.p3, §2.p4)
- **Modulus ceiling:** 60 million psi / ~400 GPa. Bond-stiffness limit. (§2.p3)
- **Iron whisker measured strength:** 2.2 million psi (1940s). (§2.p5)
- **Manufacturing employment share, 1980:** >20% of US workforce. (§4.p5)
- **Agricultural workforce, 1790 / 1980 / 2016:** 97% / 3% / 1.5%. Thirty-fold productivity improvement over two centuries. (§6.p3, §6.p4)
- **GM 1980s automation investment:** $50 billion, equal to the market cap of Toyota at the time. (§4.p6)
- **Yen-dollar exchange, 1985 / 2016:** 240 / 100. The sixty-percent "discount" on Japanese debt repayment. (§5.p2, §5.p4)
- **Saudi vs. Nigerian oil production cost:** $5 vs. $90 per barrel. US fracker marginal cost <$30. (§7.p7, §7.p8)
