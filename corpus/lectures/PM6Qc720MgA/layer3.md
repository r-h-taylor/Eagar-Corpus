## §2. The aircraft wing flap: heated leading edge [03:28]

`§2.p1` For the aircraft example, I want to talk about the front flap of an aircraft wing. I worked in small aircraft — they were building the original regional jets, so when you take a shuttle from here to DC or from here to Cleveland, the shuttle type aircraft for 70 to 90 passengers will have this design where the engine is not right here, it's actually close to the tail in the back, and that makes the wings a little smaller. So it's a very narrow and elongated design. Even when your wings are smaller, you're more dependent on them. You don't want them covered with ice when you're going through a transition.

`§2.p2` So they have this heating system for the leading edge of the wing. When you take off in a storm, you've probably seen it — they have firefighter-type jets to apply the de-icing fluid, but the heating system also helps against icing. For a critical component like the front leading edge, this geometry has a lot of effect on the aerodynamics. When you pull it out — for takeoff and landing you pull the leading-edge flap out, so you have more support and you create turbulence under the wing to help keep the airplane up in the air. If it's twice as heavy, the safety factor on the arm holding it out is affected. So you need all of that to work in the same condition even if you're going through a blizzard or an ice storm.

`§2.p3` The big hole here is for air circulation. They recirculate hot air from the engine back into the wings to keep it warm. The system to do that is a little complicated, because you can't set very precisely what the temperature of the air coming off the engine is going to be. You can have some control over that, but only so much. There's a window of opportunity to collect that air, and it's typically hot. So you go through a design process where you say, my air temperature is going to be between so much and so much, and then you control the heating by the volume of air you're going to blow through the wing.

`§2.p4` But the temperature of the air that the metal here is going to see will vary. When I worked at that aircraft company, they had PhD guys putting sensors and sending the aircraft up in the air and running it to have some idea of the temperature. But they never really came up with "this is the maximum value, it's never going to see any higher temperature than that." There was a lot of variation. Given that, it was very hard from a design standpoint to say we're going to go with aluminum. You could easily get to a temperature where you get softening of a heat-treated aluminum — say above 200 centigrade for an extended period of time, and we don't really know the extent of the period of time. So for the primary structure, what is painted in green here, the design that was certified with the FAA and the Canadian equivalent was titanium, because it's able to take higher temperatures and it's resistant to corrosion.

`§2.p5` There's definitely a stiffness mismatch between the titanium rib here and the aluminum skin. Why on the aluminum skin do we say okay, let's live with it? A couple of factors. One is you always have cooling from the outside, so the skin is always going to be touching the storm air and will be cooler. Also, if the surface of the wing got a little softer, it'd be easy to figure out — if you have a crack on the exterior fuselage, you can see it. When it's on the interior, we have the benefit here of looking at a cut piece (this was a test piece), but in reality this wing is long and you don't see all the ribs. So when we're talking about an acceptance criteria for crack length, it would have to be a very long crack. It would probably have to be a fail-safe way of designing if you were to say I'm going to allow this component to crack. The way it was working out was essentially this should not be cracked — or if it's cracked, it should not grow to a significant length within the service life of that wing component, which is limited by a number of factors, and then you have to take it apart and really inspect it because it's not easy to inspect on a day-to-day basis.

## §3. Replacing the machined titanium rib with a casting [09:20]

`§3.p1` That was a design that was about 10 years old by the time I was there, and it obviously took maybe five years to certify, so it had been produced for about five years. They were getting into a reconsideration stage; they wanted to be competitive. This was a Canadian company; they wanted to be competitive with the Brazilians for the same market. So they had to start looking at their cost. In the first round they said, we want to pass the FAA, we don't want to go through all the details, we've got to be done with this. It's going to take five years; if we try to optimize everything, it's going to take longer. So they had picked that component as a wrought plate — three and a half inch thick, a slab essentially of titanium — and that's what they were using to machine out this detailed rib structure that had only about an eighth of an inch of thickness in the center.

`§3.p2` If we look at it in cross section, this is pretty much an I-shape. You have the top surface, you have the web in the middle, and the web at some locations even had a hole in it. Then you have the other surface that's contacting the wing. One of the reasons for having this large a surface is for stiffness, but it's also providing for fastening the skin. So we have the lower skin riveted through, and the upper skin with rivets here and here. It gives a tab for connecting the two components. These rivets, obviously there's a criteria so they don't extend out from the fuselage — that would create too much friction, so they have to be embedded, and therefore they can only be so big. That's one of the reasons why they put a lot of them, and they needed to put one on each side for this design.

`§3.p3` So when we're starting from a plate three and a half inches thick across, and you end up with — it's not to scale here, but only an eighth of an inch — that's a lot of loss of material. It's a very expensive process. Titanium is difficult to machine. So as part of the optimization, the question was, can we make the same geometry but out of a casting? It would be awesome. You make the mold, you cast the titanium alloy, you do some finishing, you can re-machine some critical surfaces — the outside here had to be really flat with a very specific radius shape, a curved shape to maximize the aerodynamics. On the interior, as long as it was strong enough, there wouldn't be a problem. So if I go tomorrow and cast this to be a replacement, I'll make it a little bigger so I can machine the outside, and I will leave the interior as-cast if I can get the same properties out of it.

`§3.p4` With respect to commercial aircraft, that was a big deal. We had to run all the standard tests — tensile tests to get yield, ultimate, strain; fracture toughness tests (we talked about $K_C$ in the last class); fatigue tests where you cycle a sample that has a crack in it and look at how fast it's growing over time. Fatigue with a notch, without a notch — we had all that data.

`§3.p5` But at the end of the day — and this is what's very specific to the safety factor — you can have a casting factor of safety, which means that everything is nice. I have this manufacturer that can make the mold, they pour the titanium in a very controlled environment, and they make these shapes that have great properties. But what if one day there's a piece of ceramic that takes off from the mold and gets embedded into the casting? Then everything we've done as far as property testing is off the map. The only way you can rely on a casting for this type of application, where a failure would be a big problem, was to do 100% inspection. We could not assume that the casting was going to be right more than 100% of the time, you know, to the second or third digit probability that it will work perfectly. That was the biggest concern in dealing with a casting for critical application.

`§3.p6` Titanium castings, by the way, turn out to be very strong — they have very high fracture toughness, very high casting properties — so they are a good candidate for replacement in an application like this. But then comes the reliability in manufacturing. That's where that special inspection came in. It was a full-through-the-thickness inspection; they were going to do different non-destructive testing methods to verify there weren't any little pieces from the mold that came into the pour.

`§3.p7` The good news, when you look at this by engineering, is there's clearly going to be a side in tension and a side in compression. The web — we can put holes in it and it's still fine. So if you have a little impurity in the mold, it's okay. You had essentially to verify that your top flange was going to have the properties you require. Having a little impurity on the compression side will not affect the buckling capacity, which is a controlling factor here, and same for the web. So there was only a small portion that really had to be a hundred percent detailed inspection to look for anomalies from the casting.

`§3.p8` That was the way to get away from the casting factor of safety — something that Boeing had to use a lot in their design. A lot of times it was 1.25, but you could be higher depending on the application and the consequence of not getting it right on the inside of the part. You can always have a visual criteria — they were also doing liquid penetrant testing on all the surfaces, whether it was tension or compression — but as far as looking at the inside to see if you had any impurities, that was reserved for the tension side.

`§3.p9` So it did work out, but it took a long time for the aircraft people to say fine, we can fly with castings. For a small aviation company, most of it was looking at what others were doing and justifying your decision based on what they were doing, as opposed to doing your full-blown research yourself. By the time we found examples where people did the same thing — replacing an inspection after manufacturing for the casting factor of safety — it was becoming a lot easier to make the substitution.

`§3.p10` There was still a part involved with actually making them and verifying in a full assembly that they would be equivalent. I was gone by then. But they couldn't just put it on. They still had to do a full-scale testing even though it has the same elastic properties; there was just no risk to take with respect to a component that was going to fit right in.

## §4. Loads you didn't anticipate: the landing gear torsion case [19:12]

`§4.p1` When you look at it, that's an example of substitution. Honestly, they didn't really know all the loads that could get onto that component. They were looking in a stress analysis at different scenarios, combining them, but there was a little bit of a match with what happened in flight tests. They had gauges that would look at the stresses at locations they knew were going to be high-stress locations from the design standpoint, from the computer analysis. They still had to calibrate all that with an actual physical test. The ultimate test before you could fly that wing was attaching it to a fuselage and bending it up and down about three feet. That's about how much flexibility you get out of these wings. You have to go back and forth a lot of times before something eventually lets go, and then you have to figure out, is that okay, was that something we were planning on seeing let go at a certain point in time? It was a displacement-based analysis, and they may have increased the displacement after you get more compliance out of it. It's very severe testing, for a full assembly to go through. But it was critical. If you start popping rivets in one specific location, now you go back and say, huh, my analysis didn't predict that, so what did I do wrong, because it's definitely happening now.

`§4.p2` So it's a good way for complex assemblies to really say, hey, we're giving this a shot, this is supposed to be a relatively optimized design. These are the low points, and then you test it out and find out, is that really the case.

`§4.p3` There is another project I was working on at the same time, looking at the landing gear, where in those tests where they were just applying about twice the load that it would see at service, they had this crack growing really fast. Because of a torsional effect on one of the structural components that they never really analyzed for torsion — they did the tension, the bending, but they never really thought torsion would become a dominant mode. So it was a good way to identify the problem — not so early, because they already had the weight of the aircraft sold for. And it was a very big heavy steel component, and it was not strong enough. So they had to find — it became a very special team put on this task of replacing it with something hopefully of the same weight, and if not, then it goes to the accounting people for the weight of the aircraft. If you take more weight there, now you need to take it out of somewhere else. You've got to be spending money somewhere to maintain your overall baseline.

`§4.p4` And it becomes a negotiation. Well, I don't want to change my design, I don't want to change my design, but somebody has to give up some weight, at the expense of cost. And the later it is in the design and in that kind of business, it becomes very complicated. We were very close to the end, and they were still fighting between going to titanium, keeping the steel with a different processing and a little stiffer, or just redesigning the whole thing. But you couldn't leave it as is — that was not an option. You knew you screwed up in a very specific loading mode that you didn't expect in a full assembly.

`§4.p5` When you go back and say we're going to change the design of this, you can't say, oh, I'm going to go back and do a bending test at all these components and figure out that they are equivalent. Because you don't really know all the loading that's going to be applied. If you're looking at one of the ribs, it may very well be loaded here and here, or in a diagonal mode, and you'll have some bending and maybe this is a zone of tension — how do I know? I haven't analyzed it in a full assembly. I've thought about it, I looked at the overall geometry, I have the wind pressure, the uplift, all of that, but what about everything else? Torsion along the long axis is one of them.

`§4.p6` That's also one I didn't mention when we looked at the helicopter blade last week. With a helicopter blade, you also have a torsion component that you need to take into account. The tail part needs to be able to handle shear as well. That's how you get shear stresses between your skin and the honeycomb in the back, just by the wing trying to tear this. There's only so much from a design standpoint that you want to allow for that torsional rotation, because otherwise you don't really get the attack angle you were designing for.

`§4.p7` So there's a lot that comes into every single piece. When the geometry becomes important, that's really where you have to think about strength, but you also have to think about keeping the shape and limiting the deflection to what's allowed. It's very complex. It all started with saying, oh, we want to be more critical of our processing so we can reduce our factor of safety to the same level as if we machine off that very thick component. But then in the process, you have to go through all the testing because you don't know exactly your loads. You're never going to know perfectly all the loads, at least not in anything that I worked on.

## §6. Roof joists: the demand side [27:46]

`§6.p1` Roof joists. We're looking at a couple of things here. We have the little joist, and then we have truss girders — we could think of these as more like trusses, so they are holding multiple of these little joists. The joist's typical design is relatively simple. You have the top, you have a bottom, and you hold the pieces together with a bunch of diagonals. Sometimes you may decide to put two diagonals up front, because that's really where you're going to have the highest amount of shear between top and bottom, if these are simply supported. So this is just basic structural engineering — not really part of the class, but for your benefit, you end up with these few pieces at each end that have a lot more load on them.

`§6.p2` That's why, if you go to a parking lot and they're doing an extension to a building, you may see these and they'll have a different type of diagonal on the first member than what's going on after that. It's not a guarantee, but what I have seen, when we had snowstorms and all of this, is if you didn't survive here — if these happen to be bad — then you will unzip the whole thing and have either the roof sag or just come down altogether. But if we have very bad welding right in the middle, some of these are there with very limited loads on them. So they're there to keep the shape, but there's not a whole lot of stress, because you don't have a lot of mismatch.

`§6.p3` When I apply the displacement down here, what I'm doing is stretching these, and they stretch differently. That's why I get an overall extension and compression near the ends. In the middle, they're more or less staying together. It's like a sandwich. If you push on it, then the two pieces of bread will slide with respect to each other at each end, but you'll stay together in the middle. That's kind of what's happening with a joist.

`§6.p4` Now the way you design these — you have the dead weight. This has a self-weight. You're going to put a roof on it, you'll have insulation, waterproofing, all sorts of things. That's easy to know, you can have a number for that. The snow load — the code has very specific requirements for you. So if you're designing a roof as a structural engineer (I don't suggest you do it if you're not a structural engineer), you have to go by very specific requirements for the location you're building that roof for. If you have different heights — if this is a large warehouse like Costco, and you add an addition that has a different height, now you have to worry about the snow drift. If you're going to pile up more snow, because now you have two elevations, you have to worry about whether you're close to the edge or in the middle. If you have a big mechanical system like a big air conditioning unit, that would also create some snow drift, and there are rules for all of it.

`§6.p5` So when we're talking about — say we're going to call this the demand here — well, it's very prescriptive. You can go to the code and it'll tell you this is the load by square foot overall that you're putting on, and that you have to design for. As a designer, you can say, well, I'm going to play with this thickness here if I want to. I can play with the size of the members, I can put more diagonals at the beginning. A lot of it, I found out, was based on buckling and deflection. So if I come with the amount of steel that's on the joist and I join it properly, it will buckle before it fails.

## §7. Joist welds: the capacity side and failure modes [32:10]

`§7.p1` But from a manufacturing standpoint, when we come to capacities, people come to the assumption that I need a geometry that's right. That means, say this is a compression diagonal here, this is tension, this is compression — just by the way it works out. If this is a compression, it's the size of it that I'm designing for. A lot of the time it'll be, say, a round bar that's full, because it's cheaper to buy than to buy an empty tube that's harder to weld at the connection and cut. A lot of it is based on cost.

`§7.p2` When I come down to it, the reason I'm talking about this is the connections. What about the connections? They can't fail, especially those that are in tension. That's really an area where the manufacturer has to play a role, because there are some inspection criteria for the welds, but they're not perfectly respected by the people in the industry. So even though something is written, when people go out, they don't necessarily do all these inspections all the time.

`§7.p3` And the question becomes, what are we designing for? What you'll see people doing for the critical connection is, some people will come up with a design that's easier to inspect. If I'm asking somebody to make a weld that's difficult and I can't really inspect it easily, it's a lot more risky a design than if I tell them, do me a fillet weld — and we talked a bit about fillet welds — and I'm going to look at it, and as long as it's not too convex we're good to go, or it doesn't have too low of a contact angle.

`§7.p4` So that's the idea — you have to — I would say it's more on the capacity side that there is judgment. Anybody could — this is like an accounting process where the code tells you this, the code tells you that, the code tells you if you have this utility just figure out what your extra potential drift load is based on this and that. So it's all going to be computerized. It's not perfect, but to some extent it's a consistent approach.

`§7.p5` What happens on the capacity side — and what I found out, of nine different collapses that I looked at over about a one-year period, there was only one job where they didn't have any welding issue. It's not because you couldn't make these connections strong enough; they were just not built strong enough. Now it doesn't mean that all the joists have low-quality welds, because obviously I went on to what came down primarily. We did have some inspection on existing structures that were not under distress. But when we talked about a failure in that specific industry, what I have seen is a lot of this.

`§7.p6` When it comes to the welds or the connections to be a little more general, they didn't really know. Nobody could come and tell me, yeah, when we test this, it's going to be good for so much. There were stories of people just using a prying device to just test it out, see if it will hold. That's not to the same amount of load that you could have in service. It's just a way to figure out if your welding had some fusion.

## §8. Weld fusion failures: convexity and one-sided welds [36:26]

`§8.p1` Talking about weld fusion — maybe the last thing — these are some of the weld failures. You see here that it's just breaking off. We're looking at this location, but from underneath. The issue becomes, it shouldn't fail just like that and right at the interface between the angle of the joist and the welded bead. So you're looking at the welded bead here and it's just splitting apart. It was very common.

`§8.p2` That's a big warehouse for tomatoes — greenhouse — that I worked on. Same story, where you look at this weld and we talked a little bit about convexity. So maybe today you'd be able to say, if you go somewhere and you see this, oh, this looks like it's a little sort of deposited on the surface, as opposed to being really well fused. It has a low contact angle here. This is the same type of weld that, after the fact, you can see it just pull off from the surface of that chord, as opposed to ripping the metal apart with it.

`§8.p3` We had some cross-section views of this, so you can see on this side the contact angle — it's between this location and this — it's less than 90 degrees. And you see the interface right here, so it's not fused. This is the diagonal that has a little bend with a flat portion to be welded, and you have the lower chord of the joist, and right here you have very limited — you have a little bit of grab, but if you start cracking right here, it will pull right off. On the other side, you have one here where the contact angle, between this and this, is probably 130 degrees. So this is actually a very high quality fusion, and it penetrates here. You can see the line of the welded metal.

`§8.p4` If you don't have a good way to avoid a low-fusion weld a certain percent of the time, you need to be able to live with the consequences of having one that fails. So are you going to weld on both sides, or just on one side? You could argue, I'm very unlucky if I get a bad weld on both sides — that's less likely than getting it wrong just on one side. But if you weld just from one side, now your chances of a successful connection are going down. And you have a more complex loading because you have eccentricity.

`§8.p5` That's what they had here — they had this weld connecting it off-centered from that rectangular tube, and they had no welds on the opposite side. You can see here there's this little amount that's coming off, the paint's coming off, and there was no weld further down the road on the other side of that round shape. So no redundancy, and unknown capacity, because it's a lot of welds — how exactly are you going to go and inspect those?

`§8.p6` In the other class, I talked a lot about why that weld is difficult to do. The bottom idea is you're looking at a very heavy section — the diagonal coming down — and it's got to be welded to this rectangular tube, but the tube is thin. So you're looking at very different thicknesses of metals to be welded in a tight spot. Instead of having a fillet weld that's ready just like this and you make a nice bead, now you have to deal with — it's on a round. You have this empty route, and you're just trying to somehow deposit something in there. It's a lot harder weld to make than just at a corner.

`§8.p7` Your arc is bouncing up and down, so you have to be hitting with the arc — you need to be hitting one side or the other. The way to do it that I've seen the most successful welders do, they'll really weave their arc one side to the other, and they'll spend a little more time on this side and on this side. But you're becoming reliant on the expertise of your welder. If you do so, you better have a program to make sure the guys really always do it consistently. And when you have somebody new coming to work, they also have to qualify before they get into the production. That's not always easy.

## §9. Wrap-up: factors of safety across the semester [41:41]

`§9.p1` I think that's going to be it for the factors of safety for this semester. Let me wrap up very quickly. We talked about different factors of safety between industries, and we sort of trimmed it down to say in reality it's not that different. The one I was hitting on the most was the safety factor for the pressure vessel, where I pointed out, you have water hammers, you have other loads, you have welds that come into the design, and they're not really accounted for. You don't need to say, oh, I'm going to have maybe a water hammer — everybody can have it. It's part of the code, and it's built into that factor of safety of four.

`§9.p2` We talked about demand and capacity in different industries. You can see in some industries the code — the joist is a good example — will tell you, this is the demand you design for. And when you go to capacity, well, there's only so much you can do if it's dependent on the ability of a welder. Your strength depends on the quality of the welder. You will have more variability, so you need a factor of safety that's higher for that than if it's only going to be a bolted connection where you just have to make sure the bolt isn't loose with a nut. So when you look at capacity — how difficult is your design to manufacture, and what kind of inspection are you going to enforce in practice? You can always say, yeah, inspect it, but is it really part of the contract? Are you going to check for it, or are you just going to say, yeah, it's going to be a half-inch weld of good quality or whatever. There's all sorts of email communication — do me this — okay, well, good quality to me is, I guess, a little subjective. And that goes back to one of Professor Eagar's lectures: if you can't quantify what's good, then how are you going to control it later?

`§9.p3` The probability of failure — we touched on that again at the beginning — there will be a different safety factor, or a final acceptable safety factor, for different types of structures. So I'm not going to rule that out. We can't say, we're over-designing everything by 20 percent. It's not true. It will vary depending on how willing you are to risk a failure. We had that propeller shaft example that was designed to fail within 400 hours, and therefore the safety factor for a 10-year life is less than one. It's just the way it is.

`§9.p4` This one I think is what I'd like you to always keep in mind. You start with a factor of safety. We build something together, we go and test it out here on Memorial Drive, and we come back, everything is fine. But as this product goes through a life, it may get beat up in a certain area, or get some scratches. You do need to know that your factor of safety is going to come down. Depending how long you expect this to be in service, you have to start with — maybe you can start with a 1.2 safety factor, that's the propeller shaft, more or less. Or the roof joist — you start with two, and then the guys screw up the welding, and you're close to one right from the get-go. You just don't know how likely it is that all of this will happen, but over time your safety factor is going down. It's not because you start with a safety factor of three that two or three years down the road you can say, oh yeah, you can put 20 percent more load on it.

`§9.p5` That's really what I want you to keep in mind. When we do a bridge, it's not because it started with a safety factor of two that you can add 20% of construction load at any point in time on it. You don't know what the actual factor of safety is for this structure as it's being built — it was built a while ago, went through service, corrosion, everything you name it. They already added lanes — anything that can happen will happen sometimes. It's all eating into that shrinking pie. If you start twice as strong, how much are you going to lose in manufacturing? Maybe you don't lose anything, that's your prototype, but in real manufacturing some welds are not as good, so now you take out 50 percent. You just don't know, I think that's the bottom line.

`§9.p6` And the service history is another thing I hope you keep in mind. When something has been used a long time in the same way, you know it's safe, but how much can you modify it becomes the question, because you still don't have all the groundwork on where you stand with respect to demand and capacity. You know you're fine. Are you too good, or just exactly where you should be? That requires analysis. It's doable, with those statistics that we talked about and looking at other designs. But it's not something you can say, oh yeah, we know it's designed with a safety factor of three and we never had a failure, so we're going to add 20 percent load. You need more justification than just the idea that you have a large safety factor and therefore nobody wants to go to the calc again and see if we're still meeting code. You can modify if you're still within a history envelope, like a code. It's when you go beyond that, you're more or less on your own.
