## §4. The consulting parable: the foreman knows [18:17]

`§4.p1` Students would ask me how do you consult for a company. One of the problems in consulting with a company is that management called you in as a consultant because they had their own engineers who'd been looking at this problem for several weeks and can't solve it. So they're going to call someplace like MIT and find somebody who supposedly knows something. They're going to bring them down, pay them hundreds of dollars an hour. And the engineers who are making $75 an hour or whatever are not really all that happy about having someone come in. And the worst thing that expert can do is walk in and shoot from the cuff and give the answer in the first five minutes.

`§4.p2` So you go into a meeting when you get there as a consultant, and you've got the managers who want the problem solved but they don't really know what the problem is — that's why they brought you in — and their engineers can't explain it to them. They just start talking engineering, and it's like two people talking different foreign languages. The managers will introduce you to the engineers, the engineers will explain the problem. But they don't want to really explain everything, because if they think you're going to solve it quickly, it's going to make them look bad.

`§4.p3` So you listen to all this, and then you ask, well, can we go out in the plant and take a tour? They all say yeah, they're all ready for a break. Got to get a new cup of coffee. You go out in the plant and you're walking around, and you find the foreman, the hourly worker. You find a chance to talk to that guy. First you have to establish a little rapport with him about this pretty interesting machine he's working with here. And you ask him — or her — what the problem is, and they will tell you. They know what the problem is. The answer is right there in that company.

`§4.p4` But the managers and engineers don't respect the hourly worker who's been doing this for 40 years and knows exactly what the problem is, and has tried to tell them, and has gotten to the point where he refuses to tell them anything else. It's all a communications problem. So he tells you what it is, he tells you in his jargon, his language. You go back into the conference room, you translate it into manager-ese and engineering-ese for everyone else. You walk out having solved the problem. You didn't solve the problem, you didn't know what the problem was — all you did was help them. And they still don't even understand it afterwards. Even if you tell them the story, they don't understand. The answer was right there in their own factory, and they just didn't have the respect for each other to ask each other, and they didn't try to speak each other's language.

`§4.p5` It's mostly communications problems, folks. It's not technical problems.
