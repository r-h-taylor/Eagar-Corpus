## Cases referenced in this lecture

### GE 1950s vacuum tube laser repair
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric vacuum tube laser repair" — matches canonical "General Electric vacuum tube laser repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Earliest commercial laser welding application. Used to illustrate that any new heat source gets tried for welding.
- **Materials/systems:** Tungsten/molybdenum posts inside vacuum tubes; CO₂ or ruby laser through glass envelope
- **Era:** Mid-1950s

### Naval Surface Warfare Center particle beam weapon program
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval Surface Warfare Center particle beam weapon development" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** Post-Cold-War repurposing question — could relativistic electron beams weld submarines? Tom's answer: too much power, too much shielding, not practical. Used to illustrate the upper end of the heat-intensity scale and the limits of "just throw more energy at it."
- **Materials/systems:** Pulsed-power relativistic electron beams; nuclear submarine steel
- **Era:** Early 1990s (workshop ca. 1992-93); program built late 1980s at White Oak

### Fourier's law of heat conduction
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** *figure, not a case* — framing physics anchor for the lecture
- **Frame in this lecture:** q = -k∇T. The mathematical backbone of the entire one-dimensional heat-intensity argument.
- **Note:** Tom slips and says "Forte's law" — recurring spoken slip, corrected as `Forte's [Fourier's]` per catalog §9.

### World Trade Center collapse article
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`, `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "World Trade Center collapse" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 2001 article (written three weeks after 9/11) used as a handout to teach flame chemistry. The teaching point: steel did not melt — it can barely be melted with oxy-acetylene in air. Bessemer's 1856 process exists *because* melting steel is hard.
- **Materials/systems:** Structural steel, hydrocarbon fires, nitrogen-diluted combustion
- **Era:** 2001 (article); building 1973

### MAPP gas discontinuation
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`, `§3.p5`, `§3.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MAPP gas cylinder drop and bending incidents" — closest canonical match; this lecture's mention is about the EPA shutdown of the Chicago manufacturer, not drop incidents. **PROPOSED:** distinct case "MAPP gas discontinuation and propylene substitution"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Live torch demonstration comparing C₃H₈ (propane) vs C₃H₄ (MAPP) — MAPP heats faster, steel reaches red heat first. Used to illustrate enthalpy-of-reaction effect on flame temperature. Side teaching: MAPP no longer manufactured (EPA closure of Chicago plant ~2011); plumbers now use propylene C₃H₆ in yellow cylinders.
- **Materials/systems:** Hydrocarbon fuel gases, steel welding wire
- **Era:** EPA shutdown ca. 2011; demo current

### Elihu Thomson and the invention of resistance spot welding
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`, `§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** **PROPOSED:** "Elihu Thomson invention of resistance spot welding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Historical origin of automotive spot welding. Thomson — MIT EE professor, one-year MIT president, GE co-founder with Edison in Lynn, MA. ~380 patents (#2 after Edison's ~400).
- **Era:** 1890s

### Automotive spot welding (GM and Ford volumes)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`, `§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Automobile spot welding quality" — partial match; this is the volume/cost framing rather than the quality issue
- **Frame in this lecture:** 3,000 spot welds per automobile to get 2,000 good ones; nickel per weld → ~$150/car. GM ~50 billion spot welds per year. Aerospace combustor cans: ~$3/weld due to QC overhead.
- **Era:** Ongoing

### Lawrence Livermore plutonium weld observation
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lawrence Livermore plutonium weld observation" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom shown a weld cross-section with no visible heat affected zone. Material concealed; Tom pinned them down after asking four or five times. Answer: plutonium, very low thermal conductivity. Used to illustrate that HAZ size scales with thermal conductivity.
- **Materials/systems:** Plutonium
- **Era:** Unspecified

### Avco Everett 25 kW laser welder development
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Avco Everett laser welder demonstrations (early 1980s)" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student hired in early '80s to keep the laser working when customers came for demos — 90% of the time it wasn't running. Used to illustrate that high-power laser welding was a research curiosity, not a production tool, despite the capital investment. Avco facility location: Everett, across from BNY Mellon building.

### Naval Research Laboratory 25 kW laser welder
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Navy 25 kilowatt laser welder at Naval Research Laboratory" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** First non-classified 25 kW laser welder in the country, mid-1970s. Cited as a Navy investment that didn't pan out economically because shipyard work lacks both aerospace value-add and automotive volume.
- **Era:** Mid-1970s

### Ford catalytic converter electron beam welding
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford catalytic converter electron beam welding" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** Six out-of-vacuum EB welders at Ford sealing stainless-steel clamshell converters around ceramic filters. Speed ~60 cm/s. Used as the canonical automotive high-volume EB case. Maintenance nightmare on out-of-vacuum head (deferred to later lecture).
- **Materials/systems:** Stainless steel sheet, ceramic substrate
- **Era:** Tom's reference point: "a few years ago" from 2013 → ca. late 1990s–2000s

### GE titanium compressor case EB porosity (the acetone case)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3` through `§8.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric titanium compressor case electron beam welding porosity" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended teaching case (~9 minutes, the longest narrative unit of the lecture). Two $30K titanium rings welded circumferentially in a chamber half the size of the room. Persistent porosity. Tom diagnoses: industrial-grade acetone (oil-contaminated, demonstrated via Newton's rings on a glass slide) + dust falling on the joint surface in the welding room. Fix: reagent-grade acetone. Savings: $500K/year. Tom: no credit, no payment, no letter of commendation; engineer Lyle got a letter.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium (compressor case alloy, ~Ti-6-4 likely); industrial vs reagent acetone; electron beam weld porosity
- **Era:** Mid-1980s (concurrent with Tom's Japan posting and the SWAC spot-welding contract)
- **Related case:** GE SWAC spot-welding adaptive control failure (§8.p4-p5) — see below

### GE SWAC (Spot Welding Adaptive Control) failure
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`, `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric adaptive weld control feedback timing failure" — matches canonical; closely related to "General Electric spot weld adaptive control system"
- **Frame in this lecture:** GE Research Schenectady spent $7M over 5-6 years developing adaptive feedback control for spot welding combustor cans. Tom (with postdoc and grad student, while Tom was in Japan as ONR liaison) demonstrated the algorithm didn't start measuring until 0.2 sec into a process that completes in 0.1 sec — controlling after the fact. Tom was funded as a "whipping boy" to discipline GE Research; he received ~$40-50K against their >$1M/year.
- **Materials/systems:** Spot welds in combustor can stainless steel
- **Era:** Mid-1980s

### Bimetallic bandsaw/hacksaw blade electron beam welding
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5`, `§9.p6`, `§9.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bimetallic bandsaw and hacksaw blade electron beam welding" — matches canonical
- **Frame in this lecture:** Springfield (western Mass) firm with vacuum air feed-through EB system welding strip of high-speed steel (Mo/W tooth alloy) to spring-steel backing strip. Goes back to late '40s/'50s, among the earliest EB applications. Teaching point: niche EB application where the production-volume and value-added economics work.
- **Materials/systems:** High-speed steel, spring steel, electron beam weld
- **Era:** Late 1940s / 1950s onward
