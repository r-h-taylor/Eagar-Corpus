# Case Index — Lecture Aq3Q1CyX8uU (Fusion Welding, Summer 2013, session 4/6)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§1.p3`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is dominated by arc-physics teaching (plasma arcs, arc ignition, plasma jets, arc blow, heat flow) rather than by failure cases. The center of gravity is the historical lineage of welding heat-flow theory at MIT (Rosenthal → Christensen → Chai → Dave) and the composite-submarine workshop episode of ~1985–86 that emerges from the titanium-submarine digression.

## Major case: Composite submarine DARPA workshop (~1985–86)

### Composite submarine repairability failure
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`–`§7.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Composite submarine repairability failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Congress, frustrated with the SSN-21 (later Seawolf) program and still chasing the leapfrog idea even after the titanium-sub case had collapsed, zeroed the Navy's $100M design budget and redirected it to DARPA to design an all-composite submarine. After the Secretary of the Navy lobbied back the $100M, DARPA retained its parallel funding and convened a three-day Reston, Virginia workshop with metals and composites communities. The composites group's conceptual design featured large hatch doors for equipment removal — exposing the fact that the aerospace composites community (which had not yet flown an all-composite aircraft; the V-22 Osprey was the first) had no understanding of submarine interior geometry or the constraint that composite hulls cannot be cut and repaired. The Patrick Henry's hull patchwork was shown as the cautionary case.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon-fiber composites, titanium hulls, HY-100 steel, V-22 Osprey composite airframe
- **Era:** ~1985–1986
- **Related clusters in canon:** U.S. Navy heavy-section titanium submarine program; SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem; USS Patrick Henry hull degradation; V-22 Osprey aircraft; Soviet Alpha-class submarine

## Cases referenced in passing

### Plasma torch discovery by Bob Gage
- **Anchor:** `§1.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Plasma torch discovery by Bob Gage"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Bob Gage at Union Carbide Speedway Labs (Indianapolis) in the 1950s was studying water-cooled GTA arcs on copper and serendipitously drilled a hole in the copper plate; the plasma flame shooting through the hole was the first plasma torch and led to constricted-arc processes.
- **Materials/systems:** GTA, water-cooled copper, plasma
- **Era:** 1950s

### NASA X-33 / Space Shuttle main tank welding (variable polarity plasma arc)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`, `§4.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle External Tank (2219 aluminum)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** NASA prefers variable-polarity plasma arc (VPPA) over GTA for welding the Shuttle main tanks because the higher heat intensity is better matched to aluminum's high thermal conductivity, and the AC component helps disrupt the stable aluminum oxide.
- **Materials/systems:** 2219 aluminum, VPPA
- **Era:** 1980s–2000s

### Sweden cadmium electrical switch fire hazard
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Cadmium oxide electrical switch ban and house fires"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Silver–cadmium-oxide contacts (~1% CdO) double the current-carrying capacity of light-switch contacts and prevent sustained arcing on switch-off. Sweden banned cadmium on toxicity grounds ~30 years prior to the lecture and within three years had to reverse the ban after house fires from un-extinguished switch arcs. Paracelsus's "the dose makes the poison" framing invoked.
- **Materials/systems:** Ag-CdO contacts, pure Ag contacts (limited to ~7A)
- **Era:** ~1980s

### Liberty Ships and submerged arc (Union Melt) welding
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`–`§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Submerged arc welding (originally Union Melt, Union Carbide) is invoked as the engineering solution to plasma-jet instability at high currents: the flux blanket constrains the molten metal so currents of 600–700 A become workable. Roosevelt's letter to Churchill describing the new process for Liberty Ship welding is cited.
- **Materials/systems:** submerged arc, flux, Liberty Ship steel
- **Era:** WWII

### Rosenthal traveling-heat-source equation
- **Anchor:** `§8.p7`–`§8.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Daniel Rosenthal's escape from Nazi Germany and development of Rosenthal equation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Daniel Rosenthal — Jewish, fleeing Germany in 1939 through the Netherlands and Morocco, arriving at MIT mechanical engineering by 1940 — solved the traveling distributed point heat source problem during the journey and published three ASME Journal papers. Later UCLA materials science professor.
- **Materials/systems:** point heat source, traveling arc
- **Era:** 1939–1940s

### Nils Christensen and the dimensionless Rosenthal solution
- **Anchor:** `§8.p9`–`§8.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Nils Christensen dimensionless Rosenthal solution and MIT doctoral thesis theft
- **Frame in this lecture:** Norwegian freedom fighter during WWII (skiing across the Sweden–Norway border), Christensen came to MIT post-war on scholarship to work with John Chipman on welding fluxes. His first doctoral thesis manuscript was lost — Tom relates Christensen's surmise that Soviet spies at MIT stole it; he redid the work in nine extra months. Later, laid up in a Trondheim hospital bed in the mid-1960s, he extended Rosenthal's solution to dimensionless form, published in the *British Welding Journal* (1966). Returned to MIT for a sabbatical in the early 1980s; now deceased.
- **Materials/systems:** welding flux thermodynamics; dimensionless heat-flow solutions
- **Era:** WWII; mid-1960s; early 1980s
- **Related clusters in canon:** Niels Christensen's doctoral thesis theft and redo; Niels Christensen's hospitalization during welding research

### Nunsen Chai distributed-heat-source thesis (early 1980s)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p19`–`§8.p21`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Nunsen Chai distributed heat source PDP-11 computation
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student in the early 1980s, using a PDP-11/23 (16-bit, 10 MB hard drives at ~$1000) with weekend-long runs, extended Rosenthal's point-source solution to a distributed heat source on the GTA arc, computing variable depth-to-width ratios via Green's function superposition. Chai is now number two at Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing.
- **Materials/systems:** GTA arc heat distribution; Green's functions
- **Era:** early 1980s

### Vivek Dave subsurface heat distribution work (early 1990s)

### Ragnar and Else Holm electric-contacts research
- **Anchor:** `§3.p9`–`§3.p14`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Ragnar and Else Holm electric contacts and arc ignition theory
- **Frame in this lecture:** German émigré scientist Ragnar Holm (with his wife Else, both PhDs, of Saint Marys, Pennsylvania) at Westinghouse wrote *Electric Contacts* (now in its 4th edition) and derived the contact-asperity heating equivalence between the voltage and temperature fields (both Laplace's equation). His table of softening/melting/boiling voltages (Ag at 90 mV softening, 0.41 V melting Cu, 1.1 V melting W, etc.) explains that arc ignition in welding occurs by boiling metal asperities during contact separation at sub-volt voltages — *not* by Paschen breakdown or field emission as welding handbooks still claim. The IEEE Holm Conference is named for him.
- **Materials/systems:** Ag, Cu, W, Mo, WC-Co contacts; Laplace's equation
- **Era:** 1940s
