# Case Index — Lecture 2019-09-18 (Additive Manufacturing)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§2.p4`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is structured around several developed teaching cases (eyeglass hinges, cold heading, the 1975 Pratt & Whitney laser disk experiment, the buy-to-fly economic argument) and many briefer references to industrial-historical anchors Tom uses across the corpus.

## Cases developed in this lecture

### Eyeglass hinges additive manufacturing case study
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1` through `§2.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Eyeglass hinges additive manufacturing case study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's extended demonstration that the most prominent commercial example a $420M-funded additive manufacturing company (Desktop Metal) can show after three years is an eyeglass hinge — a part already manufactured for under a penny by Leach & Garner's progressive-die fancy-wire process. Used as the central illustration of "solutions looking for problems."
- **Materials/systems:** Powder bed fusion with binder; titanium, brass, stainless steel candidates
- **Era:** 2018–2019

### USS cruiser brass airline failure (Second Gulf War)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS cruiser brass airline failure (Second Gulf War)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to motivate Navy investment in additive manufacturing for ship repair — wrong-alloy brass elbow failed by stress corrosion cracking, taking out the entire 3,000 psi air system mid-assault.
- **Materials/systems:** Brass; stress corrosion cracking
- **Era:** Second Gulf War (2003)

### Leach and Garner gold mill consulting
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Leech and Garner gold mill consulting" (canonical name retained from aggregate despite spelling; same firm)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Reference to their "fancy wire" division — progressive-die shaped-wire production sliced "like a piece of bologna" to make eyeglass hinges at sub-penny cost. Establishes the competing process the additive method has to beat.
- **Materials/systems:** Brass, gold-filled wire
- **Era:** Long-standing (Attleboro, MA "gold capital of the world")

### Pratt & Whitney 1975 laser disk additive manufacturing experiment
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pratt & Whitney 1975 laser disk additive manufacturing experiment"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The historical precedent for laser-based additive manufacturing — Pratt & Whitney demonstrated in 1975 that going slowly enough you can achieve 100% density. Tom uses this to anchor the claim that the physics has been known for over 40 years; what's new is the commercial packaging.
- **Materials/systems:** Laser glazing; nickel superalloy turbine disk
- **Era:** 1975

### Post-WWII cold heading machine phosphate-stearate surface treatment
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6` through `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Post-WWII cold heading machine phosphate-stearate surface treatment"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended description of cold heading — the post-WWII innovation of phosphate + stearate surface treatment that enabled cold deformation of steel wire into precision fasteners at seven parts per second. Used as the competitive baseline that pixel-by-pixel additive cannot match for commodity fasteners.
- **Materials/systems:** Phosphate-coated, stearate-lubricated steel wire; 3/8 inch diameter
- **Era:** Post-WWII

### Pittsburgh cold heading plant
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pittsburgh cold heading plant"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first-person visit as a young assistant professor — "you have never been through a plant any louder than that plant."
- **Era:** Late 1970s (Tom's early career)

### General Motors steering gear cold/warm heading
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Motors steering gear cold/warm heading"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The other cold heading plant Tom visited — five-to-ten pound steering gear parts, some warm headed at 300–400°F because pure cold working was infeasible at that scale.
- **Materials/systems:** Steel steering gear components
- **Era:** Late 1970s–1980s

### Thomas Edison concrete homes
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Thomas Edison concrete homes"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Historical precedent for unconventional construction methods. Used to qualify the additive-concrete demo: "you can do it, but is it actually competitive?" Some Edison concrete homes still exist in New Jersey.
- **Era:** Early 20th century

### Air Force Buy-to-Fly Ratio in Aircraft Manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4` through `§8.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force Buy-to-Fly Ratio in Aircraft Manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The central economic argument for aerospace additive manufacturing. Titanium 6-4 T-bracket as worked example: 17:1 buy-to-fly via CNC machining means 95.4% of titanium becomes chips. Why the Air Force spent billions on near-net-shape manufacturing in the 80s–90s.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium 6-4
- **Era:** 1980s onward

### Watertown Arsenal titanium development
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Watertown Arsenal titanium development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief historical anchor — Ti-6Al-4V was developed at Watertown Arsenal after WWII. Now the dominant high-strength titanium alloy.
- **Era:** Post-WWII

## Figures referenced

- **45,000 hinges per 4-hour shift** (§2.p1): the additive manufacturer's productivity claim.
- **$0.08 per hinge** (§2.p3): Tom's amortized cost estimate for the additive process.
- **<$0.01 per hinge** (§2.p4): comparable Leach & Garner cost.
- **$420M raised** (§2.p5): venture capital into the additive eyeglass hinge company (GE $80M, Google $100M).
- **50% green porosity → 3% sintered porosity** (§3.p2): the standard powder-metallurgy figure Tom uses to bound precision claims.
- **≤ 1 mm/hour vapor deposition rate** (§6.p2): the kinetic-theory ceiling Tom invokes against vapor-phase building.
- **17:1 buy-to-fly ratio = 95.4% chips** (§8.p5): the headline aerospace machining waste figure.
- **15:1 to 30:1 historic aerospace buy-to-fly range** (§8.p8): the Christodoulou quote.
- **18 days vs 40 days, 2.5× cost reduction** (§8.p9): Boeing's claimed system-cost win with Digital Alloys process.
