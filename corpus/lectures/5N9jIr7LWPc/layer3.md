## §5. Across the periodic table: yttrium, lanthanum, titanium, zirconium [22:25]

`§5.p1` So we've got all these structural materials, or elements in the periodic table. We've now talked about magnesium. Carbon is for plastics. Silicon is for stone — and they are structural materials. Scandium we've talked about; about the only application of scandium I know is alloying in aluminum. Yttrium — what's yttrium used for, anybody know? Stabilizes zirconium. Adam Powell yesterday said there's only so much yttrium in the world. Yttrium is also used as an oxide growth promoter on turbine blades. Cobalt, chrome, aluminum, yttrium. If you want to have the absolute best high-temperature oxidation resistance, you put a little yttrium in with the cobalt-chrome-aluminum that you may have in your nickel-based superalloy. Or if you don't have any cobalt because it's expensive, you have iron in there, and you will grow an oxide on the surface that has about 200 degrees higher temperature capability of oxidation resistance. Most of our turbine blades out there in the world are CoCrAlY or FeCrAlY alloy. You have to have like a thousand parts per million or less of yttrium, but you need yttrium.

`§5.p2` Lanthanum — I don't know any use of lanthanum except to make lanthanum hexaboride filaments for scanning electron microscopes. It's the lanthanide series, so it's famous for that. Titanium — 60,000 tons. Titanium is a fantastic metal in many ways. I have a little pacemaker can from forty years ago. Pacemaker that you put in your heart, to signal your heart with a pump. They've been making them out of titanium for fifty years, because titanium just doesn't corrode in the body. It also doesn't corrode in heat exchangers. It's got good high temperature capability. The Concorde flies on temperatures — the SR-71 Blackbird had a titanium skin. It was one of the first titanium aircraft. Aluminum wouldn't make it to 90,000 feet at the temperatures you get from frictional heating at the speeds those things go, Mach 3 or 4. The B-2 bomber is mostly titanium in terms of its skin.

`§5.p3` So aerospace — it's relatively lightweight. It's not as light as aluminum, but in terms of melting point and temperatures — and because it's lightweight and can be superplastically formed, we use it on the compressors of jet engines. We can make extremely complex structures. But it is very reactive. If you get above 900 degrees centigrade — it's very corrosion resistant, it has reasonably good oxidation resistance up to 900 degrees, but above 900 degrees it will dissolve its own oxide, and all of a sudden it catches fire. It's like a great big magnesium flare. They've had engines go — just a great big ball of fire — and end up with a burned-out engine. Completely burned out. Not a good day. That's why you have multiple engines. It's also why you should control the way you operate the engine, so they're not designed to get to those temperatures. But some good old Air Force pilots have been able to do it.

`§5.p4` Zirconium — there are only two primary uses I know of, both structural. Reactor fuel cladding: you've got this uranium or plutonium oxide pellet, and you put it inside a zirconium tube. I have a little piece of zirconium tube. Goes for about a hundred bucks a foot. It's also a hexagonal close-packed metal. It's not the easiest thing to form. Beautiful metallography — you can anodize and get wonderful colors. So why is it good for nuclear reactor fuel cladding? It's neutron-transparent. It has one of the lowest neutron cross sections. It's like it doesn't exist to neutrons. It's measured in barns. Whatever a barn is, but it's some measure of neutron flux.

`§5.p5` There's another material on this periodic table that I've skipped: boron. Boron is a neutron poison. It accepts neutrons very well. Put a little borax in your water in your nuclear reactor, and you can stop that reaction very quickly. Gadolinium is another wonderful material. GE actually made stainless steel alloys with a little gadolinium in them. Other than that, gadolinium's claim to fame is that it's one of the few ferromagnetic elements. Iron, nickel, cobalt, and gadolinium are the four ferromagnetic elements, at least at room temperature. And gadolinium just barely made it. It's got a Curie temperature only a few degrees above room temperature.

`§5.p6` So there are these nuclear materials — zirconium, boron — having opposite effects. It turns out hafnium has a big neutron cross section. So in order to make zirconium fuel cladding, they had to get very low hafnium content, very low impurity. These things right here in these columns of the periodic table tend to be found together in nature. If you have a zirconia ore, it's going to have lots of hafnium, or vice versa. Same thing about niobium and tantalum — two Greek gods. Sadoway — Professor Sadoway — told me once that they're called Niobus and Tantalus because they were Greek twins. I looked that up once; I'm not sure they were twins, but they have something to do with Greek mythology, and they were named that because it was so difficult to separate them. They're found in the same ores. That was what his doctoral thesis was about.

## §9. Delrin and the exploding toilets [52:38]

`§9.p1` I don't have a piece of Delrin here, but DuPont — did I tell you the Delrin story? Polyacetal, probably — but it's basically a very simple polymer. DuPont came out with polyacetal, which they called Delrin as their trade name, D-E-L-R-I-N, in 1958. There's a paper that has 200 different solvents, and they said this is the plumbing material of the future. They had 200 different solvents that it was resistant to. You could pour any organic you wanted, any solvent you wanted down the sink, and nothing in your food was going to touch it. The one solvent they didn't check — water. I'm not kidding. You read this paper, they never checked water. It turns out, if it's very pure water — in a nuclear reactor, water would be great, Delrin would last forever. But if it has half a part per million chlorine, it will start to decompose, and it will crack.

`§9.p2` About ten years ago — first of all, Hoechst Celanese, who made — had a large part of the market, not as big as DuPont — they had a 900 million dollar class action settlement. DuPont had a class action settlement, but it's confidential — but we know it had to be above a billion dollars, for all these plumbing applications of Delrin. A half a part per million chlorine would cause the stuff to decompose. People would talk about the exploding toilets. Well, they weren't exploding, but they would make — the valve that, when you flush the toilet, the water rushes in and flushes, and then you have to have a valve that lets more water in, with the little float that stops everything — that valve used to be made out of Delrin. By EPA standards, it's not potable water if it has more than 100 parts per million chlorine. Cambridge water is like 5 ppm chlorine — ten times what Delrin can support. You know those little blue tablets that they put in toilets? They're basically salt tablets full of chlorine, and you put one of those in, you start seeing the blue color, and it's probably 3000 ppm chlorine, and those things will just destroy the Delrin within months.

`§9.p3` So what would happen is, the valve would crack, and you'd get 60 psi water shooting straight up against the top of the ceramic lid, and it would push the ceramic lid off, and it would come crashing to the floor. People would hear this big crash and they called them exploding toilets. They weren't exploding; the water pressure was knocking the ceramic cap off, and the ceramic was falling to the ground and crashing. Lots of floods. I made a lot of money off that.

`§9.p4` When they tell you plastics are corrosion resistant, I always say, in what environment? The corrosion engineers now define themselves as environmental degradation of materials. Metals corrode, but plastics and ceramics environmentally degrade depending on their environment. Everything will corrode over time, except gold — we find it in nature — platinum is usually pretty good. But if you look at just the earth, and you go to places that have been weathering away for a billion years, you don't find much except old worn-out stones.
