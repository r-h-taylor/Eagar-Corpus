# Case Index — Lecture 4

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§2.p4`). The lecture is a wide-ranging session on cutting tools, ore economics, steel productivity history, and the gold-mill consulting world. Most cases are touched briefly as examples within larger teaching arcs.*

## Cases in this lecture

### Diamond-coated asphalt tool development
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p4`, `§2.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Diamond-coated asphalt tool development`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom passes around two physical samples — one with a silicon carbide tip brazed to a steel shank, one with a sintered diamond tip. Used to introduce the productivity-vs-replacement-market argument and to set up the Ashby plot. Production cost of sintered diamond couldn't be justified because silicon carbide gives adequate one-shift performance.
- **Materials/systems:** Silicon carbide, sintered (man-made) diamond, steel shank, copper-bearing braze alloy.

### Paper slitter knife company evaluation
- **Anchor:** `§2.p8`–`§2.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Paper slitter knife company evaluation`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Consulting case ca. 1984, Danvers MA. Canadian timber baron client considering acquisition of a small slitter-knife maker who had developed a new heat-treating and brazing process delivering six times blade life. Tom's recommendation: don't buy unless you can grow the market — six-fold life on a $25M business shrinks the business absent new applications.
- **Materials/systems:** Tool steel, undisclosed braze process.
- **Era:** 1984.

### Norton asphalt reclamation carbide-tip braze joint
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`–`§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Norton asphalt reclamation carbide-tip braze joint`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate step brazing — diamond to silicon carbide at ~1300°F first, then silicon carbide to steel at ~1100°F to avoid remelting the first joint.
- **Materials/systems:** Step braze, dissimilar materials.

### Automotive spot-weld electrode wear improvement (1980–1990)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Automotive spot-weld electrode wear improvement (1980–1990)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Galvanized steel adoption in automotive sheet collapsed copper electrode tip life from 8 hours (one shift) to under 1 hour. Ford imposed a 2,000-weld spec on steel suppliers — sized so that tip changeout could happen on the 15-minute coffee break. Illustrates the "one shift" productivity barrier.
- **Materials/systems:** Galvanized steel, copper electrode, zinc-copper interaction.
- **Era:** Early 1980s.

### Technetium-99m cardiac imaging (Eagar's personal case)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`–`§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Technetium-99m cardiac imaging (Eagar's personal case)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Personal medical anecdote (stress test, prostate cancer follow-up, sinus infection — all at Mount Auburn Hospital, radiologist Dr. Abner an MIT physics alum). Used to set up the case below and to illustrate that technetium has practical use despite a 10.5-hour half-life.
- **Era:** ~2006–2014.

### Kentucky school fire forensic case
- **Anchor:** `§5.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Kentucky school fire forensic case`
- **Frame in this lecture:** $8M arson/electrical-fire case. SEM-EDS analysis returned by a Kentucky lab found 13% technetium in the nail samples. Tom's punchline: "We can solve this case — just sell the nails." Garbage data. Case settled before he testified. Lesson: question your data.

### Technetium lab data credibility case
- **Anchor:** `§5.p10` (same as above, paired)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Technetium lab data credibility case`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom flagging that the school-fire and Texas-A&M-Hispanic-students episodes share a methodology lesson: question your data even when it comes back on letterhead. Cross-references §1.p5–p6.

### Bingham Canyon copper mine
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Kennecott Bingham Canyon copper mine operations`
- **Frame in this lecture:** World's largest open-pit mine, ~1 mile deep, ~0.5% copper ore. Used to illustrate why copper energy cost is rising: you must move 4,000 pounds of dirt per pound of metal.

### Mesabi Range iron ore depletion
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Mesabi Range iron ore depletion`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Counter-example to copper — Mesabi Range and Indian iron ores were ~40% iron, near the theoretical max for pure iron oxide. Steel doesn't have the entropy problem copper does.

### U.S. Steel industry decline — historical productivity arc
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `US steel industry productivity transformation (1980-1990)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** The long arc from Saugus Iron Works (1600s, 3,000–4,000 hours/ton) through Bessemer and Carnegie (~100 hours/ton, 1850s–60s) through the BOF (Linz 1958, commercial mid-60s), continuous casting (Japanese, late 60s), and onward. The §8 spine of the lecture.

### Tom Eagar's steel company experience (Bethlehem Steel)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`, `§8.p9`–`§8.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Tom Eagar's steel company experience`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom recalls Bethlehem as a "dinosaur company" still doing ingot casting in the mid-70s with 63% yield. Mike Kimchuk (DMSE alum, BS VP-adjacent role, MIT Educational Council chair for eastern PA) was charged solely with improving yield by 1% — worth ~30% of profit. And the 1930s Eugene Grace bonus story: highest-paid US executives, bonus paid on tons *poured* not sold, during the Depression.

### Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse
- **Anchor:** `§8.p11`–`§8.p13`, `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Today ~97% yield via continuous casting; eight-story-tall plants pouring I-beams via a curved water-cooled copper mold, cut to length with oxygen torches (no fuel gas needed). Tom uses a photograph as a teaching aid. Lineage note: gold and copper were continuously cast decades before steel.

### Leech and Garner consulting retainer
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`–`§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Leech and Garner consulting retainer`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first major industrial consulting engagement, 1978. $5,000/year retainer at the time of his $20,000 MIT salary. Required by his bank for the Belmont house purchase ($85K then, >$1M now). Leach and Garner (Attleboro MA, founded 1899) ran two continuous casters for two-inch-diameter karat gold round when most steel mills had none.

### Leech and Garner gold mill consulting
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5`–`§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Leech and Garner gold mill consulting`
- **Frame in this lecture:** $10M of gold in a single small pot during a 1980 cast that turned out to be set up just for Tom's benefit. Three-day-vs-four-day throughput is the difference between substantial profit and loss because of the interest carry on the gold inventory. Findings (earring posts, watchband clasps) sold to jewelry suppliers including Schwank. Consulting relationship ran into early 1990s, ended when a Canadian gold-foundry acquisition (forced on Leach and Garner by a supplier loan default) lost $10M to internal theft.

### South African gold mine salt shaker smuggling
- **Anchor:** `§9.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `South African gold mine salt shaker smuggling`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Chemistry-lab worker smuggled gold out as gold chloride (visually indistinguishable from table salt) in his daily egg-and-salt lunch. Defeated metal scanners because gold chloride is not metallic.

### Attleboro brass-rat jewelry company ring theft via river
- **Anchor:** `§9.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Attleboro brass-rat jewelry company ring theft via river`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Unnamed Attleboro company making MIT brass rats was losing whole rings. Found employees were tossing them out the windows into the stream that runs through Attleboro to dive for them on weekends. Company bought the town's mineral rights so they could police the river.

### Attleboro jewelry plant toilet smuggling
- **Anchor:** `§9.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Attleboro jewelry plant toilet smuggling`
- **Frame in this lecture:** After firing the river-diving employee, rings kept disappearing. Turned out his girlfriend, working in the same plant, was flushing them down the toilet to spite the company.

### Leech and Garner floor drain gold recovery
- **Anchor:** `§9.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Leech and Garner floor drain gold recovery`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Warren Morris (head of engineering, claimed great-grandson of Samuel F. B. Morse — flagged in editorial register) put a filter in the casting-shop floor drain. Recovered $110,000 of gold in the first week. Eventually excavated the entire drain line because it had been accumulating gold since 1899.
