# Case Index — Corrosion (Summer 2016), Lecture 5/5

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p4`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is dominated by a single extended case (the National Academy / NSF Alvin replacement cost study, 2003–2004) with several supporting historical and engineering cases woven through, and a closing pivot to the naval-shipyard radiography productivity scandal.

## Cases referenced

### Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development
- **Anchors:** `§1.p1`, `§1.p2`, `§2.p2`, `§3.p1`–`§3.p4`, `§4.p1`–`§4.p4`, `§5.p1`–`§5.p3`, `§6.p1`–`§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's role on the ~36-person National Academy of Sciences committee (2003–2004) tasked with evaluating whether NSF's $25 million capital budget could fund a new Alvin replacement. Used to teach: cost-per-pound analysis methodology, infrastructure loss in US shipbuilding, the gap between Woods Hole's $6.5M estimate and Tom's $16M estimate and the actual $37M outcome, and the syntactic-foam buoyancy technology.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-100 steel hull (original Alvin 1964); titanium hull (post-1970 Alvin and Sea Cliff); syntactic foam (glass microspheres in seawater-resistant resin, SG <0.5); lithium-ion batteries; gas tungsten arc welding of 2¼" titanium.
- **Era:** 1964 (original commissioning) — 2012 (study window for new Alvin).

### ALVIN submarine pressure hull cost overrun
- **Anchors:** `§4.p2`, `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`, `§6.p1`, `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "ALVIN submarine pressure hull cost overrun"

### U.S. Navy titanium submarine welding research (Alvin prototype program)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy titanium submarine welding research (Alvin prototype program)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mare Island Naval Shipyard attempted to use GMAW (gas metal arc welding) technology developed at David Taylor / Annapolis to build the Sea Cliff titanium hull. Failed after six months; fell back to GTAW (gas tungsten arc), which took a year to deposit the 2¼" weld at ~4 inches/minute. Used to teach: naval shipyards' lost capability to *build* (vs. repair) vessels, and the cost-of-time tradeoff in titanium welding.

### Naval shipyard X-ray inspection inefficiency study
- **Anchors:** `§6.p7`, `§6.p8`, `§7.p1`–`§7.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval shipyard X-ray inspection inefficiency study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The closing teaching beat. Around 1996–97, NAVSEA permitted UT (ultrasonic testing) as an alternative to radiography; commercial shipyards (Electric Boat, David Taylor, Bath, Ingalls) all converted within a year. Navy shipyards continued radiography, requiring ~30 guards per x-ray session at ~$2M/shift. A 2N student around 2003 studied why. Tom's framing: Navy shipyards exist to preserve civil service jobs, not to save money. Tied back to the Alvin overrun: "the Alvin was just a minor overrun compared to x-ray inspection in naval shipyards today."

### China rare earth embargo on Japan / Chinese metals market disruption
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** *PROPOSED:* "2007 Chinese metals market disruption" — distinct from the rare earth embargo on Japan (2010). The 2007 event Tom describes is broad Chinese metals buying on the open market in advance of the 2008 financial crisis, doubling and tripling prices of titanium, aluminum, and steel. This is *not* the rare earth embargo and should not be conflated.
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to explain why the actual Alvin contract cost diverged sharply from Tom's 2004 estimate.

## Figures referenced
