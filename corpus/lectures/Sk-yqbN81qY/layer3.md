## §2. Welding martensitic stainless steels [05:03]

`§2.p1` Some things to consider when you're welding a martensitic stainless steel alloy. It's recommended that you preheat all the time when you're welding martensitic alloys. It's not absolutely required if you've got low carbon — below about 0.1 weight percent. The preheat that's recommended is about 400 to 600 degrees F. The reason is, it's a martensitic alloy, you've got to worry about hydrogen cracking, and the preheat will help minimize that. Post weld heat treatment is often also used. Sometimes you use an austenitic filler material, and in that case the post weld heat treatment is not required. But if you've used a matching or other filler material and you've got a high-stress weldment, that post weld heat treatment is a good idea to stress relieve and to temper the structure.

`§2.p2` Some other things to consider are which consumable you might use. The austenitic filler metals have a lower strength and good ductility, so they might reduce the stresses in your weld zone. The concern is that they're not as strong, so if the reason you're using the martensitic stainless steel is because you're worried about strength, then you need to use a matching filler metal or something close. With that, you will need to do a post weld stress relief treatment also, just for the hydrogen issues. And it sounds like Professor Eagar will touch on that even more tomorrow. Low hydrogen practice when you're welding these — which includes cleanliness, using gases that are clean and low hydrogen, using filler materials that are low hydrogen — that's all mandatory when you're welding on martensitic alloys.

`§2.p3` Continuing on weldability of the martensitic alloys, as far as cracking goes: solidification cracking susceptibility is generally low in these alloys. The main concern as far as cracking is the hydrogen induced cracking, particularly for the higher carbon grades. Also, just because of the martensitic structure, you get poor mechanical properties as-welded — low ductility, low toughness. The problem with low ductility and low toughness is you can have catastrophic brittle failure of your component, and so you typically need to do some post weld stress relief to reduce that. There's also something called reheat cracking, which is basically after the structure has been welded, the post weld aging or reheating of the alloy can promote some carbide precipitation along grain boundaries and within the grains, which stresses the alloy and can lead to further cracking.

## §4. Applications and metallurgy of nickel-based alloys [14:17]

## §5. Controlling properties: grain size, precipitates, and welding considerations [22:49]

`§5.p1` One thing to keep in mind about the nickel-based alloys is that they're very complex. There are a lot of things going on in these alloys, and there are various ways for controlling the properties. One is grain size. Since these alloys are often used in high-temperature applications where creep could be a problem — creep is basically the sliding or the slow movement of an alloy, usually at high temperature — if you've got less boundary area, less place for those grains to slide against one another, then that's a good thing if that's what you're worried about.

`§5.p2` Volume fraction of the precipitates also has a large effect on the hardness and the strength and the properties of the material. Precipitate coherency — the way they line up within the lattice of the material — when you're heat treating and growing those precipitates, if you've under-aged them, you haven't grown them enough, and they're not providing enough stress in the lattice to strengthen the material. If you've over-aged them, you've grown them to where they're too big and they're not providing the strength that you want. And there's something called peak age, which is where you've grown those precipitates to a certain point where you've strengthened the material to its peak strength. So those are all things that can be controlled.

`§5.p3` Another thing often done to control creep properties is the addition of boron. And another thing to keep in mind, especially if you're welding nickel-based alloys: most nickel-based alloys have not been developed with welding in mind. They've been developed to cast and then machine an airplane turbine or something like that, and welding was not always kept in mind as they were developed. Because of that, we do end up with some weldability issues.

`§5.p4` The three main weldability issues in nickel-based alloys are solidification cracking, heat affected zone liquation cracking, and strain age cracking. Strain age cracking is similar to reheat cracking that I talked about a little bit with martensitic stainless steels and also in other steels. There's also something called ductility dip cracking, which I'm not going to talk about — if you're interested in that, you can look in the book, or you can get in touch with me.

## §6. Solidification cracking: boundaries and microstructure [26:00]

`§6.p1` So, solidification cracking. Before I really talk about that, I wanted to discuss the various types of boundaries that are created in an austenitic steel or austenitic nickel base alloy as the material solidifies. What you've got is two solid regions coming together as the material solidifies. Remember I talked about the low melting phases that partition and are pushed along ahead of the solidification front — that will be the last to solidify. You've also got solidification cells or dendrites that are growing together, and as these fronts come together you get something called a solidification grain boundary, where the two solidification fronts meet and form a boundary.

`§6.p2` Within that solidification front, between the cells or dendrites, you get something called a solidification subgrain boundary. And then sometimes what happens after the two solidification fronts have come together is that the boundary is a little wavy or tortuous, and you get something called a migrated grain boundary — a boundary that is straightened out or moved. It does that to reduce the energy of that tortuous solidification grain boundary.

`§6.p3` That was a schematic. This is a real microstructure that demonstrates some of those boundaries. Right along here you've got the solidification grain boundary, where two solidification fronts came together. Within those you've got the solidification subgrain boundaries. And then you've got a migrated grain boundary, which moved away from that original solidification grain boundary. When you're worried about solidification cracking, you're worried about these boundaries and how much stress and how much liquid low melting phase is present along them.

`§6.p4` Back to this slide. With austenitic solidification, the liquid film wetting is good. *[Bauman shows a fractograph.]* These are actual fracture surfaces of solidification cracks in nickel-based alloys, and you can see the very well-defined dendritic structure. It almost looks like there's a liquid film over those — as they pulled apart, it's due to the stress and low melting phase that was along those final solidification grain boundaries.

`§6.p5` The liquid film wetting is good, and the solid-state diffusion is low, which means you end up with those well-defined, well-developed solidification structures. We've also talked about the partitioning of the impurity elements. Titanium, niobium, and aluminum — actually, those aren't the impurities, those are just the alloying elements. But impurities such as sulfur and phosphorus are generally low in these alloys, which is a good thing. If they are there, they can also lead to that low melting eutectic phase along the solidification grain boundaries. The problem with eutectic phases along the grain boundaries is they melt at a much lower temperature than the bulk material, so they remain liquid for longer as things are solidifying. If there's a liquid phase along the grain boundary and you put some stress on it, it's going to pull apart — it's going to crack.

`§6.p6` *[Bauman shows a micrograph of a solidification crack.]* This is a solidification crack in a nickel base alloy. The really dark stuff here is where the crack still exists. The gray stuff here is where it pulled apart as it was solidifying, but there was enough of that liquid material left to fill up the crack. That's called crack healing. The solidification crack is this region right here, and then you're left with some actual cracks at the end.

## §7. Preventing solidification cracking and HAZ liquation cracking [31:32]

`§7.p1` What can we do to prevent solidification cracking? A big thing is if we can minimize the restraint in the material as it's solidifying. If it doesn't have stresses pulling it back apart, then it's not going to crack. There are various ways to do that. One is to weld in a solution annealed condition, meaning everything has been heated up and dissolved, so all the precipitates are in solution. That reduces the strength of the surrounding material, so you don't have high strength material on each side of the weld and low strength solidification going on. You have a more constant, lower strength material, which will then require some post weld heat treatment after you make the weld to restore the properties you're interested in.

`§7.p2` Another big way to minimize restraint is joint design. You can use different joints when you're making the weld that don't form stresses and residual stresses in the weld as much. There are a couple of examples here.

`§7.p3` This would be considered a stringer bead — just a bead along the top of a plate. That's actually a pretty good thing generally, because these would be the solidification grain boundaries — it all just grows together. If you use the right welding parameters, you don't have to worry as much about solidification cracking, as opposed to something like this, which is a deeper and narrower weld. Because the solidification fronts are growing almost parallel to one another, and as they come together they're also solidifying and shrinking, you end up with this solidification grain boundary right down the middle that is easy to be pulled apart.

`§7.p4` When you're making a fillet weld, between two 90-degree members, you can also control solidification cracking by the amount of material that you put in there. If you have a nice convex weld bead like this as opposed to a concave weld bead like this, the solidification stresses will be less and you'll end up without cracking, in the case on the right.

`§7.p5` Some other ways to minimize restraint are to use multiple pass welds, rather than putting in a whole bunch of weld material at one time which then solidifies and has residual stresses in it. You can use a bunch of smaller welds — multiple pass welds — and that will minimize your restraint. The last thing you can do is use a "resistant" — solidification cracking resistant — material. "Resistant" is in quotes there, because it depends on what you're welding and what filler metal you use. But some of these alloys here — well, these are actually solid solution strengthening, this is a pure nickel alloy — you get less of the strengthening and hardening going on in the weld, which leads to lower restraint and less solidification cracking susceptibility.

`§7.p6` The next weldability issue is heat affected zone — HAZ — liquation cracking. The heat affected zone is the region of material next to the material from the weld that was molten or liquid — so it was not melted, but it was definitely affected by the heat of the weld. If you remember from the phase diagrams Professor Eagar has shown you, and the stainless steel phase diagrams I showed you, as the temperature drops in an alloy, you go from liquid to a liquid plus solid region, and then to a completely solid region.
