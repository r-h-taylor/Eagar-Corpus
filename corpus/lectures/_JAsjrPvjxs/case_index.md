# Case Index — Lecture `_JAsjrPvjxs` (Welding Metallurgy 3/18, Summer 2015)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p2`). Layer 2 carries the timestamped passage.*

This lecture is primarily a history-of-welding and history-of-steelmaking lecture, with several teaching cases woven through. Student-driven discussion of shipyard practice occupies §5–§7.

## Cases referenced

### Liberty ships and welded merchant vessel failures (WWII)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`, `§1.p3`, `§3.p2`, `§3.p3`, `§11.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide)" (also "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady", "1940s welded ship cracking", "WWII ship welding moisture control", "WWII riveted vs. welded ship brittle fracture comparison")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom reads from the postwar damage report — 4,694 ships, 970 with fracture casualties, 24 complete hull fractures, 26 lives lost. Frames the rest of the lecture as the story of how welding technology had to be invented to support mass-produced welded ships.
- **Era:** 1940s; postwar Pellini / Cohen / Weck research late 1940s.

### Big Inch pipeline (Louisiana to New Jersey, 30-inch)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Big Inch Pipeline"
- **Frame in this lecture:** First critical welded structure, early 1930s. Predecessor and proof-of-concept for welded ship construction in WWII.
- **Era:** Early 1930s.

### Forging press weld repair (Pennsylvania, 17-inch thick steel)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pennsylvania forging press repair" (also possibly "7,000-ton forging press weld repair" or "Forging press repair weld failure" or "Forging press casting repair" — Tom does not specify which press; the 17-inch-thick wall is consistent with a multi-thousand-ton press)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stage-direction case — Tom passes around a section of the repair weld to demonstrate the ~100 stick-electrode passes required to weld through 17 inches.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-section carbon steel, shielded metal arc weld with stick electrodes.

### Nova Scotia sailboat trailer weld failure (Route 24)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Trailer weld failure sailboat accident" (also "Sailboat trailer weld failure on Route 24", "Nova Scotia sailboat trailer weld failure", "Boat trailer weld failure on highway")
- **Frame in this lecture:** A driver from Nova Scotia returning from Newport sailing races on a home-built trailer experienced a slug-welded joint failure — a bolt had been dropped into the angle iron joint and welded over. The boat broke loose at highway speed. Tom uses it as the one slug weld he has personally seen, and as an illustration of why slug welds are unacceptable.
- **Materials/systems:** Angle iron, stick electrode, hidden bolt as filler.

### Quonset Point submarine hull cap welding
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Quonset Point submarine hull cap fixturing and welding practice
- **Frame in this lecture:** Student-led description of automated tractor-welding of pre-triangulated submarine cap heads, ~100 weld passes per joint, back-gouging, inert gas shielding, 3mm defect limit. Tom then adds the secrecy aside about Soviet satellite measurement of hull thickness.

### Submarine torpedo tube distortion control
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Submarine torpedo tube distortion and machining" (also "Torpedo tube welding distortion control")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first student at Electric Boat developed an in-process distortion-monitoring technique using feeler gauges, allowing real-time compensation by adjusting weld sequence and grinding-and-reweld corrections. Reduced finishing pass from quarter-inch machining to skin pass.

### Carrier nuclear piping corrosion repair
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Nuclear carrier piping misalignment and repair" (also "US Navy nuclear submarine steel pipe corrosion (30-year ship life)", "US Navy ship life-extension piping failure transition (1990s–2000s)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Student-side: original construction jacked misaligned pipes into place and welded them, locking in residual stress. Years later, corrosion-driven repair requires refabrication of entire piping systems because the locked-in stress prevents simple cut-and-replace.

### Shipbuilding tolerances and solar thermal distortion
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Shipyard thermal distortion during construction"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1 part in 20,000 tolerance for shipbuilding (vs. typical 1 part in 2,000 for general manufacturing) — the precision is so high that solar heating changes the shape of a thousand-foot ship and must be welded around. Student adds Pascagoula sun-shade practice.

### Boeing fit-up specification (five-pound rule)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`, `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing aircraft rivet hole fit-up specification"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Boeing general spec: cannot apply more than five pounds of force to bring two parts into registry before riveting, to limit residual stress around rivet holes. Tom uses it to contrast Boeing's fatigue-driven specification with the Navy's looser fit-up tolerance.

### John Chipman / MIT and the iron-carbon phase diagram
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: John Chipman MIT steelmaking research (Manhattan Project era through 1973)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Chipman applied physical chemistry to 1500°C steel melts in Building 8 basement, developed the modern iron-carbon phase diagram (1973), worked on the Manhattan Project developing sulfide ceramic crucibles for uranium and plutonium melting. His treatises appear enshrined in Plexiglas in Chinese libraries as the seminal text on steelmaking.

### Saugus Iron Works wrought iron production
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1630s wrought iron production, illustrating the inefficient pre-Bessemer process of heating, reheating, and oxidizing carbon out.

### Bessemer converter (1856)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`, `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Bessemer converter introduction (1856)
- **Frame in this lecture:** The counterflow preheating innovation that made it possible to reach 1500°C using hydrocarbon combustion, by preheating incoming air with exiting combustion gases.

### Basic open hearth (Carnegie, 1880s)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p4`, `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Basic open hearth furnace (Carnegie 1880s)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Half-football-field-sized brick-lattice regenerative furnace, three-hundred-ton tap per day, the standard process for steel for the first hundred years.

### Basic oxygen furnace (postwar Austria)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria" (also "Austrian BOF development post-WWII")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Supersonic-velocity pure-oxygen lance creates froth of steel; 300 tons of cast iron converted to steel in 20 minutes vs. 24 hours in basic open hearth. The shaving-cream-vs-surface-diffusion analogy.

### AOD (argon-oxygen decarburization) stainless steelmaking
- **Anchor:** `§10.p2`, `§10.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Argon oxygen decarbonization (AOD) process development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** "Christy" (Chipman doctoral student, 1950s) studied the carbon-monoxide reaction in steel melts using argon-diluted oxygen. The technique went from MIT thesis to Crucible Steel + Union Carbide commercial process. All 5M tons of stainless steel today made by AOD; price reduced to one-third of 1950s value.

### Pellini and Naval Research Laboratory brittle fracture work
- **Anchor:** `§11.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pellini explosion bulge test development" (also "Explosion bulge test (Pelini underwater weld testing)", "Navy brittle fracture study - welded vessels and ships")
- **Frame in this lecture:** One of the three postwar research centers studying weld brittle fracture; Pellini later joined MIT Course 13 as a lecturer in the 1980s before retiring on Cape Cod.

### Welding Institute (Abington, Cambridge UK)
- **Anchor:** `§11.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "British Welding Institute founding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Richard Weck pedaled his bicycle through Cambridge to Abington and decided that's where British welding researchers should be — founded 1947 or so as the third major postwar weld-fracture research center.

### Morris Cohen / George Krauss / MIT brittle-fracture research
- **Anchor:** `§11.p2`, `§11.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Morris Cohen MIT postwar brittle fracture research
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cohen was the postwar Mr. Steel at MIT studying brittle fracture of welded ships; Krauss was his doctoral student and inherited the Mr. Steel title; *Steels* (Krauss's textbook) is Tom's source for the carbon-vs-hardness curve.

### Medieval steel quenching (Nubian slave / blood quench)
- **Anchor:** `§11.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Medieval Islamic sword quenching practice
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's undergraduate history paper material — Muslim swordsmiths during the Middle Ages believed quenching a hot blade through a living person or in blood produced a better edge than quenching in water or oil.
- **Related canon entry:** "Damascus sword quenching practice", "Medieval sword tempering practice".

## Figures referenced

- **WWII Liberty ship damage statistics.** 4,694 ships; 970 with fracture casualties; 24 complete hull fractures; 8 lost or broken by brittle fracture; 26 lives lost. (§1.p1)
- **Stick electrode deposition rate.** Four to six pounds per hour for the WWII welder. (§4.p1)
- **Manufacturing tolerances.** Typical: 1 part in 2,000. Shipbuilding: 1 part in 20,000. (§6.p3)
- **Basic open hearth productivity.** ~300 tons per day per furnace. (§9.p5)
- **BOF productivity.** 300 tons of cast iron → steel in 20 minutes. (§10.p1)
- **AOD economic impact.** Stainless steel price reduced to one-third of 1950s value; ~5 million tons of stainless made worldwide today via AOD. (§10.p3)
- **Iron-carbon eutectoid temperature.** 723°C at 10% carbon equivalent; 910°C at zero carbon.
