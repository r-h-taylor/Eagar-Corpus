# Case Index — Lecture 11 (Spring 2016)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor. Click-through to layer 2 reveals timestamp range and raw transcript.*

This lecture is structured as a productivity history of the steel industry. Cases appear as historical-industrial units (Saugus, Bessemer, POSCO, Burns Harbor, Allegheny Ludlum, Chaparral) and as personal-anecdote cases (Carnegie/grandfather, Simmons private plane).

## Cases developed in this lecture

### 1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Opening case for the "first energy crisis" arc — a 1558 law forbidding tree-felling to make charcoal, exempting the Weald due to iron-industry lobbying, with a 24-fold price increase in wood by 1581 leading to a further 1581 Act.
- **Era:** 16th century England.

### England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`, `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "England 16th century 17th century deforestation and energy crisis"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The structural argument for why American iron industry began — England exhausted its forests for charcoal (iron, shipbuilding, glass) and forced colonial relocation.
- **Materials/systems:** charcoal, blast furnaces, cast iron, oak (shipbuilding).
- **Era:** 1500s–1600s.

### Saugus Ironworks
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`, `§5.p4`, `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** First American iron mill (Saugus, MA, 1642–1688). Used bog iron and oyster-shell flux. Eight tons per week. Failed by 1688 from litigation and timber depletion. National Historical Site.
- **Materials/systems:** bog iron, oyster-shell calcium carbonate flux, pig iron casting, sow bar.
- **Era:** mid-17th century.

### Jamestown glassworks (and Colonial Williamsburg context)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Jamestown glassworks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference in the Chesapeake forges context; Tom uses Williamsburg/Jamestown as biographical detour rather than as a developed teaching case here.
- **Era:** early 17th century.

### Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`, `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Post-WWII Austrian innovation: water-cooled copper lance, supersonic oxygen jet, 20–30 minute refining vs. 24 hours for open hearth. The productivity revolution that displaced Andrew Carnegie's basic open-hearth furnaces.
- **Materials/systems:** liquid oxygen, copper lance, cast iron decarburization.
- **Era:** 1950s.

### U.S. Steel's last large open hearth furnace
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Steel's last large open hearth furnace"
- **Frame in this lecture:** U.S. Steel built a 400-ton basic open hearth in the early 1970s — the last of its scale — at the moment the BOF was displacing the technology globally.
- **Era:** early 1970s.

### Argon oxygen decarbonization (AOD) process development
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`, `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Argon oxygen decarbonization (AOD) process development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** AOD reduces carbon from 300 ppm to 30 ppm by argon blowing. Basic science done at MIT under John Chipman by Krivsky in the 1950s; process invented when Krivsky moved to a small steel company. 99% of stainless steel today made by AOD.
- **Materials/systems:** stainless steel refining, argon dilution.
- **Era:** 1950s science, 1960s process commercialization.

### Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse
- **Anchor:** `§10.p1`, `§10.p2`, `§10.p3`, `§11.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Continuous casting yield: 65% (ingot) → 97% (continuous). Tom's anecdote about the Bethlehem Steel yield-improvement VP. Combined with BOF, the productivity doubled in 20 years, halving employment from 500K to 250K in the 1980s.
- **Era:** Japanese perfection in 1970s; U.S. impact in 1980s.

### POSCO (Korea) government-built steel mill surpassing Nippon Steel
- **Anchor:** `§11.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "POSCO (Korea) government-built steel mill surpassing Nippon Steel"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1972 Korean state-funded steel mill built with U.S. Steel engineering, led by Colonel Park under President Park. By the late 1990s POSCO surpassed Nippon Steel as world's largest. Tom held the POSCO professorship.
- **Era:** 1972 founding; late 1990s dominance.

### Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor
- **Anchor:** `§11.p3`, `§14.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Last large-scale privately-financed steel mill in the world (1968–1973). Bethlehem nearly bankrupt during construction; mill survived as ArcelorMittal property after Bethlehem's bankruptcy. Still the most efficient U.S. mill.
- **Era:** 1968–1973 construction; present-day operation.

### Lakshmi Mittal steel mill acquisition strategy
- **Anchor:** `§14.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lakshmi Mittal steel mill acquisition strategy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Closing case. Indian industrialist bought distressed U.S. and European mills at ten cents on the dollar in the 1980s–90s when Wall Street wrote off steel as a dying industry. Became multi-billionaire when overcapacity worked itself out.
- **Era:** 1980s–1990s acquisitions.

### Allegheny Ludlum Steel—1980s profitability under Dick Simmons
- **Anchor:** `§12.p1`, `§12.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Allegheny Ludlum Steel—1980s profitability under Dick Simmons"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Dick Simmons (MIT '52) became Allegheny Ludlum president. Two 1980s leveraged buyouts made him a half-billionaire. The only U.S. integrated steel company with no unprofitable quarter in the 1980s. Tom's anecdote about Simmons's private plane.
- **Era:** 1980s.

### Allegheny Ludlum AOD Bearing Failure
- **Anchor:** `§12.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Allegheny Ludlum AOD Bearing Failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's consulting visit for an AOD-vessel bearing failure. Used as the occasion to observe hourly workers' regard for Dick Simmons.
- **Era:** 1980s.

### Gordon Forward mini mill competition strategy
- **Anchor:** `§13.p1`, `§13.p2`, `§13.p3`, `§13.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Gordon Forward mini mill competition strategy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Gordon E. Forward (MIT, student of John Elliott who was student of John Chipman) as president of Chaparral Steel. Sent mill workers with salesmen to customers. Discovered the 3/8" rebar price premium and the split-rolling innovation: split bar in two then four through the rolling mill, halving speed but doubling productivity.
- **Era:** 1970s–1980s.

### 3/8 inch rebar rolling mill productivity case
- **Anchor:** `§13.p2`, `§13.p3`, `§13.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "3/8 inch rebar rolling mill productivity case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The specific Chaparral product/market discovery: smallest-diameter rebar carried a price premium because rolling speed bottleneck; Chaparral solved by splitting strands rather than accelerating.
- **Era:** late 1970s–1980s.

### Steel mini mills rise (1970s–1990s)
- **Anchor:** `§13.p1`, `§13.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Steel mini mills rise (1970s–1990s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Generic framing: scrap at $100/ton vs. pig iron at $200/ton in the 1970s opened the electric-furnace mini-mill business model.
- **Era:** 1970s onward.

### U.S. Steel industry decline and management failure
- **Anchor:** `§11.p1`, `§11.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Steel industry decline and management failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** U.S. and Bethlehem Steel sold obsolete plants to third-world buyers who then dismantled and re-exported steel back to the U.S. at lower labor rates. Washington misread productivity-driven employment loss as industry death.
- **Era:** 1980s.

## Personal-anecdote cases (Tom-voice biographical material)

### Andrew Carnegie / Eagar grandfather story
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Andrew Carnegie and Eagar grandfather University of Chattanooga donation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's grandfather (former mayor of Chattanooga) sought $25,000 from Carnegie to complete a $500K matching grant (Rockefeller's $250K) for the University of Chattanooga. Carnegie asked his education level; grandfather said 13 months; Carnegie revealed he too had 13 months and granted the funds. The anecdote serves as a Carnegie character study and a generational bridge to the steel-industry narrative.
