# Case Index — Lecture 2017-09-12

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p9`). This lecture is largely framework — Ashby plots, energy-cost economics — punctuated by short case mentions used illustratively.*

## Cases referenced

### American Electric Power Texas wind turbine investment
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "American Electric Power Texas transmission line investment"
- **Frame in this section:** $1.2B AEP wind-turbine investment in West Texas without transmission lines to the demand centers (Austin, Dallas). Illustrates regulatory externality — renewable-energy mandates imposing cost regardless of infrastructure readiness.
- **Era:** mid-2010s

### GE GTD-111 superalloy heat-treatment patent
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4, §1.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "GTD-111 superalloy patent dispute" (also: "GE GTD-111 nickel superalloy patent dispute", "General Electric GT-100 patent litigation")
- **Frame in this section:** GE patents GTD-111 in mid-1970s; competitors design it in as the patent expires (1980s–90s); GE then secures a successor patent on heat treatment in the late 1990s, twenty years after filing, forcing redesigns. Illustrates intellectual-property externality.
- **Materials/systems:** GTD-111 nickel-base superalloy; land-based and aircraft turbine engines
- **Era:** 1975–1998

### Houston oil company clad-pipe project cancellation
- **Anchor:** `§1.p6, §1.p7, §1.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Houston oil company clad-pipe offset project cancellation"
- **Frame in this section:** Student summer project — corrosive crude oil required clad steel pipe; host country's offset regulations (worker quota, 15-year-experience visa requirement for managers) made project unworkable. Illustrates offset externality and the unintended-consequence dynamic of national content laws.
- **Era:** ~2014 (story is "three years ago")

### Boeing/Airbus offset agreements
- **Anchor:** `§1.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Boeing-Airbus aircraft offset agreements (Africa, South America, Japan)"
- **Frame in this section:** Used to generalize the offset dynamic — Japan's high-tech base absorbs offsets easily; smaller countries' offsets force unworkable concessions.

### US copper tubing industry survival
- **Anchor:** `§1.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. copper tubing manufacturing"
- **Frame in this section:** Five US copper tubing manufacturers still operating in mid-1990s because the transportation cost of "ninety-five percent air" exceeds the cost of importing copper from Chile and extruding domestically. Illustrates transportation externality.
- **Era:** mid-1990s

### Jamestown and Saugus — wood-fueled industries relocated from Britain
- **Anchor:** `§1.p10, §1.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Early American iron and glass industries—British energy crisis relocation" (also: "1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal", "British iron industry wood shortage crisis", "Jamestown glassworks", "Saugus Ironworks", "England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis")
- **Frame in this section:** British deforestation in the 1600s drives ship-mast, iron-charcoal, and glass industries to North American sites with abundant wood. By the 1630s, Jamestown has a glassworks; Saugus has the first US ironworks. Illustrates transportation/energy externality and historical industrial relocation.
- **Era:** 1607–1630s

### Tom Eagar's 1974 employment in the steel industry
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3, §3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Tom Eagar's steel company experience"
- **Frame in this section:** Tom worked in steel industry 1974; at that time US Steel controlled twenty-five percent of world market while internally believing they controlled seventy-five percent. At Bethlehem Steel the cost breakdown was 45/45/10 (labor/raw materials/profit) in the mid-1970s.
- **Era:** 1974–1975

### Soviet aluminum dumping (Siberian hydroelectric / canned electricity)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet aluminum dumping and Alcoa/Norsk Hydro market collapse" (also: "Soviet Union aluminum dumping (1980s)")
- **Frame in this section:** Post-Soviet-breakup Siberian hydroelectric capacity converted to aluminum production for export — the only viable way to transport Siberian electricity into foreign-exchange markets. Aluminum dumped at half the price of Alcoa.
- **Era:** late 1980s–1990s

### Obama hydrogen economy
- **Anchor:** `§3.p7, §3.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Hydrogen vehicle safety concerns (Obama hydrogen economy)"
- **Frame in this section:** Storage problem (700 bar / 3500 psi pressure vessels in passenger cars); invisible-flame hazard at filling stations. Hundreds of millions of dollars spent on storage solutions.
- **Era:** 2008–2010s

### MIT MechE aluminum-water hydrogen-generation thesis
- **Anchor:** `§3.p8, §3.p14`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Johnny Slokum aluminum-water hydrogen generation"
- **Frame in this section:** MechE student defending Friday; aluminum spheres treated with gallium alloy (0.2–0.3%) convert to hydrogen in water in 10–20 seconds via rapid corrosion. Tom proposes this as a safer alternative to compressed hydrogen for vehicles. Conversion efficiency over ninety percent.
- **Era:** 2017

### Sumitomo scandium oxide production
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sumitomo scandium oxide production initiative" (also: "Soviet scandium aluminum alloys research")
- **Frame in this section:** Sumitomo announces 100 tons/year scandium oxide production. Tom believes actual production will be higher. 0.2% Sc in aluminum doubles alloy price; refines grain size; reaches 100 ksi (stronger than Boeing aircraft-wing aluminum). Soviet origin of the discovery.
- **Era:** ~2015–2017

### Attleboro gold capital (Leach & Garner)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Attleboro gold alloy and casting operations" (also: "Leech and Garner consulting retainer", "Leech and Garner floor drain gold recovery", "Leech and Garner gold mill consulting")
- **Frame in this section:** Sheffield silverplate process adapted in Attleboro for gold-filled jewelry. Tom started consulting for Leach & Garner (founded 1899; sons Phil Leach and Steve Garner) in 1978 or 1980. Three continuous casters processing seven tons of gold per year. Composites — gold over brass — let consumers pretend to be rich.
- **Materials/systems:** gold-filled brass; silverplate
- **Era:** 1899–present (Tom's involvement: 1978–)
