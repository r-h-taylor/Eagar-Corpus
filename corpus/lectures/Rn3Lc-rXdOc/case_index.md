## Cases

### Alexander Kielland disaster
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p4`, `§2.p5`, `§2.p6`, `§2.p7`, `§2.p9`, `§2.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alexander Kielland disaster"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended case study (sustained across §2). Maintenance welders welding without proper procedure controls created a hydrogen crack at a hydrophone-mount weld; the crack grew by fatigue to 50 feet over ~18 months until one of five legs separated and the platform capsized in a North Sea storm. Source attribution: Easterling textbook.
- **Materials/systems:** Medium-carbon steel pipe (hydrophone mount) welded onto low-carbon process-controlled legs; jack-up rig converted to flotel; ~250 berths.
- **Era:** ~1980.

### 1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists (Paton Institute)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`, `§4.p5`, `§4.p6`, `§4.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended travelogue/case sequence. Tom's 1980 visit to the Paton Welding Institute in Kiev. Two specific demonstrations: (a) underwater wet welding electrodes producing weld quality unattainable in the West, later licensed (Tom suspects) to Broco; (b) titanium welding under Gurevich, the welding expert who taught the Soviets to weld the Alpha submarine.
- **Materials/systems:** Underwater shielded-arc electrodes; titanium electroslag and related processes.
- **Era:** 1980 visit; technology transferred to West in early 1990s.

### Electric Boat torpedo-tube distortion control
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Torpedo tube welding distortion control" / "Submarine torpedo tube distortion and machining"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first bachelor student (named only as "John"), graduated 1977-78, went to Electric Boat. Solved the distortion problem in welding ~30-foot torpedo tubes by real-time dial-indicator monitoring during circumferential welding, varying start point of next pass, grinding out and re-welding where needed, leaving only a clean-up skin pass.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-wall steel pipe, submarine torpedo tubes.
- **Era:** 1980s.

### Bethlehem Steel low-sulfur A360 marketing
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Bethlehem Steel low-sulfur A360 competitive failure (1970s)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's experience as Bethlehem Steel engineer. Japanese suppliers selling A360 I-beams with 0.005 sulfur as standard; Bethlehem trying to extract a $20/ton (8%) premium for low-sulfur and failing. Part of the broader U.S. Steel industry decline narrative.
- **Era:** Tom's tenure at Bethlehem, ~1970s.

## Figures referenced (not cases)

- **Paris law** (`§2.p5`) — fatigue crack growth scales with stress cycles to the 3.5 power. Tom attributes to Paul Paris (Ohio State). This is a teaching figure, not a case.
- **Fatigue 10/90 rule** (`§2.p6`) — first 10% of length grows over 90% of life; last 90% of length in last 10% of life. Rule of thumb, possibly 80/20.
- **Iron-hydrogen solubility:** 30 ppm in liquid, ~10 ppm on solidification (`§5.p3`); monatomic hydrogen solubility ~50× diatomic (`§5.p6`).
- **Aluminum vs. steel solidification shrinkage:** 6% vs. 3.2% (`§6.p1`).
- **Sulfur in steel, historical:** U.S. 1970 = 0.025; spec 0.040 from 1940s; spec 0.050 from 1920s; U.S. today 0.005; Japan 1970s 0.005 (`§3.p3`–`§3.p4`).
