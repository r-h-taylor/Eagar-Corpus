## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Three Mile Island insurance dispute
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Three Mile Island"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to introduce the stress-corrosion-cracking vs. stress-assisted-cracking distinction as an insurance-policy issue. GPU's attorney reads the policy, finds corrosion is excluded, and the terminology gets rewritten — a "day and a half" later — to remove "corrosion" from the press releases.
- **Materials/systems:** reactor pressure vessel, stress corrosion cracking
- **Era:** 1979 (event); recounted as personal experience

### Bettis nickel-alloy electron-beam weld specification
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3` through `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Westinghouse Bettis inconel electron beam weld cracking specification"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Eastern Massachusetts EB shop making pipe welds for Navy reactors in nickel-based alloys. The Bettis specification is "the first spec and the only spec I've ever seen in welding where cracks are allowed" — cracks permitted below ~1mm, justified by fracture mechanics. Tom was qualifying samples for six months; the shop kept failing the spec. Tom was kept out of the technical details due to lack of security clearance.
- **Materials/systems:** nickel-based alloys, half-inch material, electron beam welding, Navy reactor piping
- **Era:** unspecified (Tom's "five years doing hundreds of failure analyses" consulting period)

- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric boiling water reactor stress corrosion cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as the exemplar of attacking a problem across all three of Tom's circles (metallurgy / mechanical / corrosion) rather than just the metallurgist's circle.
- **Materials/systems:** reactor materials, stress corrosion cracking
- **Era:** unspecified

### USS Patrick Henry hull penetrations
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Patrick Henry hull degradation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 1988 picture of the Patrick Henry showed a "patchwork quilt" of hull penetrations. Used to make the point that hull lifetime is limited not by the steel's corrosion or metallurgy but by geometric deviation from a perfect cylinder after thirty years of cut-and-reweld work.
- **Materials/systems:** submarine hull, penetration welds, pressure-hull cylindricity
- **Era:** 1988 (photo); 30-year-life context
