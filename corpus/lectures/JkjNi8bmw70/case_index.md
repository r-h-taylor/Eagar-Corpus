# Case Index — Lecture 4 (JkjNi8bmw70)

*3.371 What is Engineering, Fall 2015, session 4 of 12. Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§9.p5`).*

This lecture is primarily a history-of-engineering and history-of-MIT survey. Most of its content is narrative-historical rather than case-based in the corpus sense. The entries below are the named units that surface; many are figures (numeric or historical anchors Tom returns to across lectures) rather than forensic cases.

## Cases referenced

### Egyptian-Israeli tank war (1973) as a West Point teaching exemplar
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Egyptian-Israeli Tank War`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as a metaphor for the West Point pedagogical style — "they tell you how it's done" — contrasted with the Harvard Business School case-study method and the MIT Sloan quantitative method. Not a forensic teaching case here; cited rhetorically.

### Automobile spot welding quality (3000 welds / 2000 good)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Automobile spot welding quality`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as a self-illustrative example of communication-driven pedagogy. Tom recounts giving the 1989 Gatlinburg keynote on resistance welding and discovering that the memorable framing ("three thousand spot welds because you need two thousand good ones") got quoted back to him a year later. The case stands in for a recurring corpus topic (automotive spot weld reliability) but is deployed here as a story about how to teach, not about the welding itself.

## Figures and recurring numeric anchors

### US share of world steel production (1945: 75%, ~1970: 25%)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: `US share of world steel production (1945–1970)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's setup for the Sputnik-as-wake-up-call narrative. The 75%-to-25% collapse over twenty-five years is a framing statistic Tom uses across multiple lectures to motivate discussions of the US steel industry decline and Japanese competition.
- **Note:** Figure, not case. May warrant promotion to the figures index when the figures-vs-cases taxonomy is consolidated.

### Materials engineering share of practicing engineers (~60%)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: `Materials engineering share of practicing engineers`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom cites a study finding ~25% of practicing engineers are directly doing materials work and another ~35% indirectly — for ~60% total. Used to motivate the Course Three / materials subgroup point in §4.p3.
- **Note:** Figure, not case.

## Named historical persons and institutions surfaced (not cases proper)

These are biographical/institutional anchors used in Tom's history-of-engineering narrative. Listed here so corpus search across lectures can pick them up; not promoted to case status.

- **École Polytechnique** (§4.p5) — first engineering school, France, military origin.
- **West Point** (§4.p5) — 1802, first US engineering school.
- **Rensselaer** (§4.p5, §4.p6) — 1824, civil engineering distinguishing itself from military.
- **University of Michigan** (§4.p6) — 1840s engineering, four students ("bears or raccoons").
- **William Barton Rogers** (§4.p6, §6.p2, §6.p4) — MIT founder, 1861 charter, land-grant petition.
- **Eli Whitney / Cyrus McCormick / Colt** (§4.p6) — early American manufacturing and interchangeable parts.
- **Henry Clifton Sorby** (§5.p1) — 1873, English geologist, founder of metallography.
- **Andrew Carnegie** (§5.p1, §6.p5) — richest man in the world; later, $100K gift for Harvard's Allston land.
- **Edison vs. Westinghouse / AC vs. DC** (§5.p2) — birth of electrical engineering, ~1879–1880.
- **Arthur D. Little** (§5.p3) — 1888, MIT chemistry grad, founder of consulting firm and chemical engineering as a discipline.
- **Jerome Hunsaker** (§5.p4) — founder of MIT aeronautical engineering; transatlantic seaplane flight.
- **Manson Benedict** (§5.p5) — 1958, founder of MIT nuclear engineering.
- **Justin Smith Morrill** (§6.p2) — 1863 Land Grant Act; Tom misstates his state as Illinois (correct: Vermont).
- **Frederick Taylor** (§6.p5) — father of industrial engineering, Harvard Business School 1921, time-and-motion studies at Watertown Arsenal.
- **George Eastman** (§7.p3) — anonymous $6M donor who rescued MIT; suicide 1929 [actually 1932].
- **Alfred Sloan** (§8.p1) — MIT grad, GM consolidator, endowed Sloan School in 1950.
- **Ed Schein** (§8.p2) — Sloan organizational behavior faculty; source of the "Harvard is West Point, MIT is Bell Labs" formulation.
- **Lester Thurow** (§9.p1–p8) — Sloan dean, exemplar of communication-driven teaching; the "extinct volcanoes" line.
- **Robert Merton / Black–Scholes** (§9.p5, §9.p6) — derivatives formula, Merton's Harvard hire, Nobel Prize.
- **Paul Krugman** (§9.p6, §9.p7) — Princeton economist; the "to live well a nation must produce well" line.
- **Francis A. Walker** (§10.p2) — MIT president 1881–1897; the 1891 "schools of applied science" quotation.
- **William H. Walker** (§10.p3, §10.p4) — chemical engineer; MIT chemical engineering practice school; antagonist of Noyes.
- **Arthur Amos Noyes** (§10.p3, §10.p4, §10.p5) — physical chemist, briefly MIT president, lost the industry-engagement battle, co-founder of Caltech.
- **Robert Millikan** (§10.p4, §10.p5) — physicist of the oil-drop experiment; co-founder of Caltech with Noyes.
- **Theodore von Kármán** (§10.p5) — Caltech engineer, founder of Jet Propulsion Lab.
