## Cases referenced in §6–§9 (glass industry)

### Sleepy Hollow Glass and bullseye glass economics
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Sleepy Hollow Glass bullseye demonstration
- **Frame in this lecture:** Historical: untaxed scrap glass over door lintels for light without windows; today: art glass at $55 for a 6-inch square. Pricing inversion (scrap → art).
- **Materials/systems:** hand-blown soda-lime glass
- **Era:** 18th century historical; contemporary art-glass pricing

### Saint-Gobain and Versailles
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`, `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Sango Bath (Saint-Gobain) founding and long-term strategy`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Abraham Thévart, 1688, pours glass on metal table, rollers, anneal, grind, polish. Saint-Gobain provides windows and mirrors to Versailles. French trade secret in stirring molten glass (Guinand 1798, perfected 1805) holds for ~100 years before British acquire it. MIT departmental tie: alum Kapoor ran Saint-Gobain Northborough research center; funds MadMec.
- **Materials/systems:** plate glass; molten glass stirring
- **Era:** 1688–present; key historical period 1688–1805

### Pittsburgh Plate Glass (PPG) mile-long building
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long factory` (also `Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long facility`)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Turn-of-20th-century Pittsburgh facility: cast at one end, grind and polish moving straight (glass is brittle, can't be turned), exit as plate glass typically ½" or thicker. Now superseded by float glass, but PPG remains active.
- **Materials/systems:** plate glass; grinding and polishing lines
- **Era:** early 1900s onward

### Libbey-Owens-Ford / Ford Motor Company continuous ribbon glass
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Ford Motor Company and Libby Owens Ford float glass development`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Continuous ribbon production invented at Ford 1920; in production by 1926 (glass over weir from tank furnace). Pilkington Brothers commercialized; developed continuous grinding-polishing by 1925. Motivated by Model T / Model A windshield demand.
- **Materials/systems:** flat glass; tank furnace; weir
- **Era:** 1920–1926

### Pilkington float glass process
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`, `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Pilkington float glass process`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Post-WWII development; molten tin bath, 15 ft wide, few inches deep; pull thin glass (⅛" to ¼") off; anneal and cut. Footprint reduced from mile-long PPG building to ~couple hundred yards. Tom toured an Ohio float glass plant; furnace at 1550°C, half a football field.
- **Materials/systems:** float glass; molten tin bath
- **Era:** 1940s development; ongoing technology

### Jena Germany window (plate glass artifact)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`, `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Jenna Germany historical window`
- **Frame in this lecture:** 200-year-old plate glass window with visible chemical-inhomogeneity distortion; modern replacement pane (float glass) in same frame shows undistorted reflection. Used to illustrate pre-stirring vs post-stirring glass quality.
- **Materials/systems:** pre-1798 plate glass; modern float glass
- **Era:** ~200 years old; observed contemporarily

### Corning trade-secret manufacturing culture
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`, `§9.p4`, `§10.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Corning trade-secret manufacturing culture
- **Frame in this lecture:** Highest-security site Tom has ever entered (NDAs, no cameras, Corning takes/reviews/sends pictures). Inbred ceramics workforce that moves to Alfred University in retirement. Two 1970s product lines: incandescent light bulb envelopes and TV cathode ray tubes. Losing CRT business spawned new businesses including LCD substrate glass.
- **Materials/systems:** trade-secret process IP; CRT glass; light bulb glass; LCD substrate
- **Era:** 1970s onward

### Corning ultra-thin slim glass for LCDs
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1–§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Corning Willow Glass development` (best match — Corning thin/flexible LCD glass)
- **Frame in this lecture:** 0.3 mm thick (12 thousandths of an inch), available in 1.5 × 1.8 m sheets. Fusion-drawn alkaline-earth boroaluminosilicate. Melting furnace nozzle and tube made of platinum (~$10M of platinum per furnace).
- **Materials/systems:** alkaline-earth boroaluminosilicate glass; platinum tooling
- **Era:** contemporary (c. 2014)

### LCD stack (six-layer composite)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Liquid crystal display glass-stack architecture
- **Frame in this lecture:** Six-layer build: polarizing film / glass substrate with indium tin oxide / twisted nematic liquid crystal / second glass substrate with ITO / polarizing film / reflective or backlit rear surface. Aside on indium tin oxide as a rare conductive-transparent material; "transparent aluminum is only on Star Trek."
- **Materials/systems:** indium tin oxide; twisted nematic LC; polarizing film
- **Era:** contemporary

### Glass water-vapor corrosion (E-glass fiber strength decay)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5–§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Glass fiber humidity degradation in fiberglass manufacturing`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Freshly pulled E-glass (aluminum borosilicate): 600–700 ksi tensile. One hour later: 500 ksi. Next day: 400 ksi. Month later: brittle. Hence epoxy-coat within 24 hours for fiberglass. Critical flaw size from Ashby fracture-toughness plot: ~10⁻⁴ mm, i.e. tens of angstroms — only modest surface etching is needed.
- **Materials/systems:** E-glass aluminum borosilicate; epoxy coating
- **Era:** general process knowledge

### Bob Rose freshly-made light bulb demonstration
- **Anchor:** `§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Bob Rose light bulb classroom demonstration`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's thesis advisor would stop at Sylvania (Danvers, MA — Corning-licensed light bulb plant) before teaching 3.091, bring fresh light bulbs, throw them at the wall — they bounced. Next day same bulbs shatter. Water-vapor surface corrosion in <24 hours.
- **Materials/systems:** soda-lime light bulb glass
- **Era:** Tom's graduate years onward
