# Case Index — Lecture KfZ44zfZh6I (3.371 Fall 2013, session 2/14)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p10`). This lecture is largely thematic — a price-and-availability framework — but it surfaces several named teaching cases and reference points.*

## Cases referenced

### Leaded brass plumbing fittings phase-out
- **Anchor:** `§3.p6`–`§3.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Leaded brass plumbing fittings phase-out"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to explain why leaded brass was historically standard (machinability — lead inclusions act as lubricant during machining, 5× speed advantage) and why it's being banned. Connects to lead-tin solder ban (1978 Massachusetts).
- **Materials/systems:** Cu-Zn-Pb brass alloys; lead-tin solders.
- **Era:** Pre-1978 (lead solder ban) through ~2014 (leaded brass phase-out).

### Lead-tin solder ban and plumbing failures (1978)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lead-tin solder ban and plumbing failures (1978)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief mention — Massachusetts outlawed lead-tin solders in 1978. Used as evidence of the regulatory shift away from lead in plumbing.
- **Era:** 1978 onward.

### PEX (cross-linked polyethylene) brass fitting failures
- **Anchor:** `§3.p10`–`§3.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PEX (cross-linked polyethylene) brass fitting failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stage demonstration with PEX-A and PEX-B samples. West Coast material scientists alleging brass fittings are defective via dezincification; "hundreds of millions" in Las Vegas settlements, "billions" pending. Tom frames the litigation as bogus — predicted five-year lifetimes on 20-year-old installations. Connects to the externalities discussion (homeowners underwater on mortgages suing builders).
- **Materials/systems:** PEX-A (electron-beam cross-linked), PEX-B (chemically cross-linked); leaded brass fittings.
- **Era:** ~2010s litigation wave.

### Las Vegas brass fitting corrosion litigation
- **Anchor:** `§3.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Las Vegas brass fitting corrosion litigation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Named site of the dezincification class actions; Colorado River water cited as aggressively corrosive.
- **Era:** 2000s–2010s.

### Kresge Auditorium lead roof
- **Anchor:** `§3.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Kresge Auditorium lead roof"
- **Frame in this lecture:** "Thinner than an eggshell in ratio to its radius." Used to make the point that lead is atmospherically durable.
- **Era:** 1955–present.

### US Army materials study
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Army materials study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Million-dollar study, ~mid-1990s, asking what the material of the future for the Army would be. Conclusion: steel. Used as the punchline of the cost-strength-toughness Ashby argument.
- **Era:** ~1993–1998.

### 1980s all-ceramic engine development initiative
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1980s all-ceramic engine development initiative"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference — "in the mid-80s the ceramists were touting that ceramics were going to take over the world." Used as a foil for the steel-as-future argument.
- **Era:** Mid-1980s.

### Catalytic converter cold-start emissions problem
- **Anchor:** `§6.p8`–`§6.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Catalytic converter cold-start emissions problem"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stage demonstration with two catalytic converters — ceramic (Corning extrusion) and a metallic Fe-Cr-Ni-Al-Y alloy substrate. The metal version was studied as a lightweight, fast-heating alternative because cold-start emissions occur before the heavy ceramic substrate reaches operating temperature. Inspection-station advice to run the engine 20 minutes connects to this.
- **Materials/systems:** Cordierite ceramic; Fe-Cr-Ni-Al-Y oxidation-resistant alloy sheet; platinum/palladium catalysts.

### Andrew Mellon all-aluminum Pierce Arrow automobile / 1930s all-aluminum Duesenberg automobiles
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1930s all-aluminum Duesenberg automobiles"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief mention as evidence that all-aluminum automobiles are not a new idea — used to undermine the 1990s prediction that steel would disappear from cars.
- **Era:** 1930s.

### Audi all-aluminum automobiles
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Audi all-aluminum automobiles"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as a real but niche product proving the cost barrier — you can buy an all-aluminum Audi, but you can't buy an all-aluminum Ford Taurus at Taurus prices.
- **Era:** 1990s–present.

### Ford Taurus aluminum body cost analysis
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford Taurus aluminum body cost analysis"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom recounts his disagreement with Professor (Joel) Clark's student's thesis, which concluded an aluminum Taurus would cost only $500 more. Tom challenged the analysis on fabricability grounds; the student rewrote a page but, in Tom's telling, missed the point.
- **Era:** Early 1990s.

### 1973 Arab oil embargo
- **Anchor:** `§5.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1973 Arab oil embargo"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Pivot point in the world economy — before 1973, steel controlled the world economy; after, oil did. Tom said "'72" on tape; silently corrected to '73 (see editorial register).
- **Era:** 1973.

## Figures referenced (not cases)

- **Steel production rate:** ~1 billion tons/year globally (§5.p4, §5.p6).
- **Aluminum production rate:** ~40 million tons/year (Tom hedges: "or maybe 20 million") (§5.p4).
- **Steel price:** ~20 cents/pound (§5.p5, §8.p2).
- **Nickel superalloy price:** $100–$200/pound (§5.p5).
- **Material energy content:** Aluminum 300 GJ/ton; steel ~50 GJ/ton (one-sixth); copper ~100 GJ/ton (§2.p4–p5).
- **Gold:silver:copper price ratio:** ~100:1:1, gold-to-copper ~10,000:1 (§2.p6).
- **Carbon fiber cost threshold:** $2/lb (Tom's threshold for structural use); $10–15/lb (then-current); ~$6/lb (most recent quote Tom heard) (§6.p5).
- **Diamond price multiplier:** ~200 million times steel on a per-ton basis (§4.p2).
- **Materials cost as fraction of product cost:** ~10% (§8.p1); steel at 20¢/lb becomes $2/lb fabricated (§8.p2).
- **Steel recycling rate:** 70–80% (§5.p6); aluminum recycling rate: 50–60% (§5.p6).
- **Cost-reduction → market-growth slope (Westbrook plot):** factor-2 cost reduction yields factor-4 volume increase (§7.p4).
- **"Future of Metals" 1990 market projections:** ceramics $100B, composites $50B, silicon $100B, steel $500B (now ~$1T) (§5.p8).
