*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§6.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is primarily a materials-properties catalog (titanium, aluminum series, magnesium) with extended digressions. Cases appear as illustrations of alloy properties or industrial practice rather than as developed forensic teaching units. The longest case-like passage is the aircraft brake / engine lease-by-the-hour digression in §7.

## Cases referenced

### Air Force turbine blade repair (Oklahoma City)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force turbine blade repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Illustration of weld-buildup additive repair of high-value turbine blades. 25 weld passes, ~7mm buildup, on a $500,000 blade. Justifies HIP-cast-blade economics.
- **Materials/systems:** Cast nickel-base superalloy turbine blade; gas tungsten arc weld buildup; HIP densification.

### Space Shuttle main tank (2219 aluminum)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle External Tank (2219 aluminum)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Example of why 2219 was chosen for the Space Shuttle main tank — more weldable than other 2000-series Al-Cu alloys. ~4.5% copper.
- **Materials/systems:** 2219 aluminum, Al-Cu eutectic system.
- **Era:** 1970s–early 2000s.

### Wright brothers' aircraft engine
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2` (also referenced in §3.p4)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Wright brothers' aircraft engine"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used twice. First in §3 as the date-marker for 2014 aluminum alloy ("over a hundred years"). Second in §5 as the cracking-on-welding example — GTAW the Wright brothers' crankcase and you'd get equation-melting cracks in the HAZ, same problem as Monel.
- **Materials/systems:** 2014 aluminum (Al-4.4Cu); gas tungsten arc welding; heat-affected zone cracking.
- **Era:** ~1903.

### Coast Guard 103-foot cutter cracking
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Coast Guard 103-foot cutter cracking failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Cutters had to be scrapped due to high-strength steel welding issues. Tom uses it to motivate HY-100 as the working high-strength steel for naval use.
- **Materials/systems:** High-strength steel hull; HY-100 alternative.
- **Note:** the surrounding classroom aside about impressed current cathodic protection is cut from layer 3. The case reference itself is preserved.

### Aluminum beer cans (40% of world aluminum)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Aluminum beverage can industry tonnage"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to anchor the 3000-series (Al-Mn) aluminum alloy in industrial scale. Largest tonnage use of aluminum at one time was forty percent into beer cans.
- **Materials/systems:** 3000-series Al-Mn alloy, non-heat-treatable.
- **Note:** related to but distinct from the canonical "1980s aluminum beverage can design optimization using supercomputers" cluster — this is the *industry tonnage* framing, not the *design optimization* case.

### Navy magnesium-carbon diving composite (Panama City)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`–`§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval diver thermal protection using magnesium alloy" / "Navy diver salvage gas generation system (magnesium composite)" / "Fort Fundy magnesium-graphite composite diving application"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Two applications of the same magnesium-carbon composite: salvage (generates hydrogen on contact with seawater, lifts objects via plastic-bag flotation) and diver thermal protection (corrosion heat warms diver in cold water). Demonstrates "magnesium corrodes really well" point.
- **Materials/systems:** Pressed magnesium-carbon powder composite; seawater electrochemical corrosion; hydrogen evolution.
- **Note:** three near-identical canonical clusters exist for this case in the aggregate; this lecture covers both the salvage and thermal applications in one passage. Reconciliation candidate.

### Magnesium in automotive dashboards
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Department of Energy 9% nickel steel qualification effort" — *no, this is the wrong cluster.* PROPOSED: "DOE magnesium automotive lightweighting program"
- **Frame in this lecture:** DOE has spent tens to hundreds of millions per year over fifty years on magnesium-for-automotive lightweighting. Currently magnesium is used in cast substructure under the dashboard, where it's protected from corrosion.
- **Materials/systems:** Cast magnesium automotive components.

### Boeing 777 brake certification testing
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 777 brake certification testing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Aircraft must land, stop in specified distance, and the brakes can catch fire, but the airframe must hold three minutes before fire-crew intervention without engine damage or hydraulic leak.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon-carbon composite brakes; 450-ton aircraft kinetic energy dissipation.

### Honeywell brake leasing / Pratt & Whitney power-by-the-hour engines
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`–`§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Commercial aircraft lease-by-the-hour maintenance economics"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Honeywell leases brakes to airlines, gets paid per landing, and so has incentive to design long-life brakes. Pratt & Whitney and GE (or brokerage firms) do the same with engines on a power-by-the-hour basis. Trend extending toward aircraft-by-the-hour leasing.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon-carbon brakes; gas turbine engines.
- **Note:** this is an *economic structure* case rather than a forensic-failure case. It illustrates how maintenance incentives shape materials/component design.

### Deer Island sewage treatment plant (aluminum piping)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Deer Island wastewater treatment aluminum piping"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Example of aluminum's freshwater corrosion resistance. Lower-strength alloys carrying dirty water at Boston Harbor cleanup facility.
- **Materials/systems:** Lower-strength corrosion-resistant aluminum alloy.

### Aluminum foil cooking (Boy Scout foil dinner)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Aluminum foil cooking water-cooling demonstration"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Teaching anecdote. Aluminum foil melts above the temperature of a campfire, but water in the cooking food keeps it cool. Put a dry steel bolt in the foil and the foil will melt around it.
- **Note:** likely a recurring rhetorical anecdote across lectures rather than a developed case. Reconciliation needed.
