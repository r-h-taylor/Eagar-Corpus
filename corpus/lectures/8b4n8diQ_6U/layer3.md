## §3. Specifications, measurement, and Lord Kelvin [07:58]

`§3.p1` We're going to talk about codes and standards, but today I want to get into what — when we're dealing with codes and standards, it's sort of an agreement between the purchaser of something and the seller of what you're going to buy. If you go to buy a house, you want so many bedrooms, you want such and such in the kitchen, you want such and such in the garage, and you have specifications for what you want.

`§3.p2` You also may specify things in a little more detail about the quality of what you want. If you want real plywood as opposed to oriented strand board in your house, you're going to have to specify it. If you want a certain quality of blueboard gypsum as opposed to cheaper gypsum, you've got to specify it. Otherwise people are free to come in and use whatever they want. So if you want to maintain quality you have to specify it, and you also have to be able to measure it.

`§3.p3` And as Lord Kelvin said: "When you can measure what you're speaking about and express it in numbers, you know something about it; but when you cannot measure it, when you cannot express it in numbers, your knowledge is of a meager and unsatisfactory kind. So part of your MIT education is to learn to be quantitative in your opinions. Believe me, if you can be quantitative in your opinions, you will win many arguments. Because most people don't know how to be quantitative. They just say, well, I think — and if building a ship or any other product is a faith-based exercise, then what you believe would be wonderful. You need to learn how to specify things, and as Lord Kelvin says, how to measure things and quantify things.

`§3.p4` So what I want to talk about today is what I call observables. What can we observe, what can we measure. ## §6. LIGO and the most precise measurement ever made [27:28]

`§6.p1` If you're dealing in parts per million, you have to worry about these things. But who's dealing in parts per million? Well, some people are doing better than that. The most precise measurement ever attempted is in Livingston, Louisiana, and Hanford, Washington. Anyone from either the state of Washington or Louisiana ever heard about this? It's LIGO, the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory.

`§6.p2` The LIGO project was originally funded at $300 million by the National Science Foundation. It was a joint project between MIT and Caltech. The chief scientist of LIGO is Ray Weiss, a retired professor of physics here. It's two Michelson interferometers. Anybody know what a Michelson interferometer is? You didn't learn this in freshman physics? Let's see if you can recall it. A Michelson interferometer — you can have a bench-type interferometer, but basically you have light coming in, you split it with a mirror, one beam goes down some length L1, the other typically at 90° goes down length L2, you have a mirror at the end of each, and the beams reflect back. As the wavelengths beat together, you can measure the phase angle difference between them, and you can tell the difference between L1 and L2 very precisely, because now you're measuring the shift in phase angle. It's the same beam of light, and the speed of light is a constant, so far as we know. So you can measure these two lengths very precisely because now it's a fraction of a wavelength of light. A Michelson interferometer is a very precise way to measure length, or the speed of light. If you know the length, you can measure the speed; if you know the speed, you can measure the length.

`§6.p3` LIGO has one of these in Livingston, Louisiana, and another in Hanford, Washington, a couple thousand miles apart. The length is 4 kilometers each. `§6.p4` Because the density of air can change the speed of light — the speed of light is defined in a vacuum — they had to build stainless steel tubes 4 km long and pull a vacuum on it at about one-trillionth or one-quadrillionth of an atmosphere. `§6.p5` In order to run this thing, they had to align the mirror at one end with the mirror at the other within 1 cm over 4 kilometers. One centimeter over 4 km is one part in 400,000. That's pretty precise. No one had ever needed to survey 1 cm over 4 km before. They had to develop new surveying techniques using GPS. Anybody know what wave GPS is? I've heard of it. You use the satellites up there in space, but you have a reference point here that helps calibrate all of them to a reference position. `§6.p6` Kathleen, you were in Aero and Astro, right? You know John Hansman, Professor Hansman? Okay, you didn't know him. `§6.p7` In the LIGO project, the Australians are now talking about building one, and then they'll have them 10,000 miles apart as opposed to 2,000 miles apart, because they can up their accuracy. ## §7. Triple point of water and the temperature standard [36:27]

`§7.p1` For temperature, what's the triple point of water or any pure substance? Where all three phases exist. The triple point is where solid, liquid, and vapor coexist. It is an invariant point thermodynamically — if you believe thermodynamics, and who even understands it, so how can you believe it? If I look at the triple point of water, there's pressure and temperature, and I have something that looks like this, where this is vapor, liquid, and solid, and there's a triple point. According to Gibbs, the phase rule says that's an invariant point. If I can come up with some way to get solid, liquid, and vapor to coexist, that temperature is uniquely defined. In fact the temperature scale is defined as the triple point of water is 273.16 degrees Kelvin. What's the melting point of ice at one atmosphere? 273.15. The triple point varies from the one-atmosphere melting point by a hundredth of a degree.

`§7.p2` *[Tom holds up a triple-point-of-water cell.]* For $1,500 you can go and buy from Fluke these cells. These cells were probably developed by some national laboratory somewhere, but it's nothing more than borosilicate or quartz tubes — the quartz is more expensive — filled with water. But not just any water. This is multiple distilled water, super pure water — they've gotten rid of all the salts and stuff — because this water has to have the same isotopic concentration as Vienna mean sea water. People have measured lake water, rain water, ocean water, and they all have a different isotopic concentration of oxygen-16, oxygen-17, oxygen-18, hydrogen, deuterium, tritium. All of those will change the triple point depending on isotopic concentration. Because these things have accuracies of — let me just read it to you.

`§7.p3` The uncertainty is less than one millionth of a degree, or one ten-thousandth of a degree centigrade, for these things. So if you want to calibrate your thermometers, you can buy these little special cells of water. You can also buy them in gallium, ultra pure copper, and you can do a whole set of temperature scales if you want to be accurate within a fraction of a Kelvin. We don't usually need that, in most cases. But some people do, when they're trying to make very precise measurements such as the fundamental constants.

## §9. Positive materials identification — the Canadian tar sands case [56:48]

`§9.p1` The point is, there are physical properties of materials, and they're not very sophisticated. We're not measuring things to six parts per million in order to tell the difference. Let me tell you about positive materials identification — anyone ever heard of PMI? You haven't run into that in a shipyard? It's that the alloy is what it's supposed to be. It's part of a quality control program. None of you have actually worked in a shipyard yet, and you haven't gotten into PMI. You don't have to; it depends on what you're doing in the shipyard.

`§9.p2` In some chemical plants, a chemical refinery, I've seen data of different steel pipes where they've gone in and done a very careful positive materials identification, and 6% of all the pipes are mislabeled as to what type of steel they are. You say, well, it's no big deal, it's all steel. Well, not necessarily. I was involved 25 years ago in a case up in the tar sands in Canada, where someone was supposed to repair a pipe. They had cut out a little piece — they were putting in an 18-inch piece of 6-inch steel pipe, welding it back in. They had to open it up to get in and clean out the pipe, because a bunch of coke deposits had formed inside the pipe and they had to clean it out — it was getting hardening of the arteries and couldn't get the oil to flow through anymore. So they cut out a piece, went in with their reaming tools, cleaned it out, and welded a piece back in.

`§9.p3` The piece they welded back in was plain old carbon steel. It wasn't 1% chrome-moly steel, which has better sulfur corrosion resistance. About three years later the wall had thinned down from a quarter-inch to 0.007 inches — about four sheets of paper. The pressure blew it open, started a fire that cost a billion dollars. People were not happy, particularly the people who had to spend the billion dollars.

`§9.p4` Positive materials identification is a big deal. In that case they were blaming the welder. "He should have known when he was grinding it that it wasn't carbon steel." I said, how would he know when he was grinding it? Now, if he was working in a steel mill or in a shipyard in the 1930s or '40s or even '50s and '60s, he might have known. Or his father or grandfather might have known. Because there's something we used to use for PMI in a steel yard fifty, sixty, seventy years ago, called a spark test.

`§9.p5` Different steels, when you grind them, give different sparks. You didn't know that, did you. If you go look up "spark test" in Wikipedia — high carbon steel, manganese steel, tungsten steel, molybdenum steel — they all give different-looking sparks. *[Tom takes a chrome-moly sample to the grinder.]* This is a chrome-moly steel, the kind of thing that ball bearing races are made out of. Watch the sparks. See the secondary sparks. *[Tom grinds a carbon steel sample.]* This is carbon steel. Doesn't look a lot different to you, does it. But there are guys who've been doing this for 30 years and they can pick out the difference between those with dead accuracy.

`§9.p6` *[Tom grinds a 440C sample.]* Here's 440C. Can you see the difference? This is a stainless steel; these are both carbon steels. These burn up in the air. Stainless has a chrome oxide and doesn't burn very well. *[Tom grinds a 304 stainless sample.]* 304 stainless. I can tell stainless from non-stainless, but someone really discriminating on a spark test could pick out every one of these, because they do it every day. I look at someone else's children — twins or triplets — I can't tell the difference, but their mother can. Even their father can.

`§9.p7` So there are very simple tests that we use all the time, whether it's magnets or spark tests. *[Tom produces a chemical spot test kit.]* There are spot tests you can buy. Little kits — this isn't one of them, but a kit like this has a bunch of drops of different chemicals, and you sand the surface, put a drop of the chemical on, and watch how it foams.

`§9.p8` Anyone ever gone to a pawnbroker and tried to sell some gold? Neither have I. But a reputable pawnbroker — assuming there is such a person — will take out some nitric acid and put a little drop of it on the gold, and he'll look at how the nitric acid reacts on the karat gold. He can tell usually within plus or minus one karat number what the karat is. He can tell 14 from 18 from 10 karat by how vigorously the nitric acid reacts with the other elements in the gold. Simple little tests. If you Google "chemical spot tests," you'll find all kinds of simple little tests.

`§9.p9` *[Tom holds up an XRF analyzer.]* But that's not what we use today. For $30,000 you can have this little gadget. It's basically got a radioactive isotope, and you just stick it up against the surface of something and pull the trigger. The radioisotope generates X-rays, it's got an X-ray detector in there, and it gives you a computer printout of all the elements in the metal. So we don't have a lot of people who know how to do the spark test with accuracy anymore, because now we have people who go out with a $30,000 instrument and pull the trigger. But still, you go down to a laydown yard for pipes and 6% of them are liable to be mislabeled.

## §10. The Persian Gulf destroyer brass fitting case [64:01]

`§10.p1` So who cares? Why should you care if you're in the US Navy? A few years ago, when we were doing our corrosion course in January — actually the first or second year we ever did it — I had a student get up and give a presentation. It was during the second Gulf War. They were on a destroyer. The war didn't last but a couple of days, but it was the first day, the first few hours, and this one destroyer was there in the Gulf going out on assignment, and they lost their 3,000 psi air system.

`§10.p2` The 3,000 psi air system runs probably most things on the ship. It's your pneumatic system, and it runs lots of things just like electricity runs lots of things. The compressed air system runs lots of things. When they lost their system, they had to pull back. The admirals weren't happy — they lost a capital ship in the middle of the thing. Not that they lost the ship, they just couldn't use the ship. You can imagine how pleased your commander would have been. You can imagine how pleased the captain of the destroyer was to know that his career was now over.

`§10.p3` Afterwards they looked at this brass fitting, and it was stress corrosion cracking. The student got up and gave this little presentation of what they had found. It turns out another student in the class was working in the Philadelphia Supply Depot about the same time. When they found that it was the wrong brass alloy — it was not the correct alloy specified by NAVSEA for this application, and that's why it had failed by stress corrosion cracking — of course they go and say, well, check our Supply Depot and make sure all the stuff in stock is correct. 80% of the stock in the warehouse was the wrong alloy. That student wasn't planning to present that, but as part of our discussion she said, oh, I was in the supply at that time and we found 80% of the stuff was bad.

`§10.p4` So PMI is important. Going back to Lord Kelvin, you have to know how to measure what you've got to get some real data. It doesn't have to be sophisticated. But it creates big problems. It created a billion-dollar problem up in Canada. It created a ship that had to come offline in the middle of an assault. Not good things. Mistakes happen, but they happen because people make mistakes, and because of those mistakes they can get multiplied.
