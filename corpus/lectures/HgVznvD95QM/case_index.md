## Cases referenced

### Molten Metal Technologies
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`, `§2.p6`, `§3.p1`–`§3.p9`, `§4.p1`–`§4.p10`, `§5.p1`–`§5.p4`, `§6.p3`–`§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Molten Metal Technologies"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended case study of an MIT-licensed startup (1989–1997) that used molten steel baths for toxic waste processing. Tom narrates: the technology origin (Chris Nagel, ex-US Steel); MIT's John Preston as licensing officer with conflicting financial interest; venture promotion through Vic Gatto, Al Gore, Maurice Strong, Michael Porter; the technical failures (refractory destruction by sodium/chlorine, phosgene formation, dioxin/furan production, bismuth/antimony steel contamination); MIT institutional conflicts of interest; bankruptcy in 1997; Preston's later fraud conviction.
- **Materials/systems:** molten iron, refractory ceramics, sodium and chlorine contamination, phosgene (COCl2), dioxins, furans, bismuth, antimony, low-level radioactive waste
- **Era:** 1989–1997 (with Preston follow-on through later)

### Molten Metal Technology tenure case
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`–`§4.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT conflict of interest in Molten Metal promotion" (closely related); see also "Molten Metal Technology tenure case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A junior MIT materials faculty member receiving MMT research funding from Nagel was denied tenure after Tom (as department head) refused to suppress his publication. Tom alleges his predecessor as department head, who was on MMT's scientific advisory board, polluted the tenure case by influencing external letters (notably from Professor Fuwa of Tohoku University). The professor was later denied tenure at MIT and became a tenured full professor at Boston University.
- **People named:** Chris Nagel, Professor Fuwa (Tohoku University), John Chipman (referenced — Chipman Room), Bob Brown (Dean of Engineering, later Provost; named in §7.p1)

### Nashua Corporation board collapse and acquisition failure
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`–`§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Nashua Corporation board collapse and acquisition failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's personal experience on the Nashua Corporation board (1993 onward). A new CEO took the half-billion-dollar company to a quarter-billion in one year; pushed for a twenty-five-million-dollar European acquisition; twelve other board members voted yes despite all twelve having articulated reasons against. Tom abstained. He was later given a Steuben glass mountain lion sculpture as a parting gift — the "mountain lion ready to pounce" — and a consulting retainer. Used as the closing demonstration of the lecture's lesson about going along with the crowd.
- **Physical artifact:** Steuben glass mountain lion sculpture (shown to class)
