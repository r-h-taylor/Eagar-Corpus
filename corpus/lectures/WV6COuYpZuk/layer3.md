## §1. Quality, codes, and the discontinuity-defect distinction [00:07]

`§1.p1` Hello again. My name is Mike Balfour [Balfor]. I'm back to lecture today on weld inspection, and a little bit about Certified Welding Inspectors — the people who typically are called upon to do weld inspection. As we go through this, if anybody has any questions, feel free to ask.

`§1.p2` Before we get into the details of inspection, I wanted to talk a little bit about quality. What is quality? When you hear the word, various things might come to mind. But in terms of manufactured products — and I don't know if Professor Eagar has covered this — in order for something to have quality, it has to have a measurable conformance to a specification. I have some handouts for you today, so if you want to pass those around you can follow along.

`§1.p3` To establish product quality there has to be something that's measurable. In order for something to be measurable and to determine whether it meets that quality, there has to be a specification or a code — something that states what the requirements are. When we talk about quality of welded materials, we talk about welding codes and welding specifications.

`§1.p4` To be the quality person or the certified inspector, wherever we fit in the process, we need to be able to read and understand and apply the provisions of those governing documents — the codes and standards that are often cited in the contract or the purchase order or the job specification.

`§1.p5` Another thing that comes up a lot in welding quality is the difference between a discontinuity and a defect. You might say those sound like the same thing — that's what most people think. But a discontinuity is anything that interrupts the general flow or the uniformity of an object. A defect is a discontinuity that doesn't meet the specifications, that doesn't meet the codes, that doesn't meet the provisions of those codes.

`§1.p6` Later on we're going to talk about some of the specific discontinuities and defects we might find in welds. One of them is porosity. If you have a single small pore in a weld, it's a discontinuity. But generally if it's small enough, it's not a defect — it doesn't violate the code, because the code will allow you to have a certain number of pores in a certain length of weld at a certain size. It becomes a defect when it violates those provisions of the code.

## §2. Document hierarchy: codes, specifications, recommended practices, guides [03:52]

`§2.p1` A few words about the different documents that would be used. A code is a body of laws arranged systematically for easy reference and use — "easy" in quotation marks, because sometimes the codes are somewhat complicated. Because a code has legal status, by definition it's mandatory.

`§2.p2` An example of a code is the Structural Welding Code that you've probably heard of in this class. It gives the legal rules. It uses the word "shall" when something is mandatory, and "should" when something is just recommended. Anytime there's something in the code that says you "shall" do this, and your contract specifications say you need to follow AWS D1.1, then that's mandatory. There's no debating it.

`§2.p3` The second document type is a specification. A specification gives a detailed description of the parts of a whole, or a statement of particulars. It talks about size and performance and quality. It describes all of the pertinent technical information for a material or a product or a system or a service, and how those requirements should be met.

`§2.p4` One type of specification we deal with is a weld procedure specification. It tells you how you should make your weld; it tells you how to meet the code requirements. But it doesn't have all the code written into it — it's one level down from the code.

`§2.p5` The next level is a recommended practice — a non-mandatory description of generally accepted industrial methods or techniques. The American Welding Society puts out a lot of recommended practices for gas tungsten arc welding or for shielded metal arc welding. It's industry knowledge that gives you a great idea about how to go about doing these things. It's just not a code, just not mandatory.

`§2.p6` One level down is a guide. That just provides information on methods to accomplish certain tasks. For example, there's a guide for the visual examination of welds. It says, if you're going to look at welds, these are the kinds of things you should be looking at.

## §3. The Certified Welding Inspector: role and requirements [06:50]

`§3.p1` So who does these inspections? There are a lot of people involved in weld quality. You have your quality assurance person at the place where you might be working. You have quality control personnel. But the person I wanted to spend the most time on is the person actually out there doing the inspections — the Certified Welding Inspector. If anything of significance needs to be inspected, they require a CWI. They can't just have Joe Blow on the shop floor looking things over, although that does happen sometimes.

`§3.p2` What does the CWI do? He performs inspections. He verifies that the work being inspected and the records that were maintained conform to the requirements of the applicable standards. The big thing he does is compare what's actually there with what's required to be there — actual with required product specifications.

`§3.p3` As things are going along, he's looking; he knows what the requirements are; he's checking to make sure what's actually happening is meeting the requirements. When things are done, he'll get out his weld gauges, his magnifying glass or his flashlight, and he'll look things over to make sure what's actually there is consistent with what was required in the specifications.

`§3.p4` The big thing is that appearance alone is rarely the determination of product quality. It's more than just looking. It's having an understanding of the whole process, having an understanding of what went into making that welded product, and being there as that process goes.

`§3.p5` What's required to be a CWI? You can't just decide you're going to be the CWI for your company. There are education and experience requirements, all spelled out in great detail on the AWS website, aws.org. For any of you here, because anyone who has an associate's degree or higher — you're still required to have three years of on-the-job experience. There are other levels: if you're only a high school graduate you have to have five years; if you made it through eighth grade, nine years; if you made it through less than eighth grade, twelve years experience before you can apply to become a CWI.

`§3.p6` Once you fill out the paperwork, put in your application, and send AWS your money, there are three two-hour sections — basically an all-day exam. The first is a fundamentals section, which talks about the different welding processes and the non-destructive techniques for testing those welds.

`§3.p7` The next section is a practical application section, using techniques we'll talk about later for visual inspection. You actually have some little plastic molds of welds, they give you some tools, and they ask you a bunch of questions about how you can practically do the weld inspection.

`§3.p8` The third section is an open-code-book section. They ask you a bunch of questions on your choice of codes. Most people do either the D1.1 code or the API 1104 code, which is used for the welding of petroleum pipelines.

`§3.p9` When I first went to do this, I thought, well, this one is really thin, I should do the API code. But it actually turns out to be easier to do the D1.1 code, because for one thing the D1.1 code has an index, whereas this one doesn't. And this one has a lot more information — it's actually easier to find things. The API code is just a bunch of pages; unless you really know it, you have to look hard to find everything.

## §4. CASH: the inspector's inherent attributes [14:05]

`§4.p1` Those are the formal requirements. These are some of the inherent requirements, I guess you'd say. There's a nice little sheet I found in one of my books that says a CWI brings CASH to the job — Knowledge, Attitude, Skills, and Habits. So he's got his four-drawer file cabinet up there with all of these various attributes that a good inspector would have. We'll talk about a few.

`§4.p2` It's important to have a professional attitude. You're dealing with a wide variety of people — the actual people making the welds, supervisors, schedulers. You're dealing with a lot of different people, trying to ensure that the quality is high. Depending on the interest of that person you're dealing with, they may or may not feel as strongly about it. So it's important you have a professional attitude and that you work well with these people.

`§4.p3` It's important to have good physical condition. Sometimes you're climbing around a crane or a bridge, or a pressure vessel. Sometimes you're down on your knees, sometimes you're climbing over things. So it's important you're in fairly good physical condition.

`§4.p4` It's important you're ethical. If you're not willing to stand up for what you know is right, if you're not willing to make sure those codes are followed, then you're not going to be a very good welding inspector.

`§4.p5` You need to be a good communicator. You need to be able to communicate with those various people and explain things so they can understand them. If you find a defect in the weld that needs to be repaired, you need to be able to communicate that to the welders so they know what to repair.

`§4.p6` And you need to have a good general knowledge of welding, welding metallurgy, and welding in general. We'll talk about welding positions, joint types you might come across, the welding symbols you might come across, and the electrode identification systems. The reason those are important is, before you ever start inspecting welds, when you go to a job you're often going to look at their documentation. You need to be able to look at their weld procedures and the things they've got describing what they're doing. If they've got weld symbols on there, or it's talking about a certain weld joint, you need to understand what that is.

## §5. Welding positions [17:17]

`§5.p1` First off with welding positions. You have a couple of different kinds of welds that can be made. One is a fillet weld, which is a weld between two pieces of metal at 90°. *[Mike draws at the board.]* If you've got a piece of metal here and a piece of metal here, you're going to put a weld right there. That's a fillet weld.

`§5.p2` The second type is a groove weld. Looking at a cross-section, you've got a couple of pieces of metal you want to weld together, and you're going to put a weld in here. That's a groove weld, because you're filling up the groove.

`§5.p3` That can be done in a variety of positions. The flat position is if you're welding straight down from above. That's the easiest, and so it's designated as a one. If you have a 1F fillet weld, it would look like this — you're welding here, that's 1F.

`§5.p4` 2F is horizontal. If you just tilted this a little bit, that's a 2F fillet weld. Or if you put this up on its side and you're welding along like that, that's a 2G.

`§5.p5` The other positions you could have are vertical and overhead. When you're welding pipe, you can actually rotate the pipe. If you're welding on a pipe here and you're rotating the pipe underneath, and you're just welding from up here, it's actually in the flat position. You might have a 1GR, which means it's rotated.

`§5.p6` You might also have a fixed pipe — that's what five and six are. If it's in the horizontal position and it's fixed, it's a five. If it's in a 45-degree position and it's fixed, it's a six. `§5.p7` This first set right here: if the plates are horizontal and you're welding from the top, that's a 1G groove weld. 2G — your weld is horizontal, your plates are vertical. The three position, which I mentioned was vertical, is like this — your plates are vertical and your weld is vertical. Overhead is when it's upside down and you're welding overhead.

`§5.p8` This is the same type of thing for fillet welds rather than groove welds. This is what I drew right here — a 1F fillet weld. This is a horizontal fillet weld, or 2F. `§5.p9` Just to show you a couple on the pipe: this is a 1G, because the pipe's being rotated. This down here actually looks the same, but it's a 5G because the pipe is fixed rather than rotated. This would be 2G — the pipe is vertical and the weld would be horizontal.

`§5.p10` I talked about the restricted on the six position. This would be a regular 6G pipe weld. This would be a 6GR, because you've got something in there restricting your access — you may have a flange or something around that pipe.

## §6. Joint types and edge preparation [22:36]

`§6.p1` The next topic is welding joint types. There are five main joint types we would make in welds: butt, corner, T, lap, and edge. I'll show you pictures in a minute.

`§6.p2` There are some rules that go with those. You might hear of a single-bevel butt joint, for example. Where I drew this here, this would have actually been a double bevel because both plates were beveled. A single bevel just means only one plate is beveled.

`§6.p3` "Double" means both, or welded from both sides. "Bevel" refers to one of the members, so you can single-bevel that or double-bevel it. This would be a single-bevel weld, because it would all be welded from this side. You can also have a double bevel — your piece looks like this, and it's welded from both sides.

`§6.p4` Another type of edge preparation we sometimes have is a J groove, which looks kind of like a J. You can have a single J, or you can have a double J, which becomes a U groove.

`§6.p5` Here's an illustration of the various types of joints. A butt joint — you're butting two things together, just like it sounds. A corner joint is just like it sounds — you're making a weld on a corner of two plates or two components. A T joint is just like it sounds — the parts are making a T. A lap joint — the parts are overlapping; you would weld that with a couple of fillet welds, or at least one fillet weld. An edge joint is where you've got two pieces on edge and you're welding along the edge.

## §8. Electrode identification systems and the AWS D1.1 code [31:48]

`§8.p1` Next I wanted to talk a little bit about electrode identification systems. This is part of that general knowledge. When a weld procedure says you've got to weld with shielded metal arc welding — which is stick welding — and you've got to use E7018 electrodes, you need to understand what that means.

`§8.p2` Shielded metal arc welding, as I said, is stick welding, and those electrodes are always going to start with an E. The following two digits, if they're 70 or 60 — they can even be three digits, 110 or something — that's the tensile strength required of the filler material. The third digit is position. So a 7018 can be used in all positions; one refers to all positions in these systems. The eight in 7018 refers to the fact that it's a low-hydrogen electrode. The last digit is a coding or operating-characteristics identifier.

`§8.p3` In gas metal arc welding, which you might know more commonly as MIG welding, it's always going to start with ER. The R stands for rod. Then you've got a couple of digits that identify your strength again — you might have an ER70. Then you might have an S3 or something, which indicates your silicon level, the chemistry of your filler material.
