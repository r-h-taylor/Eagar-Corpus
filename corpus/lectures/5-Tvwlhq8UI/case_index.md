# Case Index — Lecture 10

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§5.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is organized around the productivity revolution in American steel-making. Cases function both as developed teaching units (Copperweld, Chaparral) and as historical-framing references (Saugus, Carnegie, the MIT Chipman lineage).

## Cases developed in this lecture

### Copperweld continuous caster breakout
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p2` (also referenced `§6.p8`)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Copperweld continuous caster breakout"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Hourly foreman vs. engineer dispute. Engineer overrides foreman's "but —" and forces a tap when no railroad car is in position. 200 tons of molten steel poured on the casting shop floor. Both lose jobs. Moral: respect the hourly employee's tacit knowledge.
- **Era:** circa 1970s.

### Chaparral Steel 3/8 inch rebar productivity case
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p5`–`§8.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "3/8 inch rebar rolling mill productivity case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Gordon Forward (MIT graduate, Canadian) at Chaparral. Mandatory week-per-year with the salesman discovers 20-30% premium on 3/8 inch rebar. First attempt: roll faster — instabilities, maimed workers. Second attempt: slow down and split the bar — one in, two out, then one in, four out. Took over the 3/8 inch market with quality gains as a bonus.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon steel rebar from 100% scrap; mini-mill electric arc furnace.
- **Era:** 1970s–1980s.

### Bethlehem Steel labor and worker injuries
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`, `§5.p1`, `§5.p6`, `§8.p3`, `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Tom Eagar's steel company experience"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's 1975 employment data point (45% labor, 45% materials, 10% profit, vs. 1980s 55% labor and 10% loss). Foreman pay comparison. Suicide-in-the-bath anecdote. Bethlehem PA hospital "for maimed steel workers." Bethlehem Steel near-bankruptcy in early 1960s. Burns Harbor as last US private integrated steel mill.
- **Era:** 1965 (Burns Harbor build), 1975 (Tom's employment), 1980s decline.

### Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`–`§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Last private integrated steel mill ever built in the US, 1965, cost Bethlehem $5 billion (their entire year's gross product). Used to make the "no private company can finance an integrated steel mill anymore" point. Setup for Clayton Christensen / mini-mill discussion.

### U.S. Steel's last open hearth furnace
- **Anchor:** `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Steel's last large open hearth furnace"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 450-ton open hearth installed in the 1970s when everyone else had moved to BOFs. Probably world's largest steel furnace ever made. Symbol of US Steel conservatism.

### Saugus Ironworks
- **Anchor:** `§3.p9` (foreshadow), `§4.p3`–`§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Established 1620s in response to English wood shortage. Failed within 10-20 years. Iron industry migrated to Chesapeake Bay (Northeast Forge 1735, principal forge 1724) for water power.

### Japan Manufacturing Study (Tom's 1984-85 sabbatical)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`–`§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1984-1985 Japan Manufacturing Study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Year on sabbatical with U.S. Office of Naval Research, visiting research labs and manufacturing facilities. Concluded Japanese productivity was not superior to US — competitiveness driven by exchange rate (250 yen/dollar). Anecdotes: $36,000 Tokyo rent, $15 Cheerios, C-5A Coca-Cola pallets, $2 hot dogs, embassy commissary access.

### MIT Commission on Industrial Productivity
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT Commission on Industrial Productivity - Steel Industry Study"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Started ~1985 in School of Engineering conference room; published 1989 as *Made in America*. Led by Dertouzos (computer science), Lester, and Solow (Nobel economist). Tom served on the steel-industry subcommittee.

### Continuous casting adoption (US vs. world)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`, also developed `§6.p7`–`§6.p8`, `§4.p7`, `§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Steel industry continuous casting adoption failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 65% yield (ingot) → 97% yield (continuous casting) was a transformative productivity gain. Foreign governments backed the risk; American boards of directors would not. Setup for the Airbus/Boeing subsidy parallel.

### Bismuth-tin solder steel contamination
- **Anchor:** `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bismuth-tin solder replacement causing steel scrap contamination"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Lead-free solder researchers proposing bismuth-tin alloy; Tom's objection at conferences that putting bismuth into automobiles would destroy steel recyclability (bismuth segregates to grain boundaries, embrittles). "Dead on arrival" — the solder metallurgists didn't know steel-making.

### Airbag explosive in steel mill shredding
- **Anchor:** `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Airbag explosives in steel mill shredding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief example of why you can't simply shred automobiles for scrap. Workers have been injured by airbag explosives going off during shredding.

### MIT Chipman / Elliott / King steel chemistry lineage
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`–`§6.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** *not yet in aggregate v2* — proposed as `PROPOSED: MIT Chipman steel chemistry lineage`

## Figures referenced

- **Steel productivity, 1975 → today:** Tom's 1975 Bethlehem-era figure (labor 45%, materials 45%, profit 10%); today ~20 minutes per person-ton. (§4.p2)
- **BOF vs. open hearth productivity ratio:** factor of 50 (30 minutes vs. one full day for a heat). (§5.p4)
- **Continuous casting yield:** 65% (ingot) → 97% (continuous). (§7.p1)
- **Scrap vs. cast iron cost:** $100/ton vs. $200/ton, against finished-steel price ~$400/ton. (§8.p1)
- **Integrated steel mill cost:** ~$15 billion today vs. $5 billion in 1965 for Burns Harbor. (§8.p3)
- **World steel and cement production:** 1 billion tons/year steel (80-90% recycled); 1.5 billion tons/year cement (essentially zero recycled). (§2.p5, §2.p7)
- **Aluminum recycling rate:** 50-60% (vs. steel's 80-90%). (§2.p5)

These are framing statistics Tom returns to across lectures; they are not cases but recurring numeric anchors.
