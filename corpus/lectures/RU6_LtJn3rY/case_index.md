### Canadian aluminum-silicon carbide composite casting development
- **Anchor:** `§1.p4`, `§1.p5`, `§1.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Canadian aluminum-silicon carbide composite casting development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Late 1970s / early 1980s Canadian company that developed a cast aluminum-SiC composite. Used by Tom as the prototype example of a composite that traded property gains in strength for losses in toughness, formability, joinability, corrosion resistance, and cost.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum matrix, silicon carbide particle reinforcement, casting process.
- **Era:** Late 1970s–mid 1980s.

### Fiberglass truck bed trial at coal mine
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Fiberglass truck bed trial at coal mine"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Joel Clark, working with Ford, supplied resin-molded composite truck beds to a coal mining operation for a six-month wear test alongside steel beds. - **Materials/systems:** Polymer resin-mold composites vs. steel truck beds.
- **Era:** 1990s.

### Longfellow Bridge guardrail perforation
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`, `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Longfellow Bridge guardrail perforation"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Hundred-year-old Longfellow Bridge handrails are rusted through, perforated, where snow (pH 5.5 frozen acid rain) accumulates and melts. Used to teach atmospheric corrosion rate (~4 mil/year) and the square-root-of-time scaling.

- **Era:** Bridge ~1900, current corrosion observation.

### Black iron pipe corrosion rate field observation
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Black iron pipe corrosion rate field observation"

### Steel-toe boot heat treating tank stress corrosion cracking / hydrogen embrittlement
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`, `§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Steel-toe boot heat treating tank stress corrosion cracking / hydrogen embrittlement"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Heat-treating shop near Boston airport built a large welded steel tank for sodium nitrate quench bath of steel-toe boot toes. Tank cracked at the welds. Literature said sodium nitrate causes stress corrosion cracking; Tom's U-bend test with anodic polarization wouldn't crack the specimen, but reversing the polarity (cathodic) cracked it within 24 hours. The real mechanism was hydrogen embrittlement, not SCC.
- **Materials/systems:** Welded high-strength low-alloy steel; sodium nitrate quench bath.
- **Era:** Tom's consulting case, era not specified in this lecture.

### USS Sea Wolf submarine hull cracking
- **Anchor:** `§5.p7`, `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Sea Wolf submarine hull cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Around 1992 at Groton (Electric Boat), the Navy moved from HY-80 to HY-100 for the Sea Wolf hull. A grinder noticed swarf lining up along sub-eighth-inch cracks in completed welds. Re-inspection found all welds (18% of hull complete) full of microscopic hydrogen cracks. Required scrapping the completed portion, delaying the program a year and costing ~$2 billion. Fix: preheat the steel to 400°F, with welders working in recirculated-liquid blue jelly suits inside heated submarine compartments.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-100 steel, welding hydrogen embrittlement.
- **Era:** ~1992.
