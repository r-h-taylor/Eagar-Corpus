## §2. WPS, PQR, and welder qualification [03:11]

`§2.p1` Does anybody have any questions on what we've been doing? We've been talking about post-weld heat treatment, and Mike had talked about PQRs and WPSs and such things, and I wanted to explain a little bit more in my simple-minded lay-person's terms.

`§2.p2` Codes and standards — whether you're talking aluminum or titanium or whatever — you have to have a WPS, which is a welding procedure specification. Mike gave you a handout on these things. You can think of this as the recipe for how to make the weld. Then there's the procedure qualification record, which is *bake the cake* in my terminology. And then there is *a cook*.

`§2.p3` So you have to have a welding procedure specification, which basically says: use this preheat, use this contact angle, if you're using this electrode you've got to do a post-weld heat treat — it's the recipe. The procedure qualification record means someone goes out there with a qualified welder and makes a weld on the material you're using, whether it's this particular aluminum alloy or whatever. You actually make the weld and you look at it visually, you x-ray it, you do magnetic particle, you break it open, do tensile tests — there's a whole set of things in the codes, and you test the weld. So it's a procedure that you're going to qualify, and this is the record to show it passed. The cake was properly done, it wasn't burnt, it wasn't undercooked, it didn't fall apart, it works.

`§2.p4` And then that welder is not going to weld everything on some huge project. You actually have to test a bunch of welders and see if they can reproduce it, and so we have a welder qualification. For decades, someone would say "I'm a certified welder," and I used to say there is no such thing — it's a *qualified* welder under the code. However, in the last 10 to 15 years there is such a thing as a certified welder.

`§2.p5` It used to be that every company — if you were building a bridge — you'd have to have your company specification for taking A36 steel, which is garden-variety steel plate, and you'd have to have your recipe that your company has. And then you'd have to test it, and this testing might cost $2,000 or $3,000. Then you'd have to qualify your welders. If that welder was working for American Bridge of US Steel and he went to work for Bethlehem Steel Erectors — Beth's out of business now — or if he went to work for Brown and Root or somebody else that does construction, he would have to be retested for that company. His qualification only belonged to the company he was employed by. He goes somewhere else and he's no longer qualified — well, of course he's still qualified, but on paper he's not.

`§2.p6` Until about 15 or 20 years ago, the American Welding Society decided — well, *first* I was going to say "there's money to be made here," but no, they decided they would simplify things, and you could be certified. Back in the old days, the recipe could cost you anywhere from $1,000 to $2,000 to $10,000 to have some welding engineer write up the recipe. The procedure qualification record might cost $5,000 to $10,000 to make the weld, do all the tests, and show that it's good — and this is just on a little test plate. And then the welder qualification, every single welder, would typically cost about $1,000 to have him make the weld and have it tested.

`§2.p7` Welder certification means that the American Welding Society will now administer the test, probably for a couple of thousand — I don't know exactly what it is, prices have gone up over the years. The welder will make the test at an approved testing facility, they'll submit the results to the American Welding Society, and the AWS will keep a record saying, yeah, this guy made a weld 10 years ago and it was a good weld.

`§2.p8` Now there are some recertifications. On some jobs — nuclear work or something — you may have to be requalified, retested every three years, or if you haven't welded for a whole year you have to be retested, because welding is actually a skill. It takes hand-eye coordination, and so it's like playing a video game: you reach a certain level and then you quit playing and you come back two years later and you're not as good as you used to be, because you've lost some of the skill. There are all kinds of rules about when you have to be recertified, but now you can be a certified welder. The qualification can be carried from company to company, so you no longer have this restriction that it's going to cost a company $1,000 every time they hire a welder to qualify them. Does that make sense?

## §6. Allotropic transformation and acicular ferrite in steel welds [25:52]

`§6.p1` Let me talk a little bit about — there's certain things about welds that make them, particularly steel welds and titanium welds. They actually transform. In steel, you go from BCC to FCC as you heat up your crystal structure, somewhere between 723 and 910 you change your crystal structure, and when you cool back down you change your crystal structure again. That helps you maintain very fine grain size. You're going from one crystal structure to another, you nucleate new grains, they take time to grow, and if you do this at the right cooling rates according to your welding procedure, you can get fine-grain, high-strength, high-toughness welds.

`§6.p2` But there's more to it than just that. It turns out the weld metal in steel — and the same thing occurs in titanium alloys, because you have hexagonal close-packed at low temperatures and BCC at higher temperatures — you have a crystal structure change which allows you to have fine grain size in your weld. So titanium and steel, because of this allotropic crystal change, can give you weld metals that have as much strength as the base metal. If you pull a tensile test, you'll find it'll fail in the base metal, even though the weld metal is sort of an as-cast structure.

`§6.p3` This is a picture of a submerged arc weld in steel, two passes. This is the interface between two of these passes. This big ferrite blob here is a transformed structure; it's in the heat-affected zone. You see the weld metal is generally finer structure. The reason for that, it turns out, is in steels when you weld you usually get some oxygen from the air or from the slag flux you're welding with. The base metal might have 20 or 30 parts per million oxygen; the weld could have 300 to 2,000 parts per million oxygen. If you look at that weld metal, it's just full of little bitty spherical oxide inclusions. You might say that's bad, except it turns out it's good, because it causes nucleation. When you have this transformation, as it's cooling down from FCC to BCC, those little oxide inclusions will nucleate what we call acicular ferrite.

`§6.p4` A regular steel grain cooling down, if you look at it in a microscope, will look like a bunch of grains of different sizes. These should be 120-degree angles at the intercepts. But the weld metal will be a bunch of needles, and at the center of each one of these needles, if you look at it in a transmission electron microscope, will be an oxide inclusion. I can't draw them all, but it's a fine grain structure with needles — that's what *acicular* means, needle-like — and these are high-angle grain boundaries. When the crack tries to go through there, it's blunted, it changes its direction, and it gives you very high toughness, very high strength for the composition of the weld metal.

## §7. Overmatching vs. undermatching weld metal [29:30]

`§7.p1` So even if I'm welding A36 carbon base metal with 0.3 carbon, I might use a 0.15 carbon weld metal, because I have this finer structure. I don't need as much carbon to give me strength, and I can match the strength of the base metal to the weld metal, and get slightly overmatching strength. I want slightly higher strength in the weld metal than the base metal. You mechanical engineers, or you materials engineers, do you know why I would rather have a structure where the weld is slightly stronger than the base material?

**Student:** *[response about stress concentrations]*

`§7.p2` You're welding at stress concentrations, and because you're at a stress concentration — a corner or something — if the thing gets loaded in any way, whether bending or tension, if it's undermatching you're going to pull the weld metal and it's going to yield before the base material. That means all the strain in your structure is concentrated right on that weld. Even though your structure may only undergo 1% strain, if the weld metal is only 5% of your overall structure, that 1% gets multiplied by a factor of 20, and all of a sudden I've got 20% strain in that weld, and the weld is over-strained. So you can't think of it in terms of stress as much as you should think of it in terms of localized strains.

`§7.p3` The Navy and other people have spent millions and millions of dollars over the years trying to see if they could use undermatching weld metal, because if you could use a lower-strength weld metal you can tolerate a lot more hydrogen. You could save millions of dollars on your preheating cost. But every time they've done it, they've had to weld up whole structures and shoot at them with ballistics if you're the Navy, put them in great big testing machines at Lehigh University, or out in Boulder, Colorado at the National Institute of Standards and Technology, where they have multi-million-pound test machines — things you can put a huge I-beam in and just tear it in two. You can take a whole structure panel from a bridge and bend it and tear it apart.

`§7.p4` With undermatching weld metal, you concentrate the strain at the weld. With overmatching weld metal, you force the base metal to strain, and that's 90, 95, 98% of your structure, and so now your structure behaves like you would like it to. A 1% strain can be absorbed by the structure as a whole. But if the weld metal is undermatching, a 1% strain gets concentrated in the weld. Now you start seeing 20, 25, 30% strain and the thing fractures. It doesn't really matter whether it's slow tensile failure, slow bending, or impact fracture. In impact fracture, the deforming volume also decreases if the weld's weaker, and it will absorb less energy in an impact test. So whether it's dynamic fracture or slow fracture, undermatching is not good.

`§7.p5` Fortunately, I haven't seen another millions-of-dollars worth of testing for about 25 years, but before that, about every 15 years, someone would go out and spend millions of dollars trying to find out if they could use undermatching filler metal. The advantage in steels is, you get this acicular ferrite that will give you strengths up to 120 ksi, which means you can weld steels with base metal of 100 ksi, and you'll be overmatching. You'll have a 20% overmatch. You'd like to only have 10% overmatch, but usually you shoot for 10 to 20% overmatch.

## §8. Martensite, the Seawolf, and the limits of overmatching [34:04]

`§8.p1` If you go above that, you're going to get martensite. You're going to have a weld metal composition that will give you martensite, and you can go up to 180 ksi without too much difficulty, but you're going to have to do post-weld heat treatment — and that might be 160 ksi with post-weld heat treatment to temper the martensite.

`§8.p2` You're not Navy folks, but this summer when the Navy guys have to watch some of these lectures — that's what happened to the Seawolf submarine. The Seawolf was supposed to be welding HY-100, but they had a weld metal that was a little too rich in chemistry. They said high-side chemistry — the carbon was at the maximum of the range, the manganese was the maximum of the range, the chromium, everything was at the maximum of the range, and they were getting 130 ksi weld metal. It was more of a martensitic structure rather than acicular ferrite, and all of a sudden they ran into all kinds of problems with hydrogen cracking. Cost an additional $2 billion for one submarine.
