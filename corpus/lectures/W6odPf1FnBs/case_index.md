## Cases referenced

### Neighbor's water main leak and Boston Gas pipe discovery
- **Anchor:** `§1.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Neighbor's water main leak and Boston Gas pipe discovery"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Boston Gas crew arrives with chewing gum to plug the leak the moment the backhoe uncovers the pipe — illustration that buried steel gas pipe is universally leaking after sixty years, the leaks plugged passively by moist soil.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon steel gas pipe, residential gas distribution.
- **Era:** anecdote ~1993; pipe installed ~1933.

### Speaker's home gas service sleeving (three-quarter inch pipe)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Speaker's home gas service sleeving (three-quarter inch pipe)"

### Gas pipe corrosion and home explosions
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`, `§1.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Gas pipe corrosion and home explosions"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Framing case for why the polyethylene pipe was developed — sixty-year-old steel pipe corroding through, century-old cast iron pipe failing.
- **Materials/systems:** cast iron, carbon steel, polyethylene gas distribution piping.

### Cape Cod sailboat anodized aluminum corrosion failure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Cape Cod sailboat anodized aluminum corrosion failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first Cape Cod consulting visit. A 40-foot sailboat owner had aluminum rails pitting because the anodizer failed to seal the porous coating. Illustration of the trade-off between anodizing-for-bonding (leave porous) and anodizing-for-corrosion-resistance (seal in boiling water).
- **Materials/systems:** anodized aluminum, marine environment.
- **Era:** early career — likely 1970s.

### Cold bonding of integrated circuits and transistors
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Cold bonding of integrated circuits and transistors"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Indium bead used to cold-bond the metal can to the silicon-chip base on early discrete transistors. Illustration of why oxide-to-metal hardness ratio matters in practice.
- **Materials/systems:** indium, vacuum-tube-era transistor packaging.
- **Era:** 1950s–1960s historical.

### Brewery filling line consulting project
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`

### Welch's grape juice packaging versus beer

## Figures referenced (recurring numeric anchors, not cases)

### Tylecote 1954 oxide-to-metal hardness data
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`, `§2.p2`
- Tylecote, *British Welding Journal*, 1954. Plot of bond strength versus deformation for indium, aluminum, tin, iron, copper. Indium achieves bond at 10% deformation; iron and copper require much higher.

### Stefan equation force-time product
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`
- F·t = 3μV / (1/H_f⁴ − 1/H₀⁴) — Bikerman's derivation; force-time scales with one over thickness to the fourth power. Joint strength scales as 1/H².

### Bikerman joint-formation time table
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`
- Worked examples: 14 PSI / 1 cm radius / 10 mil → 75 sec; etc. From Bikerman, *The Science of Adhesive Joints* (1961).

### Skeist critical surface tension table
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`
- PVC 40, PVDC 39, polyethylene 31, PVF 28, PVDF 25, PTFE/Teflon 18 (dynes/cm). From *Handbook of Adhesives*, 2nd ed., chapter authored by a Naval Research Lab chemist.

### Egon Orowan Finnish coastline viscosity estimate
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`
- 10²² poise — Orowan's rhetorical upper bound on viscosity. Recurring framing figure in Tom's lectures.
