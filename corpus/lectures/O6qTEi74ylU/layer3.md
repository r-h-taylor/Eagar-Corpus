## §1. Heat-affected zone liquation cracking [00:03]

`§1.p1` A problem in nickel-based alloys is melting along the grain boundaries in the heat-affected zone, which then, because of the restraint that's inherent in welding — the residual stresses — those sometimes pull apart along those melted grain boundaries.

`§1.p2` There are two ways the grain boundaries can get liquated or melted. One is a segregation mechanism: there are solute or impurity elements that segregate to the grain boundaries. Because of the heat of welding, the diffusion is higher, and some of these elements can diffuse to the grain boundaries, and what happens is they suppress the local melting temperature along the grain boundaries.

`§1.p3` Another mechanism for the grain boundaries in the heat-affected zone to liquate is that there might be a particle, or just a region of the microstructure that has a lower liquidus temperature, and that might get melted from the heat of welding. You've got grain boundaries that are moving around, and so sometimes you've got a particle that melts, and then you've got a grain boundary that sweeps along, and as that grain boundary sweeps into that melted particle, the liquid can run along the boundary. That's actually called constitutional liquation.

`§1.p4` The gamma double prime alloys, which are strengthened by niobium carbides, can have that form of heat-affected zone liquation. So what I just tried to explain in words, I'll show you a picture of and maybe it can be a little more clear.

## §3. Preventing heat-affected zone liquation [04:28]

`§3.p1` Here are a few things we can think about to try to prevent this liquation in the heat-affected zone. One is to minimize the grain size. What that does is it increases the grain boundary area and spreads the liquid around. Instead of having a few grain boundaries that are really wet by the low-melting constituents, you've got a bunch of grain boundaries that might just have a little bit on them. The problem is that sometimes in these alloys you want large grain size to control creep, and so you've got to balance that.

`§3.p2` You can also make sure you've got low impurities, which would lead to those liquated films. And another thing that you have to balance with your creep properties is that a lot of times they use boron additions to improve creep properties — but those very boron additions might increase your grain boundary liquid [liquation] and increase your susceptibility to this type of heat-affected zone cracking.

`§3.p3` As with all of these, we want to minimize restraint as much as possible. We can do that with joint design; we can do that by welding in the solution-annealed condition. Another thing that would be quite expensive but could be done is to butter the substrates. If you're welding on an alloy that's highly susceptible to heat-affected zone liquation cracking, you might butter or weld over the surface — almost clad the surface — with an alloy that is less susceptible, and do that under controlled circumstances. That would be one method for preventing these cracks as well.

## §4. Strain-age cracking: mechanism [06:39]

`§4.p1` The last weldability issue with nickel-based alloys that I wanted to talk about is strain-age cracking. This is a phenomenon that only happens in the superalloys, or the precipitation-strengthened alloys. It happens because of the rapid precipitation of the strengthening precipitates — the gamma prime precipitate especially. It's similar to reheat cracking in steels.

`§4.p2` The mechanism that has been put forth for this is that these precipitates of nickel, aluminum, and titanium, as you're welding, dissolve because of the heat. That leads to a softening of the weld metal and the heat-affected zone. Then after you've made your weld and created that softened region around the weld, you want to post-weld heat treat to restore the properties. And you get intragranular — meaning within the grains — precipitation of that gamma prime strengthening precipitate.

`§4.p3` When that happens, you end up with strain at the grain boundaries because of the strengthening within the grains. Because you're strengthening the inside of the grains due to the precipitation, your grain boundaries are often weakened, and you end up with cracking along the grains of the heat-affected zone. That's called strain-age cracking.

`§4.p4` **Student:** [Is that the same as sensitization?]

`§4.p5` That's a different phenomenon. The cracks are both intergranular, yes. But with sensitization — that's a stainless steel phenomenon where you're actually forming carbides along the grain boundaries, and you're reducing the corrosion resistance of the boundary because you're pulling the chromium into those carbides. Here you're actually strengthening the inside of the grain, which then allows cracking along the grain boundaries.

## §5. Preventing strain-age cracking [09:41]

`§5.p1` A few ways to prevent strain-age cracking. You can select a resistant material. Alloy 718 was developed largely for this reason. It's strengthened by the gamma double prime strengthening precipitate rather than the gamma prime, so it's got lower titanium and aluminum. Research has shown that by lowering titanium and aluminum in these alloys, you can form alloys that are resistant to strain-age cracking. Alloy 718 falls down here — this is kind of the boundary between resistant and susceptible. By keeping your aluminum and titanium low, thereby not forming the gamma prime but forming the gamma double prime or some other strengthening precipitate, you can reduce the susceptibility to strain-age cracking.

## §6. Design and procedure to reduce weldability cracking [13:42]

`§6.p1` Much the same as with the other things we've talked about, you can always reduce susceptibility to these various weldability issues with your design and your welding procedures. You can design to reduce residual stresses. You can use the stringer bead technique I talked about, making a series of smaller welds rather than a large weld. You can use processes that put a low heat input into the material. And you can try to minimize the PMZ — the partially melted zone — which is that region in the heat-affected zone that's melted along the grain boundaries.

`§6.p2` As you're designing your welded structure, you can put your welds in locations that will not create as much stress, or be in as stressed a state. As you do those things, you can reduce the cracking phenomena.

`§6.p3` The main thing to remember in all of those different cracking problems is — I'm sure you could draw a Venn diagram for that too — whenever you've got restraint pulling on these, because of the nature of the way these solidify and the way they melt, you're going to have regions of lower melting or even liquid. If you can minimize the pulling apart on that, then you'll be able to minimize the cracking that takes place.
