# Case Index — Lecture 17, Welding Metallurgy Summer 2015

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§2.p3`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is structured around the heavy-section titanium welding problem (Sea Cliff, Alvin, Soviet Alpha submarine). It surfaces numerous cases as Tom develops the technical narrative; the densest case-work is in §2 (porosity), §4 (submersibles), §5–§7 (Soviet welding technology), and §8 (armor).

## Primary cases

### Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding failure
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`–`§2.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Engelhard NJ contractor cannot produce porosity-free titanium ductwork welds for the catalytic converter. Tom diagnoses oil residue from commercial-grade acetone; reagent-grade acetone solves it. Reagent-grade compliance lapses; problem recurs; cleaning discipline solves it again.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium ductwork; gas tungsten arc welding; reagent-grade acetone cleaning
- **Era:** 747-400 development era (late 1980s)

### General Electric titanium compressor case electron beam welding porosity
- **Anchor:** `§2.p8`–`§2.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric titanium compressor case electron beam welding porosity"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Parallel case at GE Lynn — $70,000 titanium compressor parts, electron beam welding, same hydrogen-porosity-from-residual-oil problem. Tom's free half-morning visit exposes the technician using non-reagent acetone; ambient dust contamination demonstration. Same fix.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium compressor parts; electron beam welding
- **Era:** late 1970s–1980s

### Watertown Arsenal titanium development
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`–`§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Watertown Arsenal titanium development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Local geographic anchor for Ti-6Al-4V development. Used to set up the BRAC closing story (50 million decontamination cost; DoD says "so what"; site becomes Watertown Mall) and Tom's later failure-analysis consulting role there.
- **Materials/systems:** Ti-6Al-4V; research reactor
- **Era:** post-WWII; BRAC ~2000

### Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`–`§4.p3`, `§4.p6`–`§4.p7`, `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alvin and Sea Cliff deep-ocean research submersible development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Material evolution from HY-80/HY-130 steel (original Alvin, 1960s) to Ti-6Al-4V (new Alvin, early 1970s, 40-year service life). Sea Cliff (~1980) is the larger 8-foot prototype using heavier-section titanium. Heavy-section welding (2 to 2.25 inches) is the central technical problem of the lecture.
- **Materials/systems:** Ti-6Al-4V; Ti-100 (Ti-6Al-2Nb-1Ta); hot wire TIG; gas metal arc
- **Era:** 1960s–present

### Draper Autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) prototype welding
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`–`§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Draper Autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) prototype welding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Two hot-dog-shaped autonomous subs, quarter-inch titanium, ~4 feet diameter, ~50 feet long. Built at Pratt & Whitney type aerospace facilities under DARPA rapid-prototyping push after first Gulf War. Tom consulted at $50/hour on DoD rates.
- **Materials/systems:** quarter-inch titanium; aerospace fabrication
- **Era:** early-to-mid 1990s

### Soviet titanium submarine (Alpha-class) creep-fatigue failures
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§5.p5`, `§7.p4`–`§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet titanium submarine (Alpha-class) creep-fatigue failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Soviets built the Alpha subs using heavy-section titanium that the US could not match. Gurevich at Paton Welding Institute did the research from the 1960s, stopped publishing in 1972 when the program went black. Tom estimates $100M of research funding. Soviets didn't crack creep-fatigue interaction either, NRL eventually did.
- **Materials/systems:** heavy-section titanium; multiple welding processes
- **Era:** 1960s–1980s

### 1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`–`§5.p5`, `§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1980 technical exchange with Soviet welding scientists"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Carter-era exchange program; first delegation of 30 American scientists, then 8, then 2. Tom is one of the final 2, accompanying Julius Szekely. Two-hour interview with Gurevich at Paton Institute in Kiev. KGB surveillance.
- **Era:** 1980 (during Reagan transition / Carter program sunset)

### Soviet titanium electroslag welding technology
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`–`§6.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet titanium electroslag welding technology"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom realizes mid-conference at David Taylor Annapolis that the Soviets used electroslag (not submerged arc) for heavy-section titanium. First free-world titanium electroslag weld made next door to Tom's lab. US Navy later acquires foreign technology samples (shipyard scrap), confirming electroslag welds in Soviet construction.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium 2-inch plate; electroslag welding; calcium chloride flux
- **Era:** late 1970s–early 1980s

### Foreign technology acquisition at David Taylor Naval Shipyard
- **Anchor:** `§6.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Foreign technology acquisition at David Taylor Naval Shipyard"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom hints — without disclosing — at how the Navy obtained Soviet titanium weld samples for analysis. "Someone basically robbed a scrapyard, a shipyard." 30-35 years before the lecture (~1980).
- **Era:** ~1980

### Deep TIG (Deep Gas Tungsten Arc) development for thick-section horizontal welding
- **Anchor:** `§6.p10`–`§6.p13`, `§7.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Deep TIG (Deep Gas Tungsten Arc) development for thick-section horizontal welding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Gurevich's semi-submerged arc / deep-TIG technique: half-millimeter painted flux layer on top surface enables deep penetration via surface-tension-driven convection (Marangoni). Ken Mills at National Physical Lab hypothesized mechanism for stainless. Tom and Szekely modeled it in 1983, award-winning paper.
- **Materials/systems:** titanium plate; painted flux layer; gas tungsten arc
- **Era:** 1970s–1980s

### Naval Air Rework Facility titanium engine case repair
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval Air Rework Facility titanium engine case repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Norfolk VA 1968, glove-bag titanium disc repair using gas tungsten arc.
- **Era:** 1968

### Naval Research Laboratory fatigue-creep interaction cracking
- **Anchor:** `§7.p6`–`§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval Research Laboratory fatigue-creep interaction cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** NRL eventually solved the creep-fatigue interaction problem in heavy-section titanium that the Soviets had hit but not understood. Demonstrates that fundamental science can leapfrog empirical practice.
- **Era:** 1980s

### Aberdeen Proving Ground kinetic energy penetrator armor defeat (1984-1985)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`–`§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aberdeen Proving Ground kinetic energy penetrator armor defeat (1984-1985)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 36-inch depleted-uranium penetrator shot through three lined-up tanks (six layers of armor). General "tossed his cookies." Within two years, armor caught up and no munition was undefeatable.
- **Materials/systems:** depleted uranium penetrator; tank armor
- **Era:** 1984-1985

### MRAP vehicle armor selection and development
- **Anchor:** `§8.p11`–`§8.p17`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MRAP vehicle armor selection and development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Secretary of Army Aberdeen rush program post-Iraq invasion. Tested titanium, steel, aluminum, glass. Glass worked best. Six pieces three-quarter-inch glass on MRAP undersides. Why-it-works classified; that-it-is-glass visible to any soldier.
- **Materials/systems:** layered glass armor
- **Era:** mid-2000s

### Iraq War Humvee IED vulnerability and MRAP armor development
- **Anchor:** `§8.p12`–`§8.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Iraq War Humvee IED vulnerability and MRAP armor development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Secretary of Army mission-driven response to IED casualties. Friday 4pm conference calls with commanders. Tom uses this as exemplar of mission-driven lab effectiveness vs. budget-driven trashcan-lab failure mode (Watertown Arsenal counterexample).
- **Era:** post-2003
