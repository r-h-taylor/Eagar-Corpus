## §1. Introduction and what stainless steel is [00:04]

`§1.p1` So just by way of introduction, my name is Mike Balmforth. I was a student of Professor Eagar a few years ago. Before that I did some work at Ohio State, where I did a fair amount of research on the welding metallurgy of stainless steels. Tom has asked me if I would come and talk to you about stainless steels and the welding metallurgy of those today. Currently I work with Dr. Eagar in a consulting group, and so it's nice to be able to work with him on a professional level and also to have the opportunity to come and talk to you guys a little bit about stainless steels today.

`§1.p2` I wanted to start off by just talking a little bit about what is a stainless steel. Most of you probably, when you think of stainless steel, you think of this nice shiny silver metal that doesn't rust. In most cases that's true. But stainless steel is not always stainless. There are things like sensitization, things like pitting, things like intergranular corrosion. If you mix up your stainless steel with your carbon steel and you get contamination, you can get rusting that way. So as we talk about the metallurgy of stainless steel, we're going to be talking about things that could happen to that stainless steel to cause it to not behave like you want it to. It could be corrosion, could be rusting, it could also be cracking. There's a variety of things that can go wrong, and so we'll talk about some of those things.

`§1.p3` Stainless steel at its very basic sense is an iron-chromium alloy. Many of the alloys also have nickel in them. There's always going to be a little bit of carbon and a variety of other elements that we will talk about. But the main thing that makes stainless steel corrosion resistant, or makes it stainless, is the chromium content. That's because chromium forms a very tenacious passive oxide that protects the underlying steel. It's a Cr2O3 oxide. Whenever that oxide is there, and nothing is wrong with that oxide, then your steel's going to be stainless. But if it gets a scratch in it, or if it gets a corrosion pit in it, or a variety of things like that, then that's when we can run into problems.

`§1.p4` Stainless generally is very resistant to chemical discoloration, and that's why it works great for your silverware and nice shiny instruments and stuff like that. It's also generally very corrosion resistant, and it's got in most cases good high temperature oxidation resistance. There are a wide range of stainless steel types and alloys, so there's a wide range of strengths and ductilities. It generally has pretty good fabricability — the ability to be formed and welded and so on.

## §2. Classifications and microstructure [04:09]

`§2.p1` Next thing to talk about a little bit is classifications of stainless steels. Stainless steels are classified by their microstructure. I hope that most of you have a basic understanding that microstructure is the structure when you look at a polished specimen of steel under a microscope. You can see a variety of microstructures and phases. The way that we identify stainless steels is by that microstructure. There are five different classifications that we'll mention today, and if we have time we'll talk about three of them. I don't think we'll get to duplex and the PH stainless steels.

`§2.p2` Martensitic stainless steel is just like it says — it's a steel that is mostly made up of a martensitic microstructure. Same with the ferritic, mostly a ferrite microstructure. The austenitics, which are kind of the workhorse stainless steels — they're more used than the others — are a mostly austenitic microstructure, although when we get to talking about them you'll see that we do like to have a little bit of ferrite in the structure. A duplex stainless steel is a stainless steel that is generally typically about 50% ferrite and 50% austenite. And then the precipitation hardenable stainless steels are steels that are hardened by the strengthening precipitates that can form during heat treatment.

`§2.p3` A lot of people might tell you that you can tell if something is stainless or not by putting a magnet on it. That's true for the austenitic alloys, because austenite, being a face-centered cubic structure, is not magnetic. But if you put a magnet on a martensitic or a ferritic stainless steel, it will actually stick, and so that's not always the way to tell.

## §3. Alloying elements and the Schaeffler diagram [06:40]

`§3.p1` I wanted to talk a little bit about some of the different alloying elements that we end up putting in stainless steels. They're broken down into two categories. One is the austenite formers, which promote the formation of austenite. The other is the ferrite formers, which promote the formation of ferrite. Austenite formers actually end up being martensite, because when the austenite cools down it can transform to martensite.

`§3.p2` Like I said earlier, the main alloying elements you'll see are chromium, which is a ferrite former, and nickel, which is an austenite former. There's various other things that are attributed to each of these alloys and reasons that we put them in there. I don't think I'm going to take the time to go through each one of those individually, just because I've got a lot of information that I want to stress to you today. If you're interested in that, it's easy to look that up in a reference.

`§3.p3` These various alloying elements that are put in the batch when you mix up a stainless steel lead to a steel with a certain structure, which then leads to certain properties, and you can use it for certain applications. So it's very important to know what kind of a structure you're going to get and how to best use that.

`§3.p4` This diagram was developed a long time ago by a guy named Schaeffler. What it does is it predicts the microstructure of a given stainless steel. If you look at the axes, you've got a chromium equivalent along the x-axis and a nickel equivalent along the y-axis. What they've done is they've taken the various alloying elements that you might find in your stainless steel and they've made them equivalent to either chromium or nickel. In doing the research to develop this type of diagram, you have to determine for example on the nickel equivalent that carbon is about thirty times more potent in forming austenite than the nickel is, whereas manganese is about half as potent.

`§3.p5` You can take the composition of the steel that you're going to be welding on, you can plot it on these axes here, and you'll find that your alloys are going to fall in various regions depending on your composition. Up here you'll have the austenitic alloys. Like I mentioned earlier, these lines here are called iso-ferrite lines. This region over here is completely austenite, or 100% austenitic structure. But these lines here tell you that you've got maybe 0 or 5% or 10% ferrite in that structure. It's actually a good thing when you weld on an austenitic stainless steel that you get some ferrite in the material — it prevents cracking.

`§3.p6` Down here is where the ferritic alloys fall. The martensitic alloys fall over here. And you actually see that even in the martensitic alloys sometimes you get a little bit of ferrite. That's actually where most of my research was, in this region down here. I developed one of these type of diagrams for this martensitic to ferritic region. The duplex alloys, like I said earlier, are in here, where you've got both ferrite and austenite. This and the diagrams that have been developed subsequent to it, which are a little more accurate, are very useful when you're welding on a stainless steel to be able to predict the kinds of structures that you're going to get and how those can be used.

## §5. Austenitic stainless steels — composition and grades [14:23]

## §6. Austenitic applications, environments, and welding practice [20:28]

`§6.p1` Some typical applications for the austenitic alloys — they're really used in a wide range of applications. They can be used structurally, for containment, for architectural or kitchen equipment, medical products. One of the reasons that they're widely used is because they are fairly corrosion resistant, but also they're readily formable and fabricable. They're probably the easiest to weld on as far as the stainless steels.

`§6.p2` One thing that is good to mention is that the austenitic stainless steels are not suitable for chloride-containing environments, or highly caustic environments, because those are the environments that do lead to stress corrosion cracking and intergranular corrosion and other types of corrosion with austenitic stainless steels.

`§6.p3` **Student:** [Question about chloride environments.]

Yeah, anything with chloride in it. It's really a problem where there's some kind of stress, but when you've got welding you've always got some residual stress. So we often find when something has cracked or there's been a problem, it's called stress corrosion cracking. Professor Eagar, if he hasn't talked to you about that, will be talking to you about that in more detail. If you've got stainless steel pipes under your sink and that's where you store your bleach, sometimes it can be a problem.

`§6.p4` Some things to consider when you weld austenitic stainless steels — because they're not hardenable, there's no need to preheat them. It is important, because of the phases that can form, that you do watch the interpass temperature if you're doing multiple welding passes on the austenitic stainless steels.

`§6.p5` Again, because they're not hardenable, there's not really a need to post-weld heat treat the austenitic stainless steels either. You can stress relieve if necessary, but you need to consider the carbides that could form, and you need to consider sigma and other phases, which are brittle phases that can form in these alloys.

`§6.p6` Filler metals that are typically used in welding of austenitic stainless steels are either matching, or 308 and 309 are very common for welding on 304 for example. That's because of the solidification microstructure that you end up with, which has some ferrite in it — which is what we're going to talk about in a minute.

`§6.p7` It's very important with stainless steels that you weld in an inert environment, because at high temperatures the chromium oxides form very rapidly, and you get what's called sugaring. You get this black oxide all over everything. That can be done in a variety of ways. It can be done with shielding gas, where you blow gas through the welding torch. A lot of times you have to set up some kind of a backing gas system as well, where you're blowing gas on the backside of it. I used to work for a national lab where we welded stainless steel parts in glove boxes, because we wanted to be very careful that there was no outside contamination at all.

## §7. Solidification cracking in austenitic welds [24:55]

`§7.p1` Next I wanted to talk about some of the issues that can arise when you're welding on stainless steels. Probably the biggest issue that you need to be aware of when you're welding on austenitic stainless steels is solidification cracking — basically cracks that form in the material after you've welded on it, as it's resolidifying.

`§7.p2` What you find is that the fully austenitic alloys are more susceptible. Also impurity levels like sulfur and phosphorus — because when a material solidifies, those impurity elements, as the material grows together, get transferred to the boundaries that are created as the material grows together. They end up creating a low melting phase, or a eutectic phase, which is much more susceptible to being pulled apart as the shrinkage stresses are created during solidification.

`§7.p3` Some of the things that you can do — you can try to minimize your restraint, you can watch your heat input, you can also use the right filler material, which we talked about. 308 and 309 are typically used.

`§7.p4` This is a diagram that demonstrates solidification cracking susceptibility of austenitic alloys. This is total crack length, which basically is a measure of how much cracking you get. You add up all of the cracks and that's your total crack length. This is a chromium-nickel equivalent relationship — you would take chromium and nickel equivalents like those I showed you on the Schaeffler diagram, calculate them, and then divide the nickel equivalent into the chromium equivalent. You'll see that when you get a value that's below about 1.5, you get much more cracking than when you're on the other side of that value of 1.5. In fact, you get a small region in here where you get very low crack susceptibility.

`§7.p5` What this region up here is telling you is that this solidified as austenite. Gamma is the symbol that represents austenite. Here is austenite plus ferrite, or delta. And then this is completely delta, completely ferrite, over here. So in order to avoid cracking in your stainless steel welds, you really want to solidify right in this region right here, which is austenite with just a little bit of ferrite.

## §8. Solidification modes and the WRC 1992 diagram [28:27]

`§8.p1` Solidification behavior of stainless steels is actually something that you could teach an entire course on. I'm going to try to go through it quickly enough, but in enough detail that I can give you some understanding.

`§8.p2` When an alloy solidifies, you can use those phase diagrams that I showed you before. You can either solidify as austenite from the liquid, and then you can have some transformations in the solid state, or you can solidify as ferrite, and then as it cools you can have further transformations in the solid state. There's something called austenite solidification, or A-type solidification. You can get fully austenitic if you're far enough over on the phase diagram. If you're a little bit closer to the boundary, you'll still end up with a little bit of ferrite, and that's called AF solidification.

`§8.p3` Or you can solidify as ferrite, which will then transform partially back to austenite as it cools. That's called FA solidification. These are different types of ferrite that will be formed. Down here you get something called Widmanstätten austenite in a ferrite matrix. That's where it pretty much solidifies all the way as ferrite, and then at the very bottom, sometimes along the grain boundaries, you get a little bit of austenite that reforms. That's called F-type solidification.

`§8.p4` This is graphically demonstrating what I was just talking about. Over here is the austenite solidification, and the furthest one on the left is A-type solidification, where as you're solidifying from bottom to top in this picture, basically everything is transforming to austenite. The white is austenite, and you get a completely austenitic structure as it solidifies.

`§8.p5` If you're a little bit further over on the phase diagram, as you solidify austenite, along the boundaries of those solidification dendrites — that's what these are called — you do get a little bit of formation of ferrite. That's called the AF solidification mode. These two here are the FA-type solidification modes, where you're actually solidifying as ferrite, but then as it cools, you're getting a transformation back to austenite. What it leaves is this little lacy or skeletal structure of ferrite, basically at the cores of those dendrites.

`§8.p6` Something that you don't see very often in stainless steels actually is complete ferrite solidification, or F-type solidification mode.

`§8.p7` This is just to show you kind of how you get these phase diagrams that I was talking about earlier. If you had an iron-chrome-nickel phase diagram in three dimensions, this is kind of a two-dimensional representation of what that would look like. What you can do is take a slice of that, and that's what's shown here. If you slice this off basically at 70% iron, which is a typical composition of austenitic stainless steels, then you can look at this surface here, which is exposed by making this slice. Then you've got what we call a pseudo-binary phase diagram. Here's where you see that over here you get solidification completely as austenite, whereas over here you get solidification completely as ferrite. Right in this middle region is where you get sometimes austenite and ferrite together. That's where most of our stainless steels actually fall, in that region there.

`§8.p8` Something that was developed much later than the Schaeffler diagram is this, developed by the Welding Research Council — it's called the WRC 1992 diagram. They modified the chromium and nickel equivalents. Basically this is the region between austenite and ferrite, so it's just a small section of that Schaeffler diagram. They also drew on here the solidification modes. Above this line here you get completely austenitic solidification. You can then see the AF and the FA and the F-type solidification as well on this diagram.

`§8.p9` What they've done over here with these iso-ferrite lines is — well, they've changed to something called a ferrite number, which instead of being a percentage of austenite and ferrite is actually a magnetic reading. There are various instruments that can be used to determine the ferrite number. One is called a magnet gauge. I think Professor Eagar has one of those in his lab. Basically you stick a magnet on there and then you measure the force that's required to pull it. The higher the force, the more of the magnetic ferrite that's actually in the material.

`§8.p10` In order to avoid cracking, like I said earlier, you want to really be in this FA-type solidification range. As you figure out the chromium and nickel equivalents of the material that you're welding on — and you can also figure those out for the filler material that you're using — you can figure out where you're going to end up on this, and whether you're going to be susceptible to cracking or not.

## §9. Intergranular corrosion and sensitization [35:55]

`§9.p1` There are other issues with welding of austenitic stainless steels. One of them I wanted to talk about is intergranular corrosion, which occurs due to something called sensitization. I think the easiest way to demonstrate that is with this picture here.

`§9.p2` When you weld on one of these types of stainless steels, you've got boundaries between the grains in your microstructure. If you get this intergranular corrosion, you get all these little corrosion ditches along the boundaries of the material. You can actually have complete grains that will just fall out of the material. The reason that happens is when you heat the material due to welding or some other reason, along the grain boundaries you've actually got carbide precipitation that happens between the chromium and the carbon that's in the material. When that happens, it leaves a chromium-depleted region along the grain boundaries.
