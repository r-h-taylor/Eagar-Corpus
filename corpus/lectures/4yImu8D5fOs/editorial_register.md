## Note on speaker

This is a guest lecture. The voice throughout is Mike Balmforth's, not Tom Eagar's. The conventions for cutting filler, smoothing self-corrections, and handling student questions apply identically; the bracketed-slip convention (e.g. "Ford [Forward]") is reserved for Tom's recurring slips and does not arise here.

## Sectioning

The lecture divides naturally into nine teaching units, marked by topic pivots Balmforth signals himself ("next thing to talk about", "next I wanted to talk about", "something that was developed much later"). The pivots from §6 to §7 (applications/practice → solidification cracking) and from §8 to §9 (solidification modes → intergranular corrosion) are the cleanest. The seam between §3 (Schaeffler diagram, alloying) and §4 (iron-chromium phase diagram) is a shift from the engineering chart to the underlying thermodynamic diagram and is also Balmforth-signaled. Section §5 ends after the second student-question pair and the centerpiece diagram; §6 then turns from grades to applications and welding practice.

The lecture as captured runs ~37 minutes (Layer 2 ends at the start of the sensitization mechanism). Nine sections fall within the 4–8 sections-per-lecture norm slightly on the high side, but Balmforth moves through many distinct teaching units and each section is 2–5 minutes, which matches the convention.

## Conventions applied

- Filler thinning: "um", "uh", "you know", "I mean", "basically" cut except where load-bearing. "Like I said" preserved where it anchors back to a real earlier statement in the same section; cut where pure filler.
- Self-corrections smoothed: "discolorization, discoloration" → "discoloration"; "equivalated — equivalent — what's the right word — made them equivalent" → "made them equivalent"; "or you can solidify as ferrite, and then as it cools you can have further uh transformations in the solid state. And so uh there's something called…" preserved with light smoothing.
- Student questions: the two inaudible student questions in §5 (p20, p21) and the one in §6 (p26) are rendered as `**Student:** [Question about X.]` followed by Balmforth's response. The inaudible content is described rather than reconstructed, since the audio is not recoverable.
- "Inner pass" → "interpass" (silent captioner correction in §6.p4: "watch the inner pass temperature" is the standard welding term *interpass temperature*).
- Captioner uncertainty preserved: "Professor [Roher?]" in §4.p1 — the name is unclear from the L2 transcript and is flagged with the bracket-question convention rather than silently guessed.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot). This applies equally to guest lecturers in the corpus: Balmforth is alive but is not being ratified for this edition either.
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
