## §1. Engineers, scientists, and the spectrum between them [00:00]

`§1.p1` I have lots of toys to pass around, so you guys need to come up here — it's not going to work with everybody scattered. Close your laptop, turn your cell phone off. Please come up here. Look, I got toys. Lots of toys.

`§1.p2` Now, with these toys, think about an engineer and a scientist. I don't get in that debate, because while those people are debating it, I'm out creating new stuff. Think of it as a spectrum. There are people who do engineering who never write an equation — people call them engineers because they put Legos together. Then there are people who never do any Legos, all they do is gather data and analyze and try to see what the data says. Two extremes, but the real truth is there's very little of this purity, and you shouldn't care. Most of the action's in the middle, where a real engineer is going to solve problems. They're going to use math to help them solve the problems — I'll tell you why. And when they have things they can't answer, that's fine, but they're good enough to get it solved. Then they'll either come back to it later, or they'll tell a scientist buddy: look at this cool thing I ran into that I can't figure out, maybe you can.

`§1.p3` Okay, here's the first thing I'm going to pass around. *[Slocum hands out small parts.]* I want you to play with this. Let me explain why this matters. I have a hypothesis — so now I'm being a scientist — that everybody here was born. And I have a hypothesis that everybody here is going to die. This body, whatever happens afterward, whatever you believe in is fine, but in conventional thinking, you're going to die, right? Does anybody have a problem with these two hypotheses? I've got data for the first one: you have been born because you're here and you're no longer pooping on the floor. I can say that because I have a new granddaughter, and every now and then…

`§1.p4` So you're saying, what's he doing, he's off on another tangent. No, I'm an engineer. I observe — that's the scientist in me — but the engineer in me says, how can I design a better diaper that doesn't leak? That's what an engineer does. A scientist will observe and develop a hypothesis. The engineer will do that, but also ask how can I make it better. The reason you want to be an MIT engineer — and not the engineer that just says, I got this from the catalog and this from the catalog, and the catalog says put the two together, okay now they're together — you see, they're not put together. A real MIT engineer will say, that doesn't seem really right. Why? This is where the science of the engineering comes in. There's a difference between good enough and *really* good enough. Understand?

## §2. White powder, broken shafts, and St. Venant's principle [03:41]

`§2.p1` This birth-and-death issue is really important, because you're going to get to the end of your life, and you're going to look back and say, holy jeez, if I sum up all the time I just spent bored, I could have had an extra three weeks of snowboarding. The magic is when you go to create something. Always look at opposites.

`§2.p2` There's a theory that the internet is the great wonderment, but it's also the most horrible thing for the people in the world who don't have it. Those who have it just zoom forward — this is where the wealth divide is coming from. If I have access to the internet and I know how to use it, and you have access but don't know how to use it, I'm going to roll right over you. This is where the Conquistadors come into Central America with guns: a hundred conquistadors with guns wipe out tens of thousands of natives with their happy little spears. Unless you can do this, you're just a happy little native with a spear, and I've got a machine gun and I'm in a tank. That machine gun and tank is mathematics, or analysis. Because Legos and stuff out of a catalog, that's the crack cocaine of the engineering world.

`§2.p3` Let me show you a real physics example. I have to admit, I have a white powder habit. You've heard about old people, senior people who make a lot of money and get into the white powder, right? I love heli-boarding. I love going out to the Canadian Rockies. Me and my buddies get in a helicopter, I get on my snowboard, and we'll do 50,000 vertical feet of powder in a day. *[That's the other white powder.]* I've paid for more heli-boarding trips with this simple little thing.

`§2.p4` *[Slocum holds up a small lead-screw assembly.]* Motor, shaft on motor, pulley on shaft, belt driving another pulley, to a screw that drives the nut, then a car to go. You have engineers who make this in the lab. Oh, this is beautiful, look, it's on the front page of the web page. And Alex goes — *[gesture]* — teleport. Why? Because they do the crack cocaine. They take what they saw in a catalog and they —

`§2.p5` Ask why. With everything I see or do — because I have the training, and you have the training as MIT geeks, and this is what will separate you from the herd — everything I do, I look at it and say, what's the physics, what's the free body diagram, where are the forces? Here's a belt. Remember torque. So there's force. When this belt is transferring torque, there's a force in the belt, right? There has to be a force pulling, which means there's a force bending the shaft. *[To latecomer.]* You're late, right? Sorry, don't be late. I love you, but don't be late. This just cost you one heli-boarding trip, because the knowledge you've lost means you won't know how to answer this question, which means you'll screw up and you'll have to hire me as a consultant. I go heli-boarding. More money for me, right?

`§2.p6` Here are bearings in there. You've all had 8.01. The other thing with engineering and science is, an engineer understands how to take the mathematics and turn it into a philosophy, apply it, and then do the actual analysis when needed. I need a volunteer from the studio audience. *[Slocum recruits a student to hold one end of the assembly.]* Hold that. Don't let me get away.

`§2.p7` Now we'll do the same — reciprocity, she gets a turn. Now you try to get away. What's the difference? This is called St. Venant's principle. The most amazing thing that's ever happened in mechanics. It's the principle of characteristic dimensions. The characteristic dimension of this system is your wrist, your hand. You can't control me with only one characteristic dimension, but I can control you if I have three to five. So much in engineering: when you do the analysis you'll see a curve, performance versus something, and it slowly achieves true happiness. When you have something held by three to five characteristic dimensions, you can get to the eighty or ninety percent wonderfulness factor.

`§2.p8` So look at the length of this shaft, the diameter of this shaft — this is actually drawn to proportion. And where is the force applied with respect to the motor and the bearings? The force is applied at three to five characteristic dimensions *away* from where the bearings hold the shaft. The force controls the shaft, dominates the shaft, and causes early failure of the shaft and bearing. So when I'm looking at machines, I'm always running through this — the philosophy of the physics. What are the characteristic dimensions, what are the free body diagrams, where are the forces flowing? Forces flow like pouring a glass of water.

`§2.p9` So what happens? This is awful, this is painful, you break the shaft. People design production machines and they're beautiful, they're wonderful, and everyone says oh that's so creative, and they start shipping this stuff. Hundreds of thousands of parts get made and shipped — millions, sometimes hundreds of millions of dollars of equipment — and it's all wrong, it's all coming back for warranty repair. They won't pay me the consulting rate up front to design it properly. They say, we can go to Design Services, we can get it for one-tenth the price. I say, fine, you'll be paying ten times that later. These guys use blue slides and wear suits and ties — good, pay them a hundred times more. And when this stuff fails, they're panicking. No one will pay for good food or go to the health club, but they will pay when you're in trouble. That's why doctors earn more money. So they've made millions of these and now they need someone to fix it.

`§2.p10` When you're in pain, lying on your back and you're really uncomfortable, what do you do? Roll over. Reciprocity. So the first, most powerful principle in all of engineering is St. Venant's principle — proportions. The second is Maxwell's reciprocity: if you have a shaft supported like this, and I push here and measure deflection there, I can take this force to that point of deflection and I'll get that same deflection over there, for a linear system. The philosophy of it: if it hurts, try the opposite.

`§2.p11` So how would you fix this? Think about the free body diagram. I put the hub like that, here's the belt, I flip this around — the force is now near the bearings, the shaft dominates the force, and I don't fail the bearing. You can do the math: the forces on the bearings go way down, and I no longer have the stress concentration of the keyway. If you have a keyway or a setscrew in there, the flow of forces goes through that stress concentration in the shaft and breaks the shaft off. The other philosophy book you need to read is *If You Give a Mouse a Cookie*.

`§2.p12` So this is really what engineering and science is, in my mind. It's not this or this — it's both. You do it with philosophy, keeping the math in mind, and applying the math when needed. Or, if weirdness happens, you find a really deep data-gathering super-ultra-mega analyst applied mathematician. Everybody groovy? Those parts I just passed around are called *flexures*. And the Legos everybody played with — Legos go together because nothing is infinitely accurate, but when you put all those points together, called *averaging*, everybody gives a little bit.

## §3. The kinematic coupling and Teradyne [15:31]

`§3.p1` I solve a lot of problems like this for industry. When I solve them they pay well, and then I go on crazy vacations — snowboarding, scuba diving, long-distance events. *[Slocum produces a kinematic coupling.]* This is called a kinematic coupling. It's a three-legged milking stool, thousands of years old — a tripod. These three balls go in these grooves. If I tip it up, they fall over. Three grooves, six contact points. Or you can have three-two-one — tetrahedron, groove, and flat. There's a magic in here. Something is wrong with one of the sides, and we'll see who can look at it, just thinking of 8.01 physics, and say, oh, that was done wrong. Let me pass it around.

`§3.p2` I teach a class, 2.75. The first project: everybody designs something using a kinematic coupling. It can be a wine bottle holder, a coffee cup holder — one guy did a unicycle holder. Just something you want to hold carefully. Play with it in this configuration, where the grooves all point to the center. Then rotate the grooves — even just some of them, so the grooves are sideways — and ask: what happens, and why?

`§3.p3` I had a student who did a doctoral thesis on a self-leveling version of these kinematic couplings. We did a lot of work with Teradyne — Alex d'Arbeloff's company. There's a fun shaggy-dog story about how he and I met over rubber chicken lunch. I got there, I was dressed like this, I actually had my lab coat with hydraulic juice on it. My junior faculty colleagues were all wearing suits, brown-nosing this old guy at the front of the table who had huge ears. They were all talking about the need for collaboration and teamwork, and he was saying, oh collaboration, team, yes sir, yes sir.

`§3.p4` So I didn't say anything. Then the old guy says, Professor Slocum, what do you think? I said, I think it's all a little crap. He says, what do you mean? I said, you can take ten engineers working in a brilliant wonderful team, and they're all a bunch of ignorant people, and all they care about is being nice and kind and beautiful to each other, and everyone's afraid to actually say when something is wrong. You will have the space shuttle disaster. I differ with you. And I said, look, you make Water Piks, you don't know anything about real engineering. He goes, *God damn it Slocum*, slams the table — *it's Teradyne, not Teledyne, we make semiconductor manufacturing equipment, not Water Piks.* I said, oh, well, I work in semiconductors too, but I still think you're wrong. He goes, huh? I said, take your hardest mechanical problem that your teams of happy pretty people have not been able to solve. If I can't solve it in ten minutes of looking at it, I'll buy you dinner wherever you want. He says, deal.

`§3.p5` So I went over there. At Teradyne they make testers. Here's this big machine called a handler. The computer chips come in, the robot takes the chips and presents them up. Teradyne makes a big thing that sits on top — like an outside air-conditioning unit, a meter in diameter, a thousand pounds — and it jams a whole tray of chips up inside the device. Little prongs touch each chip and you see if the chip is good or not. All semiconductor devices are tested 100%. Periodically you take this thing off, flip it over, replace the test boards, then put it back. It has to be precisely relocated on the handler. You can't just use vision, because the robot is XY, it can't account for all the tilt. It takes them about half an hour to do the realignment. This is about one to two million dollars of equipment.

`§3.p6` If you do three or four changes a day, that's one to two hours per day of downtime on equipment that costs that much — how much would you be willing to pay for a system that knocks one to two hours down to five minutes? This is the other thing the engineer does: look at the problem and analyze what resources you have. Not the catalogs — how much money can I spend to solve the problem? Regular engineers don't do that, but the Harvard manager guys do, and that's why they get paid the big money. In this case: you can solve this for less than $50,000, we will sell endless months of —

`§3.p7` So I got there and I looked at it. I said, oh, I see what the problem is, I'm going to use a kinematic coupling. Three balls, three grooves, I can repeat to submicron. The head of engineering said, is what you're saying right, can you do it? I pulled up my computer. James Clerk Maxwell was using these for his optics experiments — the original Maxwell documents said, these are really cool, I wish I knew how they worked. I was the silly guy at the Bureau of Standards who figured out not only the kinematics but the contact stresses. The kinematics are really simple, the contact stresses aren't too difficult, but no one had ever taken the two together, because opticians had used these for years to precisely align — everybody knows they work because it's three points contacting.

`§3.p8` Now we're going to do an in-class experiment. The educationists tell us we need to do in-class hands-on learning. Get out your pen. Take the pointy end and put it up to your temple. Now push until it hertz. *[Pun on Hertz.]* Heinrich Hertz, one of the most famous mathematicians — the guy who actually invented frequency. That's why hertz is called hertz. His railroad friend in Germany said, I pull trains, and if I add weight to the locomotives I can pull more, but then I dent the tracks. How much weight can I put on? Now use reciprocity: instead of pushing with the pointy end, push with a round smooth end. Can you push harder before it hurts? He's the guy who figured out the math — that's why it's called Hertz contact stress analysis.

`§3.p9` So I figured out how to do it, I wrote the spreadsheet, we put it out for free — how to design these so you can have infinite ginormous loads and never hurt them. I talked the engineers through how to do it. Over the next week they built a prototype, and Alex bought me the most wonderful dinner, and then wrote a check to MIT for $20 million. The lunch we had was him checking us out to see if we were worthy of him endowing a chair, and I got that chair. That's why the administration doesn't want to kill me.

`§3.p10` This turned into a doctoral thesis for one of my graduate students, because Teradyne wanted these things to be servo-controlled to adjust. They've sold over 10,000 of them now at $50,000 a pop. The patent is still valid, it's a huge seller. Ten thousand times fifty thousand — that's $500 million of kinematic couplings sold in the last ten years. And the other thing: that's only $50,000 — you can only buy these if you buy the tester. It's a million-dollar tester. So now it's, if you want this feature, you've got to buy ours.

`§3.p11` This is *If You Give a Mouse a Cookie*, the Mouse that Roared, the inverted pyramid. Everybody see the power of not just putting Legos together, but the philosophy and doing the mathematics and then optimizing? The key in mechanical engineering — in anything — is constraints.

`§3.p12` Let me show you. When you look at the Hertz contact equations: point rollers, this is a point, this is actually a half-meter diameter ball. *[Holds up a large ball.]* You can imagine that if you put a small ball on the surface and push, it'll hurt quickly. But if the ball were this big, it would take a lot more load, right? But it still only touches a point. So you take the big ball, you chop it, you bring it down — it looks like a canoe bottom. If you rock it, the contact zone moves out along the V. If it's too misaligned, the contact zone goes to the edge and you get *edge loading*. So you have to model how much misalignment you'll get. This is the danger of *catalog-itis* — that's what I call it. It gives you a terrible rash. People with catalog-itis haven't done any analysis of what the limits are.

`§3.p13` Whenever you have something that's super-ultra-wonderful, my God, this is too good to be true — it *is* too good to be true. The mathematics is your bond.

## §4. The mechanical psychotherapist [27:13]

`§4.p1` Now, having burbled all that, I'm going to launch in. I think it's irrelevant, because the Earth was born and it's going to die — the question is when. And I think it's happening very fast. ## §5. APHRO — Advanced Pumped Hydro Reverse Osmosis [33:54]

`§5.p1` So I asked, what are you going to do with the next $2 trillion? What I said back then was: if you take $4 per installed watt of renewables, solar and wind — and that's a lot of money, $4 — you could have 500 gigawatts of CO2-free 24/7 electric power. That's actually the instantaneous average consumption in the United States. We could have said to the rest of the world: we don't know what we did to piss you off, we're not admitting anything, but instead of spending the next ten years and $2 trillion on war, we're going to make you irrelevant. We're going to sell that technology all over the world to make oil irrelevant everywhere. That really scared people off. Nothing makes a person more scared than being irrelevant.

`§5.p2` Do you know how Stalin tortured my uncle? They picked you in a big barn with a pile of dirt, and everyone got a wheelbarrow. You fill up your wheelbarrow, take it to the other end of the barn, dump it, fill it up again, come back. Continually doing this inane thing. It drives people crazy, they start screaming. The last man standing got to live. My uncle lived. You know what he was doing? He was designing construction machinery in his head — this is before computers — how would I make a machine to do this? He became a designer of transfer lines for automated assembly in Detroit, many years later.

`§5.p3` We still need oil. I'm one of the inventors of revolutionary drill bits for hot deep drilling. I solved the problem of seals — when you drill on this propeller, you fish, you knew there'd be a problem with the seals. I got rid of the elastomers. The way you get horizontal drilling, which is where all the fracking comes from: the drill bit is rotating, and to go down, a little flapper pushes the bit down. To go left, as the drill rotates, a hydraulic signal pushes the valve to the left. But when you go deep, it gets hot, and the rubber seals die. The colleagues I was originally hired with were hiring the elastomer gods to figure out rubber that would last at high temperature. I figured out how to get rid of all the rubber. It works far better, doesn't wear out, and it's cheaper.

`§5.p4` Then there's the *Britney effect* — oops, it's going to happen again. I had a doctoral student — we have a neat new company. I was a member of Secretary Chu's team on the Deepwater Horizon. I have three sons but also three nieces, and what happens when they leave my house: they take showers, and long hair comes out. We designed a machine to send wire into a blowout preventer and create a hairball. We didn't do it for the Deepwater Horizon, that's another long story. It's a new company in Houston, got great funding, run by a former doctoral student. It's the Hairball Generator. It *will* happen again — but this time, you're going to fix it right away.

`§5.p5` Energy and saving the planet — solar. I'm still a believer in oil, you can't get rid of it, but you want to get it responsibly. I was looking for where the money is on solar. You've heard about solar thermal, big towers. I look at this stuff and say, too many parts. I love snowboarding, my goal is to get rid of parts because I listen for complaints. I go talk to the plant managers, and the problem with the big solar thermal towers is they have all these tubes. Physicists haven't figured this out yet, but at night the sun goes down and it's dark. Things are really hot during the day, then at night —

`§5.p6` When you do that, solid things breathe and die. No problem, I'll make them thicker, add more heat tape, see — *malcooking*. Let's just get rid of them. Let the sunlight beam directly into a container of hot salt. Hot salt is actually clear; sunlight goes in and absorbs through the volume. You can't hurt molten salt. So a big part of what we're doing now is molten salt receivers — a big project with Masdar. We also looked at undersea energy storage: big concrete spheres on the bottom of the ocean, or pipes used to anchor wind turbines, and you pump the water out of them. Pumped hydro — everybody understands it: mgh, potential energy, lake up high, water runs down through a turbine, when you want to store energy you pump the water up. This is just pump the water *out* instead of up. It was still too expensive. But this leads to my afro.

`§5.p7` Listening to people, they say renewable energy is too expensive. I can just burn coal in Utah, so it doesn't matter — I'm in California, I like smoking dope and having clean air and driving my limo and being a real liberal dude, I don't have to worry about pollution, I burn coal in Utah. The Germans say, no nuclear power, it's dangerous, turn it off right now — fortunately we have France we can buy power from when the solar is dark, and they have nuclear too. The complaining I'm hearing is: renewables are too expensive. And — we don't have any water to drink because we keep killing the planet and climate is changing. No problem, we have desalination — which means we can burn coal in Utah to make electricity for the pressurized water. I have a lot of friends in Utah, so that's not going to happen.

`§5.p8` So I listen for pole-zero cancellation. When the Earth was created, someone was really smart. Here's the ocean — they knew that people who actually think about math would realize that during the day you want to be on the beach having happy fun times, right? I grew up in LA, I can say this. And sometimes I don't want to go to the beach, I want to go snowboarding. So I'm going to put big mountains right near the beach, within ten miles. I only have five days to create everything, because on the sixth day I've got to make Walmart and Amazon. I have to cut a few corners. I'm going to design fluid mechanics such that the optimum head height for a hydroelectric system is 500 meters — about 1,500 feet — which is 50 atmospheres of pressure.

`§5.p9` But I don't have enough time also to do the chemistry for reverse osmosis. They're going to screw up the planet, cause the temperature to go haywire, and they'll need a membrane to run seawater past at high pressure so fresh water goes through. I'm just going to make the optimal pressure for reverse osmosis the same as the optimal head for hydro. That's the little magic I found: the pressure you need for reverse osmosis is the same pressure you want for hydro. So in areas where you have beautiful coasts but no water, if you pump your water up into a reservoir when you have excess electricity from solar or wind, then run it down through your desalination plant and your water turbine, you get two birds with one stone. Symbiotic.

`§5.p10` As an engineer, the world is full of people who just think, oh, these wonderful — what you want to do is write a spreadsheet or MATLAB, whatever you like, of the physics. *[Shows spreadsheet.]* Let's say I want to provide electricity and fresh water for three million people — one-third of LA. One kW of average power per person, 500 liters of water, which is more than enough for all your needs. You run through all the numbers, the efficiency, the process, and you check your wonderful brilliant ideas — because nine times out of ten my wonderful brilliant ideas are just because I'm a farmer and I need manure to fertilize my crops. But this one was real fruit.

`§5.p11` If I make a lake which is 2 km by 2 km and 30 m deep, that holds all the water I need up high, to supply all the fresh water and all the electricity for three million people. I can solve California's problem. We went around the world using a higher resolution than Google Earth — this happens to be Southern California, and these are lake sites in the mountains. The red indicates prime afro locations, and no one lives there at all — these are state forest. If we take this little piece of land and make a lake, okay, the Sierra Club arborists may not be happy, but it means we won't have to burn all this coal in Utah and kill the rest of the planet. We get water and power for all of LA. If you go south into Tijuana and Mexico, there are even better sites. We can help address the immigration problem — not by killing people or building a fence — by making it so they don't want to come here, because there are better opportunities where they are.
