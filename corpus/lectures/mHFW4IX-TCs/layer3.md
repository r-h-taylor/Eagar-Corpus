## §3. The scientific method and confirmation bias [07:54]

`§3.p1` But I want to take you through the scientific method. We talked about the scientific method before. Do people remember the four key points of the scientific method? I wasn't that impressed with how many people rattled them off. You first collect the data. The reference I gave you started with defining the problem, but assuming you know what your problem is, you've got to collect data about it. You have to analyze it. Develop a hypothesis and test it. And only then can you draw conclusions.

`§3.p2` Now this has been around for thousands of years. Aristotle and others used it. If you develop a hypothesis and test it and it doesn't work, you've got to go back and develop a new hypothesis, and you keep on doing this until you develop a hypothesis that cannot be refuted. You're trying to falsify your hypothesis. Your test should be designed to show that your hypothesis is false. If you do your testing and you cannot show it's false, then the conclusion is it must be true. There's a quote from somebody famous — I can't remember who — when we've tested something, the only thing that remains, no matter how improbable, must be the truth, if we've eliminated all the other possibilities. Maybe it's Sir Arthur Conan Doyle — good old Sherlock Holmes. Used deduction to solve the problems.

`§3.p3` Now there's also induction. The thing I gave you, about three or four pages on the scientific method, is the most complete description I've ever seen. It talks about confirmation bias, where people draw their conclusions in the beginning too early before they've laid out enough hypotheses to test, and they don't eliminate all the other possibilities. For example, the thing out of Kahneman's book, where someone had looked at the data and they found kidney cancer was worst in the poor rural counties. You can define that as the cause. It also turns out, if you look at the data another way, you find that kidney cancer was least in all those counties. So it was the worst and the least, depending on what county you're talking about. That was, as Kahneman said, the statistics of small numbers — if you're not averaging over a large enough cohort.

`§3.p4` There's expectation bias. You think that is the cause, and so you end up proving it's the cause. Confirmation bias I often call the reverse scientific method. You draw your conclusion, you throw out all data that doesn't fit, and you go backwards. It's circular reasoning. You start with your conclusion and end up concluding the same thing. You're just going around in circles. You don't accept any data that doesn't fit. So if you avoid confirmation bias, you will test all the potential theories.

## §4. John Snow and the Broad Street pump [11:40]

`§4.p1` So Tufte gives us two examples. One is John Snow. John Snow was actually the anesthetist to Queen Victoria. Why she needed her own anesthetist — have any of you ever been anesthetized? Anyway, he was a medical doctor. Tufte likes to point out that depending on how you display your data and analyze it, you may get better results than others. He gives us a successful story and an unsuccessful story.

`§4.p2` What happened was cholera broke out in the Broad Street area of central London in 1854. John Snow had investigated earlier epidemics and suspected the water from a community water pump was contaminated. This is before we really had clean water. In the nineteenth century people were lucky to have clean water. People didn't understand bacteria in 1854. Tufte talks about what some of the theories were — that you contracted cholera through the air, things like that. But Snow had a hypothesis. He had a good idea, and he was going to use that hypothesis to test the data, and then he was going to evaluate things. He goes through the whole process of considering other things even after you think you've proven things.

`§4.p3` At the bottom is a graph showing the death rate starting on August 31st. People started dying, peaked the next day, and then it started to wane. Here's the cumulative death rate. Six hundred people died during the same epidemic of only a few days — about a week's time. You've got some of these graphs if you picked up one of these things in the back. What he did is he plotted on a map of London, and it turns out right here in the centre there's a little dot. It says "pump." This is the most deaths. Each one of these represents a little line, and the longer the bar — you can see the individual lines. This thing's not sharp enough to distinguish them. You can think of each one as a little coffin. He has a picture later of little coffins.

`§4.p4` It turns out on September 7th, Snow talked to the local town officials in that part of London, and he explained that he thought it was the water that everybody was drinking from the community pump. The next day the town officials took the handle off the pump, and the death rate decreased. Now you have to consider alternative explanations and contrary cases. For example, it was already decreasing before he even did this, right? So you have to say, well, why is that? Maybe it's not the right hypothesis, if it already had started to decrease. But in fact what had happened: everyone was fleeing the neighborhood. After two days of a hundred and eighty people dying in one day, or whatever the number was, a lot of people decided to go visit their relatives in the country. Good idea. So you didn't have a constant population, and if you could have corrected for that population, you may have found that it was staying constant.

`§4.p5` He gives a lot of anecdotal examples. There was this one elderly woman who loved the taste of the water from the Broad Street pump. Once a week her nephew or son would go into London and bring back some water from the Broad Street pump. He did, and she died the next day, even though she was a long way away from central London. Cholera may take two days, but if you're elderly it may take only one day as you get sick. The stories in here, I don't remember all the details, you can read it — it's not something in my general knowledge. But he goes through a couple of pages to talk about how they tracked down some of the outliers. As you can explain the outliers — the ones that seem to disprove your hypothesis — you find that those have an explanation too. All you're doing is reinforcing your hypothesis.

`§4.p6` He points out that you can plot the deaths on a daily basis, the same plot we showed you before, or you can plot it on a weekly basis. Sometimes when you aggregate things — in this case you still see the decrease — but sometimes when you start averaging things you can get really bad data. He gives you another example from something else that shows you've got a little peak here, but if you average over a certain amount, you lose it. Averaging is something that electrical engineers and mechanical engineers do a lot in signal analysis. They may have a moving window, or a Kalman filter — different types of ways to filter the data to take the noise out. Sometimes you can take the real data out too, if you filter out enough; you just get a straight line. And here's the little plot of Queen Victoria, John Snow, and a plot of coffins. You can read the whole story yourself. It's actually good reading when you're doing nothing.

## §9. Engineer, contractor, owner: who is responsible? [45:11]

`§9.p1` On page one, which is general requirements, 1.1 is the scope of the code and what it involves and the limitations on the code, and the definitions. First thing: the engineer shall be defined as a duly designated individual who acts for and on behalf of the owner on all matters within the scope of the code. It's not going alphabetically. The second one defined is the contractor. This is the person who's going to do the welding. The contractor shall be defined as any company, or the individual representing a company, responsible for the fabrication, erection, manufacturing, or welding in conformance with the provisions of this code. The owner shall be defined as the individual or company that exercises legal ownership of the product or structural assembly.

`§9.p2` So the owner could be some financial group on Wall Street. The engineer is some guy who is going to have to put his stamp of approval on the design of this thing, and he's usually working for the owner. This code does not provide for a test of engineer's competence or ability.
