# Case Index — Lecture mHFW4IX-TCs (Fall 2015, 10/12)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p3`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is structured around two paired case studies from Tufte (a "successful" and an "unsuccessful" application of visual / statistical thinking), framed by methodological material on the scientific method and bracketed by Tom's own pedagogy on the structural welding code. Several lecture-recurring asides also appear (Lawrence Livermore NIF, the Cambridge courthouse Columbia anecdote).

## Cases developed in this lecture

### 1854 Broad Street cholera epidemic
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1854 Broad Street cholera epidemic"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tufte's "successful" example of visual statistical thinking. John Snow plots cholera deaths on a London map, identifies the Broad Street pump as the cause, has the handle removed. Tom uses it to teach hypothesis-falsification, consideration of alternative explanations (people fleeing the neighborhood), and the role of outliers (the elderly woman who had pump water shipped to her).
- **Era:** 1854.

### Space Shuttle Challenger
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`–`§6.p12`, returning at `§7.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle Challenger"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tufte's "unsuccessful" example. Solid rocket motor o-ring blow-by at low temperature. Tom teaches the engineering design of the SRM, the recoverable-vs-expendable architecture, and contrasts solid-fuel and liquid-fuel control. The launch politics (NASA pressure, $1.3B continuation contract, State of the Union, Christa McAuliffe) frame Boisjoly's failed warning. Feynman's o-ring-in-ice-water demonstration closes the case.
- **Materials/systems:** Rubber o-rings, solid propellant, segmented rocket motor architecture.
- **Era:** 1986.

### Bosco Lee Morton Thiokol O-ring engineering decision
- **Anchor:** `§6.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bosco Lee Morton Thiokol O-ring engineering decision"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The specific engineer (Tom calls him "Bosco Lee"; actual name Roger Boisjoly) who tried to stop the launch. The "take off your engineer hat and put on your manager's hat" line is the teaching beat. Tom returns in §7.p1 to note that Boisjoly resigned from Thiokol and went on the lecture circuit.
- **Era:** 1986; lecture-circuit aftermath ongoing through 1990s–2000s.
- **Note:** See editorial register flag — Tom consistently uses "Bosco Lee" for Roger Boisjoly. Canonical cluster name preserves Tom's slip; bracketed correction in L3.

### Space Shuttle Columbia
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle Columbia"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's Cambridge courthouse anecdote. While testifying about an aircraft engine failure, he predicts NASA will lose another shuttle within 15–20 years; Columbia disintegrates the next morning. The opposing side settles the case. Tom uses it as a story about the strange theater of expert witness testimony.
- **Era:** February 2003.

### Aircraft engine failure litigation (Cambridge courthouse)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aircraft engine failure litigation (Cambridge courthouse)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The unnamed aircraft engine case Tom was testifying on when Columbia disintegrated. Used here as the setting for the Columbia coincidence, not for its engineering content.
- **Era:** ~2003.

## Cases referenced in passing

### National Ignition Facility (NIF) and the dual-use laser
- **Anchor:** `§5.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Lawrence Livermore National Ignition Facility dual-use"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's neighbor's visit to Lawrence Livermore. Tom explains to her that NIF's stated mission (fusion energy) is cover for the real mission (simulating nuclear weapons effects after the test ban). Used as a parallel to the space-laser-on-the-shuttle case to teach how military programs are described to the public versus to Congress.
- **Era:** post-1996 (Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty); anecdote ca. early 2010s.
- **Note:** May overlap with existing clusters on Lawrence Livermore or weapons-lab visits; flagged as proposed because no exact match in the aggregate.

### Space-based laser weapon and the cargo bay
- **Anchor:** `§5.p9`–`§5.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Space-based chemical laser weapon (Rockwell, 1978)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom triangulates the classified laser-weapon design from three unclassified sources — Air Force 50 MW superconducting generator program, chemical laser efficiency at 10%, and Professor Bowen's one-meter potassium-chloride window forging at Lincoln Lab. Friend at Rockwell confirms the figure and the cargo-bay fit. Used to argue the shuttle's real purpose was military.
- **Materials/systems:** Potassium chloride / fluoride / cesium chloride single crystals; chemical lasers; superconducting generators.
- **Era:** ca. 1978.

## Figures and standards referenced

### Codes and standards as a "gold mine"
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p5`
- AWS D1.1 Structural Welding Code — Steel: ~$75 twenty years ago, ~$400–500 today (400% increase).
- ASTM x-ray radiograph reference standards: $200–300 in mid-1990s, ~$2,000 today.
- Mil Handbook 5 / MMPDS-1: $110 from Government Printing Office; ~$700 after handover to private contractor.
- ASME Boiler & Pressure Vessel Code: ~$16,000, new edition every three years.

### Shuttle launch reliability
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`–`§6.p8`
- NASA pre-Challenger estimate: 1 in 10,000 launch failure probability.
- Feynman's correction from post-WWII rocketry record: 4% historical failure rate.

### Shuttle payload cost
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`
- Pre-shuttle: $10,000/lb to orbit.
- Shuttle marketing claim: $1,000/lb (10× reduction).
- Actual (per Tom): $20,000/lb.
