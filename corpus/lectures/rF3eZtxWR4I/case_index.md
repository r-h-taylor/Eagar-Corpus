## Cases developed in this lecture

### Space Shuttle Challenger O-ring failure
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle Challenger"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Opening anecdote on why things fail at joints. Tom recounts his on-the-day prediction that *Challenger* failed at a weld; correct on "joint", wrong on "weld".
- **Materials/systems:** Solid rocket booster O-ring seal.
- **Era:** 1986.

### Alexander Kielland offshore platform collapse
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alexander Kielland disaster"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended case on why non-critical welds matter. A 16-inch sonar-bracket pipe welded to a 24-foot-diameter leg, with a thumbnail-sized crack in high-carbon steel, grew by fatigue to 20+ feet before a storm snapped the structure. Over 200 dead.
- **Materials/systems:** High-carbon steel pipe welded to platform leg.
- **Era:** ~1980, North Sea.

### Etruscan gold-bead diffusion bonding / Pratt & Whitney 1972 patent
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Etruscan gold bead diffusion bonding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Etruscans used tin to bond gold beads to copper objects ca. 600 BC by furnace heating, producing diffusion bonds. Tom's recurring joke: Pratt & Whitney patented the process in 1972 only because the Etruscans didn't file an infringement.
- **Materials/systems:** Gold, copper, tin.
- **Era:** 600 BC / 1972.

### GE vacuum tube laser repair
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric vacuum tube laser repair"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Earliest commercial laser welding application. GE used a soft electron-laser pulse to make contact between failed tungsten/molybdenum wires inside a sealed glass vacuum tube, then a power pulse to weld, saving the tube without breaking the vacuum.
- **Materials/systems:** Tungsten/molybdenum wire, glass vacuum tubes.
- **Era:** Mid-1950s.

### AVCO Everett 25 kW laser welder demonstrations
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Avco Everett laser welder demonstrations (early 1980s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** AVCO Everett Research Labs developed 25-kilowatt lasers under government contract for heavy-steel laser welding. Tom's student worked there; his main job was making sure the laser worked for customer demos, because 90% of the time it didn't.
- **Materials/systems:** High-power CO₂ laser, heavy steel.
- **Era:** Mid-1970s.

### Falklands War HMS Sheffield Exocet strike
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "HMS Sheffield"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A single Argentinian-fired French Exocet missile started an aluminum fire that wiped out a British capital ship. Used to motivate US Navy particle-beam weapon development.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum ship superstructure.
- **Era:** 1982.

### US Navy particle-beam weapons program
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Navy particle beam weapons welding research"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Navy spent a quarter billion dollars on proton-beam anti-missile weapons through the mid-80s to early 90s. After the Cold War ended, the program looked for applications. Tom was invited to a workshop to assess welding nuclear submarines with the 5-megawatt beams; he pointed out you only need tens of kilowatts. He got a mid-90s research project to evaluate the technology for welding and heat treatment.
- **Materials/systems:** Proton beam, nuclear submarine steel.
- **Era:** Mid-1980s through mid-1990s.

### Paton Institute / Soviet welding research
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet underwater welding program at Paton Institute"
- **Frame in this lecture:** B. E. Paton Sr. weld-repaired Soviet tanks during WWII; his son took over the institute named after him. As one of seven members of the Soviet Academy of Sciences and head of the Ukrainian branch, Paton directed disproportionate national research funding to his 10,000-person institute. Tom: "Corruption at its best." Used as background for Soviet titanium submarine welding capability.
- **Materials/systems:** Welding science writ large; titanium welding specifically.
- **Era:** 1940s–1990s.

### US Navy heavy-section titanium submarine welding (Eagar 1977 project)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy heavy-section titanium welding (1980s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first research project, 1977: Navy contract to weld 1-inch-thick titanium for submarines. Shown as physical artifact in lecture. US never built a titanium submarine other than *Alvin*.
- **Materials/systems:** 1-inch titanium plate.
- **Era:** 1977–early 1980s.

### Soviet Alpha-class titanium submarine creep-fatigue failure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet Titanium Aircraft Program (Docket 3)" — *note: cluster name is ambiguous; better match may be* "Soviet titanium submarine (Alpha-class) creep-fatigue failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Soviets built titanium submarines using Paton Institute technology. Within 2–3 years, they parked them in harbor due to leaks. - **Era:** ~1978–early 1980s.

### Egon Orowan's cleaning-lady rug analogy for dislocations
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Egon Orowan dislocation discovery (rug anecdote)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Hungarian cleaning lady, working in Orowan's office, moves a Persian rug across the floor by introducing a small fold and kicking it across the room. ### Iron whiskers — 1950s near-theoretical strength single crystals
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`, `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Iron whiskers (1950s screw dislocation studies)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Single-crystal iron whiskers grown in the 1950s with only a screw dislocation (no edge dislocations), measured at ~1.2 million PSI — close to the theoretical Lennard-Jones prediction of ~2 million PSI. Used to validate the dislocation theory of weakening.
- **Materials/systems:** Single-crystal iron.
- **Era:** 1950s.

### Asbestos fiber tensile strength measurement
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Asbestos fibers strength measurement"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Asbestos fibers measured at ~700,000 PSI. Used to compare claimed (untested) carbon-nanotube strengths against historically validated single-crystal fiber strengths.
- **Materials/systems:** Asbestos fiber.
- **Era:** 20th century.

### Carbon nanotube structural applications hype cycle
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`, `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Carbon nanotubes structural applications (overpromised research)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Twenty-five years and a billion dollars of research on carbon nanotubes (and later graphene) for structural applications, promising 3 million PSI carbon-carbon bond strength. Nobody has measured the strength of an actual carbon nanotube. Vacancies forbidden by statistical mechanics at any T > 0 K mean real nanotubes will be 1/5 to 1/10 the predicted strength. Tom: "Don't invest in carbon nanotubes for structural applications."
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon nanotubes, graphene, bucky balls (C60).
- **Era:** Late 1980s to present.
