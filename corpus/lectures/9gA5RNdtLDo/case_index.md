*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p5`). This lecture is structured as a survey rather than a forensic case study; most cases appear as brief illustrations of points about materials properties, fracture, or welding history.*

## Cases referenced

### Aerogel as structural material
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aerogel as structural material (New York Times article case)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as Tom's exemplar of "boutique materials" — researchers chasing the lightest material in the world without having an application for it. Twenty-five years of research without payoff.
- **Materials/systems:** Silicon-oxygen sol-gel cellular material; ~10% density of water.

### Concorde supersonic transport
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`, `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Concorde skin temperature limitation" (primary frame here); also touches "Concorde maintenance and operational failures" and "Concorde supersonic transport operations and economics"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Skin-temperature-limited operation, not speed-limited. Personal anecdote about flying on a poorly-maintained Concorde.

### SR-71 Blackbird titanium skin
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "SR-71 Blackbird"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mach 3 / 100,000 ft skin temperatures exceeded aluminum capability, requiring titanium skin.

### X-33 space plane liquid hydrogen tank
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "X-33 space plane" (also "X-33 hydrogen tank adhesive joint failures", "NASA X-33 liquid hydrogen tank failure")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Physical sample held up. Carbon-carbon composite with Nomex honeycomb at $12,000/lb.
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon-carbon composite, Nomex honeycomb.

### Soviet titanium submarine (Alpha-class)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p5`, `§3.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet titanium submarine (Alpha-class) creep-fatigue failures" (also "Soviet Alpha-class submarine", "Soviet Titanium Aircraft Program (Docket 3)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Why the US chose not to build titanium submarines despite Soviet leapfrog. Hull cracking from creep-fatigue interaction. Faster than US destroyers; deeper than US depth charge collapse depth.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium hull.
- **Era:** Late 1970s–1980s.

### U.S. Navy titanium submarine program decision
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p4`, `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy titanium submarine program decision"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Eagar's first ONR research project at MIT (1977). David Taylor work on whether to match Soviet titanium subs. Cost: ~100× HY-100 hull; ~$3,000/lb vs $25–30/lb.

### HMS Sheffield fire (Falklands)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "HMS Sheffield" (also "USS Sheffield fire (Falkland Islands War)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Aluminum superstructure caught fire after Exocet hit; charge didn't detonate. Example of why US tried to move from aluminum to waffle steel superstructure.

### USS Belknap collision and fire

- **Frame in this lecture:** Destroyer Belknap collided with JFK carrier; jet fuel ignited Belknap's aluminum superstructure. "Gallon of jet fuel beat the Exocet."
