## Cases referenced in this lecture

### NASA X-33 space plane
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p2`, `§3.p3`, `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "NASA X-33 space plane"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate how aerospace weight-savings drives composite adoption — and how the $12,000/lb fabrication cost of the composite liquid hydrogen tank ($50M for a 4,000-lb tank) killed the program. Tank size: "two-story house." Set up rapid-prototyping context from the first Gulf War.
- **Materials/systems:** Nomex honeycomb core (Kevlar trade name), graphite-fiber-reinforced skin, adhesively bonded; Inconel and titanium forgings for structural connections.
- **Era:** Early 1990s, $1.3 billion NASA program.
- **Cross-references in canon:** "X-33 hydrogen tank adhesive joint failures", "X-33 space plane liquid nitrogen tank failure", "NASA X-33 liquid hydrogen tank failure", "Lockheed Skunk Works X-33 spaceplane composite tank (rapid prototyping redesign)".

### V-22 Osprey
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "V-22 Osprey aircraft"
- **Frame in this lecture:** First all-composite major aircraft. Tilt-rotor, 300 mph, used in bin Laden raid. Started at $15M per unit, ended at $65M — "one of the more notable overruns in the Defense Department."
- **Materials/systems:** Carbon-fiber composite airframe.
- **Era:** Developed late 20th century; bin Laden raid 2011.

### USS Schenectady (T2 tanker fracture)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1952 T2 tanker fracture off Cape Cod" / "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The classic brittle fracture image. Schenectady broke in two at dry dock on the West Coast (no casualties); a separate T2 tanker in the North Atlantic broke up at sea. Coast Guard rescue off Cape Cod 1952 made into a movie. Used as the historical hinge for why energy-of-fracture matters.
- **Materials/systems:** Welded steel merchant vessels.
- **Era:** WWII Liberty ships; 1952 T2 tanker; 1946 Navy report.
- **Cross-references in canon:** "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady", "WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide)", "1940s welded ship cracking", "Coast Guard rescue off Cape Cod".

### de Havilland Comet brittle fracture
- **Anchor:** `§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "de Havilland Comet"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Square windows as stress concentrators. Alan Wells worked out the cause. Used to anchor stress-concentration intuition immediately after Griffith's equation.
- **Materials/systems:** Aluminum aircraft skin, square cabin windows.
- **Era:** Early 1950s.

### Northridge earthquake (1994)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p3`, `§10.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Northridge earthquake"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate how buildings absorbed Irwin's energy-of-fracture lesson 40 years late. "Building snapped in the earthquake and caused billions of dollars worth of damage." Triggered impact / energy-of-fracture requirements being added to the steel-in-buildings codes in the 1990s.
- **Era:** 1994; code response through later 1990s.

### Sea Wolf submarine hydrogen cracking / inspection case
- **Anchor:** `§11.p7`, `§11.p8`, `§11.p9`, `§11.p10`, `§11.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking" / "SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem" / "USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking" / "Nuclear submarine welding hydrogen embrittlement cracks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1992 discovery by a grinder noticing sixteenth-of-an-inch swarf alignment from sub-eighth-inch cracks the inspection equipment couldn't reliably find. $2B repair, 18% of hull complete at discovery. Tom was one of two individual advisors to Admiral Firebaugh, then-Chief Engineer of the Navy. Used to teach: critical flaw size in the steel is several inches; the sixteenth-inch cracks are below the detection threshold; you don't have to derate the submarines, you just inspect for growth at scheduled maintenance.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-100 steel hull (and earlier HY-80 sections); 30-foot diameter, 100-ksi yield, classified wall thickness.
- **Era:** 1992; built at General Dynamics Electric Boat, Groton, CT; vertical-seam welding facility in Rhode Island.
- **Cross-references in canon:** "USS Sea Wolf submarine hull cracking", "SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem", "Submarine hydrogen cracking consulting case", "US Navy Sea Wolf submarine acoustic emission study".

### George Washington Bridge approach building crack (NYC)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "New York City building crack near George Washington Bridge" / "George Washington Bridge building crack inspection"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Forensic consult. Nine-foot crack in a nine-foot beam at the bottom of a 20-story building over the GWB approach into Manhattan. Bridge-style redundancy ("egg-crate construction") meant ten other beams held the building up; one or two approach lanes had to close. The tenants' "wanted to go get some of the crack" punchline preserved.
- **Era:** Tom's career — undated in lecture.

### Air Force acoustic emission crack-growth flight test (Australia)
- **Anchor:** `§11.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force acoustic emission crack-growth flight test"
- **Frame in this lecture:** "The general said: you're not flying one of my aircraft with a crack." Study had to move to Australia because Australian generals knew their planes were full of cracks. Used to make the point that aircraft fly with cracks; we manage them, we don't pretend they aren't there.
- **Era:** 1980s.

### Boeing $188/lb LGO weight-savings study
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing aircraft disc weight savings analysis (1990 LGO study)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1990 MIT LGO students at Boeing found the actual value of weight saved on a commercial aircraft was $188/lb — the source of Tom's "round to $200/lb" figure.
- **Era:** 1990.

### BLISK (bladed disc) development
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force blisk (bladed disc) welding development" / "Blisk (bladed disc) development program"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Air Force's long-running motivation for welded (vs. mechanically joined) bladed discs: lighter rim means smaller hub means cascading weight savings.
- **Era:** Ongoing Air Force initiative.

### Johnson & Johnson surgical instruments consulting
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Johnson & Johnson surgical instruments consulting"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's longstanding J&J consulting on minimally invasive surgical instruments — specifically the rongeur for arthroscopic cartilage work. Tom anchors with his own torn-cartilage history. The point lands as: smaller instruments need to remain stiff, which is why you cannot just keep slimming the cross-section.
- **Cross-references in canon:** "Arthroscopic knee surgery tool design and materials selection", "Rongeuer (orthopedic surgical pliers) consulting work", "Johnson & Johnson medical device manufacturing defect".
