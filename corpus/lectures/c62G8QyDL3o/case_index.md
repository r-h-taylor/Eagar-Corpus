# Case Index — Lecture 31 (Welding Metallurgy, Summer 2014)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§7.p3`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is primarily a technical exposition of aluminum welding (alloy selection, design codes, joint geometry). Cases surface as digressions that illustrate the technical points.

## Cases referenced

### MIT Lincoln Lab 300 GHz Radar Dish Structure
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3` through `§7.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT Lincoln Lab 300 GHz Radar Dish Structure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Air Force wanted to retrofit the old MIT Millstone steel pedestal/bearing with a new 150-foot aluminum radar dish at sub-quarter-inch tolerances. No shop would bid; ex-Electric Boat welding company eventually took the contract and succeeded. Used to illustrate (1) the scarcity of qualified aluminum welders in New England and (2) the use of many fillet welds and gussets rather than groove welds in precision aluminum structures.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum sheet structure on steel bearing, fillet-welded gussets, radome enclosure
- **Era:** circa late 1990s–2000s (retrofit of 1950s physics-research antenna)
- **Related anchors:** §9.p6 (gusset detail returns to this case)

### Boomer Submarine Trident Tube Retrofits
- **Anchor:** `§7.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boomer Submarine Trident Tube Retrofits"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief mention. The Electric Boat–trained welder whose shop took the Millstone contract was simultaneously doing SSGN conversion work — inserting clusters of ~19 non-nuclear warhead tubes inside former Trident tubes as part of SALT-era nuclear arsenal reductions. Used to establish the welder's credentials.
- **Materials/systems:** high-precision steel tube inserts in submarine missile tubes
- **Era:** SALT period (referenced in passing)

### Alaska Pipeline Welding Union Control
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alaska Pipeline Welding Union Control"
- **Frame in this lecture:** All field pipeline welding worldwide is controlled by the Tulsa, Oklahoma union. Automated welding technology was available in 1976 for the Alaska Pipeline; it was built manually instead, and remains manual forty years later. Used to make the point that welder availability/scarcity is sometimes political rather than technical — paralleling the technical scarcity case of aluminum welders in §7.
- **Materials/systems:** field pipeline girth welds
- **Era:** 1976 (Alaska Pipeline) — present
- **Related anchors:** parallels §7 thematically (welder scarcity, technical vs. political)
