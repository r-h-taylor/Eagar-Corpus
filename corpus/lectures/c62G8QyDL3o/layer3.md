## §4. Allowable stress design and aluminum's lower forgiveness [13:36]

`§4.p1` The big thing in the steel business now is what they call allowable stress design — a different philosophy. The old time they just said this is your safety factor. Now, after the Northridge earthquake in the early 90s, '91 or '92, in Los Angeles, the steel construction folks went back and said, let's get the design out of the 1920s and bring it into the 1980s. And they did, and it's called allowable stress design. You probably didn't catch it because I didn't emphasize it, but I brought in the steel construction manual. I said this used to be the bible — before the allowable stress design bible came out in the late 90s. And that was really as a result of a lot of the problems they had with buildings in the Northridge earthquake in Los Angeles.

`§4.p2` So the steel guys got together and said, we need a more modern design, that's not as weak in some areas and too strong in other areas. Now it's the allowable stress design, and I have no idea what they're talking about when I go look at it. You have to be trained as a civil engineer. They have sections on material properties, sections on weld properties. In steel you usually can say the fatigue life or allowable stress for the base metal is typically 0.6 of the yield strength. And the weld is 0.3 of ultimate tensile strength — so they upped the safety factor for the weld by a factor of two.

`§4.p3` If I've got a 60 ksi weld metal in steel, I can design it to 20 ksi — a factor of three or 3.3. If I look at the code for the base metal, it's half of that. I can actually have the base metal stressed to 35 ksi or so. In aluminum I might have 25 ksi as a yield, not an ultimate, and I may only be allowed seven ksi as my design strength. Because aluminum is less forgiving. Why is aluminum less forgiving? It's not as tough as steel. If I went back to that plot I showed you in the very beginning, steel is the toughest material — has the greatest combination of strength and toughness and cost of any material. That's why we use one and a half billion tons. Aluminum we use 45 million tons a year. But aluminum doesn't have the same type of toughness. So aluminum is not as tolerant of large defects. I told you the critical flaw size for ripping a sheet of paper — if it was in an HY-100 steel, it'd be two feet in most cases. But in aluminum it might be two inches.

`§4.p4` The toughest aluminum alloy — ten times tougher than any other aluminum alloy — is one that was developed in the mid-twenties. They use it for propellers, aluminum propellers. It will bend 180 degrees before it'll break. But we don't use that for aluminum structures because we need more strength. It's actually 2025 alloy. If you look in here for 2025, there's all kinds of data in the old Mil Handbook. Its toughness is five to ten times greater than 2024, but its strength is probably about two-thirds as much. It can bend like steel. But it's the only aluminum alloy that comes anywhere close to that. Because we're usually trying to push the aluminums — just like baseball bats — to higher and higher strength, which means more and more corrosion problems, stress corrosion cracking and other things. We're using aluminum because we need the performance of the lightweight for whatever structural reason.

## §6. Filler metal selection for aluminum [19:45]

`§6.p1` One of the things about aluminum alloys I've already alluded to is you have to avoid mixing the alloys with the wrong thing. This comes out of the welding handbook but it's in any book on welding aluminum. There are lots of notes on this table — it goes for two pages in the welding handbook. But what it is, is a table of how to select a welding filler metal. If I'm going to weld 1060 aluminum — which is 99.4 percent aluminum — to a casting of aluminum 201, which is aluminum-copper casting, I've got to use 4145. ER just means electrode. If I'm going to weld it to 356 or 357, two very common aluminum castings used on motorcycles and all kinds of things, you're going to use a 4043 filler metal. That's the workhorse filler metal. If I'm going to use 6061 and weld it to 5083 — something the Navy might do, a lot of other people too — they're going to use a 5356 filler metal. If you're going to weld 6061 to itself, use a 4043.

`§6.p2` If you try to use some of these other welding electrodes and don't follow this table, you're going to get cracking. You're liable to not be in the range where you want to be — lightly alloyed when you mix the weld metal with the base metal, or highly alloyed. You'll be in the wide freezing range and you're going to have cracking. So when people come to me and say I want to weld a piece of steel, I say give me the composition, give me the thickness, and I need to know hardness and hardenability of that steel. On aluminum I need to know composition so I can pick a proper filler metal. And I need to know composition on both sides if it's dissimilar alloys.

`§6.p3` If you're doing 5083 to 5083, you should use a 5183 filler metal. You're not going to buy 5183 plate — 5183 is an electrode, an alloy designed for 5083. So they make it 5183. It's not like steels where it tells you the carbon content. These are just thousand-series alloys, and you have some new alloy and you apply to the aluminum association and get it. I'll give you a little case study on how to select aluminum alloys in a little bit. Anybody have any questions? I want to talk about design of aluminum welds first.

## §9. Aluminum joint designs from the Aluminum Design Manual [31:31]

`§9.p1` They have different designs. This is actually sort of a lousy design — to put intermittent welds in this double-lap design, rather than a straight butt weld. You'd like to have nice clean straight butt welds. A lot of times you can do a straight butt weld in aluminum, but a lot of times we do other things.

`§9.p2` Here are some designs out of an Aluminum Design Manual. One of them is, you're going to make a U-shaped joint. You could do two fillet welds on the outside, or four fillet welds. Or, since aluminum is relatively easily formed at low temperatures, you can basically just get a simple lap joint and you don't put anything at the corners. We really like to avoid welds at the corners in aluminum. Steel has lots of toughness and so we don't mind corners in steel — we put the welds in the corners and usually do pretty well. But in aluminum you don't like to put welds at corners. They will tend to crack — either crack or fatigue.

`§9.p3` Here's a little lap joint where they actually machine the edge of the aluminum. Aluminum is relatively easy to machine compared to steel. You actually put two partial penetration welds in to make a lap joint. You can do this if it's not a fatigue-loaded situation. So it depends on the stress in your application, depends on what room you have available. Here's a design where they're making a T-joint — they actually use an extrusion to a plate, and you put a fillet weld here and groove weld here to try to get rid of some of the corner welds.

`§9.p4` It gets to be pretty extreme. You're going to say, well, I've seen lots of aluminum structures that are welded just like steel structures. Yes, but I took about a two-thirds hit in my safety factor or my allowable stress in order to build it that way. If you really want to make aluminum and get the same type of percentage of yield stress that you get in steel, you've got to start worrying a lot about your design of your joints.

`§9.p5` Here's one that's a little bit shocking. Ordinarily you take a butt joint and in steel you just make a pipe weld. In aluminum you might put a sleeve over it, and you might even put a weld in there, but you don't trust that weld — particularly if it's a heat-treatable alloy and you can't get full joint efficiency. So you put another sleeve around and make a bunch of fillet welds. It doesn't take a lot of high qualification for a welder to be able to make that joint.

`§9.p6` Other types of improvements in aluminum joints that we don't usually worry about in steel so much: in steel you might make a joint like this, or a full weld all the way around. In aluminum you'll start to taper the edges. They used a lot of gussets on this thing at Millstone for Lincoln Lab. In steel you'd bring a pipe into the side of a web of a steel flange, and you just bevel the pipe and weld a circle around it. Not in aluminum — you weld on a gusset, and then you put a little slit, and you slip that over and you do a bunch of fillet welds. So now you have lots of weld area for the gusset up against the web. You also have lots of fillet weld area. Again, a fillet weld is very easy to weld.

`§9.p7` And in aluminum, where you need the extra strength of lots of weld metal, you'll taper down to get more area. You don't want to have the stress concentrations from abrupt right angles that you can tolerate in steel. Let's not say you can't tolerate them in aluminum, but you're going to have to take a hit on your safety factor or your allowable stress in order to do so. Any questions on that? Okay.

`§9.p8` So there are lots of design considerations, most of which, if you think of a Navy ship made out of aluminum, they don't do right. And now you wonder why you got cracking. They take a chance.
