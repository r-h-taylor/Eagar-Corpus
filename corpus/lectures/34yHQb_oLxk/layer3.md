## §2. Innovations that actually changed the world: optical fibers [01:15]

`§2.p1` We're supposed to be talking about additive manufacturing, and up here we have the Digital Alloys website. I need to do a little full disclosure. `§2.p2` One is optical fibers. Corning worked on something that everyone said was impossible. You know how they make optical fibers? They draw it. It's actually sort of a cleanroom environment of making glass. They start with a very pure quartz fiber that they've drawn down, and then they flame-vapor-deposit layers on this thing as it's rotating. They end up making something that's four or five inches in diameter and three feet long. As they do it, they can functionally grade the composition of the glass.

`§2.p3` Now when they heat that up — glass has an interesting property, it's very close to being a Newtonian fluid. If I knew we were going to talk about this I would have brought my play-doh. At the right temperature in the right glass compositions, you can pull this big billet of glass — which you may have spent a million dollars producing, because it's a very slow process. It's an additive manufacturing process, by the way. You start out with something small and you keep adding different compositions, layers, as you go along, and then you draw it down into a fiber ten thousand miles long. Because it has different refractive indices as you go through the diameter, the light will go down rather than out. The light is trapped to go down a tunnel of easily refractive center glass. That's an additive manufacturing process. We've been doing that for thirty-five or forty years.

`§2.p4` In the late 1970s, copper was starting to go through the roof. New York City didn't know what to do. `§2.p5` So times change, but optical fibers changed the entire world of telecommunications on earth. `§4.p2` Why is that important? Why are strong magnets important? Motors. You can go to higher power, but it turns out — you'd have to take my welding course. This process has to be at 10 to the 6 to 10 to the 8 watts per square centimeter as the incident heat flux. If you don't have a million watts per square centimeter, you're not going to get the high speed of melting. If you have more than a hundred million, you're going to drill a hole through it. It's called laser hole drilling. You've vaporized the material and blow it away. At a million watts per square centimeter you melt a thin layer, but above a hundred million you're just going to vaporize the material, you're putting it in too fast.

`§8.p4` There are natural physical limitations, and some of those are controlled by the properties of the material in Fourier's — Fick's law that says the heat is minus K dT/dx. We know what K for metals is, it's fairly high, and you're going to have a temperature gradient. This is the watts per square centimeter — Q is watts per square centimeter, I know that's going to be somewhere in this range of a million to a hundred million. I know what K is, tell me, it'll tell me what dT/dx is. But if you go to Fourier's second law, you find that Fourier's second law always has something of the form x over the square root of alpha t — x is the thickness of this layer. Einstein actually worked out a diffusion equation mathematically and showed this will always be the argument. If you have an exponential it's going to be e to the x over square root of alpha t. If you have a Bessel function, cylindrical or spherical geometry, it might be x-squared over alpha t. This is a dimensionless number, and it comes out of the form of Fourier's second law. The differential equation — you can parse it around, and Einstein showed you always get something of this form. It has to be a pure number inside an error function or an exponential or Bessel function. There's all kinds of ways to solve the specific geometry, but you're always going to have this.

`§8.p5` This says that the faster you go — alpha is a function of K over rho Cp, where rho is density, Cp is heat capacity. These are all known quantities. This is time. If I want to go faster, x gets thinner, and it gets thinner faster as the time gets longer. Eventually the faster you go, the thinner this gets until you have no melting — you're just heating the surface, and you're going so fast you're not even melting it. If you try to go to higher power densities you start vaporizing the surface. So you have physical limitations, vaporization. All of these processes that are directed energy deposition — arc plasma, laser, electron beam — all have inherent limitations. Joule printing doesn't actually melt; it just heats it until it's nice and soft, just by resistive heating. I happen to know, because I've been studying heat transfer in terms of welding for thirty years, this has about a ten to a hundred fold advantage in terms of the heat in the process. I don't have to melt things, I don't have to put in the heat of fusion and then take it out, I just have to get it hot so it'll lay down. You saw the picture of it. That's not actually melting, that's just getting so hot it sort of fuses together.
