# Case Index — Lecture 2019-09-17 (Additive Manufacturing)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p2`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is a tour of materials innovations that actually disrupted their industries, followed by a hard-headed economic critique of additive manufacturing hype. Tom invokes a dozen cases, most as compressed teaching units, before settling into the analytical case against most AM applications in §8–§10.

### Corning optical fiber development
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`–`§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Optical fiber manufacturing (Corning)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Lead case for the "real innovations" tour. Flame-vapor-deposited preform, drawn into ten-thousand-mile fibers. Solved New York City's late-1970s copper-wire crisis.
- **Materials/systems:** doped silica, gallium arsenide (separately for transmitters)
- **Era:** 1970s–present

### Copper-to-zinc transition in US penny
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US copper penny material transition"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1970s copper price spike; pennies switched to zinc with copper plating to prevent melt-down arbitrage.
- **Era:** 1970s

### NYC underground copper wire capacity crisis
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "New York City underground copper wire capacity crisis"

### Motorola first cell phone
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`

### Nakamura gallium nitride blue LED
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "Nakamura gallium nitride blue LED development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Enabled white-light LEDs; saved trillions in lighting energy. Predicted in the 1950s.
- **Era:** 1990s

### Neodymium-iron-boron magnet development at General Motors
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric/General Motors neodymium supply shortage threat"
- **Frame in this lecture:** GM held the basic patent; Sumitomo fight; GM declined to be primary metal supplier. 300× stronger than regular magnets. Enabled fist-sized starter motors, power seats, Sony Walkmans.
- **Materials/systems:** Nd-Fe-B, Sm-Co, Dy-Fe
- **Era:** 1980s

### Samarium-cobalt magnet development at GE Schenectady
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "GE samarium cobalt magnet development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's ~1970 student field trip to GE Research with Professor Flemings. Pre-Nd-Fe-B competitor; expensive samarium.
- **Era:** ~1970

### Chevy Bolt dysprosium-iron motors
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Neodymium-iron-boron magnet thermal failure in electric vehicles"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's two Chevy Bolts use Dy-Fe magnets (180°F tolerance) rather than Nd-Fe-B (140°F). Military interest for nuclear-reactor-powered ships.
- **Era:** 2010s–present

### Sony Walkman miniaturization
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sony Walkman and neodymium-iron-boron magnets"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Nd-Fe-B enabled portable tape players (Walkman); before that, "tape player was the size of a football."
- **Era:** 1980s

### Allied Signal metallic glass production
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Metallic glass (met glass) transformer sheet production"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Dave Hill's Conway, SC plant. Half-million-degrees-per-second cooling on copper rolls. Intended for transformer cores; off-spec product sold as anti-shoplifting tags.
- **Era:** 1980s–present

### Polyethylene off-spec hula hoop application (1950s)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Polyethylene manufacturing and hula hoop market development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** First high-value-added plastic. Off-spec polyethylene from a new plant became hula hoops for ~18 months until process control caught up.

## Figures referenced

- **$200–220K/lb value-of-pound-saved for jet engines vs. ~$2/lb-over-vehicle-life for automotive** (§7.p5). Tom's recurring framing figure for which industries can absorb AM costs.
- **$200K/metric-ton wrought titanium; $3,200/metric-ton wrought steel; $5M/metric-ton AM ($5/cc); cost ratio 25:1 for titanium, 1500:1 for steel** (§10.p1–§10.p2). The cost-ratio slide that closes the lecture's economic argument.
- **10^6 to 10^8 W/cm² incident heat flux window for directed-energy melting** (§8.p3–§8.p5). Physical-limit framing for why you cannot simply speed up laser/electron-beam AM.
- **Half a million degrees per second cooling rate for met glass production** (§5.p1). Recurring rapid-solidification anchor.
- **300× regular-magnet strength for Nd-Fe-B; 140°F vs. 180°F transformation temperature for Nd-Fe vs. Dy-Fe** (§4.p1, §4.p5).
