## Conventions applied

Per the §1 worked example and convention catalog:
- Filler ("uh," mid-clause "okay," "you know") cut.
- Self-corrections smoothed.
- Captioner garbling fixed silently where unambiguous.
- "Okay" preserved only where it closes a thought; cut elsewhere.

## Per-paragraph decisions

### §1.p1 — Consulting rates
- **Captioner garbling fixed:** "To do a discounted rate, blah, you they don't have to do a discounted rate" → "On a discounted rate — they don't have to do a discounted rate." Tom's opening fragment "To do… blah, you" is captioner noise around a self-restart; the corrected reading is what he said.
- **Self-correction smoothed:** "no one asked me about my consultant" → "no one asked me about my consulting rate" (Tom mis-says "consultant" for "consulting rate"; context from the next clause makes the intended word unambiguous). This is a captioner-or-Tom slip on a noun; not bracketed because the correction is mechanical and the slip carries no teaching content.
- **Cut:** terminal "okay" treated as mid-clause filler in "you tell them the rate okay."
- **Smoothed:** "a day, if you do hourly rates as a consultant uh on your commercial stuff. It's easier to give them daily rates okay when you work for the government" — Tom restarts the sentence; layer 3 reads the completed thought.

### §1.p2 — The professor-on-vacation setup
- **Cut:** opening "Okay" — discourse marker into a new topic; in a normal multi-section lecture this would mark a section break, but since the whole fragment is one section, it reads as filler here.
- **Bracketed correction:** "how can the health [tell] professors work" — Tom (or the captioner) substitutes "health" for "tell." Following the convention for Tom's spoken slips, bracketed on first occurrence. The remainder of the paragraph uses "tell" correctly.
- **Cut:** "Well you can't say well he's not in his office, you know. Well how can you tell?" — collapsed to "You can't say, well, he's not in his office. How can you tell?" Repetition and "you know" cut.

### §1.p3 — The Jonesy anecdote
- **Captioner error fixed silently:** "backboard" → "blackboard" on second occurrence (first occurrence is correct; second is a captioner slip).
- **Tense smoothed:** "And the only intelligent answer written on the backboard is" → "was" (Tom is narrating a past event; the present tense is a spoken slip).
- **Cut:** "Okay" mid-paragraph as filler. Cut "I'm, you know," as pure filler in "And if he's never around, I'm, you know, how can you tell."
- **Preserved:** "Anyway, that's my cynical view of that" — closes the anecdote rhetorically.

### §1.p4 — Trailing fragment
- **Preserved verbatim** (with light smoothing). Tom is mid-transition to a new topic when the recording cuts. The dangling "I did want to talk a little bit about —" is preserved because it is the actual end of the tape; cutting it would misrepresent the source. The "so we're basically done with that, in the last couple of minutes" reads as Tom orienting to the clock at the end of the final session of the course.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could do this because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases that Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
