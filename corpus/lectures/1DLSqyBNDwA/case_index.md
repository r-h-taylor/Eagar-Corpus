# Case Index — Lecture 5, 3.371 What is Engineering, Fall 2015

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§6.p1`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is primarily a historical/institutional survey rather than a case-heavy teaching session. The "cases" surface mostly as illustrative anchors for Tom's themes about the engineering-vs-science divide and the scope of engineering work.

## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Boeing 747 longeron distortion
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Boeing 747 long geronde beam distortion" (likely matches existing cluster; "longeron" is the correct spelling — "long geronde" in the canon appears to be captioner garbling of the same case)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mentioned in passing as the kind of focused problem Tom considers a good ten-minute student presentation topic.
- **Materials/systems:** aircraft longerons / structural beams
- **Era:** Tom's consulting work, era unspecified here

### America's Cup hydraulic cylinder failure (7,500 psi)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "America's Cup hydraulic cylinder stress corrosion cracking" (probable match)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as a sample focused-failure topic appropriate for a student presentation: a 7,500 psi hydraulic cylinder that failed and had to be redesigned to fail safe.
- **Materials/systems:** hydraulic cylinder, sailing hardware
- **Era:** Tom's consulting work, era unspecified here

### Anodized aluminum medical laser instrument
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Anodized aluminum medical laser instrument design"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate the engineer's mode of recombining existing materials for new applications. Tom and J&J obtained a patent; Tom received his hourly consulting rate (about four hours' worth).
- **Materials/systems:** anodized aluminum, medical laser surgical instrument
- **Era:** unspecified

### LIGO project (gravity-wave detection)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "LIGO construction and welding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's primary illustrative case for the section "where is the best engineering at MIT done?" — used to make the point that the physics department, not engineering proper, hosts MIT's most ambitious engineering project. Tom focuses on the surveying-precision challenge (1–2 cm over 4 km, accounting for earth curvature) as engineering achievement.
- **Materials/systems:** Michelson interferometer, four-kilometer vacuum tubes, GPS-based survey instrumentation
- **Era:** project started ~1986, ongoing in 2015
- **Locations:** Livingston, Louisiana; Hanford, Washington (Tom misstates as two detectors per site; actually one per site — see editorial register)

### Manhattan Project / General Groves
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Manhattan Project infrastructure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate the eclipse of engineering credit by scientific credit. Tom notes Groves was an MIT civil engineer whose prior assignment was building the Pentagon; argues the Manhattan Project was as much engineering (building Oak Ridge, Los Alamos in weeks) as physics.
- **Era:** late 1930s–1945

### Liberty ships (production speed)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as an example of WWII engineering achievement (two-week keel-to-launch) that competed with the physicists' Manhattan Project for postwar prestige.
- **Era:** WWII

### Panama Canal / Suez Canal / D-Day artificial harbors
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_ids:** "Panama Canal", "Suez Canal construction", "D-Day artificial harbors"
- **Frame in this lecture:** S.L.A. Marshall's Hoover Medal address invoked these as the engineering achievements that justified the medal's award to Eisenhower. Tom cites the 72-million-ton earthmoving figure for Panama as concrete scale.
- **Era:** late 19th c. (Panama, Suez); 1944 (D-Day)

### MIT Foundry / Building 35 (Metals Processing)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: MIT Building 35 Metals Processing construction (John Wulff)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom notes John Wulff built Building 35 after being exiled from physics to metallurgy. Brief institutional-history anchor.
- **Era:** post-1930s

### Slocum hydroelectric / desalination concept (Hawaii collaboration)
- **Anchor:** `§10.p4`, `§10.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Alexander Slocum hydroelectric-desalination integration concept"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as an example of large-scale engineering-for-clean-water work. The ideal head height for hydroelectric (500–700 m, ~1000–1200 psi) matches the optimal pressure for reverse osmosis desalination — a coincidence Slocum proposes to exploit. Two draft papers (July 31, 2015) to be assigned reading.
- **Era:** 2015
- **Note:** unclear whether this concept recurs elsewhere in the corpus; flagged as PROPOSED.
