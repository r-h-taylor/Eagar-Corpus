## §1. Review: the three Ds and the Venn diagram of cracking [00:02]

`§1.p1` This is just the review of where we were. We talked about the three Ds yesterday, and you want to be at least above 50 degrees Fahrenheit before you start — well, not to heat the steel, just to remove the layer of moisture that you can't see. If you get down to frost temperatures you'll see the ice form on something. But at 33 degrees there's actually a layer of moisture on the metal that you might even be able to feel with your finger but you can't see, because it's just a water barrier. Once it freezes you can see the frost. There's a layer of moisture even at 40 degrees, and it's more moisture than you really should be welding through.

`§1.p2` Between 100 and 300 degrees F, typically we're just trying to slow down cooling and keep the thing in that 100 to 300 degree range. That will accelerate the hydrogen evolution — bubbles of hydrogen coming out at the surface — and that allows the hydrogen to escape. If you take this Venn diagram, and I hope you've noticed I've been doing them consistently, the three circles are stress, hardness, and hydrogen content. Other people might order them differently, but for this class I've been keeping stress at the top, then microstructure and hydrogen content.

`§1.p3` So what are you doing on the first D? Removing hydrogen. On the second one we're trying to shrink the microstructure circle. On the third, if you're above 300 degrees you get tempering, which reduces the hardness, and you're also letting the hydrogen escape even faster. So you're actually reducing two circles. You haven't changed the residual stresses, okay.

`§1.p4` This is a tempering curve I showed you before. If you take a piece of steel and you quench it to its maximum hardness — for a 1010 steel the maximum hardness, which is a function of carbon content, might be Rockwell C 37 to 38. Get above 150 C, 300 degrees F, and the thing starts to soften by tempering. You temper between 300 and 600 degrees Fahrenheit. You avoid 600 to 1100, because you get something called temper embrittlement in steels. This isn't a metallurgy-of-steels course — this is supposed to be a welding course — but eventually we're going to see that we do post weld heat treatment up in the 1100 to 1200 degree range.

## §2. Post weld heat treatment: what it does and what it costs [02:57]

`§2.p1` Post weld heat treatment is another process we might do for difficult-to-weld steels, where we heat them in the 1100 to 1200 degree Fahrenheit range, typically for one hour per inch of thickness. You measure the temperature on the surface — you put it in a furnace, and when the surface gets up to 1100 degrees, you then need to soak it so the temperature on the inside gets there. It's like a roast beef in the oven — you might get it done on the outside, the inside still rare. So you need about one hour per inch of thickness, with the thermal conductivity of steel, to allow the center to heat up. I want medium roast beef, not rare or rusty.

`§2.p2` So for something between one and three inches in thickness, one hour per inch of thickness is about right. You start going to 10 or 12 inch thick steel, and it turns out you can sort of get away with a half hour per inch of thickness. Nowadays they have little computer programs that will tell you exactly how long it takes to get the inside up to a reasonable temperature, but in the old days, one rule of thumb you'll often hear is one hour for each inch of thickness. That only applies in the couple-of-inches range; above that you can do a little quicker.

`§2.p3` You temper to lower the hardness — that's what this temperature range does. You're not losing the martensitic structure, but you're getting to be relatively soft compared to the maximum hardness, and that's going to be good in terms of lowering the hardness circle. You're also relieving the residual stresses. The steel at 1100 to 1200 degrees — this is called stress relief — actually loses about half of its yield strength when you get to 1100, 1200 degrees. That means the steel can start to deform and relieve those locked-in stresses. So a piece of steel that, if you quenched it and cut off the top layer, it might warp because you took off the tensile or compressive surface layer — if you put it in 1100 to 1200 degrees and then machine it, it will stay flat. It's been stress-relieved.

`§2.p4` You're also removing hydrogen big time, because at 1100, 1200 degrees the hydrogen is going to come out actively. So what you've done with a post weld heat treatment is reduce all three circles, so you get no overlaps. Remember, the overlap is what causes the danger of cracking in our little diagram. So those are the reasons for post weld heat treatment.

`§2.p5` Now let me tell you, post weld heat treatment is not cheap. Post weld heat treatment will usually cost as much as welding the part to begin with. It isn't hard if you've got a part the size of this table, and a furnace bigger than that. When you stick it in the furnace in air, you might get a little oxidation. You can clean that off with a sander, or you can peen the surface and knock the scale off, or you can paint over it. It's not a thick oxide, it's not a terribly bad oxide. It's a black oxide that's fairly adherent — it's what we call black iron piping.

## §3. Stress relief on very large pressure vessels [07:02]

`§3.p1` So stress relieving is not too bad on something the size of this table. But what about a pressure vessel the size of this room? It's a bit of a problem unless you have the furnace. Some of the pressure vessel shops do — there are heat treatment furnaces at a pressure vessel shop that could be 20 foot square by 30 feet long. If you go to Cooper Heat — Cooper Heat has been purchased by something else, but for many years they were a big company around the world doing this — they had a picture of a 118-foot long pressure vessel that would be stood up as part of an oil refinery. Probably 3 or 4 inch thick steel, probably going to have 100 atmospheres of gas in it, 1100 degrees or so. It's thick-walled, all welded construction, and you're going to have all kinds of residual stresses. If you don't do something about the residual stresses, you have problems. You can have fatigue problems because the fatigue's already got a head start — you might apply some other stress in service, but you've already kind of given it a head start with tensile residual stresses in some locations.

`§3.p2` Cooper Heat will come to your site for a fee, and they will build a furnace around your part. For the 118-foot long vessel they had little railroad tracks, and they had a box furnace 118 feet long full of insulation around it. In other cases, they make it a local stress relief. You have a nozzle in a pipe coming off a big pressure vessel and you just have to do the nozzle. They actually have heating blankets, which are little ceramic pieces with wires in them, and a power supply bigger than a welding power supply — pump a couple of thousand amps to that resistance heater and heat the whole thing up locally. Put a bunch of refractory blankets around it and locally heat treat it.

`§3.p3` There are all kinds of rules on local heat treatment, because local heat treatment somewhere on the part is going to get into this intermediate range where you get temper brittleness. You don't like to be in the 600 to 1000 degree tempering range. You're going to be there if you're doing local heat treatment — obviously something's cold back here, something is in between — and you could damage your steel, depending on the type of steel, by doing local stress relief. So sometimes you have to stress relieve the whole vessel. There is really no pressure vessel so large that it can't be put in a furnace for stress relief. There are some vessels that are so large that we can't afford to do it.

## §4. The nuclear submarine: stress relief by the first deep dive [10:35]

`§4.p1` One pressure vessel that is so large that we can't do a thermal stress relief is a nuclear submarine. We're talking three, four hundred feet long, thirty feet in diameter. And even if you could have a furnace that big, it's already got a few other things inside that don't want to go up to 1100, 1200 degrees. You're going to destroy the insides of the ship. So you can't really put it in a furnace. With submarine steel, you'd have to do local stress relief, and you have to worry about what you're doing to everything else. The problem with submarine steel is you've already heat treated it to get maximum strength, and you want that strength. For pressure vessels — not saying we don't use quench-and-tempered steels for pressure vessels, we do — but for a nuclear submarine you're basically using all that strength for the strength of the vessel, to be able to dive.

`§4.p2` So they don't use thermal stress relief, but they do stress relieve these welds — they do it with another type of energy. What type of energy would you use? Mechanical. The first deep dive. The first time you dive that submarine and go a little extra deep, deeper than you want the regular officers of the Navy to take it, you're going to do a proof load test, and you're going to take it down deep enough that you will mechanically relieve the residual stresses. We don't do it at temperature, we do it mechanically, by taking it down very low in the ocean. I can't tell you how low — that would give you some idea of what the maximum depth capability is, which is classified. It's classified because then they would know how to set their depth charges and things like that.

`§4.p3` A third reason for this is actually more operational. You can't go maximum speed at maximum depth, because if anything goes wrong and you all of a sudden start going down, you can get a little too deep too quick. So your maximum speed in a submarine is at some intermediate depth, above the maximum. You're walking on the edge.

`§4.p4` But the best quality control technique I know they use on nuclear subs is that the top management of the shipyard has to go on that first deep dive. They may not have made the welds, but they're responsible for them. And yes, some Navy people also have to go. You invite some VIPs to your first deep dive — and this is a very good way of ensuring quality control in the shipyard. This is where the rubber meets the road in terms of quality control. The top management gets paid the big bucks to put their life on the line for the quality of the product they just saw. The Navy thinks it's a good way to make sure that's a very good weld they made on those hulls, okay.

## §5. When do you post weld heat treat? The code rules [14:51]

`§5.p1` Some of you yesterday said you like the stories. You have to remember the stories are good and they help you remember long term, but it's not as dense an information transfer. They sometimes have other information embedded in them, though.

`§5.p2` So one type of question is: when do you post weld heat treat? When do you have high enough residual stresses — when does that circle get big enough? I mentioned that somewhere around an inch or two in thickness you can have residual stresses. In the boiler pressure vessel code, which is the ASME code for building pressure vessels, they do not require stress relief below 1-1/2 inches thick, except for certain types of weld geometries. Some welds at an inch and a quarter you don't have to stress relieve, some at an inch and a quarter you do, at 1-1/2 inches you pretty much always have to. So it depends on the geometry as well, and they have these rules. It's UCS-56, I think it's the table — UCS means unfired carbon steel, table 56 of the section on unfired pressure vessels and carbon steel.

## §6. The Helms project: building the penstock [16:06]

`§6.p1` So what's the story on that? Well, the story is the Helms project in California. I'm from California, so you've heard of the Sierra Nevada mountains. And you've heard of Pacific Gas and Electric, the big utility on the west coast. PG&E around the late eighties decides — they have all these nuclear plants they built on earthquake fault zones and things like that, just for safety — they decided they need something to store the energy. The nuclear reactor likes to produce energy 24 hours a day at a certain level. It doesn't like to be started up and slowed down. Slow and steady wins the race — the tortoise. Nuclear reactors are tortoises that just keep on plugging along. But people like to come home at 5 or 6 o'clock, start fixing dinner and turn the air conditioner on, so there's a peak in usage each evening around five. There's another peak in the morning before they go to work. Usage of electricity is not uniform.

`§6.p2` They like to level that out so the nuclear reactors can work the way they like to work. So they decided to build a pumped water storage project. We have pumped water storage projects in western Massachusetts. Any place you've got a hill and water coming down, you can build a pumped water storage project. And they've been building these in California for a hundred years. They call the pipes penstocks.

`§6.p3` A penstock is just a pipe coming down the side of the mountain. If I've got a hydroelectric plant — water up here, turbines down here — I have to connect them with a pipe, and that pipe is called a penstock. Some of these are pretty big. In this case there was a lake already existing in the Sierra Mountains at about eight or nine thousand feet, and another lake at about 6,000 feet, and they needed to build a penstock between the two. For the energy requirements and the size of the lakes, they decided this should be a 22-foot-diameter pipe. That's a pretty good size. 22 foot diameter pipe, 188 psi water, about 7,000 feet long. Most of this penstock, they just blasted a tunnel right out of the rock and lined it with steel and concrete and built this thing — it's supported, you don't have to have lots of structural support for it. But there was this one canyon they had to cross, about 300 feet long, plus another 160 feet, something like that.

`§6.p4` Nature didn't give them rock to support it across the canyon. So they came out of one tunnel, across the canyon, into another tunnel, and they had to build a 22 foot diameter pressure vessel that would not be supported anywhere else. It was convenient to put a manhole at the bottom of this 22-foot diameter pipe, so when it was drained they could take the manhole cover off, put a ladder up there, and somebody could crawl up in there and walk around. So they were welding this 22-foot diameter pipe up in the mountains. You don't just put that on a truck and carry it up to the mountains, because the roads aren't there — you've got to build it on site. Field-fabricated 22-foot diameter pipe. This particular pipe section, with the manway — the reason I remember it — was about 140 or 160 feet across the canyon. Some sections were 40 feet long, and as I remember there were three of those, with a 20 foot section in between.

## §7. The Helms project: the bad weld [20:50]

`§7.p1` So here we have granite mountain. On the bottom of this there's a little manhole where someone can crawl up in there, down about the six o'clock position. They built this thing on site. They rolled the plates down in Fresno, in some big shop, took the sections of rolled curved plate up the mountain, set up, and started welding them together. It's easiest to do vertical welds — won't bust your lunch to do them well. So they stood up the pieces, several of them, welded them together. They cut a hole in here for the manway nozzle. And it was getting to be winter — the snows had started in October, it was now mid-November. They were going to shut the whole thing down just after Thanksgiving. They wanted to shut it down before Thanksgiving, but they were a little behind schedule. And they couldn't keep it going all winter because there would be 20 feet of snow there by December.

`§7.p2` So they had the guys welding inch-and-a-quarter-thick fillet welds on the nozzle. The fillet weld looked something like this — this is the flange and this is the pipe coming down. Big enough — let's stick weld it in the field. So there's some hydrogen in the air. It's a pressure vessel steel, but it's sort of a mild steel, medium carbon, needs some 3D. So they preheated it. This was actually another weld up here where it goes to the top of the pipe, and they were welding in the overhead position. They preheated it — well, we had pictures showing three feet of snow on top of this thing, on the inside of this pipe, on the horizontal portion. It must have been preheated to 100 degrees, because you had three feet of snow to get rid of, right? Well, the supervisors had told their families they were going to be gone for Thanksgiving. So they left in mid-November and left the welders to do the work themselves.

`§7.p3` So they didn't bother to shovel the snow off the inside. It was a lot of work — you preheated it on Friday and you're welding on Monday, right? They just welded this thing with snow on top of it, an inch and a quarter away. I don't think they got very good preheat. They're welding with stick electrodes, and who knows, maybe they're not even drying the stick electrodes — there's no supervisor around to check, right? They're just finishing up, and the sooner they can finish, the better chance they get to be home for Thanksgiving. So it's not as if they're trying to do the best job, or anyone's looking over their shoulders. They welded the thing, got out of there before Thanksgiving. The whole thing got frozen at about minus 20 for the rest of the winter season. They came up in the spring, snows started to melt around April, mounted this thing across the canyon, and they loaded it up. 188 psi.

## §8. The Helms project: the failure [24:45]

`§8.p1` 20 hours later — there are some workmen in the little canyon, and they have a little porta-potty. A guy coming out of the porta-potty just finishes his business, he's looking about 50 yards ahead at this crossing, and he hears a big crack. He sees water streaming out, and he starts heading for high ground. He makes it, along with a couple friends, but in the meantime the whole thing blows apart.

`§8.p2` They were supposed to post weld heat treat it. They didn't post weld heat treat the living body. So they had plenty of residual stresses. They hadn't done the post weld heat treatment. It probably had plenty of hydrogen because they didn't preheat properly, and it had a hard microstructure because they didn't preheat properly. So they did about everything wrong you can imagine.

`§8.p3` And here's the thing — it had gotten to 188 psi, didn't crack for 20 hours. So that's what I call delayed cracking — hydrogen has to diffuse and get to the place. Even though this is nine months later, this is the oldest hydrogen crack from welding. You've got to remember, they froze it for most of the winter at minus 20. The hydrogen can't get out at minus 20. It doesn't do a lot of damage because it's stuck, but it can't get out either. Then it warms up, and you stress it at the applied stresses, and wait 20 hours — I think it was 20 hours, more than eight hours — and then it ruptures. The thing came apart.

`§8.p4` They had a valve up at the top lake to shut it off, except it must be a 22-foot diameter valve, and they didn't have anybody at the top lake to operate it. So these guys hustled up there in their pickup truck on dirt roads, and when they got there it took half an hour to close it. In that half hour the upper lake dropped 50% of its height, and the water's rushing down the canyon, coming out of the broken end at 188 psi, 22 foot diameter, 4 million horsepower. Hits the granite wall, erodes it away a hundred feet back. They just made a 140-foot canyon into a wider area. In the meantime, the water has to go down the canyon, through the woods, and end up in the 6,000 foot lake.

`§8.p5` A few bears and raccoons and other people were very upset by this. It disturbed their habitat quite a bit. It also built a beautiful sandbar beach about a hundred yards down at the lower lake. But it's all about failure to preheat, post weld heat treat, and hydrogen. PG&E was the owner. The company that did it was American Bridge, a division of U.S. Steel. They were the contractors back in those days.

## §9. The Helms project: trial and the price of expertise [28:23]

`§9.p1` Now I'll tell you the rest of the story. It went to trial in Fresno, California in the early 90s. Why did it go to trial? It was a $100 million loss on an $800 million project. Pacific Gas and Electric really didn't want to take it to trial, but they wanted to recover the money. They went to the California Public Utilities Commission and said, we want our $100 million that it cost to repair this thing as part of our $800 million project, and we want to put it in the rate base, and this will raise everybody's electricity rates by some tens of a cent. And the Public Utilities Commission said: if you say it was a defective weld, and if you don't take American Bridge to court to sue them and get your money back, we will not allow this $100 million in the rate base.

`§9.p2` So here's a hundred-million-dollar extortion that the Public Utilities Commission is giving to PG&E. PG&E would have been delighted to just forget about the lawsuit and put it in the rate base and spread the peanut butter all over all the citizens of California. But the Public Utilities Commission is there protecting citizens. So they go to trial. Six-week trial, I was the last witness. PG&E lost. They go back to Public Utilities Commission and say, well, the jury decided it was a foundational problem — our supports, foundations for the support pipe were inadequate, they shifted, and it had nothing to do with the weld quality. American Bridge argued that — in my opinion they were wrong, but some of the highest-paid metallurgists in the world at the time worked on it.

`§9.p3` At the time I was charging like $150 an hour. The top-notch metallurgist from this big outfit located in Palo Alto, their experts were getting $275, almost double. I used to go around saying: well, if you get $275 an hour for the wrong answer, what's the right answer worth? That's when I learned a lot, 25 years ago, about how to bill. You give someone the right answer, it's worth a lot of money. You give the wrong answer, it's not worth two cents.

`§9.p4` We lost. This big-shot metallurgist from this thousand-person consulting firm had said, on the second day of the inspection, three days after the failure, something about it being American Bridge's fault. Good idea to start slandering them in the paper after only 72 hours. So they not only didn't have to pay $100 million, they received $17 million in punitive damages for slander, because the metallurgist hired by PG&E to investigate was on site and basically made a press announcement.

## §10. Knowing where to put the X: Steinmetz and Wulff [32:55]

`§10.p1` So the John Wulff story about charging fees — actually, my favorite is the story of Steinmetz. Does anyone know who Steinmetz was? Steinmetz was a great mathematician who basically learned how to use imaginary numbers to explain what AC electricity does. He worked for General Electric, but he also consulted. Brilliant man. Around 1900, Westinghouse or somebody put in this big generator at General Electric, and they hired Steinmetz as a consultant. For two days he asked for seven drawings and a chair, and he sat in front of this huge electrical generator with the drawings, trying to understand why this thing wasn't working. They had just installed it, and it wasn't working. Finally on the second day he asked for a piece of chalk. With the chalk he drew a little box and put an X in it. He said: cut the steel case open here, cut this connection in the copper windings of the generator, seal it back up, and that will fix it. They did, and it worked. General Electric was very happy, and the people of New York now had lights.

`§10.p2` Steinmetz sent a bill to General Electric for $1,000. In 1902 or 1910, whatever it was, a thousand dollars was a lot of money. And the chairman of General Electric wrote back to Steinmetz and said, well, we thank you for fixing the problem, but how can you justify a thousand dollar bill? Itemize it. Steinmetz writes back: chalk, one dollar. Knowing where to put the X, 999 dollars. So it's what you know that you're hiring when you get a consultant.

`§10.p3` John Wulff, late 40s, early 1950s, gets a call one morning. Boston Edison, who generates electricity, had just had all the stainless steel in one of their electrical generating plants fall apart overnight by corrosion. John Wulff gets the call and says, I know what your problem is, but I'm not going to tell you the answer unless you agree to a $5,000 fee. Well, $5,000 in 1950 was like two or three years' salary for an engineer. A lot of money. They said, well, if you're right, we'll pay.

`§10.p4` John Wulff was an interesting old guy. When I was a freshman he had the office where I met him for the first time, and I was taking his class. He was a crusty old guy. In World War II he knew there was something going on at MIT that involved uranium, but he didn't know what it was, because he was German, and they didn't let anybody of German or Japanese extraction know anything about certain things. But John Wulff had been studying trace metals in different ores in the world, and he knew there was something interesting. At the departmental cocktail party reception one night, he said, you know, there's a lot of uranium in the oil in the Balkans. And the next morning there were two FBI agents in his office asking him what he knew about uranium. So he was right — there was something about uranium going on, and part of the Manhattan Project was here. Morris Cohen and John Chipman were working on things.
