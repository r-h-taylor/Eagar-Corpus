# Case Index — Lecture 14 (Welding Metallurgy, Spring 2014)

This lecture is dominated by one long forensic case (the Helms penstock failure, §§6–9) that Tom uses to motivate the day's technical content on post weld heat treatment, preheat, and delayed hydrogen cracking. Three shorter cases appear: the submarine first-deep-dive practice as a teaching counterpoint to thermal stress relief (§4); the Steinmetz GE generator story as a consulting-fee parable (§10); and the John Wulff / Boston Edison Venezuelan-oil corrosion case, which is set up in §10 but truncated when the recording cuts off.

## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Helms project penstock failure
- **Anchors:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`, `§7.p1`, `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`, `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`, `§8.p3`, `§8.p4`, `§8.p5`, `§9.p1`, `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`, `§9.p4`, `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Helms Project penstock"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Full forensic case study. PG&E pumped-storage project in the Sierra Nevada; American Bridge (division of U.S. Steel) field-fabricated a 22-foot diameter, inch-and-a-quarter wall penstock across a granite canyon. Welders skipped preheat in winter (welding inch-and-a-quarter steel with three feet of snow inside the pipe), skipped post weld heat treatment, and after frozen storage and spring pressurization the vessel failed by delayed hydrogen cracking 20 hours after reaching 188 psi service pressure. Released the upper lake, scoured 100 feet of granite, $100M loss on $800M project. Six-week trial in Fresno (early 1990s); jury found for American Bridge on a foundational-failure theory; Tom testified as the last witness.
- **Materials/systems:** medium-carbon pressure vessel steel, inch-and-a-quarter wall, stick-welded fillet welds on manway nozzle; 22 ft diameter; 188 psi service pressure
- **Era:** failure ~1982; litigation early 1990s
- **Related canonical clusters:** "PG&E v. American Bridge lawsuit (Fresno, California)", "American Bridges slander case", "Hydrogen cracking in welded steel", "Nine-month delayed hydrogen crack (High Sierra mountains)"

### Submarine first-dive stress relief
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Submarine first deep dive stress relief mechanism"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as the teaching counterpoint to thermal post weld heat treatment. A nuclear submarine pressure hull is too large for furnace stress relief and contains heat-sensitive internals; instead, mechanical stress relief on the first deep dive accomplishes the same end. Tom adds the cultural point that shipyard top management goes on the first deep dive — quality control via skin-in-the-game.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-80 / HY-100 quench-and-tempered submarine hull steel
- **Era:** general practice
- **Related canonical clusters:** "US Navy submarine first-dive stress relief", "Nuclear submarine hull steel (HY-80)"

### Steinmetz GE generator consultation

### 118-foot pressure vessel (Cooper Heat)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "118-foot pressure vessel stress relief"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief illustration. Cooper Heat builds a 118-foot box furnace on rails around an oil-refinery pressure vessel for site stress relief. Used to make the point that there is no vessel so large it can't be put in a furnace, only vessels too expensive to do so.
- **Era:** unspecified; ongoing industry practice

## Figures referenced

- **One hour per inch of thickness** (§2.p1) — PWHT soak time rule of thumb for steel below ~3 inches.
- **1100–1200 °F** (§2.p1, §2.p3, §2.p4) — standard PWHT temperature range for carbon steel.
