# Case Index — Lecture 1PMeu_ZDa0M

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is structured around productivity through the ages — from one person-year per ton of iron in 1600 to twenty minutes per ton today. Tom anchors the argument with a historical narrative (English wood/iron/glass crisis, 1530–1690) and a series of modern industrial cases (continuous casting, zero-rework manufacturing, mini-mills).

## Cases referenced

### 1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal
- **Anchor:** `§4.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's central historical case for the "revolution of necessity." The 1558 law forbade felling trees for iron-making but exempted the Weald of Kent — Tom's example of how "even the lobbyists worked back in 1558." Extended in §4.p8 with the 1581 act on Thames-river and coastal trees, and §4.p9 with the 1615 royal proclamation lamenting the loss of shipbuilding-grade oak.
- **Era:** 1558–1615.

### British iron industry wood shortage crisis
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "British iron industry wood shortage crisis"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The driver of the 1558 law and of the eventual switch to coal. The conflict among iron-making, shipbuilding, and glassmaking for limited oak forest. Sets up the transition from charcoal to sea coal to mined coal, which Tom then connects to steam engines and thermodynamics.

### HMS Victory (Nelson's flagship)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "HMS Victory (Nelson's flagship)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 2100 tons of wood per ship — datum supporting the argument that warship construction was consuming forests at the same rate as the iron industry.

### USS Constitution (Old Ironsides)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`, `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Constitution (Old Ironsides)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Materials selection example — white oak (probably live white oak) from North Carolina. Tom uses it to make the point that you "selected different woods for different applications," and that the same oak is still in demand today for *Mayflower II* restoration and Navy minesweeper construction.

### Old Ironsides oak wood repair delay
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Old Ironsides oak wood repair delay"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Modern echo of the historical wood crisis — restoration projects (Old Ironsides and *Mayflower II*) delayed by shortage of North Carolina oak. The *Mayflower II* repair was reportedly delayed two years.

### Naval minesweeper wood construction requirement
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`, `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval minesweeper wood construction requirement"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Magnetic-mine sensors require non-metallic hulls; minesweepers are still built from the same North Carolina oak.

### Saugus Ironworks
- **Anchor:** `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** New England blast furnace built in the 1630s to solve Britain's energy crisis by exporting iron from a region that still had forests. The structural answer to the 1558 law.

### Jamestown glassworks
- **Anchor:** `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Jamestown glassworks"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Parallel American case — a glass furnace built in Virginia for the same reason (forests available, clean fuel for glassmaking).

### Corning float glass dual-surface manufacturing / Pilkington float glass process
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pilkington float glass process"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Pilkington's 1920s development of floating molten glass on tin to eliminate the half-mile polishing line used by Pittsburgh Plate Glass. Tom's example of how a process innovation can collapse manufacturing cost.

### Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long facility
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long facility"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The pre-Pilkington benchmark — glass plate produced on a mill that ran half a mile to a mile straight down for polishing.

### Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Voest-Alpine in Austria as the developer of continuous casting for steel in the 1950s and 1960s. (Note: Tom is using Voest-Alpine here specifically for the continuous-caster story; the canonical cluster for the BOF process is the closest match in the aggregate.)

### Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`, `§8.p4`, `§8.p5`, `§8.p6`, `§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The core teaching case of the lecture. Tom contrasts ingot casting (sixty-seven percent yield at Bethlehem Steel in the mid-1980s) with continuous casting (ninety-five percent yield), explains the 1968 Armco Steel decision to install ingot casters anyway, and frames the late-1970s retrofits as the moment US steel managers finally accepted what they should have known a decade earlier.

### Armco Steel continuous casting decision
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Armco Steel continuous casting decision"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1968 — Armco installs ingot casters when continuous casting was available, on grounds of "no risk." Tom's emblematic example of risk-aversion in mid-1960s US steel management.

### US Steel industry decline and management failure
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`, `§8.p4`, `§8.p6`, `§8.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Steel industry decline and management failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The broader management-failure case the continuous-casting story illustrates. Tom names Kim — the future Harvard Business School dean — as having defended the decision in retrospect, framing this as evidence that "business managers can rationalize stupidity."

### Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The world's last integrated steel plant built by a company — five to six billion dollars, 1965–1970, with ingot casters that had to be ripped out and replaced with continuous casters in the mid-1970s. Almost bankrupted Bethlehem.

### Japanese steel industry dominance 1970s
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Japanese steel industry dominance 1970s"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Japanese installed continuous casters and went from six hours a ton to one and a half hours a ton — the competitive threat that eventually forced US retrofits.

### US steel industry productivity transformation (1980-1990)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p7`, `§8.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US steel industry productivity transformation (1980-1990)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Productivity from 150 to nearly 300 pounds per man-hour over the 1980s, with employment dropping from 500,000 to 250,000 — Tom's "threat of extinction sharpens the mind" lesson.

### Motorola Boynton Beach pager manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`, `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Motorola Boynton Beach pager manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The zero-rework story. Manager brings PCB production from Singapore to Florida, designs the plant for zero rework, and the engineers deliver near-zero rework by fixing the process rather than building a repair area. Tom's prototype for the "self-fulfilling prophecy" argument about defect tolerance.

### Ford Mustang assembly line non-start defect
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`, `§9.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ford Mustang assembly line non-start defect"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Parallel case to Motorola. New manager bans tow-motors from the end of the line — if a car doesn't start, push it. Within months, all cars start. Tom's lesson on management of manufacturing.

### POSCO (Korea) government-built steel mill surpassing Nippon Steel
- **Anchor:** `§10.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "POSCO (Korea) government-built steel mill surpassing Nippon Steel"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1972 — President Park puts Colonel Park in charge of building POSCO with US Steel's help. Thirty years later POSCO is the world's largest. Tom's evidence that integrated steel plants are now built by countries, not companies.

## Figures referenced (not cases)

- **Productivity of steel/nail making over time:** 1 person-year per ton (1600s) → ~50–60 pounds per person-hour (1925) → 6 person-hours per ton (1980) → ~3–4 person-hours per ton (1990) → 0.3 person-hours per ton (~2013). The 18-fold improvement in 25 years (§10.p5) is the lecture's culminating statistic.
- **Yield in ingot vs. continuous casting:** 67% (Bethlehem Steel mid-1980s, ingot) vs. 95% (continuous) — §8.p4, §8.p5.
- **US steel production 1980s:** ~75 million tons/year (production) vs. ~100 million tons (consumption), with the gap filled by imports. §8.p2.
- **US manufacturing vs. mining productivity gains, 1980s:** 4% annual (manufacturing) vs. 6% annual (mining). §8.p8.
- **Workforce in US agriculture:** 97% (1790s) → less than 3% (present). §3.p4, §6.p1.
- **HMS Victory wood:** 2100 tons. §4.p2.
- **London population:** 60,000 → 500,000+ (1530–1690), eightfold in 160 years. §3.p2.
- **England/Wales population:** 3 million → 6 million (1530–1690). §3.p2.
- **Wood-price increase, 1615:** "a penny to two shillings." §4.p8.
