## Cases referenced

### HIP vessel explosion (16-ton fragment ejection)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`, `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pratt & Whitney / General Electric jet engine disc HIP vessel failure" (likely match) — also overlaps with "Hot isostatic pressing vessel explosion" and "IMT hot isostatic press vessel failure" / "North Andover hot isostatic press explosion"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tail of a case from a prior session. The world's only HIP vessel capable of all aircraft-engine disc work; a sixteen-ton piece landed a quarter mile away in random direction. Saved from being a multi-billion-dollar problem because a second vessel was coming on stream in three months.
- **Materials/systems:** HIP pressure vessel, aircraft engine discs.

### Navy internally cooled recuperator (ICR)
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`, `§1.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Navy ship recuperator heat exchanger failure" (also "Naval ship recuperator heat exchanger failure")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Heat exchanger to preheat incoming air using stack gases on oil-fired surface ships, intended to raise cruise efficiency from ~10% to 30%, eliminating off-station refueling time. First built version lasted two minutes before tearing itself apart from transient thermal stresses. Eagar served on a Blue Ribbon panel investigating it.
- **Materials/systems:** Heat exchanger, oil-fired ship propulsion, thermal-stress design.

### Zone melting at Bell Labs (Pfann)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`, `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bell Labs zone melting semiconductor purification (1950s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Bell Labs technician Pfann invents zone melting in the 1950s to purify silicon and germanium to 6-9s (one part per million) for early semiconductors. Still the basis of high-purity silicon production.
- **Materials/systems:** Silicon, germanium, partition coefficient, semiconductor industry.
- **Era:** 1950s.

### Sea Wolf welding wire high-side chemistry / hydrogen cracking
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking" (also overlaps "SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem," "Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking," "Submarine shaft clink failure (1985)" and the broader "Seawolf-class submarine" cluster)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Two-billion-dollar Navy problem traced to welding wire drawn from an enriched (high-side-chemistry) region of a twenty-ton ingot. Yield strength came in at 140 ksi instead of 100 ksi; hydrogen sensitivity scaled accordingly. All within spec but at the wrong end of the range. Used to land the teaching point that microsegregation in the original casting propagates through downstream processing.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-100 / Sea Wolf welding wire, hydrogen embrittlement, segregation.

### Single-crystal turbine blade growth in gradient furnaces
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Single-crystal turbine blades (GE/Pratt & Whitney)" (also overlaps "Pratt & Whitney single-crystal turbine blade," "Turbine blade casting development at Pratt & Whitney," "Modern single-crystal turbine blade casting (core shift defect)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** GE and Pratt cast 16–24 blades per investment sprue; Rolls-Royce uses 4. Ten-hour pull through a 1000° gradient, $3M furnace per ~16–32 blades/day. Cost per blade ~$6,000 but justified by life-of-engine fuel savings. Used to teach that the steep gradient suppresses constitutional supercooling and gives planar-front solidification.
- **Materials/systems:** Nickel-base superalloy, investment casting, gradient furnace.

### Generator rotor forging (US Steel or Bethlehem Steel)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "French electrical generator rotor forging failure" (likely a different case) — **PROPOSED: US Steel / Bethlehem Steel generator rotor forging facility**
- **Frame in this lecture:** Six-story-tall forging facility, originally used for WWI battleship gun barrels. 450-ton ingot → 200-ton forging after oxide loss and machining. Only one such facility remaining in the US; Bethlehem's steel plant is now an amusement park.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-section forging, battleship gun barrels, generator rotors.
- **Era:** WWI through 2011.

### Cold-heading insert for particle-board furniture
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`, `§5.p5`, `§5.p7`, `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Cold heading for furniture fasteners" (also overlaps "Cold heading tool wear and surface cracking (pre-WWII)," "Post-WWII cold heading machine phosphate-stearate surface treatment," "Pittsburgh cold heading plant")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Stage-direction demonstration. Eleven-step cold-heading sequence: wire chop → size → extrude hole partway → extrude further → head → punch out waste → finishing size → knurl grip → thread inside → plate. Phosphate-stearate lubrication after WWII reduced die count and made it economical. 180 dB shops; seven heading strokes per second.
- **Materials/systems:** Cold heading, phosphate coating, stearate lubricant, steel wire.

### Buy-to-fly ratio (Air Force forging cost framing)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p10`, `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force Buy-to-Fly Ratio in Aircraft Manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Air Force framing: 11-lb finished part from 210-lb cold die forging (BTF ~20) vs. 72-lb hot die forging. At $100/lb alloy and BTF 20, you're paying $2,000/lb for flying metal before processing. Justifies near-net-shape investment.
- **Materials/systems:** Aerospace forging, near-net-shape processing.

### Isothermal forging — Pratt & Whitney
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`, `§6.p15`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Nickel-based turbine disc isothermal forging case study" (also overlaps "Titanium aircraft part isothermal forging case study," "Isothermal forging sample misappropriation")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Process developed by Pratt & Whitney based on 1938 German superplasticity work (lost in WWII). Dies heated to ~1000°C, slow squeeze (10–15 min), single set of dies. 250 lb → 15 lb conventional becomes 126 lb → 15 lb isothermal.
- **Materials/systems:** Nickel-base superalloy, turbine discs, heated dies.

### Backofen's rediscovery of superplasticity / Taunton candy-dish die
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`, `§6.p7`, `§6.p8`, `§6.p9`, `§6.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Backofen superplasticity rediscovery" (also covers "German superplastic tensile bar (1934)" and "German superplasticity research (1938, lost in WWII)" and "Backov aluminum-zinc candy dish forming")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Al Backofen and grad student Don Avery, early '60s, working titanium and zircaloy through phase transformations to ultrafine grain (1–2 μm). Found m (strain-rate sensitivity) goes up dramatically with fine grain; 1000% elongation possible. Backofen took an aluminum-zinc alloy to Taunton silversmiths, used a candy-dish die, and pulled in a single draw what would normally require four or five operations. Tom keeps the resulting sample as the Smithsonian-grade demonstration.
- **Materials/systems:** Bismuth-tin eutectic (German 1934 sample), aluminum-zinc (Backofen), titanium, zircaloy.

### Reynolds Aluminum origin (cigarette foil)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Reynolds Aluminum foil manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Reynolds founded an aluminum company to produce foil for packaging cigarettes — wanted moisture control for the tobacco product. Used as the why-foil-matters framing for pack rolling.

### Davenport Iowa Alcoa plate gauge variation / air knife
- **Anchor:** `§8.p7`–`§8.p15`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alcoa Davenport aluminum rolling mill thickness variation" (also "Alcoa air knife implementation failure" — these may be the same case under two cluster names; flag for reconciliation)
- **Frame in this lecture:** World's largest rolling mill, 8-ft × 15-ft rolls, rolling 747 wing plate. ~20% off-gauge plate. LFM student thesis ~1990 mapped the variation along plate length. Root cause: water from roll-cooling pooling on the curled end of hot plate, locally cooling and stiffening the aluminum, producing thicker ends. Solution: a $10,000 compressed-air knife. Alcoa's NIH culture refused to implement. Tom bet Owen Richmond $10,000 it wouldn't happen; a year later he was right.
- **Materials/systems:** 7000-series aluminum plate, hot rolling, gauge measurement, roll cooling.
- **Era:** ~1990.

## Figures referenced

- **§1.p5** Fatigue gain from HIP: ~20% in stress → ~400% in life (high-cycle fatigue, slope of S-N curve).
- **§2.p4** 6-9s pure = 1 ppm; 7-9s = 0.1 ppm = 100 ppb.
- **§3.p3** Sea Wolf weld wire: 140 ksi yield (high-side chemistry) vs. 100 ksi (spec target).
- **§3.p5** Secondary dendrite arm spacing ~ (local solidification time)^(1/3); range from <10 μm rapid → ~mm slow.
- **§4.p2** Single-crystal blade gradient furnace: ~1000°C over 5–6 in., 10-hr pull, $3M furnace, 16–32 blades/day, ~$6,000/blade.
- **§5.p3** Newtonian m = 1; glass ~0.9; metals ~0.2; superplastic metals can reach m ~7.
- **§5.p10** Buy-to-fly ratio ~20 for cold die forging (210 lb → 11 lb); BTF ~6.5 for hot die forging (72 lb → 11 lb).
- **§8.p1** Roll-flattening limit: sheet thickness / roll diameter ~ 1/500 is the no-deformation asymptote.
