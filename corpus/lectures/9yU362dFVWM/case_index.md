# Case Index — Lecture 7 (Spring 2016)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§5.p2`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is structured as a recitation on materials substitution and competition. Three large case clusters anchor the teaching: refrigerator design, water conveyance, and gas conveyance. Several smaller cases surface in passing.

## Cases

### Allied Signal / Honeywell amorphous metal foil patent dispute
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Allied Signal amorphous metal foil patent dispute"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate how an inadequate NDA with a university collaborator (Professor Chad Graham, U. Penn) cost Allied Signal the patent on the magnetic-loss properties of their rapidly solidified material.
- **Materials/systems:** amorphous metal foil, rapidly solidified soft magnetic alloys
- **Era:** 1970s research; ongoing legal aftermath

### Rolls-Royce M250 blisk
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Rolls Royce M250 BLISK (bladed disk) for helicopters"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Reference example of integral blade-disc construction for weight savings on helicopter engines.
- **Materials/systems:** integrally bladed disc (blisk)
- **Era:** contemporary

### Pratt & Whitney single-crystal turbine blade
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`, `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pratt & Whitney single-crystal turbine blade"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Sample passed around. Used to teach hot-section cooling design (boundary-layer cooling, ceramic core casting, seven-thousandths wall thickness limit).
- **Materials/systems:** nickel-base single-crystal superalloy
- **Era:** blade from 1980s; teaching about contemporary limits

### Chrysler lightweight aluminum vehicle (engine size mismatch)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Chrysler lightweight aluminum vehicle design"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate that you can't get full weight-savings benefit from material substitution without redesigning the system (engine displacement) around it.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum sheet body, internal combustion engine
- **Era:** ~1991 (twenty-five years before Spring 2016)

### Aircraft landing gear as first design constraint (Boeing)

- **Materials/systems:** aircraft structure, keel beam

### Refrigerator plastic shelf failure / KitchenAid redesign
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`, `§5.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1980s refrigerator shelf failures" (matches "Refrigerator plastic shelf cracking" and "KitchenAid refrigerator successful design")

- **Materials/systems:** sheet steel (exterior), plastic (interior), threaded vs. slot joints
- **Era:** 1980s–1990s personal experience

### Lead pipe in residential plumbing (Boston area, Medford)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`, `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lead pipe in residential plumbing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Lead's atmospheric corrosion resistance and ease of forming made it the dominant water-pipe material from antiquity through 1920s–1930s. Tom's own 1938 house had a lead pipe. Forty-seven percent of Medford homes still have lead service piping.
- **Materials/systems:** lead pipe, copper replacement
- **Era:** 1920s–1930s installation; legacy through present

### Flint Michigan and Trail BC lead poisoning
- **Anchor:** `§7.p6`, `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Flint Michigan lead in drinking water" + "Lead exposure in Trail, British Columbia"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Real developmental-disability outcomes from lead exposure (Trail from zinc-smelting airborne lead; Flint from drinking water). Used in tension with Tom's view that EPA limits have over-tightened.
- **Era:** Trail: hundred-year history; Flint: contemporary (2014–2016 crisis)

### Kresge Auditorium lead roof

- **Materials/systems:** lead sheet roofing on reinforced concrete shell
- **Era:** MIT mid-20th-century architecture

### Lead-tin solder ban (1978) and Boston fire-watch regulation
- **Anchor:** `§7.p15`, `§7.p16`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lead-tin solder ban and plumbing failures (1978)" + "Boston hot-work fire-watch regulation and building cost impact"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Externalities of material selection. The 1978 ban shifted plumbers to 95-5 (tin-antimony) solder, which produced more leaky joints. Combined with Boston's hot-work fire-watch requirement (paid off-duty firefighter at sixty dollars/hour), this drove adoption of mechanical crimp-on copper fittings ("PEX-A" cross-linked polyethylene became dominant in new construction).
- **Materials/systems:** lead-tin solder → 95-5 solder; copper crimp fittings; PEX
- **Era:** 1978 legislation; 2000s fire-watch enforcement

### Boston cast iron water main failures
- **Anchor:** `§7.p17`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Cambridge water main floods from cast iron pipe failure" (closest match — same phenomenon, Boston rather than Cambridge)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cast iron pipe graphitizes (decarbonizes) over ~100 years, losing strength. Five percent of Boston's water lost through leaks; periodic main breaks cause ten-million-dollar downtown damage events.
- **Materials/systems:** cast iron water main, concrete-lined replacement
- **Era:** legacy infrastructure, contemporary failures

### US Navy nuclear submarine titanium piping
- **Anchor:** `§7.p18`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Navy nuclear submarine steel pipe corrosion (30-year ship life)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Carbon steel piping corroded out in ~25 years against the 30-year ship life. Tom's anecdote of the ensign whose entire shipboard duty was leak repair. Transition to titanium for 50-year hulls.
- **Materials/systems:** carbon steel → titanium piping
- **Era:** decades-long transition; contemporary 50-year ships

### Boeing 747-400 titanium catalytic converter welding (Englehard)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 747-400 Titanium Air Tubing and Catalytic Converter" (also matches "Boeing 747-400 catalytic converter titanium welding failure" and "Boeing 747-400 fuselage welding porosity issue")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended teaching case. Boeing's general spec required x-ray inspection of titanium welds with no flaw larger than ten thousandths of an inch. Englehard, accustomed to stainless steel cleanliness practices, was failing 25–50% of welds from hydrogen porosity caused by hydrocarbon contamination. Tom's diagnosis: reagent-grade acetone degreasing and white gloves. The case recurs: Englehard succeeded, then reverted to non-reagent-grade acetone and failed again. Tom flags an identical case at GE Lynn with electron beam welds in titanium.
- **Materials/systems:** Ti welding, hydrogen porosity, hydrocarbon cleaning
- **Era:** ~1986–1990

### Boston historical wooden gas pipes (gun-drilled logs)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boston wooden gas pipes (1880s street light system)" (also "Wooden gas distribution pipes")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Coal-gas streetlight infrastructure in 1800s cities. Logs were gun-drilled, mudded into the ground with oakum. Boston excavation work still occasionally uncovers these.
- **Materials/systems:** wood, oakum, coal gas
- **Era:** 1800s

### Corrugated stainless steel gas tubing lightning fires
- **Anchor:** `§9.p4`, `§9.p5`, `§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing" (also "Corrugated stainless steel gas pipe lightning strike fires" and "Flexible gas piping lightning strike fires")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Japanese-developed corrugated stainless steel tubing with polyethylene coating allows one-day gas plumbing of a new home (vs. three days for black iron pipe) at five dollars/foot. But pinhole "holiday" defects in the coating, while not a problem at 110V, are catastrophic against millions-of-volts lightning bolts. About a hundred home fires per year. The industry denies and admits it; half-a-billion-dollar annual profit keeps the product on market.
- **Materials/systems:** 0.010" stainless tube, polyethylene jacket, 30,000V dielectric rating
- **Era:** developed ~1980s Japan; deployed widely 1990s–present
