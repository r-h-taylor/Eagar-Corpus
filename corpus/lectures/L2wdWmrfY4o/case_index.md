# Case Index — Lecture L2wdWmrfY4o

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§5.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is a thematic and historical opener for "What is Engineering." It introduces framing concepts (Sprague's law, von Kármán's definition, Maslow's hierarchy, CAUS+E) and surfaces a number of cases in passing as illustrations rather than as developed teaching units.

## Cases referenced

### Sprague's law on material properties
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Sprague's law on material properties"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Robert Sprague, head of materials at GE Cincinnati: "whenever you first hear about a new material, write it down, because those are the best properties that material will ever have."
- **Related:** Williams's corollary on cost, same paragraph.

### Williams's law on material costs
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Williams's law on material costs"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Jim Williams' corollary to Sprague: first-quoted cost is the lowest cost a material will ever have. Tom positions Williams as a titanium metallurgist who succeeded Sprague at GE.

### Josephson effect discovery
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Josephson effect discovery"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate that engineers sometimes solve harder problems by ignorance — Josephson kept a term his professor would have thrown out, and discovered electron tunneling through insulating layers. Nobel Prize at 24.

### Margaret MacVicar niobium tunneling experiments
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`, `§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Margaret MacVicar niobium tunneling experiments"
- **Frame in this lecture:** MacVicar (Tom's thesis advisor) did the experimental confirmation of the Josephson effect at MIT — grew niobium single crystals, laid down insulating layer, did tunneling experiments in liquid helium. Tom uses the case to launch into MacVicar's career trajectory (UROP founder, Dean of Undergraduate Education, biology requirement, Exxon board, death at 41-42 of brain cancer) and to make the point that MIT physics wouldn't admit its own (the Feynman-to-Princeton aside).

### Edison's carbonized horsehair light bulb filament
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`, `§5.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Edison's carbonized horsehair light bulb filament"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate von Kármán's "engineer creates that which never was" — Edison created a working filament without understanding why horsehair worked. Lifespan ~11 hours.

### Incandescent light bulb development (Edison/Langmuir)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Incandescent light bulb development (Edison/Langmuir)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief reference. Edison's horsehair filament was superseded by tungsten; GE spent a hundred years perfecting tungsten light bulbs only to have LEDs come along. Used to make the point that engineering investments can be obsoleted by technology transitions.

### Space Shuttle O-ring failure
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle O-ring failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Feynman on the Rogers Commission. NASA's pre-flight probabilistic analysis claimed one-in-10,000 failure rate; Feynman pointed out four percent of missiles since 1947 had failed, and this was the 25th shuttle flight. Used to make a teaching point about how engineers should be skeptical of probabilistic risk analyses produced by experts paid to produce them.

### Space Shuttle cost overrun
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Space Shuttle cost overrun"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cargo bay sized for chemical laser weapon. Original promise was $1,000/lb to orbit; actual cost reached $50,000/lb over the 25-year program. Used as an example of how military programs are sold to Congress as civilian programs.

### MIT School of Engineering strategy retreat (circa 1990)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`, `§3.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "MIT School of Engineering strategy retreat (circa 1990)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 24 senior and junior faculty at a three-day Cape retreat trying to define engineering. Tom (chosen by Flemings) attended as the junior member. Output: a definition Tom still thinks was good, and which the assistant dean Donna Savicki could not locate ten years later. Used as both a meta-illustration of the definitional problem and as a parable about institutional memory loss.

### IBM summer materials science course (late 1970s)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "IBM summer materials science course (late 1970s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom and Sadoway teaching 40 IBM students "introductory materials science" — students ranged from PhDs in materials to EEs who didn't know what a material was. Lesson: go back to basics, which are simple, and everyone can find insight there.
