# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture 2019-09-10 (Additive Manufacturing, Fall 2019, video `80LMn6GeJDM`). This register records editorial judgments made in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition.*

## Sectioning

The lecture divides naturally into eight sections:

- **§1 Bias disclosure and the National Academies** (00:01–03:41). Tom's framing of his National Academy role and the conflict-of-interest convention that opens the lecture.
- **§2 Additive manufacturing as a fad** (03:41–09:07). Tom's bias disclosure proper, Digital Alloys advisory-board story, and the *Advanced Materials and Processes* magazine reading.
- **§3 Structural vs functional materials, and the billion-ton club** (09:07–16:07). Westbrook curve, Sprague/Williams quotes, billion-ton-per-year structural materials, brick-and-pottery as proto-AM.
- **§4 Concrete: reinforcement, chemistry, and curing** (16:07–24:12). Rebar, Roman concrete, light/heavy concrete, hockey rink failure case, specific properties Q&A.
- **§5 Energy as the value measure, and aluminum as canned electricity** (24:12–32:01). Historical economic anchors (food → steel → oil), Soviet aluminum dumping, scandium aside, transport-cost-per-pound framing.
- **§6 History and limits of additive manufacturing** (32:01–38:24). Surface tension problem, Sachs/Cima 3D printing invention, selective laser sintering, casting molds for hips and Super Bowl rings, pill manufacturing, steel-roll resurfacing as century-old AM.
- **§7 Codes, standards, and the MMPDS** (38:24–47:57). Digital Alloys guide, MMPDS-05 statistical requirements, Alcoa-vs-extruder Boeing wing spar lawsuit, Q&A on standard scope.
- **§8 Closing and preview** (47:57–end). Preview of tomorrow's investment-framing lecture.

Section break between §5 and §6 falls at the visible pivot from materials economics (volume-of-business figures) to AM-specific problems (surface tension). The layer 2 paragraph `p44` spans this transition; the back half of `p44` ("So why are we interested in advanced additive manufacturing of metals?...") is the start of §6.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could do this because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases that Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
