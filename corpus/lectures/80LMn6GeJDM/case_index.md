# Case Index — Lecture 2019-09-10

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§7.p8`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is Tom's opening session on additive manufacturing for Fall 2019. It surfaces a mixture of well-developed cases (the Alcoa/Boeing spar lawsuit, the Connecticut hockey rink) and brief mentions of recurring background cases (Macondo, Alvin, Soviet aluminum dumping). Several framing figures recur from other lectures.

### Soviet aluminum dumping and Alcoa/Norsk Hydro market collapse
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`–`§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet aluminum dumping and Alcoa/Norsk Hydro market collapse"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to explain why aluminum is "canned electricity" and why bulk-electric transmission limits drive the Soviet decision to export aluminum from Siberian hydropower. Affected Alcoa, Norsk Hydro, Alcan.
- **Era:** post-1985 (Gorbachev) through early 1990s.

### Connecticut hockey rink concrete failure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Connecticut hockey rink concrete failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Black polyethylene tubing in a Connecticut rink laid in August heat. Exothermic concrete cure plus solar-heated black tubing caused the tubing to collapse inside the concrete. Whole rink had to be rebuilt.
- **Teaching point:** Concrete cure is exothermic (~140°F); thick slabs need active cooling.

### 1922 additive manufacturing weld-buildup on cast iron
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1922 additive manufacturing weld-buildup on cast iron"
- **Frame in this lecture:** *Welding Journal* 1922 article showing weld bead buildup on cast iron, presented as the earliest modern example of metal additive manufacturing. Tom notes cracks at the edges.

### Alcoa v. aluminum extrusion manufacturer (Boeing wing spar data theft)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alcoa v. aluminum extrusion manufacturer (Boeing wing spar data theft)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Ongoing half-billion-dollar lawsuit. A small extrusion plant hired away workers from Alcoa's Lafayette, Indiana facility; ten years later they captured 45% of Boeing's wing-spar business. Tom is involved as expert; the contention is that the defendants could not have generated 100-sample/10-heat MMPDS data on their own. The case anchors Tom's teaching point that MMPDS qualification is so expensive it creates an incentive for data theft.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum extrusions, aircraft wing spars.

### Sachs/Cima 3D printing invention at MIT (PROPOSED)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`–`§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Sachs and Cima 3D printing invention (MIT, ~1990)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom credits Ely Sachs and Mike Cima with inventing the term "3D printing" by adapting an inkjet print head to spray binder on layered ceramic powder. Earliest applications: casting molds for artificial hips, Super Bowl/World Series rings (still at Tiffany's in Rhode Island), and pharmaceutical pill compounding.

### Selective laser sintering (Texas, ~1985) (PROPOSED)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Selective laser sintering invention (Texas, ~1985)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom identifies this as the first modern AM process — two intersecting laser beams polymerizing a liquid photopolymer at the intersection point. Used through the late 1980s for prototyping.

### Artificial hip replacement failures - early iterations
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Artificial hip replacement failures - early iterations"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Not the failure framing — here used positively, as an early AM application. 3D-printed ceramic molds for custom-fit titanium hips on $50,000 operations.
