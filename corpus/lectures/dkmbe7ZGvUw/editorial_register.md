# Editorial Register: Layer 2 → Layer 3

*Lecture 9 (Welding Metallurgy, Summer 2014). This register records editorial judgments made in lifting the cleanup edit to the readable edition.*

## Sectioning

The lecture divides naturally at eight teaching transitions:

- **§1 (00:05)** — Course logistics shading into the history-of-engineering excursus. The logistics and the history-of-engineering tangent are continuous in delivery (no clean break), so they're kept together.
- **§2 (07:18)** — Tom poses the "why metals?" question and pivots to ductility, with the undergraduate-disciplines survey inside it. The survey is part of the pivot, not a separable section.
- **§3 (11:01)** — Silly Putty and paper-tearing demonstrations. Distinct teaching unit driven by physical objects.
- **§4 (12:52)** — Liberty ships case and the 1946 report. Tom produces the bound report; new teaching unit.
- **§5 (16:27)** — Battleship armor weld sample. Tom produces a second physical object (the 18-inch weld) and tells the truck-wheel forging-press story.
- **§6 (18:25)** — The three postwar institutional studies (Weck/BWI, Pellini/NRL, Cohen-Averbach/MIT) plus the Navy 6.1/6.2 funding aside and Pellini's explosion bulge test.
- **§7 (26:22)** — Bessemer process, English wood-shortage energy crisis, Saugus Ironworks, beam theory.
- **§8 (31:18)** — Ashby plot and the closing argument for steel.

## Per-section editorial decisions

### §1
- Cut Tom's mid-paragraph hesitations on scheduling ("uh," "or maybe," "you know, we'll figure that out"). Kept the substantive content: the lecture plan, the July 4 / June 26 boundary.
- §1.p2: cut "people— presentations, your presentations" self-correction; reads "your presentations."
- §1.p7: the Texas-endowment paragraph contains Tom's slip "third richest school… second richest after Harvard." Preserved as "second richest" since that is what he settled on. The earlier "third" is captioner-ambiguous on Tom's spoken pivot; not bracketed because it's clearly a live self-correction.

### §2

Bracketed correction not used here because the slip is a stumble Tom corrected himself within the same breath; preserving it in L3 would mislead. Layer 2 preserves verbatim.

This is the only L3 paragraph in this lecture that draws from two L2 paragraphs; the merge is mechanical (cutting the dead time at the slide-advance) and does not reorder content.

### §7
- §7.p1: "back in the 1860s and 70s, people sort of learned" — the "and 60s and 70s" / "1850s and, or 60s and 70s" wobble Tom does in L2 p35 is smoothed to the form he settles on. Preserved verbatim in L2.
- §7.p3: the three uses of trees (charcoal for glass, charcoal for iron, ship masts) — Tom's first pass said "make charcoal to make glass" then "make cast iron." L3 makes the parallel construction explicit by inserting "make charcoal to make cast iron" — this is a clarification of grammatical parallel that Tom's spoken delivery elides. **Flagged as a borderline call: this adds two words ("make charcoal to") that Tom did not say in the second clause. Justified because the parallel structure is clearly what he meant — the first clause has "make charcoal to make glass," and Tom's second clause's "make cast iron" presupposes the same charcoal step. If the editor finds this overreaches, the L3 should read "Another was to make cast iron" instead.**

## Decisions NOT permitted at layer 3 (constitutional constraint)

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
