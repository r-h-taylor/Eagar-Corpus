# Case Index — Lecture 9 (Welding Metallurgy, Summer 2014)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is a thematic opener for the welding metallurgy arc: Tom argues for why structural materials are mostly metals, and why most of the metals course will be about steel. He grounds the argument in the Liberty ship brittle-fracture problem and the postwar institutional response.

## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Liberty ships and SS Schenectady
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`, `§4.p4`, `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Liberty ships and SS Schenectady`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Anchoring case for the entire brittle-fracture arc. Tom produces the 1946 Maritime Commission report; cites the 4,694-vessel / 970-casualty / 26-fatality figures; shows the famous Schenectady-at-dock and SS Manhattan-at-sea photographs; attributes the failures to brittle steel plus weld-defect notches plus wartime workmanship shortcuts (electrode stubs welded into grooves).
- **Materials/systems:** Welded merchant ship steel (Liberty class, T1, T2 tankers); stick-electrode arc welding.
- **Era:** WWII through 1946.

### WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide)

### WWII ship construction slugging welds
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `WWII ship construction slugging welds`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's account of welders at the end of a long shift throwing stub electrodes into the groove and welding over them, with no X-ray to catch the resulting defect in armor plate. Mechanism explanation for why the Liberty-ship welds had notches.

### 7,000-ton forging press weld repair / 7000-ton forging press casting weld failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** *ambiguous — possibly* `7000-ton forging press casting weld failure` *or* `Aluminum truck wheel press weld crack` *or a new cluster*
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom produces the ~18-inch-thick, 400–600-pass weld sample. Tells the story: forging press broke after 60 years of service; competent welders, no weld defects, but high residual stress; cracked off the welding platform on first attempt; on second attempt the press cracked the first time they tried to forge an aluminum truck wheel. Tom specifies "not Alcoa, the other plant," ~8,000-ton press.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-section steel forging press structural weld; aluminum truck wheel forging operation.
- **Era:** Recent (within Tom's consulting career).
- **Note:** Tom says "8,000-ton press" in this telling, which may conflict with the `7,000-ton forging press weld repair` cluster naming. **Flagged for canon reconciliation.** The Aluminum truck wheel press weld crack cluster may also be the right home. Recorded here under both possible clusters pending editor review.

### British Welding Institute founding
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `British Welding Institute founding`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Richard Weck's bicycle ride from Cambridge to Abingdon to scout a field for the British Welding Institute, motivated in part by Admiralty interest in the brittle-fracture problem. Tom's editorial color: "one of the most arrogant men you will ever meet. But you won't meet him, he's dead now."
- **Era:** Late 1940s / early 1950s founding.

### Pellini explosion bulge test development
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`, `§6.p6`, `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Pellini explosion bulge test development` (also `Explosion bulge test (Pelini underwater weld testing)` in the canon — these may be the same cluster)
- **Frame in this lecture:** William S. Pellini at the Naval Research Laboratory developed the explosion bulge test to characterize ship-steel ductile-brittle behavior. Tom describes the 14-inch test specimen, the explosive-charge protocol, the ductile-brittle transition discovery (around room temperature), and the $50K–$100K per test cost today. Tests now run at remote sites (formerly White Oak).
- **Materials/systems:** Welded steel plate; explosion-bulge testing fixture.
- **Era:** Late 1940s / 1950s development; technique still in use.
- **Note:** Canon has two near-duplicate cluster names (`Pellini explosion bulge test development` and `Explosion bulge test (Pelini underwater weld testing)`). **Flagged for canon merge.**

### Nautilus submarine hull material (HY-80) / HY-80 and HY-100 steel development
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `HY-80 and HY-100 steel development` (also relates to `Nautilus submarine hull material (HY-80)`)
- **Frame in this lecture:** HY-80 was developed at U.S. Steel under Navy program, jumping submarine hull yield from ~50 ksi (WWII) to 80 ksi, with corresponding depth-capability gain. Paired with Rickover's nuclear reactor development. Brief mention, framing the postwar payoff of Pellini's brittle-fracture work.

### British iron industry wood shortage crisis / Early American iron and glass industries—British energy crisis relocation / 1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal / England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** *cluster of related canon entries:* `British iron industry wood shortage crisis`, `Early American iron and glass industries—British energy crisis relocation`, `England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis`, and the related `1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's three-uses-of-trees account: charcoal for glass, charcoal for iron, and ships' masts. The Thames three-mile no-cut law cited from *Out of the Fiery Furnace*. Direct setup for the New World forest resource that made Saugus possible.

### Roman aqueducts
- **Anchor:** `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Roman aqueducts`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief contrast — pre-beam-theory construction relied on safety factors of five-to-ten because brittle materials (stone, brick) could not be sized to calculation. Roman aqueducts cited as the canonical example of this oversized-for-safety regime.
