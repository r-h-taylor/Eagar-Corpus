## Cases referenced in this lecture

### China rare earth embargo on Japan
- **Anchor:** `§1.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `China rare earth embargo on Japan`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to introduce the 2010–2012 cut in export quotas as a euphemism for political warfare against Japan, in setting up the scale comparison between rare-earth and steel markets.
- **Materials/systems:** Rare earth oxides; permanent magnets; cerium in steel; polishing media.
- **Era:** 2010–2014.

### Egyptian obelisk engineering mystery (Backofen / Wilkening)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`, `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Egyptian obelisk engineering mystery (Al Bakun / Wendell Wilkenning fracture analysis)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Wendell Wilkening's MIT doctoral thesis under Backofen on the brittle fracture of granite. Used to introduce microcracking at crack tips as the mechanism explaining why Egyptian obelisks survived lifting.
- **Materials/systems:** Granite (three-phase: white, black, gray minerals).
- **Era:** ~1970 thesis; ancient Egyptian engineering subject matter.

- **canonical_cluster_id:** `British Welding Institute founding`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Origin story — Richard Wek/Weck riding his bicycle near Cambridge, founding the Institute at Abington. Used to identify one of the three postwar centers of brittle-fracture research.
- **Era:** ~1950.

### WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** The 1946 US Navy report data — 4,700 Liberty ships, ~1,000 with fracture casualties, 24 strength-deck fractures, 8 ships lost, 26 lives lost. The USS Schenectady at dry dock and USS Esso Manhattan at sea cited as the canonical photographs. Used to motivate the postwar interest in Griffith's flaw-size theory.
- **Materials/systems:** Welded mild steel ship hulls.
- **Era:** 1941–1946.

### Liberty ships and SS Schenectady
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Liberty ships and SS Schenectady`
- **Frame in this lecture:** The Schenectady at dry dock as the photograph in every fracture-mechanics textbook. Subsidiary to the broader WWII vessel case.
- **Era:** 1943.

### ESSO Manhattan
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `ESSO Manhattan`
- **Frame in this lecture:** The lesser-known mid-ocean fracture, contrasted with the Schenectady's dry-dock failure to underscore the consequences when this kind of failure happens at sea.

### Ethanol production centrifuge disc failures
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Ethanol production centrifuge disc failures`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Forensic case — 3-ton duplex stainless steel disc cast in Pennsylvania, improperly heat-treated, fractured in service in France (near-miss) and China (fatality). 700 worldwide in service; critical flaw size < 2 mm at quarter-inch drilled holes 10 inches deep, raising the inspection problem. Used to demonstrate that flaw size, not material identity, governs catastrophic risk in a brittle-heat-treated condition.
- **Materials/systems:** Duplex stainless steel; 1,000 RPM rotating disc; ethanol production line.
- **Era:** mid-2000s to early 2010s.

### Navtech marine hydraulic piston failure (America's Cup yacht)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`, `§8.p2`, `§8.p3`, `§8.p4`, `§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Navtech marine hydraulic piston failure (America's Cup yacht)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Forensic case — 7075-T6 aluminum hydraulic cylinder, 7500 PSI service, 2.5 mm wall, fatigue-initiated fracture running 2/3 through before catastrophic burst. Tom's recommendation: switch to T73 temper (yield 73 → 63 KSI, K_IC 26 → 30 KSI√in, critical flaw 4 mm) for leak-before-break behavior. The signature case for the toughness-versus-strength trade-off in design philosophy.
- **Materials/systems:** 7075 aluminum, T6 vs T73 heat treatment.
- **Era:** ~2000–2006 (Tom dates the case 10–15 years before 2016).

### America's Cup design philosophy and lightweight failure margins
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`, `§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `America's Cup design philosophy and lightweight failure margins`
- **Frame in this lecture:** "If it doesn't fail periodically, you made it too heavy" — the explicit design philosophy quoted by the Navtec engineer. The Navtec case is the instance; this is the design-culture frame around it.

### Aluminum baseball bat
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Aluminum baseball bat`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Highest-strength aluminum (~100 KSI yield) off the chart of Pellini's RAD. Bats engineered to last 100–150 hits and crack, not shatter — manufacturer-favorable degradation behavior at $300 per replacement. Used as closing example of how the strength/toughness trade-off plays out in consumer products.
