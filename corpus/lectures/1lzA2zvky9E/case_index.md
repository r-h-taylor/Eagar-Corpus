## Cases referenced

### Saugus Ironworks and British deforestation
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Saugus Ironworks; British iron industry wood shortage crisis; England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis
- **Frame in this section:** Tom uses the British energy crisis of 400 years ago as the reason an ironworks was founded in Saugus, Massachusetts. Foreshadowed for later treatment.
- **Era:** 1600s

### Andrew Carnegie and the metallurgy department at MIT
- **Anchor:** `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Andrew Carnegie wealth and steel industry founding
- **Frame in this section:** Tom uses Carnegie as the historical anchor for steel's economic dominance and for the founding of metallurgy as a discipline in the 1880s.
- **Era:** 1880s

### Single-crystal turbine blades
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Single-crystal turbine blades (GE/Pratt & Whitney); Pratt & Whitney single-crystal turbine blade; Modern single-crystal turbine blade casting (core shift defect)
- **Frame in this section:** Physical-object demonstration. Used to illustrate that some single crystals are structural (mechanical-property driven) rather than functional. $4,000 per blade, ~100 blades per disk, $1M per disk in a $5–10M engine.
- **Materials/systems:** Nickel superalloy, single-crystal casting, laser/EB-drilled cooling channels

### Amtrak Acela trolley wire (Boston–New Haven)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`, `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Accel trolley wire for Boston-New Haven electrified rail upgrade; Amtrak New Haven to Boston trolley wire; Trolley wire (Boston-New Hampshire connection); Trolley wire manufacturing by Conform process
- **Frame in this section:** Tom's 48-hour consulting call to accept or reject $6M of copper. Coarse-grain casting rejected; ultrafine-grain product accepted, with the twist that "too much elongation" became the dispute. Used to illustrate that structural copper requires more than electrical specification.
- **Materials/systems:** Copper with 0.1–0.2% silver; ultrafine-grain wire drawing

### Liberty ships brittle fracture
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Liberty ships and SS Schenectady; 1940s welded ship cracking; WWII Welded Merchant Vessel Structural Failures (Fleet-Wide); Navy brittle fracture study - welded vessels and ships
- **Frame in this section:** Foreshadowed. Tom flags the 1920s glass-fracture energy research and the WWII Liberty ship fractures as the reason toughness (energy of fracture) entered the structural-materials canon. "We'll talk about it later."
- **Era:** 1940s

### 2010 China rare earth embargo on Japan
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** 2010 China rare earth embargo on Japan; China rare earth embargo on Japan; Rare earth element supply chain disruption; US-Japan rare earth metals embargo
- **Frame in this section:** Tom's flagship political-externality case. China holds 36% of reserves but had captured 97% of manufacturing; cut off Japan in 2010 with global ripple effects; under pressure resumed sales within a year. Used to teach the five-to-ten-year supply-shock recovery cycle.
- **Materials/systems:** Neodymium-iron-boron magnets; scandium-aluminum aerospace alloys
- **Era:** 2010

### Scandium-aluminum aerospace alloys
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Soviet scandium aluminum alloys research; Sumitomo scandium oxide production initiative
- **Frame in this section:** Used to illustrate the supply/cost coupling: 0.1–0.2% scandium at $2,000/lb doubles the price of an aluminum alloy. Why scandium remains aerospace-only.

### 1973 Arab oil embargo and Strategic Petroleum Reserve
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** 1973 Arab oil embargo; Oil embargo supply chain disruption
- **Frame in this section:** The five-year-recovery teaching case. 1973 embargo → Strategic Petroleum Reserve and dual-fuel utility plants → 1978 attempt much less effective. Generalized to all supply disruptions including rare earths.
- **Era:** 1973–1978

### Pennzoil Oil City refinery (1996 consulting case)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`, `§7.p2`, `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Tank explosion at Pennzoil Oil City refinery; Oil City refinery storage tank explosion; Pennzoil Oil City tank explosion and facility; Pennzoil plant environmental regulation case; Pennzoil tank farm contamination; Oil City refinery explosion
- **Frame in this section:** Tom's 1996 forensic visit after a welder was killed by an exploding 1922-vintage riveted tank. The teaching moment is not the welding failure but the legacy-cost trap: shutting down the 1890s-vintage facility would trigger EPA Superfund liability, so Pennzoil kept running it past economic life. Closed in the twenty years since; now being remediated.
- **Materials/systems:** Riveted carbon steel storage tanks (1920s construction)
- **Era:** 1890s–1996; remediation 2010s

### USX legacy costs and steel industry decline

- **Era:** 1970s–2000s

### Rhodesian chromite embargo
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Rhodesian chrome embargo; Rhodesian chromite embargo and laundering
- **Frame in this section:** Tom's flagship "embargoes don't work" case. Rhodesian chromium ore is so distinctive that any metallurgist could identify it at the steel mill — yet it flowed through transshipment with falsified paperwork.

### Alaska gold mine arsenic discharge
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** Alaska gold mine arsenic discharge
- **Frame in this section:** Tom's example of regulation that doesn't make engineering sense: a gold mine required to discharge water at <1 ppm arsenic into a river whose natural background was 7 ppm. The mine had to clean water below the level of the river it returned to. **Note: in `§9.p3` close, layer 3 silently corrects Tom's spoken slip "level of lead" to "level of arsenic" — see editorial register.**
