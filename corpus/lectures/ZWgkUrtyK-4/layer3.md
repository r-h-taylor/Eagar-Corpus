## §9. PEX and crosslinking [30:43]

`§9.p1` Plastics do corrode, many times in very subtle ways. Some plastics will stress corrosion crack in very clean water, nearly pure water. Water can attack plastics. Same thing with ceramics. They often want to use ceramics at very high temperatures, like in some engine part. Well, sulfur compounds just destroy ceramics in general. These other things do corrode. Environmentally they degrade rather than corrode. But why don't they use PEX? PEX is cross-linked polyethylene, and there are two types of PEX. There's PEX-A and PEX-B. PEX-B is electron beam polymerized.

`§9.p2` Remember Charles Goodyear and sulfur and putting it in latex and making rubber? That's how he learned how to make rubber in the 1830s, by putting sulfur in with the latex, and the sulfur would create crosslinks between the long polymer chains. I'm not getting a lot of nods here of remembering Charles. Charles Goodyear got a patent on it, but that's because he didn't know that the ancient Mayans actually used a vine that had natural sulfur compounds. They would mix that, and so their rubber ball — they used to play this game over miles and miles with this rubber ball. Mike Tarkanian, who runs the lab over here, for his bachelor's thesis was the one who rediscovered the fact that the Mayans from thousands of years ago used the morning glory vine, a type of morning glory that had natural sulfur compounds. They mix it with the rubber and they get crosslinking.

`§9.p3` You've got these polymer chains, which in polyethylene is basically just C with a bunch of H's. So you got a backbone of carbon — it could be ten thousand carbons long — with hydrogen, and at the end you have to have another hydrogen. The formula is Cₙ H₂ₙ₊₂. For every carbon you got two hydrogens, and you have to have a hydrogen on the ends, both ends, so it's 2n plus 2. That's the formula for polyethylene. It has the exact same formula as paraffin wax. One way to tell if something is polyethylene is you take a little piece, you burn it, and see if it smells like a candle. This is actually one of the scientific tests for polyethylene. You need to understand these sophisticated tests we use in the world.

`§9.p4` PEX-A, they basically get crosslinking by hitting it with electron beam, which breaks some of these bonds, and you can actually get links — now you have a carbon coming off this way and this way. You can only have two carbons at a time, but you can have crosslinks with carbon, and you get the same type of thing as taking latex and vulcanizing it. They can do it with an electron beam, or they can do it chemically where they add compounds that help break down some of the polymer chains. Here they're just using a lot of energy to break the polymer chain and hope that it reforms, and it does. This is made by a company called Uponor, this is made by almost everybody else. Now you have enough strength in all directions, and these things don't just burst on you.

`§9.p5` The best anyone knows on the PEX, which has been in service for about twenty-five years now, it shows no evidence of deterioration. The PEX will probably last about a hundred years or more. That's the best estimate right now. People are complaining about the brass fittings, but we'll talk about that when we get to the alloying. In fact that's why people like it — you don't have to use much copper. I mentioned yesterday some people are tearing the copper out of their homes to sell the copper and putting PEX back in. `§9.p6` The problem is, some attorneys in Las Vegas got about a quarter billion dollar settlement for corrosion of some of the brass fittings, and so now there's a feeding frenzy by attorneys claiming this causes leaks, except no one's ever seen one of the leaks. They do get corrosion in Las Vegas. Water is not the best water in the world. You can drink it, but it's not good for your pipes. That's another story.
