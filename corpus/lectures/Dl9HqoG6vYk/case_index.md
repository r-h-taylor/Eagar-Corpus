# Case Index — Lecture 9 (Dl9HqoG6vYk)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p3`).*

This lecture is primarily methodological — Tom is teaching how engineers think, using Kahneman, Engel, and the NFPA fire-investigation methodology as scaffolding. Three concrete forensic cases anchor the lecture, plus several biographical / historical references.

## Cases developed as teaching units

### Johns Hopkins APL sonar tow cable pad-eye failure
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Johns Hopkins APL sonar tow cable pad-eye failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's worked example of system-one heuristic ("if the metal deformed before the weld broke, the weld was good") versus system-two analysis (cable strain calculation, 150,000-psi load estimate). Used to make the broader point that engineering judgment is the interaction between fast pattern recognition and slow quantitative confirmation.
- **Materials/systems:** one-inch steel plate, welded pad eye, steel tow cable, 2,000 ft tow length, sonar payload.
- **Era:** unspecified — Tom describes the case in past tense as one of his forensic consulting engagements; APL is "at the time" the Navy's head sonar lab in Annapolis.

### Church oven gas leak fire
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`–`§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Church oven gas leak fire"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's most recent forensic case at the time of lecture ("a couple of nights ago"). Used as a worked example of the NFPA scientific method: observation (firemen extinguish, fire returns), hypothesis (aluminum gas tube failure at brass ferrule from thermal cycling and creep), system-two analysis (creep behavior of aluminum at 400°F over ~6,000 service hours).
- **Materials/systems:** commercial stainless-steel oven, aluminum gas tube, brass ferrule and steel/brass compression nut, no insulation between burner and tube.
- **Era:** 2015 (case in progress at time of lecture); oven 12 years old, so installed ~2003.

### MIT women faculty salary equity investigation (Hopkins / Birgeneau)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: "MIT women faculty equity study (Hopkins/Birgeneau, late 1990s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as a non-engineering application of the scientific method (collect the data, test the hypothesis, draw the conclusion, act on it). Also as a case study in how committee dynamics resist data collection that might surface uncomfortable answers.
- **Era:** "20 years ago" — i.e. mid-to-late 1990s. The published MIT report on women in science is from 1999 (Birgeneau-era), with the engineering follow-up under Lorna Gibson and Bob Brown a few years later.
- **People named:** Nancy Hopkins (biology), Bob Birgeneau (Dean of Science, later UC Berkeley Chancellor), Chuck Vest (President), Lorna Gibson (engineering committee chair), Bob Brown (Provost, later BU President).

## Cases referenced briefly

### Brian Josephson — superconducting tunneling discovery
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`–`§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Josephson effect discovery"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cautionary case for the "scientist solves problems that can be solved" thesis — Josephson, as a naïve graduate student, solved the harder problem his elders had been simplifying away, and discovered electron tunneling. Won Nobel Prize 1973. Tom met him in the early 1970s.
- **Era:** discovery 1962; Tom's encounter early 1970s.

### Frank Whittle — turbine engine development
- **Anchor:** `§2.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Frank Whittle turbine engine development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** The "engineer solves problems that must be solved" half of the Ragone dichotomy. Whittle's quote: "It's a good thing I was too stupid to know it wouldn't work."
- **Era:** 1930s–1940s.

## Figures and themes referenced (not cases)

### David Ragone and the Ragone plot
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`
- **Frame:** Tom credits Ragone (former MIT materials professor) with the scientist-vs-engineer formulation. Ragone is 85 at time of lecture (so b. ~1930). The Ragone plot is the standard energy-density-vs-power-density chart in battery engineering.

### Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1` and throughout §3
- **Frame:** Methodological anchor for the entire lecture. Kahneman won the 2002 Nobel Prize in Economics for prospect theory. Tom returns to System 1 / System 2 in §4 (heuristic vs. analysis on the pad eye) and §7 (heuristic vs. analysis on the church oven).

### S. Morris Engel, *The Language Trap / With Good Reason*
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p3`
- **Frame:** Tom's working reference for logical fallacies in expert-witness reports. He cites it specifically as a defense weapon in depositions — naming the fallacy by chapter (e.g., "hasty generalization, chapter 8 of Engel") provides a scientific reference for what would otherwise read as ad hominem.

### Ed Schein, *Career Anchors*
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`–`§5.p8`
- **Frame:** Sloan School professor's typology of eight career anchors. Tom identifies his own as autonomy. Used as a student-advice handout, not a case.

### NFPA *Guide for Fire and Explosion Investigations* (2014 edition)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p8`
- **Frame:** Tom's working reference for the scientific method in fire investigation. Cited as the codification of the methodology he applies in §7.p2–p6.

### Rafael Reif's MIT *Spectrum* introduction
- **Anchor:** `§2.p6`
- **Frame:** Tom quotes Reif's framing of MIT's mission ("problem solving in service to the world") as institutional confirmation of the engineering definition the school of engineering had already adopted (complex, ambiguous, uncertain problems).
