Lecture 1 is the course opener. Most "cases" here are framing references rather than developed teaching units; the lecture's substantive case-teaching begins in §4 with Oil City and continues through §7 with the U.S. steel industry. Two cases (Pennzoil Oil City; U.S. Steel 1962 price rollback; Bethlehem Steel) are surfaced for the first time in the corpus arc as previewed material.

## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Pennzoil Oil City refinery
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`, `§4.p6`, `§4.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Pennzoil Oil City tank explosion and facility`
- **Frame in this lecture:** EPA hazardous waste site externality. Tom uses the refinery to illustrate why old, inefficient facilities continue operating: shutdown triggers cleanup liability. Late-1990s consulting visit.
- **Materials/systems:** riveted steel storage tanks (1920s vintage), refinery infrastructure
- **Era:** facility built ~1900, Tom's visit late 1990s

### Titusville / Edwin Drake oil discovery
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `PROPOSED: Titusville Pennsylvania - Edwin Drake first oil drilling (1859)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Historical anchor for the Oil City story; introduces oil-bubbling-up-from-the-ground geology that becomes the bridge to Alaskan tar sands.
- **Era:** 1856 (per Tom; conventional date is 1859)

### Alaska tar sands and Prudhoe Bay oil seeps
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`, `§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Prudhoe Bay offshore ice islands` (closest existing; surface-seep framing is new)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Geological externality — how surface oil seeps gave clues to subsurface reserves and produced tar sands as the volatile-stripped residue.
- **Era:** 1900 (Strategic Naval Petroleum Reserve designation referenced)

### Fort McMurray tar sands and the Canadian goose fines
- **Anchor:** `§4.p8`, `§4.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Tar sands pond bird kills`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Environmental externality with explicit monetized fine ($100,000/bird). Propane cannons as the cost-rational response.
- **Materials/systems:** bitumen ponds, propane scare cannons

### Nick Grant tobacco company metal-powder consulting
- **Anchor:** `§5.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `PROPOSED: Nick Grant tobacco company metal powder filter research (1980s)`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's example of how even tobacco research has a material-science angle. Used to illustrate the breadth of externalities and how industry research money flows.
- **Materials/systems:** metal powders for cigarette smoke absorption

### China rare earth embargo on Japan (2010)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`, `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `China rare earth embargo on Japan` / `2010 China rare earth embargo on Japan`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Geopolitical externality. Tom uses the embargo to introduce how monopolies are built by undercutting price, then weaponized politically. Sets up the magnet discussion that follows.
- **Era:** ~2010

### Samarium-cobalt magnet development at GE
- **Anchor:** `§5.p4`, `§5.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `GE samarium cobalt magnet development`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Pre-history of high-BH-product permanent magnets. Tom's undergraduate field trip to GE research labs in early 1970s. Sets up the neodymium-iron-boron generational replacement.
- **Materials/systems:** SmCo magnets
- **Era:** 1960s–early 1970s

### Neodymium-iron-boron magnets in automobiles
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`, `§5.p7`, `§5.p8`, `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Sony Walkman and neodymium-iron-boron magnets`
- **Frame in this lecture:** ~15 lb of NdFeB magnets per modern car; starter motor shrunk from 30 lb to fist-sized. Discovered ~1980 at GM Research Lab. Connects to the rare earth embargo case (§5.p9).
- **Materials/systems:** NdFeB permanent magnets, automotive electric motors, Sony Walkman
- **Era:** ~1980 discovery; ongoing

### Mountain Pass rare earth mine (California)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Mountain Pass rare earth mine closure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** U.S. domestic capacity that closed when Chinese prices dropped; reopenable with five-to-ten-year lead time and ~$1B capex.
- **Era:** closed during Chinese price collapse, contemplated reopening post-2010

### Blood diamonds (Angola civil war)
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Blood diamonds civil war`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Social externality. Tom notes that geological-region fingerprinting via impurity content makes provenance detectable for a "really good material scientist."
- **Materials/systems:** diamonds, trace-element analysis

### Rhodesian chrome embargo
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Rhodesian chrome embargo` / `Rhodesian chromite embargo and laundering`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Political externality. The world's best chrome ore subjected to embargo during the civil war. Tom notes it's "a lot easier to detect Rhodesian chrome than blood diamonds" — visual identification rather than impurity analysis.
- **Materials/systems:** chromite ore
- **Era:** 1965–1979

### Mesabi Range iron ore depletion
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Mesabi Range iron ore depletion` (also: `Massabi Range iron ore depletion`, `U.S. iron ore depletion—Mesabi Range`)
- **Frame in this lecture:** The geological externality that made the U.S. steel industry dominant. Direct-charge ore that "ran out throughout the 1960s." Sets up the post-1945 industry-decline argument.
- **Era:** 1920s–1960s

### U.S. Steel 1962 price rollback (Kennedy standoff)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `US Steel price control by President Kennedy (1962)` / `U.S. Steel Price Rollback`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Political externality. Kennedy forced U.S. Steel to roll back prices because steel was the global commodity benchmark and the price rise would have triggered worldwide inflation. Some old-timers blamed this for the industry's decline; Tom blames the 1945 hiring cohort instead.

### Bethlehem Steel research lab and 1974 employment
- **Anchor:** `§7.p3`, `§7.p4`, `§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `PROPOSED: Bethlehem Steel research labs - 1974 employment and 1945-cohort management failure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first industry employer. The "1945 cohort" managers who never updated their mindset from 75% world share to 25%. The $600M research facility on South Mountain. Don Blickwede (vice president, MIT MatSci alumnus). Bethlehem's exploration of nuclear steel-making.
- **Era:** Tom there 1974; cohort hired ~1944
- **Note:** This is likely an instance of broader U.S. steel-industry-decline clusters (`US Steel Industry Crisis and Japanese Competition`, `US Steel industry decline and management failure`); flag for canonical merge.

### Norm Augustine and socio-engineering
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** *not yet in aggregate v2* — proposed addition: `PROPOSED: Norm Augustine aircraft cost projection and socio-engineering framing`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Augustine's log-plot prediction that by 2020 the entire Air Force budget would buy one aircraft; by 2045 the entire DoD budget would buy one. Used as the bridge into externalities-as-a-discipline.
- **Era:** Augustine's plot ~1980

### World Trade Center collapse paper
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `World Trade Center collapse`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's three-hour-write *Journal of Metals* article. Used as the pedagogical model for the course: write so a high school senior can follow it, but anchor in fundamental principles (color pyrometry, Planck's law). The conspiracy-theorist response and the 500°F vs. 900°F dispute.
- **Materials/systems:** steel thermal behavior, Planck radiation, color pyrometry
- **Era:** paper written Oct 2001, published Dec 2001
