# Case Index — Lecture 2 (Codes and Standards, Fall 2012)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§1.p3`). This lecture is primarily methodological — Tom is illustrating the range of measurement precision rather than working through forensic cases. Few canonical cases appear; the lecture is dominated by figures, anecdotes, and a hands-on exercise.*

## Cases referenced

### LIGO gravitational wave detector construction
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`–`§1.p15`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "LIGO construction and welding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's role welding the 4-foot diameter beam tubes, and Ray Weiss's hand-written explanation of the 10⁻²³/√Hz measurement target. Used as the canonical "sublime" extreme of precision measurement. Tom also uses it to make the point that the best engineering at MIT is sometimes done in the School of Science.
- **Materials/systems:** stainless steel vacuum chamber, hydrogen outgassing control, 4 km beam tubes, GPS surveying with custom base stations.
- **Era:** 1992 proposal; construction through 1990s–2000s.
- **Related cluster:** "LIGO gravitational wave detector corrosion failure" (distinct case, not referenced here).

### NIST F1 atomic clock
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`, `§6.p13`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: NIST F1 atomic clock as measurement standard
- **Frame in this lecture:** One part in 10¹⁵ accuracy — one second in 30 million years. Returns at end of lecture as the time-measurement upper bound.
- **Note:** Recurring framing reference rather than a teaching case proper.

### Spark testing for steel composition
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Steel yard identification via spark testing (1900s-1960s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's first-hand recollection from steel-company work forty years earlier; demonstration with grinder on 1018, 1045, 440C, and alloy steel samples. Used as the "ridiculous" (in the sense of low-tech, embodied-knowledge) extreme paired against LIGO.
- **Materials/systems:** 1018, 1045, 440C stainless, alloy steels.
- **Era:** Tom's steel-company experience early 1970s; technique pre-1900 origin.

### Cow magnets
- **Anchor:** `§3.p12`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Cow magnet ingestion in livestock"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Aside during the ball-identification exercise — Tom uses 400-series stainless steel cow magnets as the magnet props. Explains the livestock-medicine origin.
- **Materials/systems:** 400-series stainless steel.

### Fire assay for gold analysis
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`–`§6.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Fire assay gold analysis (MIT historical curriculum)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's account of the centuries-old gravimetric technique still standard for referee gold analysis: digest in lead bath, oxidize off base metals, separate gold-silver button, dissolve silver in nitric, weigh refined gold. One part in 5,000 accuracy. Tom notes he was tasked 25–30 years ago with developing a replacement; inductively coupled plasma is finally getting close.
- **Materials/systems:** karat gold, lead bath digestion, nitric acid silver dissolution.
- **Era:** MIT undergraduate fire assay curriculum until early/mid-20th century; technique itself ancient. Tom's replacement-process work c. 1982–1987.

## In-class demonstrations (not cases)

- **Ball identification exercise** (§3 throughout): Tom's standardized hands-on lab — 19 possible materials including 18-8 stainless, 440C, M50 tool steel, low-carbon steel, brass, aluminum, Viton, rubber, Torlon, acrylic, polystyrene, polyethylene, nylon, tungsten carbide, aluminum oxide, silicon nitride. Sourced from McMaster-Carr. Used here, also referenced in 3.014.

- **Gauge blocks demonstration** (§4.p4–§4.p9): Webber/Starrett chromium carbide gauge blocks, calibrated to international inch.

- **Farberware pot magnet demonstration** (§3.p9): Tom's standard prop — 304 stainless body (non-magnetic) with magnetic top section, used to illustrate austenitic vs ferritic stainless. Tom notes he uses this in multiple courses.

## Figures referenced (recurring numeric anchors)

- **LIGO target precision:** 10⁻²³/√Hz → 10⁻¹⁹ m measured length change (§1.p9, §1.p15)
- **NIST F1 atomic clock accuracy:** 1 part in 10¹⁵, or 1 second in 30 million years (§1.p2)
- **Steel coefficient of thermal expansion:** 10⁻⁵/°C (§4.p11)
- **Steel yard spark-test operator accuracy:** 99.9% (§2.p5)
- **Chip-to-ship precision ratio:** 10⁻⁸ m on 10⁻² m chip = 1 in 10⁶; ship plate alignment 1 mm on 300 m = 3 × 10⁻⁶ (§5.p2, §5.p7)
- **Fire assay precision:** 1 part in 5,000 (§6.p3, §6.p6)
- **Gauge block accuracy at 1 inch:** 0.8 microinches ≈ 0.02 microns (§4.p8)
