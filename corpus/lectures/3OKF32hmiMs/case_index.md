# Case Index — Lecture 2019-09-26

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p2`). This is a guest-lecture session focused on Digital Alloys' Joule Printing process; the case structure is built around the company's strategic targets (titanium aerospace, tool steel automotive) and the comparative landscape of metal additive processes.*

## Cases referenced

### Digital Alloys Joule Printing process
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`, `§7.p1–p14`, `§8.p1–p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Digital Alloys Joule Printing process development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Resistive heating of welding wire on a substrate; titanium printed at ~2 kg/hr at ~600 W, ~300 Wh/kg. Argon-purged chamber. MIT-spinout startup, ~25 people, in stealth at time of lecture.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium wire feedstock; argon environment; tool steel for second target market.
- **Era:** 2017–2019.

### Boeing titanium aerospace machining (buy-to-fly ratio)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`, `§4.p3`, `§9.p2`, `§9.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Air Force Buy-to-Fly Ratio in Aircraft Manufacturing" (closest existing canon match — same concept, Boeing-specific framing)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Boeing as Digital Alloys' early investor and strategic partner. Example part: 7 kg block → 0.5 kg machined part; 10 kg block → 1 kg part. Ninety percent of titanium ends up on the floor as machining waste. Boeing's interest in additive is waste reduction, energy savings, lightweighting.
- **Materials/systems:** Aerospace-grade titanium.
- **Era:** 2018–2019.

### Ford automotive stamping die manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`, `§9.p5`, `§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Ford automotive stamping die manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Alex visited Ford a few months earlier; saw massive die that stamps vehicle hoods from sheet plate. Wear components inside dies have complex geometries currently machined inefficiently from tool steel — Digital Alloys' second target market. Tom adds: dies weigh 200 tons, desk-height, highly polished; replacement urgency drives die-tooling economics (cannot sell cars when out of production).
- **Materials/systems:** Tool steel; 200-ton stamping dies.
- **Era:** 2018–2019 (current).

### Tesla new-vehicle tooling investment scale
- **Anchor:** `§9.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Tesla new-vehicle tooling investment scale"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Glancing reference — "something like a billion dollars to create all tooling for a completely new vehicle" before first car is sold. Illustrates the size of the prize for tool-die efficiency improvements.
- **Era:** late 2010s framing.

### Desktop Metal binder jetting (comparative landscape)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§10.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Desktop Metal eyeglass hinges" (closest existing match — Desktop Metal context)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Geographic neighbors in Burlington, MIT ties. Process makes sense for smaller, thinner parts because binder must be removed and part densified. Used to define Digital Alloys' size sweet spot ("tennis ball to beach ball") by contrast.
- **Materials/systems:** Metal powder with polymer binder.
- **Era:** current at time of lecture.

### Laser powder bed cracking in aerospace aluminum
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum aerospace-grade cracking in powder bed additive manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Powder bed processes have only been able to process casting-grade aluminum, not high-strength aerospace grades, due to cracking. Tom adds: carbon steels not a problem, titanium fine, nickel alloys (~$200/lb) going into high-temperature applications.
- **Materials/systems:** Aerospace aluminum alloys, laser powder bed fusion.
- **Era:** current at time of lecture.

### Powder bed thermal residual stress tear-apart
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Additive manufacturing powder bed part failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Photograph reference of a powder bed build that "tore itself in half" along layer lines. Tom's parallel anecdote: Joule Printing 15 months prior could not make a 6"×6" part without cracking; speeding up the machine resolved the diffusion/porosity problem.
- **Materials/systems:** Powder bed fusion; Joule Printing early development.
- **Era:** ~2018.

### Joule Printing six-inch part cracking (early development)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p7`, `§7.p8`

## Figures referenced
- Buy-to-fly machining waste: 10 kg → 1 kg, 7 kg → 0.5 kg, ~90% waste on titanium aerospace parts (`§4.p2`, `§4.p3`).
- Joule Printing energy budget: titanium at ~2 kg/hr, ~2 V, ~300 A, ~600 W, ~300 Wh/kg — "an order of magnitude less energy" than powder bed laser processes (`§8.p3`).
- Wire vs. powder vs. plate cost ratios: wire ~2× plate; powder 4–10× plate (`§4.p6`).
- Metal additive process count: ~15 distinct ways to additively manufacture metal (`§2.p2`).
- Desktop Metal vs. Digital Alloys size sweet spots: tennis-ball-to-beach-ball (Joule); smaller (binder jet); hot-dog-bead (large welding-type) (`§10.p5`).
- New-vehicle tooling capital cost: ~$1 billion (`§9.p6`).
- Stamping die mass: 200 tons (`§9.p5`).
- Brewery historical reference: additive manufacturing predates 3D printing by ~80 years if welded layer buildup counts (`§2.p3`).
