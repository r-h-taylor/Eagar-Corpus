## Cases referenced

### Conductive polymers / polyacetylene hype
- **Anchor:** `§1.p5`–`§1.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Polyacetylene conductive polymers" (also relates to "Conductive polymers hype cycle", "Electrically conductive polymers (polyacetylene) misconception", "Gary Wennick conductive polymers research talk")
- **Frame in this lecture:** A young professor's claim that polyacetylene would replace copper because of higher *specific* electrical conductivity. Tom's shaving-mirror realization the next morning: aluminum already has higher specific conductivity than copper, but we don't use it in rotating machinery — because absolute conductivity is what matters, not specific.
- **Materials/systems:** polyacetylene; copper, aluminum, silver as conductors.
- **Era:** mid-1980s talk in the Chipman Room.

### WWII silver-wound magnets from Fort Knox
- **Anchor:** `§1.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "WWII silver-wound magnets from Fort Knox" (also "Oak Ridge National Laboratory silver magnet winding (WWII uranium enrichment)")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Copper shortage during WWII forced Manhattan Project to borrow ~50 tons of silver from the U.S. Mint in Philadelphia for the Calutron magnetic separators at Oak Ridge; returned after the war undamaged.
- **Materials/systems:** silver windings; uranium isotope separation.
- **Era:** 1942–1945.

### China rare earth embargo on Japan
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "2010 China rare earth embargo on Japan" (also "China rare earth embargo on Japan", "Rare earth element supply chain disruption", "US-Japan rare earth metals embargo")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as the canonical modern example of externalities — China's monopoly came not from ore but from willingness to absorb pollution and pay low wages. Five-year hurdle for alternative supply.
- **Era:** 2010.

### AT&T regulated monopoly and the transistor
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`–`§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "AT&T telephone network mechanical switch failure risk" (also "Bell Labs transistor development")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's debunking of the basic-research transistor myth. Bell Labs' 1925 statistical projection showed mechanical switch failure rates would collapse the phone system by the 1950s; transistor development was applied research justified within the 6% regulated-profit framework.
- **Era:** 1925 statistical study → 1947 transistor.

### US copper tubing manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US copper tubing manufacturing" (also "Reading Pennsylvania copper plant deoxidation process", "Copper-to-copper crimp connector adoption in residential plumbing")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Transportation externality. Six US copper tubing plants survived 1980s–90s offshoring because the air-in-the-hole shipping cost was prohibitive. Reading Tube as the LGO thesis site.

### Saugus Ironworks
- **Anchor:** `§4.p4`–`§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks" (also "British iron industry wood shortage crisis", "England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis", "1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal", "Early American iron and glass industries—British energy crisis relocation", "Jamestown glassworks")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Seventeenth-century energy externality. England's wood crisis (cannon iron, masts, window glass all charcoal-fueled) drove the founding of Jamestown glassworks and Saugus Ironworks. "The energy crisis of 1973 was not the first energy crisis."
- **Era:** c. 1619 (Tom's date) / c. 1646 actual.

### "Future of Metals" article (1991 Welding Journal)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2`–`§7.p3`

- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's June 1991 *Welding Journal* article. 95% of world metal is steel; in 1990, steel was a $500B industry at 2% growth — its absolute growth exceeded ceramics ($5B/20%), composites ($15B/10%), and semiconductors ($100B/5%) combined.

### Lakshmi Mittal steel mill acquisition strategy
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`, `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lakshmi Mittal steel mill acquisition strategy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mittal bought distressed US steel mills at ten cents on the dollar in the late 1980s while Wall Street pronounced steel dead. Used to illustrate the productivity-disguised-as-decline pattern: US steel halved employment 1980–1990 with constant output, doubling productivity at 6–7%/year vs. 1% economy-wide.

### US steel industry productivity transformation (1980-1990)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`–`§7.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US steel industry productivity transformation (1980-1990)" (also "Steel mini mills rise (1970s–1990s)", "US Steel Industry Crisis and Japanese Competition", "MIT Commission on Industrial Productivity - Steel Industry Study")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Constant 100M tons/year output with workforce halved from 500K to 250K. Mining industry close to 10%/year productivity gain; both were the era's outperformers.
