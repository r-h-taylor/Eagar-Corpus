# Case Index — Lecture 21 (Spring 2014)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§2.p3`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is a teaching unit on fatigue design of welded high-strength steel. Cases appear as illustrative pendants to the central rule: in fatigue of welded structures, high-strength steel buys you nothing, because the weld is a notch.

## Cases referenced

### America's Cup HY-80 keel failure
- **Anchor:** `§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "America's Cup HY-80 keel failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate that 130 ksi welded keel beam, four feet wide and eight inches thick, bent under sail loads — concentrating force in a single bending moment was beyond what HY130 could carry at that geometry. Tom names it as HY130 (the Navy submarine steel), not HY-80; the canonical cluster name may need reconciliation against other lectures.
- **Materials/systems:** HY130 steel, welded keel beam
- **Era:** "about ten or fifteen years ago" from Spring 2014 — circa late 1990s / early 2000s

### General Motors and Ford minivan spot-weld fatigue failures
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3`, `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Motors and Ford minivan spot-weld fatigue failures"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mid-1980s lightweighting initiative. GM and Ford substituted high-strength steel and assumed fatigue life would scale with ultimate strength. The spot welds — stress concentrations — did not benefit, and minivan owners experienced rattles within a couple of years.
- **Materials/systems:** high-strength sheet steel, spot welds, automotive bodies
- **Era:** mid-1980s

### Northridge earthquake (LRFD origin)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Northridge earthquake"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Referenced as the empirical source for the modern LRFD (load-and-resistance factor design) approach in the AISC code. Tom credits Northridge building failures as a major input to the new methodology.
- **Era:** early 1990s

### King Street Bridge failure
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`, `§5.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "King Street Bridge failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Melbourne, Australia, 1960s. Plate-on-plate fillet weld run all the way around the closure produced a fatigue crack that brought the bridge down. Now a forbidden detail in the codes — illustrates the principle that codes encode failure history. Tom does not recall whether anyone was killed.
- **Materials/systems:** plate-on-plate fillet weld, bridge structure
- **Era:** 1960s
