# Case Index — Lecture 18, Welding Metallurgy Summer 2014

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p1`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture covers distortion, residual stress, the SCC/HE distinction, and the three-regime preheat framework. Cases surface mostly as illustrations of preheat practice or weld-failure history.

## Cases referenced

### Brooklyn-to-Japan stainless steel box delayed cracking
- **Anchor:** `§1.p1`–`§1.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Stainless steel box cracking in transit to Japan"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's opening case (recording starts mid-narrative). Used to illustrate delayed hydrogen cracking and TRIP steel transformation: the cracks appeared on the top of the formed box where deformation-induced martensite raised hardness. Magnet test confirmed the transformation gradient.
- **Materials/systems:** TRIP steel; sheet steel forming; neodymium iron boron magnet diagnostic.

### GE boiling water reactor safe-end residual stress
- **Anchor:** `§2.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "General Electric nuclear reactor safe-end cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate that restraint converts distortion into residual stress. The GE safe-end welds carried tensile residual stresses on the inside surface (where borated quench water contacted them); the fix was developing procedures that produced compressive residual stresses on the inside.
- **Materials/systems:** stainless steel safe-end pipes, 1.5–2 inches thick, 2–2.5 ft diameter; borated water.

### Japanese HSLA shipbuilding steel — no-preheat capability
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Japanese HSLA shipbuilding steel development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as the motivation for Tom's mid-1980s Japan trip on behalf of the Navy. Japanese commercial steel mills were producing one-inch plate that required no summer preheat — a millions-of-dollars savings vs. the alloy steels needing 200°F preheat. Sets up the Navy's interest in HSLA for surface ships.
- **Era:** mid-1980s.
- **Related:** "1984-1985 Japan Manufacturing Study"; "US Navy HSLA Steel Mill Investment"; "US Navy Japan steel mill visit and HSLA technology transfer."

### Idaho National Engineering Lab preheat-skipped-over-weekend story
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Idaho National Engineering Lab nuclear reactor component preheat failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Teaching anecdote from a welding-engineer friend. The Monday-morning piece was supposed to be preheated to 150°F; touch-test revealed it had been preheated Friday and allowed to cool over the weekend. Used as the punchline illustrating that preheat must be present at the moment of welding, not just nominally performed.
- **Note:** Probably distinct from the canonical "Idaho National Engineering Lab nuclear reactor component cracking" entry, which appears to refer to a cracking case rather than a preheat-protocol anecdote. Flagged for editor reconciliation.

### Turbine blade electron-beam repair at induction-heated red heat
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Turbine blade electron-beam repair and preheating"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate the upper extreme of preheat (1000°C, ~2000°F) for nickel-base turbine blade alloys. The blade is glowing red inside the vacuum chamber while the EB weld is made; economics ($50 scrap vs. $7000 repaired) justify the unusual practice.
- **Materials/systems:** nickel-base turbine blade alloy; induction heating; electron beam welding in vacuum.

### Sea Wolf submarine HY-100 weld metal cracking and blue jelly suits
- **Anchor:** `§5.p3`, also referenced at `§8.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited to make two points: (1) the redone Sea Wolf used high preheat plus the "blue jelly suits" humidity control to prevent recurrence; (2) the Sea Wolf cracking was unusual in that it occurred in the weld metal rather than the heat-affected zone, because of overmatching weld metal strength. Tom notes Electric Boat shut down production for about a year and salaried workers still had to be paid.
- **Related:** "Electric Boat welding humidity specification violation"; "SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem"; "Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking."

### Electric Boat HY-80 one-inch weld sample
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: Electric Boat HY-80 demonstration weld sample (Tom's classroom artifact)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Physical-object teaching move. Tom acquired this single-side weld with backing bar from Electric Boat ~30–35 years before the lecture; uses it to show distortion (V-shape pulls top edges in), the HAZ size relative to a quarter-inch in carbon steel, and the rationale for clear-coating black iron samples.
- **Note:** This is a recurring classroom artifact, not a forensic case per se. Treated as a "PROPOSED:" cluster pending check against the aggregate for whether the artifact is referenced in other lectures.

### Navy particle beam weapons program — submarine welding feasibility
- **Anchor:** `§6.p4`–`§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Naval Surface Warfare Center particle beam weapon development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited in the arc-welding origin-of-heat-sources thread. After peace broke out with the Soviet Union, the Navy convened a workshop at White Oak asking whether the $250M particle-beam technology could weld submarines. Tom's answer: at megawatts you could melt a submarine, not weld it (10–50 kW suffices for HY-80). Includes the "hose instability" anecdote — beam wandered in atmosphere, could shoot down a 30-inch-away missile or a cooperative 30-mile-away missile but couldn't aim.
- **Related:** "White Oak Naval Particle Beam Program"; "US Navy particle beam weapons welding research"; "Navy 25 kilowatt laser welder at Naval Research Laboratory" (contrast: appropriate power scale).
- **Era:** mid-1980s (development); early 1990s (workshop).
