## §2. Distortion and residual stress in welding [01:52]

`§2.p1` Any other questions? Okay, I talked about distortion and there were a couple of things I wanted to show you. This is 5083 aluminum, electron beam welded, from a quarter inch — 6.4 millimeters — to 38 millimeters, which is about an inch and a half thick. If you look, this is nice and straight and flat, so are these. As you get up to about 3/8 — and I told you 3/8 is where you get the maximum distortion — you don't get distortion here because it's not a v-shaped weld. Once we start getting into the more v-shaped welds, and these up here are probably multi-pass welds, you get more and more distortion.

`§2.p2` Now if the whole thing is restrained you don't get distortion, you just get huge residual stresses — tensile residual stresses right there, compressive residual stresses on the bottom. That was what General Electric was finding with their stainless steel welds in the reactor safe ends. These are two or two and a half foot diameter pipes where you'd flood the reactor with borated water if you had a problem. You quench the reactor by flooding it with borated water. The problem is, those pipes were one and a half to two inches thick, and they had tremendous tensile residual stresses on the inside. One of the things they did is they developed welding procedures that created compressive residual stresses on the inside, where it was in contact with the water.

`§2.p3` If you remember, we've got three things — material, stress, and environment — that can lead to stress corrosion cracking. I've told you many times: seventy percent of the time, metallurgists just lump hydrogen embrittlement and stress corrosion cracking together. But they are different. I'll now tell you the secret of the difference. The secret is, one occurs at the anode and the other one occurs at the cathode. Stress corrosion cracking occurs at the anode, hydrogen embrittlement occurs at the cathode. Why? Because the corrosive species in corrosion are usually negative ions, and so they go to the anode, which is positive. And if you try to remember all this — just like high school chemistry, you can't keep reduction at the cathode straight — but nonetheless, with hydrogen embrittlement you've got a positive hydrogen ion that wants to go to the negative electrode, the cathode. So hydrogen cracking occurs at the cathode, stress corrosion cracking occurs at the anode. If you're not worried about which one's positive and which one's negative, sometimes metallurgists just lump them all together and call it stress corrosion cracking — because the susceptible microstructure (of which martensite is the worst because it's the hardest), the presence of stress (the higher the stress the easier it is to crack), and the environment (either a negative ion or a positive hydrogen) — that's what makes the difference.

`§2.p4` So we have problems with distortion. You can lower the distortion — the amount of distortion varies with the amount of heat to the work piece. The real difference is you get a very v-shaped weld in gas welding, less v-shaped in arc welding, and in high-energy electron beam you can get very parallel sides. So there's no preferential distortion — no greater shrinkage at the top of the v than the bottom of the v.

## §3. Preheat: low, medium, and high regimes [07:27]

`§3.p1` Now I want to go to preheat. We have preheat and post heat for welding, and we use them in lots of different materials, not just steel. In steel there are three levels of preheat that I talk about — I'm not sure I've ever read anyone else talking about it. If I'm preheating from 50 to 100 degrees Fahrenheit: the structural welding code says you shall not weld unless you're above 10 degrees Fahrenheit, which is sort of ridiculous because you could have frost on it. But the structural welding code is usually for lower strength steels, and the preheat requirement is not as great. I told you it's almost impossible to take an A36 steel — which is what your bridges and your buildings usually are, about 36 ksi yield — and crack it. Sometimes they use something that's 50 ksi, which is similar to the EH-32 that Tom talked about welding in the rudder stool.

`§3.p2` There's DH-32, and the 32, I think, is 32 kilograms per square millimeter, which works out to 48 or 52 ksi as I remember. A36 is 36 ksi — thirty-six thousand pounds per square inch. EH32 and DH32 are 36 kilograms per square millimeter. It's been literally thirty-five years since I've worked on EH and DH steels. They are a marine type of steel; basically what the structural welding code would call a 50 ksi steel. The steel companies don't necessarily make a different steel for every designation.

`§3.p3` You have different standards. You have the American Iron and Steel Institute, which is the four-digit — like 1021 steel, 4340, 4130, 8630. Then you have the ASTM specifications, which are also used by the ASME boiler and pressure vessel code. They're like A36, ASTM A36. I showed you out of Stout and Doty's book A27, which is a cast steel — that's an ASTM steel. The boiler and pressure vessel code calls it an SA27. Then the Navy has their steels, the HY steels; the Germans have their steels. A lot of these things have overlapping ranges. So if I buy an ASTM A106 pipe, it might meet a 1020 AISI designation, it might meet an ASME B31.3. It might meet five or six standards, and you can put on your purchase order which ones are going to hold. In more recent years people just put all of them on, and sometimes they conflict, because not every standard is exactly the same. That's another problem.

`§3.p4` For preheat: if you have a simple low-carbon steel that doesn't harden very much, all you really need to do is get the moisture off. The ASME code says you shall not weld steels with less than 50 degree preheat, because even though you don't have frost above 32 degrees Fahrenheit, you have considerable absorbed water on the surface. Where do you think the frost on your windshield comes from? When it gets below 32, there was actually a little layer of dew on there, right? Below 50 degrees you actually have enough humidity in the air that every surface starts to absorb some moisture, and you shouldn't weld on a surface unless you bring it up to 50 degrees. In the 50 to 100 degree range — let's face it, when you preheat you're going to overshoot 50 degrees, and as you start welding it will start to cool down because you're no longer heating it. In that range you're not doing anything except driving off moisture.

`§3.p5` Moisture, of course, is a source of hydrogen. You can tolerate 10 or 20 parts per million hydrogen in these types of steels that have very low preheat. When you go to medium preheat, which I call 100 to 300 degrees Fahrenheit, now you may have a thicker steel, or a higher carbon steel, or a little bit of alloy content — it might be an HSLA steel that's two inches thick. If it was HY80 and it's two or four inches thick you might need three or four hundred degree preheat. If it's HSLA you may only need a hundred to three hundred, or a hundred to two hundred degrees. That's why the Navy wanted to go to HSLA plate steels, and that's one of the reasons they sent me over to Japan in the mid 80s to see how the Japanese were making these steels. The Japanese were making commercial steels for shipbuilding at the time that required no preheat. They might have one-inch-thick plate that in the old days, with an alloy steel, you had to preheat to 200 degrees — now they didn't have to preheat it at all, at least during the summer in Japan. Maybe in the winter they had to get it up there, but they didn't have to preheat it. Saved them millions of dollars in shipbuilding.

`§3.p6` What happens in this medium range: ordinarily if you heat something up, the temperature-time plot when you strike an arc will rise up rapidly because the arc is pretty hot, get up to the melting temperature, and then when you take the arc away it starts to cool down. If it cools down below 100 or 200 degrees, you run into a problem. This is where hydrogen cracking occurs. Remember I showed you hydrogen cracking is worst at room temperature, and if you're at two or three hundred degrees the hydrogen can diffuse out. Remember I poured the Sprite in — the Sprite goes flat. If you preheat, you're going to level off at a couple hundred degrees, and the hydrogen has time to escape, because this big heavy piece of steel is not going to cool off in less than an hour. So you're actually doing a one-hour post heat by preheating.

## §4. The Idaho Falls preheat story [14:48]

`§4.p1` One of my favorite stories: I was talking to a friend who was a welding engineer at Idaho National Engineering Lab out in Idaho Falls. He had gone through a shop where they were building some nuclear reactor components out of carbon steel. It was a Monday morning. I don't know if it was a pressure vessel or whatever, but he went up, and he knew the thing was supposed to be preheated to about 150 degrees, because he was a welding engineer. He didn't feel warm — if you stand next to something that weighs a few tons and it's 150 degrees, you can tell. He put his hand up to it, and he actually touched it. You can't — if you put your hand on 150 degrees you won't leave it there very long, you won't burn it immediately but you won't leave it there. And he realized it wasn't preheated. He went over and asked the welder, well, did you preheat this? He says, oh, we did that on Friday. Okay. It cooled down over the weekend, so there was no effect of preheat.

`§4.p2` Preheat does nothing metallurgically — it just slows the cooling rate so you can get rid of the hydrogen. In the intermediate range, you're slowing the cooling to evolve the hydrogen, get rid of the hydrogen. High preheat, on the other hand, when you're getting above 300 degrees Fahrenheit — which you might have to do on a thick section of HY100 in a four-inch-thick highly restrained joint — when you're doing this you're actually tempering the steel. You're getting a microstructural change. It's cooling down just like this, but it's cooling down above 300 degrees, and above 300 degrees you're going to temper the steel. Remember I told you, you can take a medium carbon steel that has a high carbon equivalent — we haven't talked about carbon equivalent yet — something like this nail file: you can quench it, it'll be fairly brittle and very hard, and then you temper it, you bring it up to 300 to 1000 degrees, typically 300 to 1100 degrees Fahrenheit for a little bit of time, and you soften it.

`§4.p3` If you go to a preheat above 300, you're going to temper and soften, hopefully to less than Rockwell C30, which is a hardness level. At Rockwell C30 — about the minimum strength of something like an HY130 — you usually don't get cracking unless you're very highly restrained. So at higher preheats, greater than 300, you're tempering. At 300 or 400 degrees you're tempering; at 500 you're tempering; at 600 you're tempering. We tend to avoid for metallurgical reasons 600 to a thousand, because you get something called temper embrittlement, which is another problem in carbon steels — not so much in alloy steels, but we try to avoid that. HY80 and HY100 have a lot of alloy addition, because they temper them above a thousand degrees. They don't want to temper them in the 800 degree range in the steel mill, to lower the hardness. You could use less alloying element, save hundreds of dollars a ton, but you're not going to get the same toughness. It's not that the toughness falls off a cliff, it's just not as good, and we always want the best.

## §5. Extreme preheats: cast iron, turbine blades, Sea Wolf [18:41]

`§5.p1` There are different levels of preheat. When you go to some very difficult-to-weld materials, like cast iron, you may have to preheat to 600 degrees Fahrenheit. Who wants to weld on something at 600 degrees Fahrenheit? If you brush up against that — you actually could probably start your cotton clothing on fire if you brushed up against it at 600 degrees. You're getting to the ignition point of a number of organic fibers and stuff. At 600 degrees, any piece of plastic — if you had a polyester shirt, it's not toast, it's melted, and it's liable to start burning. You're getting close to the ignition temperature.

`§5.p2` Some materials are very susceptible to residual stresses and cracking, such as some nickel-based alloys. If I had a turbine blade — I've been to shops where they take turbine blades and they preheat them to a thousand degrees centigrade, which is almost 2000 degrees Fahrenheit, in order to be able to weld them, because just the residual stresses from cooling and shrinkage will crack that turbine blade alloy. It doesn't have very good ductility to begin with, and it has excellent high-temperature strength — that's why you're using it in the turbine. So it won't deform in the turbine, and with that high strength at high temperatures it just cracks. They actually put an induction heater on it; the thing is glowing red when you do the electron beam weld to repair it, and they do it in a vacuum. It's kind of pricey, but that little blade might be worth seven thousand dollars repaired, and it's worth — actually it's worth about fifty dollars scrap metal — but it's worth seven thousand if you can repair it. So it's worth doing some pretty unusual preheats in some alloys, but not steels.

`§5.p3` The highest temperature I've ever seen in steel preheat is about 600, and that's for a very thick highly restrained steel. Most steels are not above about 400. But even though I told you about the Sea Wolf and the blue jelly suits, when they redid the Sea Wolf they didn't want to have it crack a second time, so that's why they went to the blue jelly suits. They actually, I think, shut down production at Electric Boat for about a year. Even that cost a few dollars, because you might be able to lay off the hourly workers but you can't lay off the salaried workers — they still got to come and eat their lunch in the cafeteria. Actually they had a lot to do trying to figure out how to solve the problem.

## §8. Carbon equivalents and zone classification [31:15]

`§8.p1` So if you look and think about it, what else do you have other than those two circles to control? There's the hardness control in the heat affected zone, because that's where the cracks usually occur — except in the Sea Wolf, where it occurred in the weld metal, which was a big surprise, because it had always occurred in heat affected zone before. They didn't have such overmatching weld metal strength.

`§8.p2` So here's the selection of the method, and the first thing you do is you calculate a carbon equivalent. I did mention carbon equivalent before — I think I talked about hardness and hardenability, but I didn't do it in terms of carbon equivalent. The carbon controls the hardness of a steel, whereas the alloying elements control the depth to which hardening occurs. If I have a carbon steel — I told you a carbon steel, it's hard to quench it fast enough to get something much more than a sixteenth of an inch — and that HY80 that I sent around had a heat affected zone that's more like a quarter of an inch. I couldn't even get high hardness over that full heat affected zone in a carbon steel, but in an alloy steel I can. If you remember, I talked about hardness versus hardenability: one was a function of the carbon content, the other one is a function of the thickness and a function of the alloy content.

`§8.p3` The carbon equivalent is basically a measure of how deep I can harden that heat affected zone in that steel with a certain alloy content. The carbon equivalent starts with the carbon. Manganese and silicon don't add as much hardenability as carbon does — you divide by six. Chrome, moly, and vanadium you divide by five. Nickel and copper you divide by 15. This is one of the formulas. There are many formulas. Out of Easterling's book, in an appendix he lists a lot, and I handed this out. `§8.p4` Here's that same carbon equivalent formula, and here are other carbon equivalent formulas that lots of people have developed over the years. So you go through and you look from the carbon equivalent, and it says figure out whether you are in Zones 1, 2, or 3. `§8.p5` You have to go to this plot, which tells you what Zones 1, 2, and 3 are. So here's Zones 1, 2, and 3. Zone classification of steels: carbon content versus carbon equivalent.
