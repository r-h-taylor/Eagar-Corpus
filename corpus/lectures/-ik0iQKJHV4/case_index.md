# Case Index — Lecture 1 (`-ik0iQKJHV4`)

*3.371 Materials Processing and Casting, Summer 2011, session 1 of 7. Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor.*

This is the course-opening lecture. It surfaces a high density of cases as illustrative material for the framing arguments (geometry × function × cost; productivity over time; materials selection). Several major cases that recur throughout the corpus are introduced here in their first-mention form.

## Cases referenced

### Attleboro gold alloy and casting operations
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Attleboro gold alloy and casting operations"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's late-1970s consulting at the Attleboro gold-stock manufacturer; used to make the "time dominates cost" point — gold processing throughput was constrained by interest cost on the bullion, not by manufacturing cost.
- **Era:** late 1970s onward.

### 1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal
- **Anchor:** `§4.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1558 English law against tree-felling for charcoal"
- **Frame:** Part of the English deforestation / energy crisis arc Tom uses to teach material-supply economics. Politicians excluded Kent and Sussex from the ban — "where it really would do any good."
- **Era:** 1558.

### England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`, `§4.p4`, `§4.p8`, `§4.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "England 16th-17th century deforestation and energy crisis"
- **Frame:** The structural setup for why early American iron and glass industries existed. By 1615 England had its first energy crisis; wood for shipbuilding, ironmaking, and glassmaking were in three-way conflict.
- **Era:** 1500s–1615.

### Early American iron and glass industries—British energy crisis relocation
- **Anchor:** `§4.p9`, `§4.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Early American iron and glass industries—British energy crisis relocation"
- **Frame:** Why Jamestown had a glass shop and New England had Saugus Iron Works — energy availability (uncut forests) determined where these industries could exist.
- **Era:** early 1600s.

### Saugus Ironworks
- **Anchor:** `§4.p10`, `§5.p1`, `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Saugus Ironworks"
- **Frame:** First iron works in the United States. Used as the productivity-baseline anchor: one person-year per ton of iron in the 1600s vs. half an hour per man per ton today — the 600,000% improvement figure.
- **Era:** 1640s.

### USS Constitution (Old Ironsides)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p5`, `§4.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Constitution (Old Ironsides)"
- **Frame:** Carolina live oak as the stronger material that let cannonballs bounce off. Used to teach that material selection (different oak species) matters even within a "single material" category.
- **Era:** early 1800s construction.

### HMS Victory (Nelson's flagship)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "HMS Victory (Nelson's flagship)"
- **Frame:** Took 2,100 tons of oak — "a whole forest." Used to quantify the timber demand that conflicted with ironmaking charcoal demand.
- **Era:** late 1700s.

### Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Basic oxygen furnace introduction in Austria"
- **Frame:** Linz, Austria, late 1950s — supersonic pure-oxygen jet replaces 24-hour open hearth with 20-minute carbon burnoff. The productivity revolution in steel refining.
- **Era:** late 1950s.

### Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`, `§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Continuous casting and steel industry capacity collapse"
- **Frame:** Bethlehem Steel yield went from 65% (ingot casting, third of steel recycled) to 95–98% (continuous casting). Tom's first-hand experience of the transition as a Bethlehem employee.
- **Era:** 1960s–1980s.

### Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long factory
- **Anchor:** `§6.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pittsburgh Plate Glass mile-long facility"
- **Frame:** Turn-of-20th-century plate glass production. Mile-long building dictated by the need to melt, anneal, and polish — each step taking real linear distance. Set up as the comparison case for the Pilkington float process.
- **Era:** 1900s.

### Pilkington float glass process
- **Anchor:** `§6.p8`, `§6.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pilkington float glass process"
- **Frame:** English process using molten tin bath to produce flat plate glass without polishing. The technology revolution that obviated the mile-long PPG building.
- **Era:** early 20th century (Tom's framing; the actual Pilkington process is 1950s).

### Corning float glass dual-surface manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§6.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Corning float glass dual-surface manufacturing"
- **Frame:** Computer/TV screen glass — the float process taken further by splitting the sheet in two and re-welding the tin surfaces together so both free surfaces are air-quality. Tom was under NDA for the visit.
- **Era:** Tom's visit not dated; likely 1980s–1990s.

### Glass Flowers degradation
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`, `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Glass Flowers degradation"
- **Frame:** The Blaschka glass flowers at Harvard's Peabody Museum — only 30% still on display because the materials science of long-term glass stability wasn't done. Used as a "materials science you forgot to do" cautionary example.
- **Era:** late 1800s–1930s creation; Tom's visits ongoing.

### Israeli Corvette propeller blade bolt failure
- **Anchor:** `§8.p6`, `§8.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Israeli Corvette propeller blade bolt failure"
- **Frame:** Morgrip bolt with cracks under the head, retrieved from an Israeli Corvette propeller. Used to anchor the bolt-stretch-vs-torque teaching unit.
- **Era:** unspecified.

### Aircraft crankshaft bolt tensioning
- **Anchor:** `§8.p7`, `§8.p8`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aircraft crankshaft bolt tensioning"
- **Frame:** Lycoming / Teledyne Continental connecting-rod bolts ground to ±0.001" tolerance; stretch measured directly to get ±5% clamp-up variation vs. ±30% from torque. The canonical example of "measure stretch, not torque."
- **Era:** ongoing aviation practice.

### Boeing 747-400 fuselage welding porosity issue
- **Anchor:** `§9.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Boeing 747-400 fuselage welding porosity issue"
- **Frame:** Brief reference — Tom says he's heard four MP35N bolts hold the 747 fuselage to the wing. The "wing-to-fuselage" framing as material-selection puzzle (which way is gravity acting).
- **Note:** unclear whether this cluster_id is the right match. Tom's reference is to bolted attachment, not welding. Flagged for the editor.

### USS Nautilus hydrogen cracking
- **Anchor:** `§10.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Nautilus hydrogen cracking"
- **Frame:** First HY-80 submarine, 1950s. Hydrogen cracking problems so severe that early subs were welded with austenitic stainless steel as a workaround. Took 10 years to get under control.
- **Era:** 1950s.

### USS Thresher
- **Anchor:** `§10.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Thresher"
- **Frame:** Tom names the *Thresher* loss as the event that triggered Subsafe. Brief reference, not developed in this lecture.
- **Era:** 1963.

### USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking
- **Anchor:** `§10.p2`, `§10.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking"
- **Frame:** *Seawolf*-class HY-100 cracking around 1990. Tom advised the captains of two pre-Seawolf ships (with HY-100 modules) to keep inspecting. His first PhD student was the Electric Boat welding engineer on those modules.
- **Era:** late 1980s–1990.

### Navy HY130 development sunk-cost fallacy
- **Anchor:** `§10.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Navy HY130 development sunk-cost fallacy"
- **Frame:** Navy spent $50 million in the 1960s developing HY-130 (originally HY-150, downgraded). Tom argued ~1990 to the chief engineer that Seawolf could not be built in HY-130 — going to HY-100 was already a stretch. "Just because you spent $50 million doesn't mean it was worth something."
- **Era:** 1960s development; 1990 controversy.

### U.S. Navy heavy-section titanium submarine program
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "U.S. Navy heavy-section titanium welding (1980s)"
- **Frame:** Tom's first research contract was on welding of heavy-section titanium for the Navy. Mentioned in passing in the relative-cost-of-metals table.
- **Era:** Tom's early career (~late 1970s).

## Cases proposed (not in aggregate v2)

### PROPOSED: Bethlehem Steel I-beam rolling and Manhattan high-rises
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`, `§1.p4`
- **Frame:** Bethlehem learned to roll I-beams around 1900; the resulting structural steel enabled Manhattan to grow beyond 10–12 stories. Tom's first-person framing (he worked at Bethlehem 35 years before this lecture, so ~mid-1970s).
- **Note:** Bethlehem is referenced throughout the corpus; this specific I-beams/Manhattan claim may warrant its own cluster.

### PROPOSED: Bethlehem Steel cost structure 1970s vs. 2010s
- **Anchor:** `§5.p5`
- **Frame:** 45% materials / 45% labor / 10% profit (mid-1970s) → 95% materials / 10% labor / −5% profit (2011). The before-and-after snapshot of the steel-industry labor productivity revolution. Recurring framing figure.

### PROPOSED: MP35N alloy applications and limits
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5`, `§9.p6`, `§9.p7`, `§9.p8`
- **Frame:** Gaylord Smith / International Nickel 1960s invention. 300 ksi tensile with 10% elongation and 50% RA — unprecedented among metals. Cold-work strengthening limits it to wire and small bolts; cannot be made into plate. Tom recommends it for aneurysm clips (non-magnetic, NMR-safe) and notes its use in propellers and reportedly in four 747 wing-attachment bolts.
- **Note:** distinct from "Medical aneurysm clip MRI safety controversy" — this is the material, not the controversy.

### PROPOSED: Accel trolley wire copper-silver casting
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`
- **Frame:** Trolley wire sample for the New Haven-to-Boston Acela upgrade. Cast from high-purity copper-silver alloy; Tom's sample is a reject with only 0.1% silver. The microstructure teaching prop.
- **Note:** likely matches existing cluster "Accel trolley wire for Boston-New Haven electrified rail upgrade" — close-but-not-identical naming. Flag for reconciliation.

### PROPOSED: Tremont Nail Company forged and cut nails
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`
- **Frame:** Massachusetts firm (Mansfield, est. 1819) supplying Tom's nail samples. Used as the historical-baseline teaching prop for the productivity arc.

## Framing figures (not cases)

- **600,000% productivity gain in steel over 400 years** — `§5.p4`. Tom's signature productivity-revolution figure. One person-year per ton (1640s Saugus) → half-hour per ton (2011).
- **Cost-of-fastener-materials table** — `§3.p3`–`§3.p5`. Mild steel = 1; stainless = 2.5; austenitic submarine alloys = 12; titanium = 75; nickel-base = 100. Used throughout the corpus as the relative-cost-of-metals frame.
- **Steel at twenty to twenty-five cents a pound, cheaper than bottled water** — `§2.p3`. Recurring framing comparison.
- **Five-to-six orders of magnitude cost gap, commercial ship to spacecraft** — `§10.p4`. Previewed at the close; developed in later lectures.
