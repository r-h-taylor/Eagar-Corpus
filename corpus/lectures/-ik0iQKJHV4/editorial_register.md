## Sectioning

The lecture divides into ten teaching units:
- §1 sets up the course frame (geometry/function) with the I-beam.
- §2 introduces the trolley-wire microstructure example and the cost-per-ton question.
- §3 develops the Attleboro gold case as the time-dominates-cost exemplar, then runs through the relative-cost table for fastener materials and closes with the Hosler/Augustine framing about why materials get developed.
- §4–§5 are the long historical arc — English deforestation through Saugus to the BOF — that Tom labels explicitly as a "case study" on nails.
- A break falls at the end of §5 (Tom announces "5 or 10 minute break").
- §6 picks up with the Blaschka glass-flowers anecdote and then runs through bullseye → plate → float glass.

The mid-lecture digression (Ho Chi Minh on the Boston gas tank, the Old Manse poem, Sturbridge Village, Tremont Nail Company chocolate, p43–p48) is omitted from layer 3. These are Boston-area sightseeing asides Tom delivers to his Navy students; they carry no teaching content for the materials course. Preserved in layer 2.

## Convention compliance notes

- **Stage directions:** Used five times in this lecture — for the forged impeller, split aluminum plate, I-beam section, trolley wire slice, bolting book search, nail samples, and Home Depot fastener assortment. Each marks a physical-object teaching move.
- **Student attribution:** Three student turns labeled. Two are inferred from clear context (moment-of-inertia answer, ultrasonic bolt-stretch explanation, Navy-diver propeller-blade context). One ("Different oak") is a one-word answer to Tom's question and is given a turn for the same reason — it changes the speaker.
- **Numerals:** Monetary as digits ($500, $50 million, $800). Years as digits. Rhetorical scale spelled out ("six-thousand-fold", "ninety-five percent"). Productivity factor "600,000 percent" rendered as digits with word "percent" because the rhetorical landing depends on the magnitude being legible.
- **Bracketed slip:** §4.p5 "Alfred the Great" → "Lord Nelson" handled in the register rather than in-text, because Tom's self-correction is immediate; the in-text bracket convention is reserved for slips Tom does *not* catch.
- **Backward-references:** None in this lecture (it's the first session of the course).
