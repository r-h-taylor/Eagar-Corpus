## §1. Closing notes on austenitic sensitization [00:01]

`§1.p1` Around these carbides, because the carbides basically suck up all the chromium, you've got these regions that are really not stainless anymore. They've got a bunch of little chromium carbides in there, but they don't have the general chromium in the matrix that's needed to be corrosion resistant. When the material is exposed to atmosphere, you end up with all these regions along the grain boundaries that can be corroded.

`§1.p2` The way to prevent that, as I mentioned earlier, can be done a couple of ways. One is to control temperatures. Another is to use the low carbon alloys — if there's not as much carbon there, there's not as much ability to form the carbides. The other is to stabilize the alloy by using titanium or niobium, as in 321 and 347. What happens there is that the carbides form preferentially with the titanium and the niobium instead of the chromium. The chromium is left behind, and you end up with titanium and niobium carbides at your grain boundaries, and that doesn't lead to a zone that's susceptible to corrosion.

`§1.p3` You want to keep it below 350. So the way you do that is, maybe you make a pass, maybe you have to wait a little while. If you just keep welding right on top of one another, you're going to get your temperatures up too high and you're going to be more susceptible.

## §2. Rapid solidification and the Suutala diagram [02:15]

`§2.p1` A couple of other things are worth mentioning. There's something called rapid solidification effects. This occurs when you're doing a welding process where solidification happens really fast — typically a high energy density process such as laser welding, electron beam welding, or even cladding. Because of the physics of those processes, the solidification of the material is much quicker than if you're doing a typical arc weld. When that happens, there's actually a shift in the solidification modes. So it's important to remember that if you're laser welding or electron beam welding on austenitic stainless steels, that shift matters — and I'll show you some diagrams that demonstrate it.

`§2.p2` This is something called a Suutala diagram. What Suutala did is he figured out — once again, here are these chromium and nickel equivalents — he figured out that at about 1.5, and this goes back to that other diagram that I showed you of cracking susceptibility as well, with standard welding processes, anything above 1.5 you didn't get cracking, whereas below 1.5 you typically got cracking. But when people started using laser and electron beams to weld, they noticed that the Suutala diagram didn't always work.

`§2.p3` More research was done, and something called a modified Suutala diagram was developed. You can see that the solidification mode actually shifts: you need to be somewhere between 1.5 and 1.6 to get to no cracking versus cracking. In the welding of stainless steels, people have developed a lot of these types of diagrams depending on what you're trying to do. The main thing to remember is that if you're welding with a high energy density process, you're going to need to be more worried about cracking than you were otherwise.

## §3. Ferritic stainless steels: composition and applications [04:53]

`§3.p1` I spent the majority of the time on austenitic stainless steels. I want to spend a little bit of time talking about both ferritic and martensitic stainless steels. Next we'll go to the ferritic stainless steel alloys.

`§3.p2` Ferritics are probably the most simple alloys. They're based generally on just the iron-chromium system — iron with enough chromium in it to be not corrosive. They also have a pretty big range of typical compositions depending on what the alloy is going to be used for. The more alloying element you put in it, the more expensive it is. You end up with a wide variety of compositions in all of these alloys.

`§3.p3` As I said earlier, the chromium content in ferritic stainless steels is typically more than ten. It gets you to avoid the gamma loop and puts you over in this region where you're going to be solidifying from the liquid into an alpha or ferritic chromium microstructure.

`§3.p4` There are two types of ferrite. There's delta ferrite and alpha ferrite. Delta ferrite is typically a higher temperature ferrite, and alpha ferrite is a lower temperature ferrite.

`§3.p5` I'm not going to spend a lot of time on this other than to show you that there are different levels of chromium leading to different groups of ferritic stainless steels. The first ones to be developed were the medium chromium stainless steels. 430 is one you would probably hear of a lot — if you hear of ferritic stainless steels, that's the workhorse ferritic stainless steel alloy. Later on, as people were trying to improve things like weldability — the more carbon you have in something, generally the harder it is to weld — they started trying to lower the carbon and the chromium levels a little bit. These are actually the most economical, as I said. They have less chromium in them and less of other alloying elements.

`§3.p6` More recently, people have started developing higher chromium alloys, and these are typically proprietary applications. There's something called E-BRITE. They're higher in purity because of the higher alloying elements — you've got to be more careful about the purity. Because of the higher chromium content, they're also the highest in resisting corrosion. And they're the most expensive.

`§3.p7` Some of the things that the ferritic alloys are typically used for really do depend on the chromium content. Automotive exhaust systems use a lot of ferritic stainless steel. The trim on cars. Where you do have chloride-containing environments and you can't use austenitic stainless steels, the ferritic alloys are a great substitution.

`§3.p8` Some of the limitations you need to remember are the service temperatures — you end up forming some brittle phases in the material when you get up above 750 degrees F. Also, just because of the nature of the alloys, toughness and ductility are not quite as good as with the austenitic alloys. And on the lower chromium alloys, you do have to worry about corrosion resistance. That's why an automotive muffler that's made out of 409, for example, is still going to rust eventually, but it's more corrosion resistant than a plain carbon steel.

## §4. Welding the ferritic alloys: preheat, post-weld, and filler metal [10:10]

`§4.p1` Some things to consider when you're welding the ferritic alloys. Preheat is really dependent on the alloy type. Preheating is intended to slow cooling rates, and reduce shrinkage stresses and the amount of martensite that might form. Many of the ferritic alloys, because of the way they fall on the Schaeffler diagram and other diagrams I showed you, do end up with a little bit of martensite in the structure.

`§4.p2` Post-weld heat treatment can also be used. Sometimes you use that as a way to temper the martensite, which is a very hard phase. Sometimes you form carbides or nitrides that are not desirable, and so you might want to try to dissolve them. And it can also reduce residual stresses.

`§4.p3` For filler metals, a lot of times you use a matching filler metal. Sometimes, depending on the application, you would actually use an austenitic filler metal. High-nickel alloys can also be used depending on the application. If you're trying to improve ductility and toughness, that's when you'd use the austenitic or the nickel-based alloys.

## §5. Cracking and embrittlement in ferritic alloys [12:11]

`§5.p1` There are basically a couple of things you need to worry about in welding the ferritic alloys. One is cracking. The other is various forms of embrittlement. Cracking due to weld solidification and liquation cracking is not as big of a concern in the ferritic alloys as it is in the austenitic alloys. But hydrogen-induced cracking can become a problem if you've got high hydrogen levels in the environment. And intergranular stress corrosion cracking and grain boundary sensitization can also occur in ferritic alloys.

`§5.p2` As I said, there are a few different types of embrittlement that can occur, due to a couple of different mechanisms. It can be due to excessive grain growth in the fusion zone or the heat affected zone. Because you don't have a lot of second phase in a lot of these alloys, as you heat them the grains can grow to a size that leads to embrittlement. You can also get precipitation of carbides and other phases. And embrittlement always reduces toughness.

`§5.p3` Along the lines of embrittlement, there's something called sigma phase. I don't know if you noticed when I had the iron-chromium phase diagram up there — I'll put it back up for just a second. This right here is sigma phase, and it can form in these alloys due to precipitation and due to temperature ranges that are deleterious. As that happens, you can lose toughness and ductility.

`§5.p4` There's also something called 885 degree embrittlement, which is the formation of an alpha prime phase, and it's most rapid at 885 degrees F — that's why it's called that. The main limitation of the ferritic alloys is the temperatures they can be used at.

`§5.p5` Along the lines of what we talked about with the austenitic alloys and the carbide precipitation, you need to watch that a little bit with the ferritic alloys too. Along the grain boundaries, you can lose some corrosion resistance. The same type of sensitization, where you form the chromium-rich carbides, leads to what's called a denuded zone, where there's not enough chromium there to keep it corrosion resistant. So those are the issues with the ferritic stainless steels.
