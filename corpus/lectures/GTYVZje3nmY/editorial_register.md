## Sectioning decisions

The lecture is short (about 17 minutes) but clearly structured around six teaching units:
1. Closing the prior austenitic sensitization discussion (p1–p3)
2. The shift to rapid solidification / Suutala diagram (p4–p6)
3. Ferritic alloys — what they are and where they're used (p7–p14)
4. Welding considerations — preheat, post-weld, filler (p15–p17)
5. Cracking and embrittlement modes (p18–p22)
6. Closing remarks and look-ahead (p23–p24)

§3 is the longest at roughly five minutes and could plausibly be split, but the eight paragraphs all hang together as "what a ferritic stainless steel is and where you'd use one." Split would be artificial. §4 is short (about two minutes) but represents a clear pivot from *what these alloys are* to *how you weld them*, and breaking it out preserves the teaching structure.

## Conventions established by the worked example, applied here

- Filler removed: "um," "uh," "you know" as discourse fillers, "I mean" as restart marker.
- Self-corrections smoothed: e.g. "shrinking — shrinkage stretches [stresses]" reads as "shrinkage stresses" in layer 3.
- Captioner errors silently fixed where unambiguous: "Sutula" → "Suutala" (the Finnish metallurgist Tapio Suutala, whose 1980s ferrite cracking diagrams are standard reference); "E-Brite" → "E-BRITE" (the trade name).
- Numerals: temperatures kept as digits with unit ("350," "750 degrees F," "885 degrees F"). Alloy designations kept as digits ("321," "347," "430," "409"). Compositional thresholds kept as decimals ("1.5," "1.7," "1.5 and 1.6").
