# Case Index — Lecture 12

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§4.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is primarily a theory-and-method session on drawing and necking, with two extended forensic cases (the gas well steel weld and the black light bulb) and several recurring industrial touchstones (Coca-Cola cans, Corning optical fiber, Sylvania light bulbs).

## Cases in this lecture

### Coca-Cola can manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§1.p3`–`§1.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Coca-Cola can manufacturing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used to illustrate multi-stage deep drawing plus ironing in one stroke, with five-micron die tolerances and supercomputer design.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum (US), steel (Japan); carbide ironing rings
- **Era:** contemporary (2012)

### Plastic two-liter soda bottle pressure dependence
- **Anchor:** `§1.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Plastic soda bottle pressure dependence"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Demonstration of how internal pressure stabilizes thin-walled containers.

### Gas well steel weld brittle fracture (forensic)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`–`§4.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Gas well steel weld brittle fracture"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's notched-vs-smooth 1045 tensile bar experiment to demonstrate that triaxiality alone — not material brittleness — can switch fracture mode from ductile dimples to cleavage. Used to refute the opposing experts' claim that brittle-looking fracture proved brittle material.
- **Materials/systems:** 1045/1046 plain carbon steel; gas well downhole weld
- **Era:** circa 2010-2011 (lecture says "year and a half ago")

### Lüders strains / stretcher strains in mild steel
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`–`§3.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Automotive sheet metal stretcher strain defects"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Historical case (pre-1960) of nitrogen-induced Lüders strains creating visible bands on painted automotive sheet metal. Resolved by improved steelmaking that removes nitrogen.
- **Materials/systems:** mild steel sheet; automotive body panels
- **Era:** pre-1960 problem; ongoing research through 1970s

### TRIP steels (transformation-induced plasticity)
- **Anchor:** `§2.p5`–`§2.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "PROPOSED: TRIP steels for automotive deep drawing"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Recent (early 2010s) automotive steel development achieving 50%+ necking strain via martensitic transformation, as an alternative to superplasticity.

### Corning optical fiber manufacturing
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Optical fiber manufacturing (Corning)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Functionally graded glass rod made by plasma-spraying glass frit on a lathe, then dieless-drawn using a temperature gradient as the "die". Exploits glass's Newtonian behavior (m = 1).
- **Materials/systems:** functionally graded silica glass
- **Era:** mature technology, ongoing

### Optical fiber transatlantic repeater reduction
- **Anchor:** `§6.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Optical fiber transatlantic repeater reduction"
- **Frame in this lecture:** From "couple hundred yards" range for early fibers to "thousands of miles without losing more than half" today, enabling sparse undersea repeater stations.

### Black light bulb eye injury case (forensic)
- **Anchor:** `§8.p5`–`§8.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Black light bulb eye injury case"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Korean hand-blown black light bulb shattered when soapy water dripped on hot bulb; teenager lost an eye. Tom proposed a drop test he wasn't allowed to run himself; students ran it and confirmed residual stresses caused the failure. Case settled.
- **Materials/systems:** hand-blown incandescent bulb glass; non-uniform wall thickness
- **Era:** undated (Southern California attorney call)

- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Professor Omen's chemically tempered glass demonstration"
- **Frame in this lecture:** A piece of bowed chemically tempered glass that could be flattened by hand without breaking, demonstrating favorable compressive surface stresses. Name uncertain ("Owen" or "Omen").

### Bob Rose light bulb classroom demonstration
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bob Rose light bulb classroom demonstration"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Thesis advisor Bob Rose stopped at the Sylvania Beverly plant before 3.091 lectures to collect freshly made light bulbs, which he then threw across 10-250 — they bounced because moisture had not yet attacked the surface.
- **Materials/systems:** soda-lime glass bulbs; surface silicate hydroxide attack
- **Era:** 1970s

### Freshly made light bulb strength demonstration
- **Anchor:** `§9.p1`–`§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Freshly made light bulb strength demonstration"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Combined with the Bob Rose anecdote; explains why fiberglass is coated with epoxy immediately after drawing — to preserve the 200 ksi surface strength before moisture corrosion creates micron-scale flaws that destroy fracture toughness.

## Figures referenced

- **Glass fracture toughness** "less than one ksi square root of inch" (§9.p3) — framing figure for why surface flaws are catastrophic in glass.
- **Fiberglass strength** "200 ksi if you actually could measure them" (§9.p1) — surface-preserved glass fiber theoretical strength.
- **Coke can wall thickness** 9 mil blank → 3.5 mil sidewall via two draws plus ironing (§1.p4).
- **Strain hardening exponent** typical values: 0.15 high-strength steel, 0.2 low-carbon steel, 0.25 lower-strength steel, 0.5 martensitic stainless / TRIP (§2.p5).
- **Lüders band angle** 55° to tensile axis from Mohr's circle plane-strain construction (§5.p3).
