## §3. Defining factor of safety: failure load over allowable load [09:59]

`§3.p1` The factor of safety — I did want to bring in the definitions at the beginning so we're clear as to what people say it is. So "safety factor" is what I tend to use, but when I go to the books, they talk about the "factor of safety." And there are two expressions for it. One is the failure load over the allowable load. To be very clear here, failure load — we can run a test for that. We can take an aircraft wing, a car, anything you want, pressurize a pipe until it bursts. This is the number, this is what it takes as far as failure load.

`§3.p2` Now, are you always going to make the pipe the same way? Is it always going to have the same strength? That's really unclear. The more you introduce human factors like welding or bolting, the more you reduce your certainty as to what the failure load is. That definition doesn't really tell you whether you're taking the minimum failure load or the maximum — it's the failure load no matter how you calculate it. The allowable load — that's the example here. This ended up being subjected to a four percent volume variation. It didn't matter too much how strong the copper was, the water wanted to grow, and this is what happened to it.

`§3.p3` It's not something that was included in the design. You've got 80 psi of pressure from the city; if somebody has a zone pump because they have their own well, maybe they'll pressurize it up to 100 psi, something like that. But that's about as far as it's going to get if you don't have freezing. So you can design for that, say 50 years, it kind of works out. But maybe you have bends with a piping system. If you put 400 feet of that copper in a basement or an attic, and you still protect it from freezing but it has temperature variation, the whole pipe may want to grow by an inch, two inches from one season to the other.

`§3.p4` And if you restrain it on each side — say it's going to a concrete wall, or it has an elbow with a very tight clamp — you can have a problem. You can create bending loads at the ends, or wherever you have a T connection, because you didn't think about the whole pipe trying to grow back and forth. In large condominium complexes it happens, but it's more in the vertical direction. So people have risers; they go all the way from the basement to the attic. And if you clamp the pipe too hard top and bottom, you can end up with uncontrolled displacement of the riser.

`§3.p5` *[Belmar draws a riser pipe at the board.]* So I'm going to hook it up here at the bottom, it goes to where the concrete floor is. And on top of all this I have these clamps that are just holding it laterally, and it bends over and distributes. Now, say we're going to make this a 10-story building. I'm making it up, but it's a hundred feet. If I have a little pipe here that comes to any utility at this location, the T will want to move up and down. I'm going to put it half an inch because I want to be very conservative. It's in the building, so say you turn the heat off to 50 degrees of indoor temperature in the wintertime because nobody lives there.

`§3.p6` Half an inch. This is my pipe right here. So if I'm holding this here because that's the utility, and then I'm moving half an inch here, it'll be a big problem because it's stiff, it is a metal connection, so it has to bend permanently, make plastic deformation back and forth. And it's not something that's included in any of these allowable loads. Now, the two issues with that are: do you need to make the material more compliant so it can take these variations, or do you have to make sure nobody does something like this? That really becomes an unknown issue for each industry. But just to bring it into perspective, that allowable load in general is not going to include things like this that will happen.

`§3.p7` The code actually says you have to account for thermal expansion when you lay out your system, but these situations do happen. So already you can get the idea that because we don't take into account things like this, we don't take into account freezing — there's so many things we don't take into account in design that we do need a safety factor.

## §4. Factor of ignorance, water hammer, and welded joints [16:57]

`§4.p1` Now, some people will call it the "factor of ignorance." And I want to be cautious with that. Obviously, as you know more about your failure load and you know more about your allowable load, you can reduce the multiplication factor that you put onto it. That's true. At the same time, when we have a code like the boiler and pressure vessel code, the safety factor of four — that's not the level of ignorance. A good part of it is there for water hammer. Water hammer is a condition you can have with steam pipes when you get too much condensate into them, and then you start building up enough clotting in the pipe that eventually the water will travel as slugs. And you can multiply the local pressure in the pipe by a factor of two or three easily. It's not that hard.

`§4.p2` And it can happen, and you don't want the piping system to fall apart because of that. But it's not included in the calculated load. If the code tells you you're designing for 200 psi of gas pressure, you can put 200 psi in it. But then the water hammer comes in, and you're up maybe to 600. And you still want the pipe to work. You can't tolerate a sudden fracture just because you had a little water hammer. You can't control all these conditions.

`§4.p3` And then part of what is left is not so much material property but joining. The welded connections in general are not as strong as the pipes. There are some reduction factors. You're supposed to put a doubler plate, like Professor Eagar mentioned yesterday. But even if you put a doubler plate, you have to weld that doubler plate into the main pipe. And at that corner you are reducing the strength locally. It's not the same as just having a plain, broad pipe. That's something that came out in the spring. I think you asked me this — you wanted to build a pressure vessel out of spring steel.

**Student:** Yeah.

So the first part of the idea is very clever.

`§4.p4` *[Belmar draws a cylindrical pressure vessel.]* We take a cylinder like this. Let's make it complete. So we have this cylinder, and we want to put pressure into it, and say we want to get as much pressure as we can for the weight of that vessel. That's a great idea — you can go to very high yield strength steel and have a thin wall. But then the question is, what are you going to do with these ends? Because you need to cap this, or maybe you even have a seam here depending on your design. You have to put a lap — maybe it's made out of a sheet that you fold over, and then you have a longitudinal joint. That is where your strength will go down. No matter what you do, if you bolt it, you can't do it. So you have welding and bolts, that's pretty much what it boils down to. And it will reduce the strength in both directions. Obviously your stress is going to be higher in the circle around the circumference than longitudinally.

`§4.p5` So in principle this joint is not as big of a trouble. If you reduce the strength by a factor of two, then you become balanced. But there is a transverse effect. So if I put this weld here, it will reduce the strength of the metal in the direction of the bead of the weld. And that's where the strength reduction comes in. And the boiler and pressure vessel code will not tell you, well, use a thicker piece of steel here if you have a welded joint — you have welded joints in general. So it needs to have this built-in reduction factor. It's not so much an excess amount of metal, it's just taking into account typical practice.

`§4.p6` You could change the boiling pressure vessel [boiler and pressure vessel] code to a smaller value if you came up with a way where you wouldn't need these welds to put sections of pipe together. On a threaded pipe system, that's something you get rid of if you have a threaded joint at this location. I don't think you can do that on a big vessel, but for a pipe then you can still design to the maximum pressure of that pipe if you can make your threaded connection resist the same force. So that's still not given.

## §5. Industry comparisons: levees, aircraft, buildings, automotive [22:46]

`§5.p1` So we're back here with respect to the different industries. I'm sure Professor Eagar started to introduce some of these values to you. If you have a levee — the New Orleans levee that came down with Katrina — it was designed with a safety factor of 1.3 because there's only so much dirt you can put. The force exerted by the water growing on top is very well known. It's just gravity times the height. The less understood aspect of this was the soil properties over time. So people made some assumptions a long time ago as to how much shear the soil was going to be able to take. But in terms of a full-scale assembly, it's very hard to do that test. So there's a lot of uncertainty as to what exactly a full-sized structure levee would be able to resist in terms of shear.

`§5.p2` My understanding — I was not really involved in this — is that's where the mistake was, that they assumed that the soil was going to be able to resist more than it actually was when it came all wet. So was the safety factor 1.3 good enough? It would be good enough if you had the right material properties. So it's always tied together. How precisely can you evaluate your material properties, and how conservative are you in your material properties? That is influencing the safety factor. You can keep the 1.3 safety factor if you say, well, I'm really going to do more testing. I'm going to take more samples of that soil, get it wet and do the shear test, and I'll put those properties back into my design, as opposed to making an estimate based on one sample of data, or no testing at all sometimes.

`§5.p3` So it really varies. I've been exposed to soil sampling mostly for corrosion resistance, and it varies a lot. You can have multiple samples on one site. You have one area that's wet, the other area that's dry, and the resistivity of the soil is going to be different. So there's always some uncertainty there, and you have to build that into account when you do your corrosion design.

`§5.p4` Aircraft structures — I put a 1.5 safety factor on it because that's the number that kept coming back. I worked in the airframe business for a little bit, and there's a range. The landing gear is designed with a safety factor of about 1.2. There are reasons for that. The main reason is, it's heavy. And it's critical. And if you think about those two aspects: you can determine the load. There's only so fast you can run into the ground. And you can only break so fast.

`§5.p5` So if you think about the load being very well predetermined, there's no welding, so the material strength is well known. And then you do very detailed stress analysis of the whole assembly. So with these three, and you have very high quality control going all over the place with this design — when you think about all of this, everything is known and estimated in a conservative way. So you just put a 20% on it, and everything is good to go. Landing gears are very reliable. More than some other things that may be designed with safety factor three or four.

`§5.p6` Because that safety factor sometimes means nothing when you don't take into account the joining, the excess loads, everything that is sort of assumed possible to happen but not necessarily in combination. There's a lot of that. If you make one mistake on a design, on manufacturing, you can be okay, but when you start combining these mistakes, that's really where the problem is. You consume your 20% or 50% or whatever your margin is. So you really want to avoid that.

`§5.p7` The piping in general for buildings — that's what we were talking about here. Not so much this example because this is a pipe, so it's got a bigger safety factor. But 1.5 to two is typical for a building. It's not always the same. You're going to go to 1.5 if you start taking into account snow load, wind load, and you become a little bit more precise as to what the load is. If you're very general, then you may be at a safety factor of two. Pressure vessels we talked about quite a bit — it's not really a safety factor of four. So if you want a very safe design, obviously, you make it super heavy and you make it cost a lot.

`§5.p8` In automotive, the rough figure — and that's a number from Professor Eagar, I think it is the material selection module — the cost of adding a pound of weight to an automotive is about two dollars for the life cycle. That's fuel, more brakes, repair, bigger wheels, tires, all factored in, it comes to about two dollars. So it's more than the price of the steel to begin with. So you may be willing to put a little bit more money on the initial material if you can reduce the weight. That's the whole concept of material selection. But there is a limit to it.

`§5.p9` And there's also a question of what's your acceptable failure rate. If a car is designed with four seats, and somebody decides to load it with lead shot on the back seat, go for a ride, and bust the rear suspension — it's designed essentially, if you break the coils or if you compress the coils too much, normally there's a seat on which it'll stop. So you're not losing a wheel. You are losing your suspension, and you're out of alignment, your camber is changing, but there's some form of fail-safe factor in there.

`§5.p10` It's the same with a tire. We make these tires able to resist pressures much higher than what you put in them for service. But it does happen that a tire blows off. But it doesn't mean an accident is going to happen for sure. You can be unlucky if you're turning and that's when the tire comes off. It's all things that we are willing to live with because we know it's there. You're not supposed to take a curve at the maximum speed you can take with your wheels on, because if you lose a wheel, if you lose a tire, you're losing control.

`§5.p11` And I'm not here to tell you, you know, don't do this at an individual level, but from an engineering standpoint, there's a limit. You can't design for all abuse. I can take a car and break it if I want to. I'm probably going to be hurt. That's how the car industry — and I worked quite a bit with ATVs, the four-wheelers — it's a little bit how they think of this. They think, I'm going to make it strong enough. But if you run into a tree, it's actually better if the whole thing deforms on you and breaks, as opposed to staying rigid. It can't really stay rigid, it would be too heavy. Nobody would want that car, or ATV, or motorcycle, on the driveway. That'd be completely uncomfortable, you get stuck everywhere. You don't want extra weight that you don't need.

`§5.p12` But that's where the line is. As far as reliability, there's always going to be a limit when you get into misuse and abuse. For cars, I think a good overall criterion is what you call a crash. If you get into a crash, it's perfectly fine to wreck the car. It's more if you are still in control, then you don't want to lose a wheel or break a piece of suspension, unless you have way too much weight on it.

`§5.p13` So we are not making structures that much stronger than they need to be when we say factor of safety of two, three, four. You can make it ten, but if you calculated differently, you may end up being the same design. So what we're getting to at this point is the factor of safety is tied to how exactly you do the design. If I'm doing the design in a very detailed way, then I use a lower safety factor essentially. So the factor of ignorance is true, although we know what we're not accounting for. It's not that much of an ignorance.

## §6. Speedboat propeller shaft fatigue failure [34:29]

`§6.p1` So if we do design, we have the demand — in this case that's the freezing water, the pressure in the freezing water — and the capacity. When we do tests on the copper, we get yield strain, ultimate strain, elongation, all sorts of things. And there's variation each way.

`§6.p9` But I thought it was an interesting concept because it shows you — you still have a safety factor of three when it leaves the factory, because it doesn't have a crack in it. `§6.p10` But in general, I would say you would like to think, especially for something like this, that you take it out on the ocean and your service person at the end of the season should be able to tell you, oh, this has been running so many hours, we've got to change this, we've got to change that. `§6.p11` It's to make sure that it doesn't fall below a certain criterion. And for those blades I would say also, at the beginning you do have a safety factor, it's going down, it's not good forever. So it just makes you more aware of what a safety factor is. It's tied to the design.
