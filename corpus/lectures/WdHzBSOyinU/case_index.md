## Cases referenced

### Attleboro gold jewelry manufacturer (largest in world) inventory shortfall
- **Anchor:** `§7.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Attleboro gold alloy and casting operations" / "Gold jewelry manufacturer inventory shortfall"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom worked 10–15 years with the largest gold jewelry manufacturer in the world, in Attleboro, MA. They went through seven tons of gold per year. His consulting project ended after an inventory shortfall in the early 1990s.
- **Materials/systems:** gold, casting and forming
- **Era:** ~1980 – early 1990s

### Attleboro platinum wedding ring manufacturer (high-power laser kerf reduction)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p8`, `§7.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Attleboro platinum wedding ring laser kerf reduction
- **Frame in this lecture:** Second-largest jeweler in the world (also Attleboro). They slice thick-walled platinum tube and turn down rings on screw machines. Switched from mechanical cutting (0.8 mm kerf) to high-power laser cutting (1/4 the kerf), $3–4M laser paid back in two months. Carrying cost on raw materials drives processing decisions in this industry.
- **Materials/systems:** platinum, laser cutting
- **Era:** ~2012 (Tom toured the plant "two weeks ago")

### Eagar wife's engagement ring (electron-beam-melted platinum-iridium)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Eagar wife's engagement ring (electron beam melt, Building 8)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom made his wife's engagement ring himself, electron-beam-melting platinum-iridium on the 4th floor of MIT Building 8 with an old radar power supply, nearly electrocuting himself. Used as illustration of unique vs. high-volume manufacturing.
- **Materials/systems:** platinum-iridium, electron beam melting

### Mike Tanyon's laminated silver wedding band
- **Anchor:** `§7.p10`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** PROPOSED: Tanyon laminated silver wedding band
- **Frame in this lecture:** Colleague Mike Tanyon made his wedding band by diffusion-bonding/laminating layers of sterling silver and silver, rolling and twisting and machining. Companion example to Tom's own ring for unique-object manufacturing.
- **Materials/systems:** sterling silver, diffusion bonding
- **Note:** spelling of "Tanyon" flagged for editor review.

### Backofen rediscovery of superplasticity (1960s)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`, `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Backofen superplasticity rediscovery"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Backofen, working at MIT in the 1960s on fine-grain-size effects, rediscovered superplasticity. Found a 1938 German paper that had been lost during WWII. Tom inherited Backofen's original samples when he took over his office as an assistant professor and uses them as teaching artifacts.
- **Materials/systems:** fine-grained metals, tensile testing
- **Era:** 1938 (original German work) / early 1960s (MIT rediscovery)

### German superplasticity research (1938, lost in WWII)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p8`, `§5.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "German superplasticity research (1938, lost in WWII)" / "German superplastic tensile bar (1934)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Companion to the Backofen case. Original superplasticity measurement on fine-grain material in a 1938 German paper, lost in the war.

### Single-crystal turbine blades (GE/Pratt & Whitney)
- **Anchor:** `§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Single-crystal turbine blades (GE/Pratt & Whitney)" / "Pratt & Whitney single-crystal turbine blade"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Physical demonstration. A 20-year-old single-crystal turbine blade ("no grain boundaries"), worth $6,000–$7,000, ~100 per wheel. Used to motivate the grain-boundary discussion that frames Backofen vs. Grant's research programs.
- **Materials/systems:** nickel single crystal, turbine engine

### Iron whiskers (1950s screw dislocation studies)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Iron whiskers (1950s screw dislocation studies)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1950s growth of single-crystal iron whiskers containing only a screw dislocation along the axis. Pulled to 2 million PSI experimental strength — the theoretical strength of perfect crystal steel. Used to argue that physicists' claims about carbon nanotube strength (the "rope to the moon") are decades behind what materials scientists already demonstrated and refuted.
- **Materials/systems:** iron single crystal
- **Era:** 1950s

### Carbon nanotubes structural applications (overpromised research)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`, `§9.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Carbon nanotubes structural applications (overpromised research)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's argument that carbon nanotubes' theoretical 2–3 million PSI strength is irrelevant because any real nanotube contains atomic vacancies (guaranteed by the second law of thermodynamics above 0 K), which act as defects and drop strength by ~10×. Physicists "getting billions of dollars to study carbon nanotubes" called "money down a rat hole" for structural use; allows for functional/display applications.
