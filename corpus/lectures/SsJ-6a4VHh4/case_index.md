# Case Index — Lecture 8 (3.371 Fall 2016)

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p2`). Click-through reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

This lecture is the fracture-mechanics centerpiece of the course. It uses the WWII Liberty ship failures to motivate the development of the field, then walks through ratio-analysis diagrams for steel, aluminum, and titanium, with forensic cases anchoring each.

## Cases

### Liberty ships and SS Schenectady
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`, `§3.p2`, `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Liberty ships and SS Schenectady"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Origin story for fracture mechanics. 4,694 welded merchant vessels built in WWII; 970 (~20%) suffered fracture casualties; 24 had complete strength-deck fractures, 8 lost, 26 lives lost. USS Schenectady fractured at the dock; SS Esso Manhattan fractured mid-Atlantic.
- **Materials/systems:** welded steel ship hull, low-temperature brittle fracture
- **Era:** WWII (1941–1945), report 1946

### 1952 T2 tanker fracture off Cape Cod
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1952 T2 tanker fracture off Cape Cod"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Seven- or eight-year-old T2 tanker broke completely in two off Cape Cod in 1952; most of the crew survived. Tom references the Hollywood adaptation *The Finest Hours*.
- **Materials/systems:** welded steel ship hull
- **Era:** 1952

### British Welding Institute founding
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`, `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "British Welding Institute founding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Founded in response to the WWII brittle-fracture problem; today one of the premier welding research centers. Alan Wells became director general; wrote Tom's tenure letter.
- **Era:** post-WWII

### Egyptian obelisk engineering mystery
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Egyptian obelisk engineering mystery (Al Bakun / Wendell Wilkenning fracture analysis)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Wendell W. Wilkening's 1976 MIT PhD thesis. With 1976 knowledge of stone toughness, an obelisk being raised from horizontal should fracture under its own weight from natural flaws. Wilkening found micro-cracking ahead of the crack tip explains how it didn't.
- **Materials/systems:** granite (three-mineral composite); fracture toughness specimen
- **Era:** 1976 (research), antiquity (object)

### Alaska Pipeline brittle fracture risk assessment
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Alaska Pipeline brittle fracture risk assessment"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom worked at Bethlehem Steel on high-toughness steel for the Alaskan pipeline. 70 foot-pound Charpy requirement to stop a running brittle crack, motivated by prior pipeline fractures that ran 30 miles.
- **Materials/systems:** pipeline steel
- **Era:** mid-1970s

### Quincy Shipyard liquid natural gas tanker construction
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`, `§6.p3`, `§6.p4`, `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Quincy Shipyard liquid natural gas tanker construction (mid-1970s)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Five or six LNG tankers built at Quincy with aluminum spheres on steel skirts. Coast Guard required 20 foot-pound Charpy; Sparrows Point plates averaged 20.5 — "just barely making it." Shipyard then cherry-picked 30 foot-pound plates for the weld-test runoff tabs. Foam insulation failure led to Coast Guard non-certification; ships sat unused for years off Newport.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum LNG sphere, normalized steel skirt
- **Era:** mid-1970s
- **Related clusters:** "LNG tanker shipyard plate selection and testing circumvention", "LNG vessel heat treatment coupon substitution and testing failure", "Quincy LNG tanker aluminum tank skirts"

### North Andover hot isostatic press explosion
- **Anchor:** `§7.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "North Andover hot isostatic press explosion"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 10-inch-thick pressure vessel designed for 12-inch critical flaw size; manufacturing errors reduced critical flaw size to half an inch. Catastrophic failure ejected a 16-ton piece a quarter mile, landing in an industrial area at 2 a.m. with no injuries.
- **Materials/systems:** thick-section steel pressure vessel
- **Era:** unspecified (Tom's "up here in North Andover" framing suggests local Massachusetts case)

### Northridge earthquake
- **Anchor:** `§8.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Northridge earthquake"
- **Frame in this lecture:** $5–10 billion in building damage from steels lacking a fracture-toughness specification. Building toughness specs only entered civil engineering codes by 2000, decades after the Coast Guard and Navy required them.
- **Materials/systems:** building structural steel
- **Era:** 1994 (Tom says "1990" — minor date slip preserved per convention)

### Rail steel derailments
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Rail steel derailments"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Rail steel specifications, developed circa 1900–1920, still lack fracture-toughness requirements; rail companies have not been forced to modernize. Example of code lag.
- **Materials/systems:** rail steel
- **Era:** specs from 1900–1920; current

### Bell Helicopter rotor blade corrosion fatigue failure (Brazil)
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`, `§9.p3`, `§9.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Bell Helicopter rotor blade corrosion fatigue failure (Brazil)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Helicopter rotor spar failed in Brazil. Initial diagnosis blamed surface sanding scratches; Tom's fracture-mechanics calculation four years later showed grinding scratches were too shallow to explain failure. Reread the photos: stress corrosion cracking under inadequate paint repair produced a 3 mm crack that did fit fracture mechanics. Tom's deposition led to the manufacturer withdrawing its alert service bulletin.
- **Materials/systems:** high-strength aluminum rotor spar, stress corrosion cracking
- **Era:** failure date unspecified; deposition four years later

### Aluminum 7075 stress-corrosion cracking in aircraft structures

- **Frame in this lecture:** Generic mechanism behind the Brazilian rotor spar failure; mentioned without alloy specified, but the high-strength aluminum + paint-protection failure pattern matches the 7075 cluster.
- **Materials/systems:** high-strength aluminum

### US Navy heavy-section titanium submarine program
- **Anchor:** `§9.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "US Navy heavy-section titanium submarine program"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Ti-100 is the Navy alloy for titanium submersibles; same ratio-analysis story as steel, with one-inch critical flaw size at the strength of interest. "They've never had the money to build" — program never executed.
- **Materials/systems:** Ti-100 alloy, submarine hull
- **Era:** ongoing through Tom's career

## Figures referenced

These are framing statistics, not cases:

- **WWII merchant ship fracture statistics:** 4,694 ships, 970 casualties (~20%), 24 strength-deck fractures, 8 lost, 26 lives lost (§3.p4).
- **Charpy energy spec evolution:** 10 ft-lb (1946) → 15 (1950s) → 20 (1960s Coast Guard) → 70 (Alaska Pipeline arrest of running crack) (§6.p1).
- **Critical flaw size scaling:** ~1 inch at low-strength high-toughness steel → ~1 mm at high-strength steel; aluminum diagram scaled down by factor of 5 (§7.p4, §8.p4, §9.p1).
- **NDT detection limit:** ~3 mm typical (§8.p4).
- **Material classes by cost:** metals ~$400/ton, concrete $20–50/ton (§10.p5).
- **Steel-to-concrete ratio in reinforced concrete:** ~10% by weight, because steel is ~10× the strength of concrete (§10.p2).
- **Billion-ton-per-year club:** steel 1.6, concrete 4.2 (§10.p6).
