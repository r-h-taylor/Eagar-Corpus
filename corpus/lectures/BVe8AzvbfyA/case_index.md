## Cases referenced in this lecture

### Ceramic coating on turbine blades thermal stress
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`–`§2.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Ceramic coating on turbine blades thermal stress"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Used as the canonical dissimilar-material thermal-mismatch problem. Zirconia chosen because its thermal expansion matches that of metals; bond coat deliberately porous to absorb strain.
- **Materials/systems:** zirconia / nickel-base superalloy / plasma-sprayed nickel bond coat
- **Era:** ongoing (modern turbine practice)

### Doctoral research on ceramic-metal joint porosity optimization
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`, `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Doctoral research on ceramic-metal joint porosity optimization"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's student's thesis showing strength peaks near 10% interfacial porosity for ceramic-metal joints; strain-energy framework as the diagnostic.
- **Materials/systems:** ceramic-metal joints generally
- **Era:** ~2003 (Tom says "ten years ago")

### Farberware cooking pot thermal warping
- **Anchor:** `§2.p3` (analogy reference)
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Farberware cooking pot thermal warping"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief callback. Used as the everyday analogue for deliberately incomplete bonding in turbine coatings — aluminum-to-stainless cold bond with 10% bonded area to allow for thermal mismatch.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum base / stainless steel pan
- **Era:** consumer product (developed mid-20th c.)

### Pentium 6 / Early 1990s large-format chip bonding
- **Anchor:** `§3.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Pentium 6 / Early 1990s large-format chip bonding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Strain-energy scaling argument made concrete: chips cannot exceed ~1 cm on a side because the 30–40°C thermal gradient required to extract heat exceeds bond capacity. Intel's solution was two chips rather than one larger chip.
- **Materials/systems:** silicon chip / substrate bonding
- **Era:** ~2001

### Motorola Defense Division classified large-format chip bonding
- **Anchor:** `§3.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Motorola Defense Division classified large-format chip bonding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Classified counterexample to the 1 cm limit: in the mid-90s DoD made 1- to 2-inch chips because they needed short interconnect distances for speed. Tom uses this to mark the limit of what strain energy permits.
- **Materials/systems:** large-format silicon chips / substrate
- **Era:** mid-1990s

### Rocky Flats activated diffusion bonding of maraging steel
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`–`§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Rocky Flats activated diffusion bonding of maraging steel"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Worked example of plating a soft, oxide-resistant interlayer (silver) to permit low-temperature diffusion bonding of high-strength steel that would lose its martensitic structure at the temperatures needed for direct bonding. O'Brien, Rice and Olson research paper cited.
- **Materials/systems:** 200 ksi maraging steel / silver interlayer
- **Era:** nuclear-warhead-era (Cold War)

### Etruscan gold bead diffusion bonding
- **Anchor:** `§5.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Etruscan gold bead diffusion bonding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Cited as evidence that soldering predates the modern process by thousands of years; the joint was effectively a transient liquid phase diffusion bond.
- **Materials/systems:** gold (Etruscan earrings, King Tut's dagger), tin solder
- **Era:** antiquity

### 3D-printed solder droplet circuit boards research
- **Anchor:** `§5.p6`–`§5.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "3D-printed solder droplet circuit boards research"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's collaboration with Prof. Sonin (MIT mechanical engineering) on jetting lead-tin solder droplets to print circuit boards. Used to illustrate how Young's equation breaks down outside equilibrium and simple geometry. "Tens and hundreds of millions of dollars" of industry effort have failed at this for twenty years.
- **Materials/systems:** lead-tin solder droplets, copper substrate
- **Era:** early 1990s onward

### Lead-tin solder obsolescence and lead-free transition
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Lead-tin solder obsolescence and lead-free transition"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Brief context. Fifteen years of industry/government effort to find a lead-free replacement has not produced anything that wets as well or as fast as lead-tin.
- **Materials/systems:** lead-tin and lead-free solders
- **Era:** ~1998–present

### VAX 9000 bonding failure
- **Anchor:** `§8.p1`–`§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "VAX 9000 bonding failure"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Extended case study. TAB-bonded chips made in Cupertino, then outer-lead bonded three to four weeks later in Arizona, well past the one-hour cleanliness window. With 400 I/O bonds per chip, yield was near zero. Tom uses this as the consequential example of the "bond within an hour" rule from §6–§7. The technical failure compounded Digital Equipment's bankruptcy.
- **Materials/systems:** TAB (tape automated bonding) tape, copper, lead-tin solder
- **Era:** late 1980s
- **Linked cases (in this lecture):** Digital Equipment Corporation bankruptcy (§8.p4–§8.p6)

### Digital Equipment Corporation bankruptcy
- **Anchor:** `§8.p4`–`§8.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Digital Equipment Corporation bankruptcy"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Strategic-management coda to the VAX 9000 case. Ken Olsen's dismissal of RISC ("RISC is a fad") combined with the failed VAX 9000 mainframe play against IBM's $40B business. Hewlett Packard, which adopted RISC, captured the printer market and grew accordingly. MIT divested its 7% Digital stake "well before they could have made a few hundred million."
- **Era:** late 1980s through mid-1990s bankruptcy

## Figures referenced

- **200–300°C yield-stress threshold (§1.p3):** Recurring framing figure — the temperature differential required to produce yield-level residual stresses in steel via α∆T. Anchors much of Tom's welding-residual-stress argument across the corpus.
- **10⁻⁵/°C steel thermal expansion (§1.p2):** Constant frequently invoked.
- **0.002 yield strain for steel (§1.p2):** Standard.
- **1 cm chip-bonding limit (§3.p5):** Tom returns to this figure as the practical consequence of the strain-energy scaling argument.
- **One-hour cleaning-to-soldering window (§6.p7, §8.p3):** Military spec, used as the rhetorical pivot from §6 (the principle) to §8 (the catastrophic violation).
- **$50,000 wetting-balance machine (§7.p3):** Lab-instrument price point, recurs across Tom's bench-equipment asides.
- **Solder wetting time ~0.5 s clean / >10 s dirty (§6.p6):** Quantitative anchor for the cleanliness argument.
