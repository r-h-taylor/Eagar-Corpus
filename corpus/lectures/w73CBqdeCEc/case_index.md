# Case Index — Lecture 12

*Citation format: layer 3 section.paragraph anchor. The lecture is a working-through of welding-procedure selection for alloy steels, threaded with several historical and forensic cases that anchor the teaching.*

## Cases referenced

### America's Cup yacht keel beam — 4340 steel welding procedure
- **Anchor:** `§6.p1`–`§6.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "America's Cup 4340 welding procedure development"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Tom's consulting case with Prof. Jerry Ingram (MIT Course 13) on a David Koch–funded America's Cup yacht. Four-inch HY-80 centerboard was bending in sea trials; Tom developed a 4340 welding procedure with 600°F preheat. Used to teach the cost/weldability tradeoff in high-strength alloy steels.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-80, HY-180 (rejected), 4340 steel; nickel-chrome-moly-vanadium
- **Era:** Likely ~1992–1995 (Tom recalls "10 or 12 years ago," then corrects to "20 years ago")

### USS Sea Wolf submarine high-side weld chemistry and HY-100 cracking
- **Anchor:** `§7.p1`, `§10.p1`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Seawolf HY-100 hydrogen cracking" (related: "Seawolf submarine hydrogen cracking", "SSN-21 Sea Wolf hull cracking problem")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Sea Wolf welding wire chemistry was on the high side of every alloying-element range, producing weld metal that behaved like HY-130 rather than HY-100. Cracking resulted. Repair required 400°F preheat with welders in cooled "blue jelly suits"; $500K hull required $2B repair.
- **Materials/systems:** HY-100, HY-130 (effective weld metal), welding wire chemistry control
- **Era:** Early 1990s

### Inland Steel HSLA development and Navy / Japan technology transfer
- **Anchor:** `§8.p2`–`§8.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Japanese HSLA shipbuilding steel development" (related: "1984-1985 Japan Manufacturing Study", "US Navy HSLA Steel Mill Investment", "Lukens Steel HSLA plate line government funding", "US Navy Japan steel mill visit and HSLA technology transfer")
- **Frame in this lecture:** Inland Steel pioneered water-spray cooling for HSLA sheet in the early 1960s but couldn't sell it to automakers. Japanese steel mills applied the technique to shipbuilding plate (sweetheart deal with shipyards). Tom's 1980s Navy-sponsored ONR trip to Japan led to $100M of Title III funding for Lukens Steel's HSLA plate line in the 1990s.
- **Materials/systems:** HSLA-80, ASTM A710, water-spray controlled cooling
- **Era:** 1960s (Inland), 1980s (Japan visit), 1990s (Lukens)

### USS Iowa turret explosion — Puerto Rico
- **Anchor:** `§10.p3`–`§10.p5`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "USS Iowa gun turret explosion"
- **Frame in this lecture:** 1989 turret explosion. Navy could not repair because they had lost the heavy-section peening technology for 14-inch armor steel welds; the turret was abandoned rather than repaired. Used to teach the disappearance of mid-century heavy-section welding expertise.
- **Materials/systems:** Heavy-section armor steel, peening for residual-stress control
- **Era:** 1989 (incident); WWII (original construction)

### Quonset Point submarine sections and Soviet satellite reconnaissance
- **Anchor:** `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Russian submarine hull thickness intelligence" (related: General Dynamics Electric Boat operations)
- **Frame in this lecture:** Submarine hull sections fabricated indoors or under tarps at Quonset Point because Soviet satellites could measure wall thickness from space, revealing classified diving depth. Used as an aside about how hull thickness encodes operational capability.
- **Era:** Cold War / 1980s

### Draper Lab titanium research submarine
- **Anchor:** `§4.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Draper Autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) prototype welding"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Quarter-inch wall, four-foot diameter (per layer 2; flagged) titanium sub built by Draper Lab. Purpose classified, construction unclassified. Used as a counterpoint to the 30-foot Navy sub for thickness/diving-depth scaling.
- **Materials/systems:** Titanium

### MGH MTA station rebuild — Stout & Doty lookup case
- **Anchor:** `§9.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1920s steel bridge repair consulting (MTA/MGH station)"
- **Frame in this lecture:** Consulting work for a Boston civil engineering company on a 1920s-vintage steel structure with no ASTM spec. Tom's procedure: get drillings analyzed for composition + thickness, match against Stout & Doty's table of 200 steels, retrieve welding procedure. Used to teach that being-an-expert is sometimes just knowing where to look.
- **Era:** 1920s (structure) / unspecified consulting period
