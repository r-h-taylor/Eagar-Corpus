# Case Index — Welding Metallurgy Summer 2014, Lecture 1

*Citation format: layer 3 paragraph anchor (e.g. `§3.p2`). Click-through in the static site reveals the corresponding layer 2 passage with timestamp range.*

Lecture 1 is the introductory framing session. Tom uses several historical and industrial cases to set up the energy-content-of-metals argument that motivates the corrosion course.

## Cases referenced

### 1930s all-aluminum Duesenberg automobiles
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "1930s all-aluminum Duesenberg automobiles"
- **Frame in this section:** Cited alongside the Mellon Pierce Arrow as proof that all-aluminum cars are not new technology; the question is economic, not technical.
- **Era:** 1930s

### Andrew Mellon all-aluminum Pierce Arrow automobile
- **Anchor:** `§3.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Andrew Mellon all-aluminum Pierce Arrow automobile"
- **Frame in this section:** Mellon's funding of Alcoa and his personal aluminum Pierce Arrow used to make the point that the all-aluminum car was demonstrated by the 1930s; what shifted is the price of gasoline.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum body, automotive
- **Era:** 1930s

### Aluminum automotive applications (1990s Ford Taurus vs. Toyota Camry)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Aluminum automotive applications (1990s Ford Taurus vs. Toyota Camry)"
- **Frame in this section:** Extended teaching case. Tom's repeated talk from the early 1990s arguing the all-aluminum mainstream sedan would not appear within 25 years; Peter Bridenbaugh of Alcoa's $4/gallon gas threshold.
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum vs. steel automotive body
- **Era:** early 1990s

### Audi-Alcoa aluminum vehicle development partnership
- **Anchor:** `§3.p3`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Audi-Alcoa aluminum vehicle development partnership"
- **Frame in this section:** Alcoa's collaboration with Audi to build the first all-aluminum Audi, cited via Peter Bridenbaugh's conference talks.
- **Materials/systems:** all-aluminum automotive body
- **Era:** early 1990s

### Soviet aluminum dumping and Alcoa/Norsk Hydro market collapse
- **Anchor:** `§3.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Soviet aluminum dumping and Alcoa/Norsk Hydro market collapse"
- **Frame in this section:** Post-Soviet aluminum export from Siberian plants as the only transportable form of stranded hydroelectric energy ("canned electricity").
- **Materials/systems:** aluminum, hydroelectric power
- **Era:** early 1990s

### Visby-class corvette (Swedish composite frigate)
- **Anchor:** `§4.p1`, `§4.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Visby-class corvette (Swedish composite frigate)"
- **Frame in this section:** Cited as the famous all-fiberglass combat ship that initially looked like a leapfrog over steel construction, with the later qualification that its flexibility under load is a structural disadvantage.
- **Materials/systems:** fiberglass composite hull
- **Era:** mid-1990s

### Mexican airline hot corrosion engine failure
- **Anchor:** `§6.p2`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** "Mexican airline hot corrosion engine failure"
- **Frame in this section:** Physical demonstration. Turbine blades from a Mexicali airline jet maintained by United Airlines used to show sulfidation hot corrosion. One corroded blade warped, one uncorroded blade straight.
- **Materials/systems:** nickel-base superalloy turbine blades, sulfidation
- **Era:** unspecified (referenced as in-service failure)

## Figures and framing statistics

### Energy content of metals (Ashby data)
- **Anchor:** `§3.p1`
- **Type:** framing statistics, not a case
- **Figures cited:**
  - Aluminum: 300 GJ/ton (80–90% of cost is energy)
  - Plastics: ~100 GJ/ton
  - Copper: 100 → 500 GJ/ton (ore grade fallen from 2–6% to <0.5%)
  - Steel: 50 GJ/ton
- **Note:** These figures recur across Eagar's lectures as the quantitative anchor for the "metals are stored energy" argument. Per the figures-vs.-cases distinction established in Lecture 11's case index, recorded here as a figure cluster rather than a case.

### Three native metals
- **Anchor:** `§2.p1`
- **Type:** framing claim
- **Content:** Gold, copper, platinum are the only metals found in nature in metallic form (with silver as a near-case, neutral on the Ashby diagram but unstable to sulfide).
