## Sectioning

The lecture is a course-overview / housekeeping session, but it contains substantial pedagogical content interleaved with administrative material. Section breaks fall at natural teaching transitions:

- §1 (00:00): introductions, scheduling, videotaping logistics.
- §2 (05:17): pivot to the available modules and what Belmar, Nielsen, and Lyons cover.
- §3 (10:55): pivot to course requirements and the "guess my lecture" autobiographical story.
- §4 (15:06): the 10-page paper requirement and the Alcoa aluminum can student-paper example.
- §5 (19:26): pivot to topic choice — past topics, proposal mechanics, and the elements (technetium, dysprosium).
- §6 (26:23): grading mechanics and Tom's no-tests philosophy.
- §7 (33:58): the broader teaching philosophy — high school math book story, Einstein/simplicity, Mars, Space Shuttle.
- §8 (40:06): half-truths in materials science — polyacetylene specific conductivity, 3D printing economics.
- §9 (45:29): communication craft — derivations, Tufte, PowerPoint.
- §10 (50:29): writing for your audience — World Trade Center article, recommended readings, sign-off.

Ten sections rather than the more typical 4-8, because the lecture covers a wider span of distinct teaching units than a single-topic session would.

## Decisions that are *not* permitted at layer 3

- Adding new sentences in Tom's voice (Feynman/Leighton/Sands could do this because Feynman was alive to ratify; we cannot).
- Reordering content across section boundaries.
- Merging cases that Tom kept separate, or splitting cases he handled as one.
- Removing factual errors Tom made on tape. (Captioner errors are corrected silently; Tom's slips are bracketed; Tom's genuine misstatements are preserved and footnoted in layer 3 only when they would actively mislead a reader.)
