## Cases developed as teaching units in this lecture

### Eastern Pennsylvania bicycle handlebar failure
- **Anchor:** `§6.p5` – `§6.p11`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Eastern Pennsylvania bicycle handlebar failure`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Hydrogen embrittlement from chrome electroplating without proper bake-out. 21-year-old rider doing jumping stunts on a 4130 steel jumping bike; handlebars fracture mid-jump. Crack initiated brittle (hydrogen embrittlement), grew by fatigue to ~60% of the cross-section, then ductile final failure. Plating shop in California didn't bake out the hydrogen per the ASM spec (1–4 hours from plating bath to oven; 375°F for 23 hours).
- **Materials/systems:** 4130 normalized steel tubing, electroplated chrome finish.
- **Era:** Not specified; appears to be a recent forensic case (the bake-out spec discussion is in present tense).

### Private Mach 2.5 aircraft crash and wing flutter failure (Peregrine Falcon)
- **Anchor:** `§7.p2` – `§7.p6`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Private Mach 2.5 aircraft crash and wing flutter failure` *(also matches: `Parag Green Falcon crash (first aircraft)` — see note)*
- **Frame in this lecture:** Kit-aircraft Mach 2 program in Nevada desert; sold engines from Pratt & Whitney military jet derivative with movie-star customers assembling the airframe themselves under FAA self-build rules. First aircraft blown up at 40,000 ft, test pilot killed. Second aircraft crashed; Tom called in by the owner (the second test pilot). Theory: supersonic wing flutter from a poorly adhesive-bonded lead damping weight detaching. No mechanical fastener backing up the adhesive bond.
- **Materials/systems:** 4130 steel and aluminum tubing airframe; lead damping weights on tail wings adhesive-bonded to steel arms.
- **Era:** "~20 years ago" as of 2014 → mid-1990s.
- **Note on cluster matching:** The aggregate has both `Private Mach 2.5 aircraft crash and wing flutter failure` and `Parag Green Falcon crash (first aircraft)`. These appear to be the same case captured under two cluster names from different lecture extractions (Tom's "Pereg— Green Falcon" stumble probably produced "Parag Green" in some prior pass). Recommend reconciliation in the aggregate.

## Cases referenced in passing

### Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor
- **Anchor:** `§2.p4`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Bethlehem Steel Burns Harbor`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Last company-built (non-government-subsidized) steel mill in the world. Burns Harbor, Indiana. Now part of ArcelorMittal. Tom worked there. Anchors the larger thesis that steel-mill greenfield construction is no longer commercially viable.
- **Era:** "Last one" (no specific date; greenfield construction in the 1960s per Tom's earlier framing).

### MIT teaching forge demonstration
- **Anchor:** `§4.p7`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Steel blade demonstration in teaching forge`
- **Frame in this lecture:** Mike Tarkanian's classroom demonstration: heat a steel bar, bend it 180°, quench in water, shatter with a hammer — showing untempered martensite brittleness. Used to anchor the "10× tougher than glass but still brittle" framing.

### Cast iron water main breakage practice
- **Anchor:** `§4.p9`
- **canonical_cluster_id:** `Cast-iron water main embrittlement and cracking` *(also relevant: `Boston and New York cast-iron water main failures`)*
- **Frame in this lecture:** Field-observation aside on how cast iron water mains are cut: sledgehammers, not saws. Tom describes 36-inch diameter water main repairs as a brittle-fracture demonstration. Used to anchor the "gray cast iron is brittle but tougher than glass" framing.

## Figures referenced (recurring numeric anchors, not cases)

- **Steel mill construction cost:** $15 billion for a complete mill; $1 billion for a hot rolling mill; $1.5 billion for a cold rolling mill. (§2.p3, §2.p5, §3.p1, §3.p2)
