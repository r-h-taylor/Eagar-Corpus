## §4. Ferrite, austenite, martensite, bainite — and how they were named [09:58]

`§4.p1` We can also transform the steel by quenching it and tempering and doing other things. Ferrite — how did we get the term ferrite? Well, there was a petrographer. Anybody know what a petrographer is? Any geologist here? A petrographer is someone who polishes rocks and looks at them in a microscope to look for the different minerals in the rock. For example, granite is composed of three different minerals: feldspar, quartz, and something else. I can't remember the third, I'm not a geologist.

`§4.p2` Henry Sorby in England was a petrographer. He said, what if I do this with a piece of steel? If you polish a piece of steel — you grind it smooth and polish it — you'll end up with a mirror, doesn't look like much of anything. He had the bright idea to etch it with some acid, and when he did that, it sort of goes frosty when you look at it with your eye. Put it in the microscope, and you can see grain structure, you can see the individual crystals. Being a petrographer, being a geologist, he said, well, it's an iron-based steel, and what do you call things that are iron? You use the Latin name of ferrite. And you put I-T-E in to make it a mineral name. So this was ferrite. Low carbon steel, the alpha form, which is from the phase diagram over here, is called ferrite, and it's unremarkable other than the grain size.

`§4.p3` Then later, people had ways to heat the steel up and do other things, and they learned about this face-centered cubic phase at higher temperatures, and they named that austenite, again using the geology-type stuff, after a British metallurgist called Roberts-Austen.

`§4.p4` People had known for centuries you could heat the steel up into this FCC, quench it in oil, water — they didn't know about air quenching at the time, they didn't have the alloys — you could quench it in oil or water, or a nice Nubian slave. One of the things the people in the Mideast used to like to do is heat the steel up into its thousand degrees centigrade, and they liked to quench it in a live slave. Because it would give virility to the steel. Didn't give much virility to the slave, but nonetheless, you can quench it in blood too if you want. Okay, we don't do it much anymore.

`§4.p5` So they ended up finally calling the hard phase martensite, after — I think he was Swedish — Martens was a metallurgist in the 1890s or so. And then bainite is a hard form of steel formed with a different crystal structure. While martensite — if I were to take martensite, I could take a piece of chalk and snap it into two, and it's brittle. So is martensite in the as-quenched condition. Bainite in the as-quenched condition, you can bend it into a U, and it has almost the same strength as the martensite. It's because of a different type of transformation.

`§4.p6` Bainite was not discovered or understood until about 1930, and Edgar Bain was a metallurgist at US Steel Research Laboratories, which later were called Bain Laboratories. Bainite is named after Bain. You can quench in oil, water, or just let it cool in the air, which they call an air quench. If you do that to martensite, it'll be glass brittle. Actually, it'll be about 10 times tougher than glass, but it's still pretty brittle. You hit it with a hammer and it'll shatter.

`§4.p7` In fact, I think Mike Tarkanian does that in the forge down there. Because martensite in the untempered condition is extremely brittle. I would say glass brittle, but it's not really glass brittle, it's about 10 times tougher than glass. In fact, cast iron is about 10 times tougher than glass, even though we think of gray cast iron as a very brittle material, and it is. It's just not as brittle as glass.
